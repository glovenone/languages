Marble Duel (originally named Evy's magic spheres) is in essence a simple marble popper.

But don't let appearances deceive you, cause it is anything but simple!

There is a bit of a backstory were a liitle orphant girl is left, as a baby, on the steps of an orphanage.
10 years later the little girl ventures into the attic and sees a shiny light behind the doors of a closet.
When she opens the closet it turns out to be a portal to some kind of fairy land.
She enters the fairy world and then encounters all kind of weird creatures which she has to defeat.

It starts out reasonably simple and straight forward, but just when you start to think: "Ohh this is easy, maybe a bit boring even...."
Then it starts to get hard, really really hard, even wicked hard and (seemingly) unfair even!

You start with 1 sphere, that doesn't have additional features and only some health points.
Beating a level gives you points you can use to boost your power-ups.
Initially you can pnly boost your health points, later on other power-ups get added/earned and in the end you have: life, attack, healing, defense, attack power up and reflection.
You also get additional spheres, which give a little boost to either your healing, your reflection, your defense or your attack.

So plenty choices!

But......
the AI is really powerfull and no matter how many power ups you have earned you seem to be always outskilled and overpowered. So you"ll face defeat after defeat after defeat after......well you get the drift!

I started to get frustrated, I started cursing at my screen, I was constantly on the verge of abondoning the game, because it is so unfair and so unbalanced!

To name a few unfair features:
-The AI always has the better view over the battle field and can reach corners you can't
-When there are separate rows for the AI and the human player, the AI always can reach your rows as well, and vice versa: nope!
-When you get to the point were you get 3 balls, instead of the original 2, then the AI always gets the perfect balls to make great line ups! Why? Because it "knows" what the 3rd ball is. You don't. So very very often you start to line up a perfect cascade only to find out that the 3rd ball is not the one you need! And then the AI uses your line up against you!
-And ohh wait, we also have exploding balls! They have a timer and have to be used in match before the timer runs out, otherwise: instant kill! No matter how many health points you still have! And those exploding balls? They only hurt you, not the AI!

Frustration upon frustration upon frustration.........

So why keep trying?

Because it is annoyingly addictive!

So at some points it must be doing things right!

How does it do things right?
Because it is just not a simple marble popper!
There is strategy involved.
Remember those points you get for power ups? They can be reassigned, over and over!
And you reaaly need to do that constantly!
Some levels are defense powers, some about attack powers, some about deflection.
High attack power certainly isn't the answer to everything!

So don't make the mistake of assigning your points and leaving them there, cause you will get beaten over and over!

Reassign them and decide upon strategy over and over!

But......
The reassigning is a reall pain!
You can't toggle them on and off: you wipe the sheet clean and have to reassign all points again!
That is A LOT of clicking! Very annoying!
Clicked a time too many? No taking it back: start over!

IMO this power up points assigning system needs to be redevised so that you can just take points down and add them to another skill. And preferably with a +/- button AND in batches of 5.
Cause in the end it is like 50 clicks to assign all points!

Can I recommend the game?
Yes, but not wholeheartedly, cause the frustration gets so high!
I can recommend it, because it is a real challenge, because it has a twist, because it involves strategy.

But there is also a number of reasons why I can't recommend this game, but those flaws I already described.

Ohh and then there is this after level 119:
http://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=718667332

Seriously!?
I tried so hard to beat this game and getting the upper hand, only to find out it isn't even finished!

To sum it up: highly recommended if you are a glutton for punishment!

Otherwise 3 out of 5 at best.

Marble Duel is a casual mobile style game created by the indie developer Hero Craft that takes a refreshing approach to the puzzle genre. This game is similar to Zuma’s revenge where spheres are thrown to stop a moving line of spheres to reach the boss in each level. Marble Duel instead sets a game up with stationary spheres making for a slower turned based game than alternatives, each colour sphere can deal damage, heals, shield or launch special attacks. The catch is that yourself and your opponent is given certain spheres to use in each game which you can switch using the right mouse click, I found it quite frustrating when the game forced me to heal in a turn.

The game’s simplistic design allows for quick loading times which is ideal for mobile gaming while moving about, thought has been made in designing the artwork for the game with typical fantasy characters. Dialogue between characters adds another level of immersion as you play through levels, hence the RPG tag under the game. I felt that this has been exaggerated as the core reason why I enjoyed the game was because of the challenging puzzles latter in the game which got me nostalgically rehooked into this genre. After completing each level, you are rewarded with gems which you may use for upgrades such as healing, health or damage which will help your effectiveness against an opponent. Gems are rewarded according to how few amount of turns you took in completing a level against an opponent and can be replayed. You are able to reset upgrades allowing you to prepare for another opponent where you may be better off focusing on healing upgrades for example.

In each duel the opponent has different levels of upgrades as well as different spheres to choose from. The upgrade system I found to be easy when playing for the first time and the brief tutorial clarified all the confusion I had in my first game. The game also has bonus rounds where you do not face an opponent and need to complete an objective such as stop a circle of spheres from hitting a beam of light. This bonus games were not challenging and felt like the same thing each time, these could be easily beaten by simply breaking spheres without mistakes by not colour matching them. The rotation of the circles made these bonus levels unnecessarily longer than needed, which is a shame as the game is brilliant in the main levels. Another thing I found annoying in this game was the background music when starting up the game and playing through dialogue, this just not to my taste but did sound familiar to other music I have listened to. Sound effects in this game however were superb and ticked something in my brain that made myself want to play more and more for the reward of hearing the sound again, ridiculous… I think not!

Each sphere in the game applies something different for your character to do, green spheres heal, blue spheres shield an attack and red spheres attack. The concept is easily understood for a new user and the game does introduce more colour spheres and further abilities gained by upgrading later in the game for a greater challenge. To apply the above trait in a turn you need to clash two or more of the same colour spheres by launching a sphere. There are combos which you can get by breaking the same colour in the middle of three of the same colour on the outside, when a combo is made you are allowed to make an additional turn. Green and Blue spheres affect your own attributes and overheal is not in this game so bear that in mind when healing at max heath! Red spheres are your fighting power and deal damage to an opponent in every game, therefore your upgrades are usually focused on attacking.

Dialogue and story in this game felt unmemorable and irrelevant to the purpose of this review therefore I have not gone in depth about this. Marble Duel offers a highly addictive game simular to Zuma’s Revenge made by Pop Cap but is far more strategic and slower passed than this alternative. I love both games, however I prefer this turn-based variation of the sphere clashing style of gameplay. This game is a brilliant choice and to be honest I have been long awaiting another game bearing this the sphere clashing style of gameplay since I got bored of Zuma’s revenge for IOS. I recommend this game for anyone who want a fun game to play on a laptop or tablet device in their spare time and is an excellent choice to buy on sale.

Marble Duel is a matching game in which you play as Evy, a little girl which in the middle of the night steps inside a closet and finds herself in an unknown and fairy land. That said, having never played a game of this sort, I kind of went blind into this one.

One thing I noticed from the ground up was the fact that the options menu is extremely lacking. There is only the option to mute sound effects and music, no sliders to adjust the volume. As for the video options, the only thing you get is being able to choose what resolution you want to run the game on. For the type of game that is, maybe the only thing missing in video options would be v-sync and the ability to choose from windowed, windowed borderless and fullscreen. I was also kind of disappointed by the fact that the dialogue is not voice acted, even if there is little of it.

If you’ve played any matching games you’ll most likely feel at home with this one. The concept is pretty simple, you arrive at this fantasy land and you progress through various areas, each with their own monsters. These areas are composed of various levels, each one representing an encounter with one of the monsters or some sort of mini-game. The combat in these encounters is turn-based, in the sense that you shoot spheres that you have and then it’s the enemy turn. However, you only start with 2 spheres per turn but by matching several colors in a row, you can gain more spheres for that turn. Each sphere has its color, and each color has its own effect. Red allows you to attack, green heals you, blue gives you a shield that absorves damage, purple increases the strength of your next attack and yellow allows you to reflect a portion of damage dealt by the enemy. The key in each of these encounters is to deplete the enemy life bar, and while this might feel easy at first, as the game progresses and as more spheres come into play the difficulty will rise. That said, sometimes I felt like the AI was cheating, in the sense that it would get the sphere it needed at the right time.

Depending on the amount of turns you take to beat a level you’ll be granted a bronze, silver or gold star. The better the star the more gems you’ll receive, and you can use these to upgrade your life, attack, healing and other abilities. If you want to get everything perfect, or if you get stuck on a level and need to upgrade your stats, you can replay previous levels. Still, you won’t gain more gems if you’ve already achieved that star rating previously.

Overall, I feel like what makes Marble Duel really worth playing (if this is your type of game), is the fact that you have the whole time of the world to make your move in what is already a very casual, satisfying and pleasant matching game. It is even more engrossing if you’re one of those that wants to get a perfect score on all the levels. The story is there, but it is skippable, even if the characters are unique and if the dialogue is nicely put together.

Marble Duel is a match-three game where you follow the story of the orphan girl Evy who finds herself caught up in a world of magic.

If you're familiar with Luxor or Zuma then you'll feel right at home playing Marble Duel, if not then don't worry too much as the gameplay is very quick to learn.

There are two main features that separate Marble Duel from Luxor and other games inspired by it, firstly is that Marble Duel faces you off against AI opponents every round and your goal is to beat them rather than amass points. Secondly the game play is turn based so rather than a continual flow of spheres, you take turns against your opponents. This makes playing a little more relaxing as you are not rushing against anything. Instead of the clock causing difficulty you have an AI. The aim of each mission is simply to reduce the health of your enemy to zero.

You start with just one sphere: the red sphere. This will allow you to match three reds with each other to deal damage to your opponent and once his health hits 0, you win. Though you only have one colour starting sphere this will quickly change; as you advance through levels you'll unlock a variety of different coloured spheres. These include blue spheres for defence (blocks a percentage of damage), green for life (heals an amount of your health), purple for attack damage (gives your next attack extra damage) and yellow for attack deflection (sends a percentage of the damage you take back to your opponent). There are also white spheres which don't seem to do anything but block matches and black spheres which deal damage to whoever matches three of them together, though you cannot fire either of these spheres yourself.

When you complete a mission you are awarded with gems which can be spent to upgrade your various spheres and your health. Each stage can be completed in a set number of turns to earn yourself gold, silver or bronze stars which will reap you more gems and consequentially more upgrades. Once a stage is completed it can be revisited at any time to earn any bonus stars you missed and this can be very helpful if you're struggling with later levels because your stats aren't high enough. The stage will remain the same difficulty and chances are you'll have upgraded your stats by then making the stage easy to complete within the allotted turns. It's worth trying to get as many stars as possible as the stats of your opponents will increase over time and you'll need plenty of upgrades to fairly compete. You can view your opponents stats before entering each level allowing you to reset and redistribute your own if you see fit.

The only thing I could see some people not enjoying about this game is the story. Whilst I enjoyed it I think a lot of people would skip it as it isn't pivotal to the gameplay at all and is a little thin at times. That being said the story itself is pretty cute and I rather liked helping Evy fight her way through a whole world she didn't mean to get involved in. The characters are enjoyable and the dialogue is fun.

So far I've sunk many hours into Marble Duel and I know I'll be playing it a lot more. The relaxing gameplay and wonderful art style makes for a great experience and I think any casual gamer or Luxor fan should give this one a try as they won't be disappointed.

Marble Duel is mess of a game that should've stayed on mobile platforms, or at least, been properly rebalanced for PC gaming.

Before I launch into everything that's bad about this game (and there's LOTS to say about that!), let me give you some background on Marble Duel.

Originally, the game was called Evy: Magic Spheres and sold on mobile platforms, as well as Desura. When coming to Steam, the name was changed to Marble Duel. Personally, I think the old name sounds better, as the current title reminds me of Marble Madness, which is nothing like this game at all.

Marble Duel looks similar to games like Luxor or Zuma, but it plays very differently. You need to match three or more colors to do something (e.g. damage the enemy, raise shields on yourself, heal yourself, or increase your attack power), but unlike those two other games, Marble Duel is turn-based as opposed to real-time action.

The goal is simple: kill your opponent before they kill you. During the game, you'll be able to increase your skills, which directly translates to differently colored marbles. You can respec at any time, so if you put points into something you don't need much, it's easy to fix.

The graphics are entirely 2D and the artwork for the cutscenes are passable. The audio is okay, but I prefer it turned off so I can play my own music.

The story is utterly forgettable and more a nuisance than anything else. In this sort of puzzle game, I'm far less interested in the storyline (especially when it's written so poorly) than I am in actual gameplay.

Now the bad stuff... yeah, what I wrote up above is what I'd consider the "good" part of Marble Duel.

The developer KNOWINGLY placed false screenshots on the store. His response to this when someone on the boards called him out on it was this: "Well, t'would be awfully hard to explain what's actually going on in the game without those graphic additions."

In other words, the developer doesn't really care that he use false imagery to sell his product.

Because Marble Duel was designed with mobile games in mind, the game was balanced for in-app purchases. Fortunately, the developer removed those before putting it on Steam, but in the process, failed to rebalance the game as well. That means the game's difficulty is such that it expects you to purchase gems or coins (or whatever stupid in-game currency was used in the mobile version) and without that feature, Marble Duel quickly becomes so difficult that it's impossible to play.

Here are three examples:

1. When playing the game, the line of marbles extends off the screen and wraps around elsewhere. The AI can apparently "see" what's off screen and can make moves based on that knowledge. The player, however, cannot see off screen and is therefore at a disadvantage.

2. Which marbles you get to shoot and match is randomized. That's supposed to be part of the strategy in learning to make moves with future moves in-mind. Unfortunately, the AI will ALWAYS receive whatever marbles it needs to make a move. If the AI needs a red marble or a green marble, it will get them, whereas the player will have to rely on pure chance.

3. I've played one stage over 60 times (and lost each time) where if I win, I get a new amulet with bonus health. The problem is the AI starts with huge advantages over me. The AI has 400 HP, and can make THREE turns each round. In contrast, the highest my health can be right now (with all available points redistributed to my health stat) is 80 HP, and each round I get TWO turns.

All in all, I'd strongly suggest staying away from Marble Duel. If I could get a refund, I would, but I've played more than two hours worth of the game.

If you're desperate for a puzzle game, go elsewhere, as there are tons of them on Steam. If you're deadset on getting Marble Duel, wait for a sale. I know the normal price is $4.99 USD, but I'd seriously recommend holding off until the price is $1 or less. Even then, the frustration you'll feel when playing may not be worth it.

Marble Duel is a match-three game where you follow the story of the orphan girl Evy who finds herself caught up in a world of magic.

If you're familiar with Luxor or Zuma then you'll feel right at home playing Marble Duel, if not then don't worry too much as the gameplay is very quick to learn.

There are two main features that separate Marble Duel from Luxor and other games inspired by it, firstly is that Marble Duel faces you off against AI opponents every round and your goal is to beat them rather than amass points. Secondly the game play is turn based so rather than a continual flow of spheres, you take turns against your opponents. This makes playing a little more relaxing as you are not rushing against anything. Instead of the clock causing difficulty you have an AI. The aim of each mission is simply to reduce the health of your enemy to zero.

You start with just one sphere: the red sphere. This will allow you to match three reds with each other to deal damage to your opponent and once his health hits 0, you win. Though you only have one colour starting sphere this will quickly change; as you advance through levels you'll unlock a variety of different coloured spheres. These include blue spheres for defence (blocks a percentage of damage), green for life (heals an amount of your health), purple for attack damage (gives your next attack extra damage) and yellow for attack deflection (sends a percentage of the damage you take back to your opponent). There are also white spheres which don't seem to do anything but block matches and black spheres which deal damage to whoever matches three of them together, though you cannot fire either of these spheres yourself.

When you complete a mission you are awarded with gems which can be spent to upgrade your various spheres and your health. Each stage can be completed in a set number of turns to earn yourself gold, silver or bronze stars which will reap you more gems and consequentially more upgrades. Once a stage is completed it can be revisited at any time to earn any bonus stars you missed and this can be very helpful if you're struggling with later levels because your stats aren't high enough. The stage will remain the same difficulty and chances are you'll have upgraded your stats by then making the stage easy to complete within the allotted turns. It's worth trying to get as many stars as possible as the stats of your opponents will increase over time and you'll need plenty of upgrades to fairly compete. You can view your opponents stats before entering each level allowing you to reset and redistribute your own if you see fit.

The only thing I could see some people not enjoying about this game is the story. Whilst I enjoyed it I think a lot of people would skip it as it isn't pivotal to the gameplay at all and is a little thin at times. That being said the story itself is pretty cute and I rather liked helping Evy fight her way through a whole world she didn't mean to get involved in. The characters are enjoyable and the dialogue is fun.

So far I've sunk many hours into Marble Duel and I know I'll be playing it a lot more. The relaxing gameplay and wonderful art style makes for a great experience and I think any casual gamer or Luxor fan should give this one a try as they won't be disappointed.
