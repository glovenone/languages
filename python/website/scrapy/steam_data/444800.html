About This Game

The year is 1994 and it is the future.


NO THING is a minimalistic surreal action game set in a totalitarian regime of future.

Tells the story of an office clerk who is sent with an important message to the Queen of Ice.

NO THING Features:

  • radical gameplay
  • migraine inducing
  • weird storyline
  • retro surrealistic full 3D graphic
  • full voiceover and speech synthesis
  • chiptune sounds
  • original cold wave music
  • simple controls
  • easy to play, very hard to master
  • platforms: Windows, Mac, Linux
  • the year is 1994 and it is the future

This is a minimalistic endless runner with 1980s style futuristic visuals envisaging a dystopian 1990s, made in the 21st century. Seems a good idea, eh?

All you do is push the left and right buttons to turn, and stay on the road. Sounds easier than it actually is.

Its levels that are just a bit too long, so I really hate it when I lose near the end of a level, because that means I have to do the exact same thing yet again and hopefully not screw up and the end.

Also, the runner doesn't run in exact straight lines parallel to the roads you run on, so if you land too near to the edge of a long stretch, your runner may fall off near the end. You can't really correct your errors - your runner can only turn every so often so for example, you can't turn then turn back again fast enough to move "slightly to the left" to correct your errors.

The game is irritating like that.

Thankfully, as you run, some charming electronic music plays and an electronic voice speaks, and it gives you something to ponder about the world of the game, whoever the voice is, and whoever your character is.

...actually, I switched the music off after a while, because it get a bit irritating after the first few hundred times because it becomes repetitive and was too chilled out and I wasn't. It's not a fault of the music, but a reaction to it. I put on some faster-paced music and found that I was playing better. Is this cheating?

Overall, I recommend this game. It's simple, and it's annoying, but it makes my brain wake up, and despite its flaws, I seem to keep coming back to it.

Posted: 23 April
NO THING is an oddly addictive runner game with a great soundtrack and a brilliant story...if you care to listen close enough

The idea of NO THING is very simple: you are a simple office clerk with a message you must deliver to the Ice Queen. The gameplay consists of you navigating levels with nothing more than your left and right keys. Though it sounds easy at first, NO THING is anything but.
You start on level one which is an easy introduction of what is to come. You must follow the path in front of you turning left and right when needed. There is a voice speaking to you as you play, telling you things, some which make sense and some which don't (not right away anyway) and a rather awesome soundtrack playing. You'll see images whilst you play that'll change depending on what's being said. You should spend your time playing NO THING listening and looking very carefully.

The levels progressively get harder, not just from level one to level two, but whilst you are actually playing them. Platforms will move, appear and disappear. Graphics will flicker, glitch (intentional) and change colour. Images will change, enlarge, shrink and move through platforms. Everything that happens is there to test your focus and concentration; this is not a game for those who suffer from motion sickness or anything similar. NO THING is all about your perception of things and requires you not only to evaluate your surroundings quickly but to also ignore all of the background distraction. Platforms come in different sizes, some being thin and some wider. There are others which are slanted, some which don't properly connect and some which are slightly off centre. You must be alert at all times whilst playing because even clicking half a second too late could be the difference between winning a level and starting all over again. As you run you gain speed continually though I've not yet worked out if this is linked to the amount of turns you take or how long you've been running for. Your speed can work for you or against you, often being helpful for flying from one platform to others that would have been out of reach at a lower speed, but it also makes things a lot harder as your reaction speed has to increase with it. You can find red blocks in certain places to reduce your speed if things start getting a little out of control.
Falling from a platform doesn't always mean death though. Many levels weave in and out of each other and are on different heights so if you can react quick enough you can find yourself at another part of the level and just continue running.
Your paths will grow as you progress in the stage and some of them require you to repeatedly run the same path multiple times before something new opens. Often you'll find this will happen quickly and throw you off guard. You can also take different paths, though they'll all eventually lead to the same place, after all, choice is just an illusion in a totalitarian world.

But the enjoyment of NO THING doesn't just come from the gameplay itself, what really grips you is the story and the way you experience it. Everything, from the dialogue to the music to the images, it just keeps you wanting to play, to find out more, to reach the end. You'll spend the game trying to work out what's going on in this world, with you, and the voice but slowly you'll piece things together bit by bit.
I found NO THING to be oddly relaxing. When the gameplay wasn't frustrating because i'd failed so many times, the pressing right and left at the right time combined with the soundtrack was oddly calming. At some points when you find your rhythm the game flows perfectly and the levels are long enough that when they aren't too hard and you know the route you need to take, it just works.

There are only two negative things I can really find about the game, the first is the lack of some kind of a free runner mode. Given the games intense difficulty it would be enjoyable to have endless (or large) levels to practice on so you don't have to continuously repeat a level you're stuck on until you beat it. This is directly link to my second issue which is the fact that the game can feel repetitive at times if you cannot complete a level. There was one particular part where I was reaching 99% and missing the last turn meaning I had to replay the entire level over and over hearing the same dialogue. Though the voice-over is what makes the game so good, hearing it for the 12th time after doing the same bit of a very long level 12 times is rage-inducing. I think a checkpoint system would help but in the same breath I think it'd take something away from the game. The grinding and repetitiveness is a good mirror of life in a totalitarian regime.

I don't know if it was the intention of NO THING to be a dark mirror of reality but I think it does it perfectly. I've never found a game for $4 that's as addictive as this and I really can't recommend this game enough, the experience has been amazing so far. Though difficult at times, the challenge is worth it. How hard can it be, after all, you are just the simplest office clerk. You have one message and you know what you should do.

I'd like to start this review by saying that I'm not a fan of the runner genre. I find this game to be immensely frustrating, as such, but I gave myself five before coming back to review it.

So what is NO THING? It's fast-paced, unforgiving, and challenging. It falls into the endless runner genre in which you keep on moving forward until you either crash into something or fall off of the track. It has a good--no *great* soundtrack and an abstract, funky art style.

It's also frustrating as all hell. Due to my own failings, of course, so I can't really blame the game for that.

.Premise.
You're a messenger of sorts. The simplest office clerk. You're delivering a message to the Queen of Ice. You'll find out more about yourself through the robotic narrator as you run through and complete the stages.

.Gameplay.
Run. You move forward automatically and it's up to you to turn left or right to avoid death. That's all there is to it. The game tries its best to distract you with on-screen visual interferences (like colour changes and graphical glitching/blurring) and a nonsensical "story" that punctuates moments in your progress with a robotic voice reading a line of text that flashes across the screen.

As you progress, things get more difficult. The speed of your run increases, and so the precision of your turns will need fine-tuning.

Focus is the name of the game here and if you're easily distracted, you'll fall out of your groove and go plummeting to your death. Once you die, you start the level again from the beginning. Then, you try again and again until, finally, you either finish the stage or hit the escape key and go play something else while you cool off.

You can use either keyboard or gamepad. Either works just as well.

.Soundtrack.
So good. I've only managed to get to the second level in my hour of play, but the first track was synthetic... upbeat... electronic... awesome and the second track was a slower, more mellow synthetic upbeat electronic awesome. I assume each stage has its own individual song that plays throughout the entire run.

.Graphics/ Art Style.
Trippy to say the least. The face on the game's banner appears throughout the stages, which are always awash in bright, solid colours. If you have a history of epilepsy, please do NOT play this game.

.Longevity.
That depends on your skill level. I'm god-awful at this game and it took me about fifteen minutes to get through the first level. The remainder of my time was spent floundering over level two. If you're an expert runner, you probably won't get as much time out of this as the average Joe.

.Bottom line.
If you're a fan of the runner genre, this game will be a great addition to your library. If you're on the fence about the genre or are new, you may still get some enjoyment from the soundtrack and surreal atmosphere. Just be warned: patience is a virtue.
