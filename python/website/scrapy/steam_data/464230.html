About This Game

As an apprentice magician and alchemist, you learn from different alchemy masters. They teach you to mix elixirs, create potions and control them on alchemist table. Prove everyone that you are a born alchemist!

Key features:
- 60 levels of varying difficulty in 5 scenarios;
- realistic fluid animation and physics;
- challenging puzzles;
- fancy hand-drawn graphics

P.S. This game was initially designed for devices with accelerometer but for PC we decided to add WASD and arrow controls, that made the game even more difficult and entertaining!

First impression: tilting labyrinth game

This game is a descendant of the wooden labyrinth where you have to guide a ball through by tilting the surface. Except that in this case, the ball is water, you can split it up into separate drops or combine them, and you must color them correctly before guiding them to their targets. It probably plays better with an analog stick than with the keyboard.

The game didn't motivate me enough to learn the skills required to master it. I believe it lacks feedback, both sound effects and scoring: there is no indicator in the level of the dealines for the score levels or for the sizes of color blobs required to complete it.

The game runs well on my old Windows XP computer.

The good parts: Interesting and clever puzzle mechanics. Charming artwork (if you like the style, which I do).

Neither good nor bad: Using WASD/arrow keys to "tilt" a board to move droplets around goes from "simple fun" to "amazingly difficult" very quickly. If you like challenging physics-based puzzles, that part's great. If you like games with lots of easy puzzles so you can enjoy the sense of progress, you're not likely to enjoy this.

Tip: After the first few levels - GO SLOW. Tiny little taps move the droplets around; too many too fast will shatter them against walls or objects and you'll never get them to move where you need them. (There's a "reset level" button; you can keep trying. But it's going to be frustrating for many players. Action-reflex players will be frustrated at how slow it is; kids--the apparent target demographic--will be frustrated because it's unforgiving of mistakes.)

The bad parts: I can't get it to run windowed; I've tried several methods. (Lack of ability to run windowed really is what convinced me not to bother playing even when I was in the mood for it.) As others have mentioned, it seems incomplete. There's no story at all, no explanation of why you're doing this "alchemy" for the wizard. No sound at all. (Someone else said there's a single music track; apparently it doesn't play on my system.) After a few puzzles to get used to the setting, there's a strong sense of "why bother?"... the best thing about it is that ctrl+esc lets it sit in my taskbar and collect cards.

"No story" is fine for some physics puzzles... but this one sure looks like it was intended to have some context.

Conclusion: I paid 49 cents for this on sale, and don't feel I was cheated - but even at that price, I wouldn't recommend it unless the buyer loved physics-based puzzles AND had time to burn on a slow-paced game AND didn't care that it's just an endless set of tasks with no rewards attached AND doesn't mind the game being full-screen.

A cross between Puddle and marble tilt games.

You tilt the playfield to move bits of liquid through color changing symbols, through static and moving pieces, and into color coded vial symbols, while avoiding falling into hole symbols or off of the playfield.

This may seem awfully mundane and straightforward, but it's actually quite challenging, varied, and at times clever - although at times it can feel pointlessly frustrating too.

The fluid physics simulation works quite nicely (as in gameplay, not realism), and the controls (using an analog stick, at least) feel, uh, fluid...

It held my attention for an hour and a half until I got tired of the game's last few levels, which are very hard. Then there's also an achievement for finishing 50 of the 60 levels under the lowest par time, which is also very, very hard.

Overall, this simple but challenging physics puzzle is worth at least an hour of your time.

Public Review on behalf of the Opium Pulses Steam group

Disclaimer: The copy of this game was provided by the developer for review purposes, yet it doesn't affect my review in any way.

Few words about the game
Colours of Magic: Aqua Teeter - A fun and entertaining puzzle game, where your goal is to lead liquid to right elixir. Sounds easy? Wait a few levels, it gets challenging after a while.

Advantages
  • Nice concept of the game overall
  • Creative levels
  • Realistic physics of your potion
  • Pleasant hand-drawn graphics
  • Very low price for fun content
  • Achievements and cards are nice bonus

Disadvantages
  • Soundtrack could be more developed
  • Buggy here and there - after minimization graphics sometimes get bugged (http://prnt.sc/ay841h), can't turn on sounds after turning them off

Summary
It's a fun game in its simplicity. For people complaining that there's a low ammount of content or anything like that - it was designed this way, to be a nice and little game to play for a while and have fun. There's quite a lot of levels with a variety of difficulties, so it's totally enough to spend few good hours in the game to pass it. In my opinion, it'd be very nice if mouse control would be added. Luckily, it's not heavily bugged game, so I can sincerely say that it was a pleasure to play it. The only thing that could be upgraded is a soundtrack, but it's a minor thing - except that, I totally recommend this game for what it is.

Personal score
8.5 / 10

Its a really nice game to pass the time, some levels are challenging at first but really easy when you come back later. Some things seem rather unpolished yet, such as some walls having holes through which the fluid can slip on accident with it hits the wall with too much force. When I start the game I get a CMD-like window that doesn't disappear until I exit the game, but on fullscreen it is unnoticable. I'd have wished for some more text guidance in the beginning, some explaination what each Wizard wants to teach me or any sort of background story.
