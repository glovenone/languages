About This Game


On Rusty Trails is a modern, illustrative platformer, set in a world constantly reacting to your change in identity. You are Elvis. Home owner, triangular metal person and lover of dryness. A sudden thunderstorm leaves your house dissolved into a pile of rubble. Only lightning-fast reactions will keep you from a looming rusty funeral. Don your Shifty Suit to incognito yourself and walk among suspicious hairy folk. Perilous puzzles and prejudiced platforms will bar your way in this deceptively deep gaming dish.

Features

  • Slip into the Shifty Suit! – Blast through this fast-paced platformer and instantly shift from rusty red to hairy blue to influence how the whole environment reacts to you.
  • Slip into the Shifty Suit! – Blast through this fast-paced platformer and instantly shift from rusty red to hairy blue to influence how the whole environment reacts to you.
  • Still stepping on the floor? – Magnetic boots deliver a unique platform mechanic in multiple dimensions. Walk both walls and ceilings, and prepare for an adrenaline rush from treacherous puzzles.
  • A gloomy tale in finest comic art! – Feast your eyes on the trademark Black Pants art style in this beautiful and bleak platformer and its miniature world with even smaller inhabitants. Eyes! FEAST!
  • Spite and prejudice! – Stimulate your brains with a story about the loss of a low-rent flat, complicated misunderstandings and a looming war. And there is even forbidden romance.
  • It creaks, it cracks, it crunches!​ –​ A dark and melodic soundtrack with an eclectic selection of music. Haunting robotic sounds for your rainy days and the industrial revolution.​

On Rusty Trails is a indie platformer game created by Black Pants Studio that revolves around fast thinking and fast reactions. It boast some very simple but fun mechanics. The base mechanic is switching between “modes” to open up different routes or platforms. Other mechanics are getting very nicely introduced by in-game visuals or NPC interactions. This together with the great level design makes for several different ways to solve every level. You have for example the fastest route, the route with all the tokens or all the checkpoints. It creates a scaling difficulty level and a replayability value if you want to complete it in different ways. Easy to learn hard to master, these are the controls of On Rusty Trails. They can really work well and create a great flow in the game, but some jumps are so hard. It takes a lot of time to practice and get it straight, it’s so hard I found myself irritated by the controls.

Visually it is very stylized, it has a special feel to it and it looks amazing. The different levels and their own atmosphere, the bright and vivid colors give a lively feeling while the darker levels give you a scared and compressed feeling. There were just some minor problems. Sometimes it was hard to see what was standable and what was background and sometimes you die or get a checkpoint while you didn’t pass or hit it. This infuriated me while trying to get my 100% run.

Auditory this game is great. I really like the soundtrack and it sets the atmosphere. It has a cool beat to keep you going in the game. The sound effects feel very alive and they have a certain effectiveness to it. While playing the game without sound I missed the sound of Elvis clinging to the blocks, the leafs clattering, the searing magma and the lasers trying to burn me. It’s also handy as a sound cue in the later stages of the game.

Overall the game is a experimental platformer with fun new ideas. It has a global leaderboard which shows how many people are faster than you and it invites to speedrun. On Rusty Trails packs a 2-3 hour playtime to complete it if you are familiar with the genre. Luckily there is quite some replay value in this game so the 2-3 hours quickly expanded to 12 hours for me. The controls weren’t always great and the visuals have a few irritating quirks. But apart from that it’s still a solid game that I would recommend to fans of the genre and non-fans alike.

Don’t you just hate it when a radioactive goo-ball destroys your house?

On Rusty Trails is a fast-paced platformer from Black Pants Studios. In ORT you play as a small red robot called Elvis. After Elvis’ home is destroyed in a strange storm he decides to venture into the city to redeem his house warranty in order to get a new house. You would have thought this would be a fairly straightforward task but a combination of environmental hazards and rising tension between the two different factions that live within the city makes Elvis’ journey a dangerous one.

Before I get underway I would just like to mention that there are a few things that I’ve made the decision to not talk about in the review. They aren’t major but they are related to the story and I don’t want to spoil it for any prospective players.

The first thing that’ll strike you when you look at ORT is that it’s a fantastic looking game. The game looks good through and through but the in-game billboards are particularly good. They’re well designed, vibrant and create a real sense of the world you find yourself in. There’s also visual variation between all the different parts of the city which gives each section of the game its own flavour.

ORT’s gameplay is focused around a colour changing mechanic and it plays into the lore of the world you’re in. Elvis is a rusty red robot, one of the two in-game factions, the other is a furry blue monster looking faction that’s reminiscent of Sully from Monsters Inc. Early on in the game you find a monster suit which allows you to switch between the two. The city is split between the two factions and some platforms can only be used by red, others by blue. Switching between the two using the suit allows you to access the platforms you wouldn’t be able to use otherwise. If you’re red then any blue platforms will disappear as you come close to them but switching to blue will make them pop back out.

The switching mechanic does to some extent make the game into a reaction based game. There are a lot of parts in levels where you have to switch colours in very short amounts of time to avoid dying (if you do die you just return to your last checkpoint). Your colour doesn’t just decide what platforms you can use though, there are other environmental hazards. Some hazards only effect one colour, others only target one colour. These hazards are often combined with colour-specific platforms (there are some platforms in the game which can be used by either colour) which makes for particularly tricky levels. You’ll probably have to restart most levels/checkpoints a couple of times before you’ll get it.

The levels themselves are fairly short though there are quite a lot of them. I had to restart from checkpoints on most levels at least two or three times and the levels generally took between twenty and forty seconds. There are a handful of levels longer than that but they’re definitely in the minority.

Speaking of length, the whole game is fairly short. It took me just shy of three hours to reach the end of the game which for a game priced at £9.99 is a tad on the short side. The length is right for the game, it tells the story it wants to without any filler but like I said, I do think the price tag is a bit steep.

Elvis’ story is engaging and does a good job of tying all of the levels together but really it’s the relationship between the two factions in the game that I found to be the most interesting story element. The tension between the two factions increases as the game goes on and the relationship between the two factions is mainly shown to the player through the billboards I mentioned earlier. The changing relationship manages to be incredibly interesting despite the fact that the player is given very little information about the game world, in part due to the fact that it mirrors our own world.

Being a fast-paced platformer, the quality of the controls are obviously very important. Most of my playtime was spent with a controller and I can’t fault the controls, they were tight and very responsive. I also tried playing with my keyboard & mouse. The KB&M controls are also very good though I did feel as though it was ‘more natural’ to play with my Xbox One controller. It’s worth noting that your controls can be re-bound for KB&M or a controller.

In case it’s not clear, the gameplay is fairly simplistic. Other than the switch-colour mechanic there’s really not much else to do in the levels. There are tokens which can be collected but these are just used to activate checkpoints (or they can be saved but as far as I can tell there’s no benefit to not using them). However, I don’t think the simplistic gameplay is a bad thing, I think it’s great. Keeping it simple keeps your focus on getting through the level which helps to keep all of the levels fast-paced.

I’ve covered most of the major stuff but there are a couple of other things I’d like to mention. The sound track and the sound effects are great, they really help to make the world ‘come alive’. ORT sort of has a leaderboard but I’d like to see some improvements in this area. At the end of every level you get your completion time, your number of ‘growbacks’ (restarting from checkpoints), the number of tokens you saved and a number corresponding to ‘where you clocked in at’ aka your performance in relation to other players. What’s already there is good but an actual leaderboard for each level, preferably in the level select screen, would be great and I imagine it’d add a fair bit of replay value (currently lacking) to the game for those who like to go back in time-trial style games to improve their score.

Verdict

On Rusty Trails is a challenging but ultimately entertaining platformer that looks gorgeous, keeps you on your toes and looks at some serious real-world issues. I do feel as though it’s priced a little higher than it should be but it’s still worth picking up for fans of platformers.

A very tight, well-designed, well-concieved, polished platformer firmly in the vein of N and Super Meat Boy and Dustforce and all that good stuff. There's just enough new mechanics here to set it apart - the polarity-swapping thing lends itself to some very cool platforming setups late in the game, and lends itself to a very nice jump-swap-jump-swap rhythm. Controls are tight and fluid in that special way that just feels good. The excellent sound design and art all hits this very Meatboy-ish line of being quirky enough to have a distinct personality but not so much that it gets on your nerves.

My only gripes are:

- It's not lacking in value for money, but it feels like a disservice to such a well-designed game that some of the coolest mechanics are only used in the very last area. Maybe I'm just neurotic, but I would've liked the difficulty curve to be a bit steeper, and perhaps another area or two to really milk every last drop of potential.

- The final boss requires a skillset that has little to do with everything you've learned up to that point. A bit of an unsatisfying mess, but mercifully short.

Anyway, I flew through this the day it came out, and it kept me pretty engaged the whole time. It doesn't quite match its influences in eliciting that twitchy platformer trance state of an afternoon of endless retries, but it does carve a nice little niche for itself where it puts on a solid effort.
