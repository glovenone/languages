About This Game

Plovdiv, the year 2XXX. Men no longer exist and the population outside this city has mysteriously vanished. What remains is wholly under the control of mysterious shapeshifters who take the form of humans and monsters. Few believe the reports of their existence, and those who discover their existence don't often live long enough to tell the tale.

Aerannis is a game in which an assassin, Ceyda Farhi, uncovers the truth. Her missions involve sneaking around, taking hostages, using human shields, laying down traps, and assassinating those who are a part of the conspiracy. Countless massive, intense boss battles are seamlessly blended in with the stealth action.

Features:

  • Gorgeous, vibrant pixel art and a stellar soundtrack
  • A large interconnected world to explore
  • Fast, intense action
  • Emphasis on stealth and tricking enemies for quick, silent kills
  • Intertwined missions that unravel the conspiracy of the world

2D platforming and shooting with stealth elements. I was sold on that description alone. And Aerannis almost works! It's a painful non recommendation.

I can't comment on the story because I didn't see it through the end. It's pretty confusing so far. I think it's trying to approach topics like prejudice and sexual identity.

Right! let's talk about the meat of the game. Action platforming is good. You got your shooting and jumping, a small variety of enemies to jump and shoot around. It's not too deep though, it gets boring after a while. It also introduces a human shield mechanic but it doesn't fit the action segments very well most of the times, as it's better to just shoot them directly. Except when you have to use human shields to open doors! that is pretty neat. It's like finding keys, but the keys shoot back.

I was looking forward the stealth mechanic but it's the most disappointing part of the game. It's hard to describe why it doesn't work because stealth is extremely delicate, and tiny details can make or break the experience. Considering they went for missions where you get insta-killed when they spot you, they should have been extra careful with it. The lack of visual cues to read the guard's patterns is one problem; They just turn around instantly. There is also no timer for the guard to go from unalerted to 'you're dead'. A chance for you to get back to your hiding spot and correct tiny mistakes, while the guard goes into suspicious mode or something. There's also the case of climbing stairs (door like stairs that teleport you to neighbor floors) that lead you straight to a guard. Guards also bug out sometimes, forcing you to get caught just to respawn.

Again, tiny details. That make the whole stealth experience is unfulfilling. It's made even worse when you get the run upgrade or, as I like to call it, the double-tap curse. When trying to move slow and carefully it's normal to tap the directional. With the double-tap curse you'll often find yourself doing quick runs in place. Not enough to actually run but enough to alert guards around you.

Then there are the bugs. I was actually willing to finish the game with the criticism above in mind already, but the game has the awful habit of bugging out during dialogues and there's no way around closing the game by force. All 3 times that happened to me made me lose a huge chunk of gameplay, as they are usually at the end of missions, before you had a chance to save. So, if you're already cursing at the game's imperfections, having to replay an entire mission because the game bugged out is a deal breaker for me.

I bought this game for 6 people, including myself. I spent as much money on this game as I did on an AAA console title. My current steam avatar is the main character, and every social media site I go on, I have "I love Aerannis" in my bio. I am in love with this game, and the rest of this review will explain why.

I'll be honest, I was first interested in Aerannis because I heard it had a trans character. Call me an sjw all you want, but I only care about trans representation because I want to see myself more often in games. And holy hell, Aerannis delivered.

Ceyda Farhi is to trans representation what Samus Aran was to female representation before 2010. She's a capable badass who I would honestly love to be if not for the whole assassin thing. More than that, she's tired and angry from a world that either hates her or ignores her, and is on the verge of losing her mind confronting something larger than she can imagine. And yet she persists, she keeps going, just because she wants to see what's next.

I love Aerannis because it finally gave me a character I can relate to on a personal level, and didn't just give me a trans character, it captured the feeling that comes with it, from the discrimination to the dysphoria. It let me see myself in a game, on a fundamental level. If you're trans, especially trans female, get this game. You'll have a character that you can, if not look up to, at least project a bit of yourself on.

As for the other aspects of the game, just look at the other reviews, I have pretty much the same opinions as they do. Really nice aesthetic, interesting little story, suboptimal (but still alright) controls, inorganic stealth sections, action sections that are fun but not as fun as Megaman, too short, and bad ending. I'd still recommend the game, even if you're not trans, because it's good enough to be worth at least $7. Plus, I really don't like being in an empty community, and if this game's community isn't empty I don't know what is.

Aerannis Is a platformer set in a distopian near future. Oh, and there's the illuminati.

The main bulk of the gameplay is platformer shoot-em-ups, with stealth options available if you prefer. You get various missions, to do with either collecting things, or assasinating targets, with a few 'boss battles' to even things out. All in all I feel as if the gameplay was a little unbalanced, with some areas much more difficult/easier that others, but it was mostly good.

You have various weapons, which help aid your style of gameplay [be it stealth or just running in head-on], and as enemies die you get tokens and health boosts. I feel as if the tokens are sort of unnecessary as all they are used for is buying more health. In my mind it would have made more sense to just have health boosts drop.

The story to the game follows you in a plot to overthrow the government who, as it turns out, are a bunch of illuminati gods. Or purple monsters. Or something. Its a bit wierd. That said, ignoring the wierdness, it's not too bad. You meet various people who've been affected by the state of life, and you end up with some choices as to how you want to play it out to the end. Worth mentioning is its very, uh, 'feminist' for lack of a better phrase. But it doesn't strain too heavily on this as to get in the way of the main story.

The music and art are very good in this game. The music has a very distopian feel to it, and matches up well with the fast paced action. There's also quite a variety of it, as opposed to just a few looping tracks. At times, if you're going for stealth, the music doesn't quite match tot his, but I didn't feel that was a major issue. The pixel art is very nice, with a wide range of environments carefully crafted. [My favourite is probably the art in the garden area].

The main menu interface is a bit hard to navigate, but it does keep track of your missing, and your inventory. I feel it would be much improved with a map though, as sometimes it can be hard to remember just where things are.

TL;DR - Aerannis is a pretty good platformer, with lots of classic shoot 'em up and some stealth levels.

Aerannis is a short, bittersweet Metroidvania with stealth elements. The control scheme is unintuitive on the keyboard, which is what I played the game using. Glitches are rare, and when they occur they're rarely gamebreaking. Hitboxes for certain actions, like grabbing enemies and ledges, are odd and often difficult to predict.

The story takes place in the Bulgarian city of Plovdiv where men have been eliminated, a revolution somehow forcing all men to undergo hormone replacement therapy, and relegating them to second class citizenship. "Snowflakes" as they're called are reviled by society, treated as little more than potentially violent pretenders only good for reproduction. You play Ceyda Farhi, a Bulgarian Turk who works as an assassin. She was also a transwoman before the revolution occurred, and due to her status as a transwoman only finds illegal work available to her. The story features intense discussions of feminism, particularly factions within feminism, and the place transwomen have in feminism. The game features two endings, one of which feels out of left field and the other more in line with the story we've been told.

A big issue with Aerannis is that it's short, I completed the game in seven and a half hours, and I'm sure a quicker run is possible.

I highly recommend Aerannis based off its story, gameplay, and the setting behind. I feel like Aerannis is a testament to what indie developers can accomplish without the pressure of executive meddling forcing them to make the next AAA title.

A warning: Aerannis features transphobic language and ideas at times, and anyone sensitive to these topics may want to keep this in mind while playing the game.

I was pretty eager to follow the development of Aerannis ever since things were greenlit here on steam. Happy to say the wait was worth it!

The city of Plovdiv and its surrounding territories are a pleasure to explore, offering a range of distinctly themed locales and characters/enemies which have all been touched in some way by Aerannis's larger conspiracy. Every one of them is unique and deep and full of little design touches, like the suspicious crowds in the background in downtown Plovdiv to the corkscrew architecture in one of the surrounding suburbs to some of the hulking (and gooey) stage bosses.

Gameplay is satisfying and best played when really observing the stealth elements, as they always pay off. With the exception of the occasional tangle while propping Ceyda up on small ledges, the controls are responsive (I've been using a gamepad for my playthrough, which I would probably recommend).

Aerannis is obviously a labor of love, and it really does show.

(And the soundtrack is fantastic as well (dev should really look into putting it up for sale alongside the game somewhere!))

I paid just shy of nine dollars for Aerannis and thirty minutes in it's already been worth it. That's basically all you need to know, but you can read on if you want to.

Control-wise, it's what people call a "MetroidVania" game, because apparently only Metroid and Castlevania are allowed to have exploration-heavy side scrolling and shooting.

You alternately sneak, kill, and SneakyKill(tm) your way to the goal while carrying out missions in an oppressive feminist future society. Yes, you read that right. We need more games like this that actually take risks with the setting instead of having every scifi game basically stealing the universe from Blade Runner.

The social commentary and humor are where the game really shines. The dialogue is witty, references to other projects and jabs at extremist feminism and the gaming industry are plentiful, and the memes are spicy.

If the game has one shortcoming, it is the palpable lack of wacky waving arm-flailing inflatable tube men. The devs really should have foreseen this problem and I hope it will be fixed in an upcoming patch or reasonably priced DLC. Horse armor would also be a good, but less necessary addition.

I was browsing Facebook when I saw a friend put up a picture of the entrance to TERF turf, and somehow the game was purchased on my steam library twenty seconds later. It's magic, I tell you! Aerannis is a strange amalgam of extremely familiar elements. It reminds me of nothing so much as Hotline Miami (the frequent checkpoints in levels cause that comparison), and yet there are a lot of metroidvania elements as well.
The controls feel floaty, which can be frustrating. I had a few deaths to them - not too many, but a few. The frequent checkpoints help to mostly mitigate this.
The plot is very science-fictiony and ... underexplained [the other thing that harks back to Hotline: Miami for me]. I felt like there could have been a lot more to the story and world, and a lot more to the main character, who is very ... well, she's what you'd expect from a shooter protagonist outside of her gender. In this story, you're basically just doing the "talk to contact, go through world to do mission, come back to contact, do other mission" loop, and it's very ... apparent that this is happening. Even with the fun touches [e.g.: the first mission is "Figure out why people buy day 1 DLC"], it all seems oddly rote for a story set in a world where there are no men in apocalyptic surroundings. You're forced through a linear path, and that path feels ... incomplete, or at least it did to me.
There are multiple endings, but you can see both of them almost immediately at the end, which I thought was a nice touch. The endings themselves ... are a bit of a lucid dream, as the rest of the plot is. To be honest, I don't think the art style does the game any favors in this area (and goodness, the elevator sprites are almost seizure-inducing), but there could have been more done, just in general, to flesh out the world.

In the end, I would rate this a maybe, but there's just a yes or no option. Upon finishing the game (it took ~6, 7 hours?) ... I didn't love it. It feels serviceable. If you love the subject matter, you won't regret buying it (I don't regret buying it). But I ended the game wanting more from the game than what I got - a character that isn't content to just follow what their handler says forever, a story that's more fleshed-out ... I wasn't really able to connect with the main character in the story. The small throwaway bits are consistent and then the larger bits didn't ... feel that way, to the setting and the story, to me. I felt along for the ride, like my actions weren't doing anything in the story - like I was watching an oddly-paced 8-bit movie about the Illum(inati) and Gods, Devil May Cry-style. And the action isn't good enough to compensate.

Very fun game all around. As I spend more and more time with it, little details stick out, like details in the background and NPCs, and different paths to take in the environment where you can either deal with enemies head-on or sneak around them. The story is really good, and though the dialog is eye-roll worthy at times, the balance between the humor the various characters possess and the seriousness of the story (which is handled pretty well, all things considered) helps keep the player engaged. The difficulty is just hard enough to not make the game a breeze while also not being overly punishing for players, especially with the controls.

And speaking of them, this game isn't flawless. The controls are definitely weird, and can take some getting used to, especially if you play with a keyboard. There's definitely been a number of times where I've failed a section multiple times because I keep pressing the wrong button since I go for the key that feels more intuitive. Customizable controls would be a huge boon.* And sometimes the music choice and screen you're playing in don't match up well and feel weird to play.

I bought this for $8.50, though I'd gladly pay $15 for it. Even with its faults, the game has me hooked, and I definitely can't wait to see what the developer has in store for the future.

* EDIT: An update was added to allow customizable controls. This helps a lot, though the initial configuration of controls is still enough of an issue to note.
