About This Game


A few hundred condemned criminals were being shipped to the Auriga system on board the prison hulk “Success”. While this was presented as a chance to earn back their place in society by working hard for the common good, they understood that in fact they would be slave labour, sent to colonize an unexplored planet. All they knew about Auriga Prime was what the probes told them: it had water, temperate zones, plant life, and plenty of metals in the crust.


In fact, the planet Auriga once hosted a major settlement of the galaxy-travelling ancestors known as the Endless. In addition, the planet was still orbited by a functioning (and well cloaked) defensive system, which sprang eagerly to life upon the arrival of the Success. Within a few minutes, the ship was nothing but a few large chunks of metal falling toward the planet.


Every set of holding cells also functioned as an escape pod, so the ship let itself disintegrate and the surviving prisoners fell bruised but (temporarily) alive and (momentarily) safe to the planet below. Safe, that is, until they realized that they had crashed through some sort of facility of the Endless, down to a sub-basement so deep and ancient it might as well be called a dungeon…

Gather A Team.

  • Form a team of heroes, each with their own strengths (and psychoses)
  • Equip them, deploy them, and earn powerful abilities
  • Manage the balance between ex-prison inmates and guards
  • Experience four player coop and watch your back

Build Your Defenses.

  • Use the Dust you gather to power the rooms
  • Use scarce resources to help your team survive
  • Build minor and major modules to hold off waves of monsters
  • Decode Endless ruins to discover life-saving technologies

Open the Door.

  • Each door is a danger; prepare yourself and your team for anything
  • Explore and discover an infinity of levels and layouts
  • Carry your crystal through waves of monsters to the exit of each level
  • Fight your way to the surface to discover the truth about Auriga

Quick Review:

Not sure if this is the game for you? Buy the Game! What could go wrong? Open the Door!

The Good: Various characters & pods (game modes) to select and rogue-like elements all adds to high replay value.
The Bad: Too Easy & Easy difficulty options can be misleading.
The Ugly: Multi-player option available, but the game’s better with only 1 or 2 players and not more.



Detailed Review:

The Good:

Dungeon of the Endless provides a great level of replay value. If it’s not the different pods that increase replay value, it’ll be the number of characters to choose from. As of this review, there’s as many as 27 to choose from and each bring their own advantages & disadvantages. By default, you start with 2 & recruit more in the dungeon, but the maximum number is 4. Fun fact, if you set your team up correctly, they’ll share some unique dialog between floors and provide each other with a boost in stats & additional skills.

Speaking of pods, they are you game modes and as of this review, there’s 8 available to select, each providing their own variety of difficulty, disadvantages, perks & modifiers. Maybe the basic Escape Pod is good enough for you or you want the toughest challenge to date that is the Sanitary Pod, maybe you feel like going on a solo run in the Refreezerator Pod or you want to see how far you can go in the Drill Pod.

The Bad:

There are no higher difficulty option choices that unlocks as you play, as previously mentioned, the variety of pods provides that choice for you. Which makes the Too Easy & Easy option at the start of the game misleading, especially to first-time players.

Not useless, just misleading. Notable difference between the two difficulties includes less abundance of dust and resources, stronger monsters (obviously), frequent rooms where you can’t build any modules & overall luck.

The Ugly:

Dungeon of the Endless also features multi-player function, which has it’s ups and downs. When looking at the ups, having multiple people manage their heroes can make it easier to plan your actions & assess the situation. But on the other hand, having multiple players, especially when playing with strangers, can also be confusing when one player is trying to figure out what the others are doing and your resources are split between the number of players.

In a nutshell, it adds more unnecessary time playing the game. Not to say that multi-player is completely unnecessary, a 2-player game with a friend is more than enough to have a good time as opposed to a maximum 4-player game. Less is More.



Since this review won’t acquire everyone’s intentions, all that’s left to be said is to buy the game & see for yourself if this game’s for you. Though really, what could go wrong? Open the Door!

==Forewarning: The developers of Dungeon of the Endless have been bought out by SEGA, this means that this game is likely going to see no more support, and no sequel. Keep this in mind when purchasing.==
An interesting mix of RTS and other genres, Dungeon of the Endless is a rewarding, but frustrating experience.
[Summary at bottom.]

You control 1~4 Survivors, from a small unlockable cast, and fight your way up 15 floors of randomly generated layouts. To complete this you can power individual rooms and build modules to harvest resources and fight the hordes of monsters, level up your Survivors to gain static benefits, and scavenge or purchase equipment and research. Each floor is started by your Crystal, which must be defended, and then escorted to the exit, which must be found.

The game is privy to an enormous amount of RNG; the only static things are the characters themselves, the 15 floors, and whatever modifiers you selected (through unlockable escape pods gained through various criteria) when you started this run. Everything else is steeped in uncertainty; from the monsters that will spawn on a given floor and from which unpowered rooms, to where and what kinds of rooms spawn and what lays within, to the presence of item chests and shopkeepers, as well as if and where Research Artifacts appear and what research is randomly avaliable, as well as floor layouts themselves (down to individual room sizes and module slots, and if theyre occupied) and finally the location of the exit of the floor, the goal of any given floor.
Learning to mitigate this RNG is critical to playing the game, moreso than any amount of game knowledge or unlockables can change; though at the end of the day you are completely at the game's mercy for the better part of any run; I've had more than a few runs end on the first floor or drag for floors on end without a single item. The game, in the vein of so many bad roguelikes and -lites, forgets the cardinal rule of random generation and gameplay: The ability to give the player mitigation. Players can only do so much in any given situation; and the game will punish you to a cruel extent if you aren't prepared in the specific way it demands from you.

The game itself is very fun, it has excellent sound design and though the pixelated artstyle isn't for everyone, it's good enough (I'm personally not a fan of the portraits, at all); and the cast and world of the Endless franchise is varied and interesting; some characters even have unique interactions if they survive together.
The gameplay is very hands-off, you only order the opening of doors, interaction of NPCs, and the destined room for a character; in addition to the building mechanic that compliments your Survivors. Combat especially is very non-player driven, there is no way to change the targets that a Survivor will prioritize, you only control their location, equipment, resource-based healing, and their 0~2 Active abilities, which have a lengthy cooldown and can only be used so many times per floor. For the most part, the player is left feeling very helpless as turns play out and choices are made, and hoping that everything just works out.

What makes this lack of control very important, is the degree at which the game punishes you for failure. There is little to no margin for mistakes in Dungeon of the Endless; if you do not immediately react to a situation and are not absolutely spot-on with healing and buildings, you will fail; and the effects of failure range from losing modules in combat, to the deaths of Survivors, to damage to your Crystal (which loses its distinct resource, used for powering rooms). Failure in Dungeon of the Endless comes fast and easily, and you have to be very familiar with the game to know what you can do about it, and any failure at all quickly snowballs into a lost run.

The game is mired in numerous small issues that gradually add up over the course of playing; things like being unable to pick the specific route of a moving survivor, the inability to use Active Abilities if you have more than one Survivor selected, survivors being unable to attack enemies moving between rooms, and the numerous issues in multiplayer (cannot pause, items often go to the wrong inventory, loading the game or changing control of a Survivor resets their 'floors survived' to unlock them, etc) are just a small, but noticable sample of frustrating details; that when added to the random nature of the game can turn a fun experience with friends into a shouting match in seconds.

SUMMARY
Dungeon of the Endless is a fun game, but it certainly isn't for everyone. The mix of genres and mechanics is interesting, but at the end of the day very unrefined and frustrating. The game is heavy on RNG, and doesn't give you many ways to deal with or mitigate it, and very little control in general; but 'most' games can be won. Expect to have a wiki open when new to the game.
If you're interested in the game; do your own research and really make sure it's the kind of game you can get into, otherwise you're going to be bitter about your purchase and everyone you were playing with.
