Early Access Game

Get instant access and start playing; get involved with this game as it develops.

Note: This Early Access game is not complete and may or may not change further. If you are not excited to play this game in its current state, then you should wait to see if the game progresses further in development. Learn more

What the developers have to say:

Why Early Access?

“Different types of players play Chime in different ways, and like all action puzzle games we need as much data as possible to help us balance the game. And while we plan to experiment with new gameplay modes and visual / UI tweaks, the real focus for the next few months is on bringing in more music, which may take some time even though the game is playable as it stands.”

Approximately how long will this game be in Early Access?

“Our estimate is 3 months, however, that depends on how quickly we get the music.”

How is the full version planned to differ from the Early Access version?

“There will be 9 more tracks, the gameplay and new modes will be better balanced and the game will be visually polished.”

What is the current state of the Early Access version?

“It's completely playable across six tracks in three different gameplay modes -- i.e. it's already "bigger" than the original Chime. It's not yet balanced, the UI needs work, the modes may be changed, and there are nine more tracks to add.”

Will the game be priced differently during and after Early Access?

“There will be one fixed price for the entire duration of Early Access. On final release, the price will be lower.”

How are you planning on involving the Community in your development process?

“We'll be watching the forums and responding to comments, as well as tracking people's scores and progress and using that to help us balance the game.”

About This Game

Chime was a much-loved music puzzle game on Xbox Live Arcade, PC and PS3. What happens if you file away the rusty edges?
New music + new modes + new visuals = CHIME SHARP.

Place pieces, paint the board, make music. Chime Sharp is an sequel to 2009's Chime, a music puzzle game with an addictive, ambient heartbeat. You tessellate shapes to cover a grid, while a beatline reads those shapes as notes. As you cover the board the music builds to a beautiful crescendo of your own design. The only way to discover Chime is to play it, but if you want a glimpse, consider what it might feel like to cross Tetris, a music sequencer and a hypnotic dream about your favourite pop song.

Chime Sharp takes Chime's classic dynamic and updates it for 2015. The core rules are the same but the aesthetic is clean and modern and the game's soundtrack has been completely refreshed. With fifteen new levels, experimental modes, new-era connectivity and sharper ways to play, Chime's finally back -- and it wants to be your new favourite mixtape.

Chime Sharp (or Chime # for those who are musically inclined) is the long-awaited sequel to Zoe Mode's c̶h̶a̶r̶i̶t̶y̶ hit game Chime that shone with tracks like Jonathan Coulton's "Still Alive". Brought to you by hundreds of dedicated fans through the graces of Kickstarter and the awesome coding skillz of Ste Curran, this is the sequel you were looking for - more tracks, more play modes, more pentominoes. The basic gameplay hasn't changed - you compete against time, placing your pentonimo tiles to make squares at least 3x3 in size that then vanish giving you points and a bit more playing time if you've cleared a new area of the playing field. Clearing squares raises your multiplier, while leftover pentomino bits will eventually start flashing and tehn disapper, setting your multiplier back to 1 again if you don't clear them up fast enough. It's a race to clear the whole screen, at which point it'll be rinse, repeat.

Doesn't sound exciting? Well, it really isn't, in a big-game GTA or Batman kinda way. It's draw is rather more sublime - really pared down and more like a meditation. I love it. Steam will tell you I spent literally hundreds of hours playing the original. I'll probably play Chime Sharp even more, because it's the game that doesn't disappoint because it doesn't even pretend to be something it isn't. Time played for money spent it's the best investment I ever made.

Simply o-r-g-a-s-m-i-c... though less than the first one.

Chime Sharp is a puzzle-music game. You have to cover a surface with blocks, so that a "quad" (generally when you have at least 3x3) can be formed and expanded. Once it's over, the quad disappear during the next passage of the beat, letting parts of the blocks used on the grid. And after a moment, they'll glow if unused and you'll loose the multiplier and the unused stuff.

It's hard to explain the game. Anyway, Chime Sharp comes with other modes, very interesting, like the one with lives (you're loosing one for each unused part after the last chance to use them). And as usual, the music is well chosen.

If the first game was in 2D, here, it's more like a 2.5 D.

Well, I'm still waiting for a real WOW for the music, for now, it's nice but nothing is compared when I heard For Silence in the first game.

Yeah, 20 bucks is high. I admit, I wasn't really happy about the price. But if this can help them to improve, I didn't hesitate.

So, yeah. Simple o-r-g-a-s-m-i-c. Gonna rewrite my review of Chime: I need to add a ² or a x2 :p.

Early Access Review
Posted: 12 November, 2015
Review updated as of CHIME SHARP update on 2016-May-25.

I am a big fan of Chime; I bought it on xbox360 after a year or two I bought it again on Steam. On Steam alone I've logged about 280 hours in Chime so far. I backed Chime Sharp on Kickstarter. Believe me when I say it kills me to write that I do not recommend this game. I will eagerly change my review when or if it becomes playable.

The 3D effects make me nauseous however there is an option to turn it off in the Options. Why can't you access the options during gameplay? I haven't tried the newest build with a controller, only mouse and keyboard.

The colors of cleared squares are so matchy-matchy with the pieces that even after playing levels multiple times I still forget which color is which and waste time trying to color in grid I've already cleared. I tried playing casual and it seems like the only different is a smaller total grid (playing area). ***THE background matching too closely with pieces has been updated in multiple levels so that there is no confusion [on the first several levels if playing in order.] Approximately half of the levels are still mostly unplayable due to the color scheme. The game cannot be played hours on end except for the first few levels.***

Suggestions: have us be able to customize the colors of background, unplayable area, colored grid, uncolored grid, and fading remains of pieces in some sort of paletter choice. Or at the very least, let us have a classic skin version for those of us who wanted the "same game" with new songs and play styles. Let us be able to see the pieces that belong in that level prior to playing the level as the original game did. Or let us be able to see it on the pause screen.

Final result: Buy Chime instead of Chime Sharp. The sequel has nice songs but ultimately the game is not enjoyable by comparison. If you think you'll like this one as well, play a demo first to make sure the graphics do not bother your eyes or concentration.

NOTE: Within a couple of hours of this review being on Steam, the dev released a notice to Kickstarter contributors asking them to review the game positively on Steam, and to note that some things just were not possible. As my review was the only one on Steam, I imagine my request for a "classic skin" or "basic skin" is what the dev will not devote time for. While I understand making a decision to not add features, I just can't agree. The visuals in this game are too frustratingly different than the original game. Different is fine if it does not detract from gameplay. The 3D effects can be turned off now which is amazing. I never knew this during my test runs as you can't access this except on splash screen. Having new colors in the game is a nice bonus to people who never played the first game, but for many people it causes the game to be frustrating and difficult. This is not what you want in a casual game. The entry point for casual games must be fairly low.
