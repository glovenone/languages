About This Game

Trawl is a first person experiential game of discovery and creativity.

First you must figure out how to operate the various parts of the boat, manipulating the wheel, throttle and the net. Then, trawl the sea to discover what secrets are hidden in its depths and record your findings on your typewriter. The sea has many stories to tell, and will reward multiple voyages.

  • --

Trawl is not a 'gameplay' heavy game in the traditional sense. Trawl takes places entirely on a small trawling vessel, and you are encouraged to engage with the low-key, though detailed ritual of operating the mechanics of the ship, while soaking in the atmosphere. If you so choose, Trawl can be seen as a sort of writing prompt, with you playing the role of sea-bound detective. How you choose to interpret and record the results are up to you.

It is difficult to say definitively how long each playthrough is, as this depends entirely on how much you engage with the game and decide to write in your in-game journal. If you speed-run the game it could be over in less than 10 minutes, though if that is how you intend to play then this game may not be your cup of tea. Most of our players enjoy returning to sea again and again to see what new secrets they'll uncover and then writing journal entries to share with their friends.


If you choose to record your findings on the typewriter, Trawl will save a text file in your Documents/Trawl/ folder.

===

Because of the nature of the game, we're very wary of giving too much away in trailers, so we hope you'll understand that the trailer focuses more on the atmosphere than on the gameplay :)

Posted: 19 May
I like the concept of giving the player a chance to express themselves, but this infuriating typewriter sim doesn't do it well.

The developer says himself that the typewriter is the centerpoint of the game but you literally cannot sit and type anything substantial without setting off a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ bell (which is exactly as loud as you'd expect a naval bell to be) that rings unendingly until you go and cycle the trawl, at which point it will be silent only so long as you don't try to write anything on the typewriter, because for some reason theose things are linked in a very un-subtle way.

Meaning you can walk around the boat in silence and stare out at the dark ocean in silence but you cannot sit down and write in silence. It's like a bad halfway-point between really cool existential gaming (where nothing is encouraged or rewarded, and the correct action is always whatever the player chooses to do) and all the other straightforward clearly-defined-goal-oriented stuff. The player that wants to relax and enjoy the experience of creating something is prevented from doing so because the developers decided that the pace at which the player discovers items must be rapid and frenzied.

However it is absurd that the devs are even considering the feelings of the goal-oriented audience in the first place since a game like this would not in a thousand years appeal to the goal-oriented audience.

So here's my advice. Get rid of the ♥♥♥♥ing bell. Make a light that turns from red to green or something. If you absolutely must put some kind of intrusive trawl-alert, make it one that doesn't interrupt and actively discourage the entire purpose of your game. Also the list that is permanently overlaid on the typewriter pages grows over time, so if you discover too many items you can't see what you're typing anymore. Fix that, too. Oh, and there's a bug where the bell triggers even if you haven't put the trawl out, so you can just sit at the typewriter and write spam and spawn discovered items non-stop.

I paid six bucks for the opportunity to give you my honest feedback so I hope you appreciate it. But I really do like the concept of the game.

Product received for free
Trawl isn't your typical game. There isn't a flashy tutorial, or a deep story. Instead you simply come to on the deck of a trawler, surrounded by the endless void of ocean. The game invites you to experiment and poke around.

For less than a fiver, you can trawl the depths in hopes of seeing something exciting in your net once pulled aboard, place them around your ship, and keep a captain's log of your ramblings.

There is no real ending to each play-through, but the game's simplicity draws you back to look for what else is out there. The endless rain sets a surprisingly relaxing environment which makes just being in trawl an almost meditative experience worth checking out.

Would definitely recommend to almost anyone.

ETA: Thought I should mention that I am close friends with the developer of this game although that hasn't swayed my opinion at all
