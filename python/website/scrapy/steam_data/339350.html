I rate the game Choice of Robots 4/5. Good.

While this is merely a text-based game in a similar style as the old Choose Your Own Adventure books it is an extremely extensive one which has a lot more to say and a lot of lessons to potentially teach. Most of the endings are interesting and meaningful and the ideas presented and the choices you get (on almost every page) are very interesting. It is a shame that more games with actual, visual graphics could not have the amazing freedom of choice and the sheer number of possible endings which this game allows. Though it is a mere text-based game, I ended up playing through about 6 or 7 times and I am certain this story has many other potential endings I have not yet discovered.

However, this game does have some serious weaknesses beyond the fact that it has no graphics. The writing seems more like an outline of a story rather than an actual story with very few visceral or sensory details though this does allow the reader to skim through sections which have already been read very, very easily. The characters seem a little 2 dimensional or slightly shallow at times as it's far too easy to classify or figure out the different characters and their motivations, underlying emotional conflicts and agendas.

Indeed, emotion is not described very well in this book/game at all as the text often seems to have a very egocentric focus on whatever you, the player/reader-character might be feeling at any given moment instead (when emotion is described much at all). Still, despite this shortcoming, just from the situations which are described, the story can still manage to feel very touching anyhow.

This story was particularly touching when I played through the game trying to be the worse, most selfish, scheming â¥â¥â¥â¥â¥â¥â¥ I could possibly try to be. This game will let you know you are being an â¥â¥â¥â¥â¥â¥â¥ in some very touching ways and will give you results that would logically spring from being that way. Does that mean that the story will kill you off if you act badly? Not necessarily. But you will get realistically punished. You may even become a zillionaire captain of industry which no one loves or wants to be around, for example. If you act in an apathetic way to your created robots they will in turn become more apathetic towards everyone. In this game, your robots are an nth degree reflection of your actions and inactions. Unfortunately the narration feels cold apathetic regardless. It is good that it does not push you in one direction or another but it is unbiased to the extent that it is cold and clinical. Robotic even.

And while it is excellent that this story/game will let you make truly stupid, rash and irrational decisions too with seemingly realistic results, the bare-bones writing style does feel like it often falls short and that every single passage could have been even better with more detail. Visceral and emotional details are the "graphics" of a text-based experience and this experience doesn't have anywhere near as much of it as it should. As in an outline of a story, there is probably too much left to the imagination causing every situation to not be quite as fleshed out as it should be. With more detail this story/game could have been more funny, felt more tragic or felt more awe-inspiring with just the addition of a few more details; The good experience that it is could have easily been a great one, a masterpiece of multiple-ending choice-based fiction. Perhaps a newer, enhanced version could still be written to achieve this.

English literary details aside, perhaps the most glaring problem with this story/book/game is how there is no back button which allows you to undo any choice you just made. Indeed, you cannot even flip back to an earlier page. As a result, unlike the Choose Your Own Adventure books of old you cannot easily figure out how the story/plot/game is structured and learn all the different options and endings which exist. While this maintains the sense of the book's mystery and forces you to live with any mistakes you made (which is more realistic) it more often seems frustrating and nonsensical that you, as a reader of a story, cannot turn back to a previous page. While this frustrating lack of a feature allows for massive amounts of replayability (or re-readability), it also means that much of that replayability will inevitably consist of carefully skimming through increasingly repetitive passages to get back to some critical choice or another which may lead you on to a different ending. The game should give you an option of being able to go back to a previous page or not and should have put that option in a menu somewhere.

Despite its weaknesses this story/game will give a sci-fi robot fiction fan many hours of enjoyment. Indeed I found that I was often captivated by the plot and adventure of trying to find out what would happen next. I read this on a computer which has a GTX 780 graphics card installed in it and is capable of easily running Far Cry 3 with all of its graphics settings turned to maximum. It's not like I don't have a choice of which games I want to play and so I read this instead. No. Truly, this game was interesting and for me, and at least initially was able compete on an even playing field with the most graphically intense games on the market.

With a choice on nearly every page the old young adult choose your own adventure stories of the 1980s and early 90's cannot hold a candle to the number of possibilities which this game presents. Indeed, most other adventure games, (most of which have just one to three possible endings) can barely keep up with the amazing variation of possible endings which this story gives you. A character who is your chief love interest in one play-through of Choice of Robots may end up being your archenemy in another play-through. In many ways this game further realizes the potential of the ideas in that old movie Butterfly Effect while at once being set in a I-Robot, Isaac Asimov science fiction situation dealing with the moral pitfalls and potentials of the advent of robotic AI sentience. The number of different potential messages and meanings which this kind of interactive fiction can hold in store for any given reader is truly staggering.

Despite its literary flaws and the absence of a back button, this is a work of art worthy of being discussed and studied in any forward-thinking university (or high-school rather due to the relatively simplistic language) English department in the nation due to its amazing potential and amazing re-readibility.

Now...if only someone would make a porn story with this many choices and potential endings...

Wow. That was such an unexpectedly wonderful experience.

As this is a CYOA (Choose Your Own Adventure), your experience may vary, but, I say with little hesitation that my own experience with the story is the most meaningful experience I've had with a game - ever.

Although wonderful in their own right, choice oriented games like The Walking Dead and Mass Effect don't even come close to what this game accomplishes.

What Choice Do I Have?

CoR gives you choices.

CoR gives you lots of choices that make the story feel like your own, fostering immersion in a way that traditional literature cannot.

CoR gives you lots of difficult choices that may have you thinking hard and agonizing about the potential consequences. The game has no manual save function. If you make choices that lead to a premature ending, you can't undo and must start over. (such branches seem rare, though, and replaying doesn't take much time once you're familiar with the text)

CoR gives you lots of difficult choices that have a real impact. Something that is uniquely possible when the creator is unconstrained by the need to create full art assets for every possibility.

Deep Dive

This "game" has no graphics at all except for the cover image, which is exactly akin to a book cover illustration. Is it a game or a book? I say it is most certainly a game. Graphics is not a requirement of a game. Interactivity is. A series of choices that impact the experience do a game comprise.

CoR's language is competent, but not "literary". It reads like a matter-of-fact narrative of events that might actually happen in your life if the stars align in just the right way. Initially, I got an "amateur" vibe from the writing, but later realized that the experience may actually benefit from the simple, contemporary writing style. As the story progressed and sucked me in deeper, I became much too busy thinking about what was going on to criticize the writing style.

CoR is (mostly) accessible. Although the fact that it was written by somebody with a Ph.D. in Computer Science does show. Having a CS degree myself, I certainly appreciated that perspective. Although lots of artistic license is taken for the sake of plot, there is lots of grounding in contemporary technology.

The story makes mostly "reasonable" extrapolations starting from the near future forward several decades. Like all good science (or 'speculative', if you prefer) fiction, the story poignantly references the present while writing about the future, framing the timeless human condition in extrapolated contexts.

To elaborate about why I put "reasonable" in quotation marks above, the story explores the future in ways that could, for the most part, conceivably happen, and many futurists would even claim that CoR's extrapolation towards the Singularity doesn't go far enough. However, the author is not afraid to explore fantastic scenarios that probably won't happen, at least not soon, and in some cases, hopefully never.

The scope of the story is indeed quite epic, following the main decades of a lifetime of the character you control. I do have to object to the game description's categorization of this as a "novel", though, as it is not nearly long enough to count as one. At best, it is the length of a (very) short story (a single playthrough takes a matter of hours). On the other hand, the sheer amount of content of all possible branches easily exceed the length of a long novel (50% longer than Moby â¥â¥â¥â¥). It's quite an accomplishment.

And, if you are worried about this being a boring, technical slog, don't be. Despite the apparently sterile subject matter of robots, there is plenty of emotion in this game - or, at least, there can be, depending on your choices.

Turn to Page 23

I've only explored one of the many, distinct branches of CoR. I will probably be going back to explore the others.

I heartily recommend CoR to anyone who can read English, especially those with an interest in technology and/or science fiction.

Note that there is also a free demo where you can play through the first 2 chapters (out of 7), either via Steam, or on the developer's web site.

UPDATES

Having now explored a few other branches and having made more "optimal" choices, I feel that choosing less optimally for the first playthrough does make for a better experience.

My first playthrough involved an above average number of failures and tragedies, which amounted to a much more poignant experience than when everything basically goes well.

I do suggest playing the game "organically". Perhaps it is unlikely to play optimally the first time around anyway. Certainly I would discourage any attempts to "cheat" by looking up optimal choices beforehand. That would truly ruin the experience. You can always go back later and "fix mistakes" on subsequent playthroughs.
