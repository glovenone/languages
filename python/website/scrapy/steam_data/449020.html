16 May

Update 1.5.1 (unlock resolutions)

Hey all,

By popular request, I've now pushed a small small update that unlocks the resolution options in the launch screen for people with non-standard resolutions. You can now also launch it in Windowed mode, and freely resize the window: hopefully this will at least allow everyone a chance to make sure all of the bits and bobs of the game are visible at the same time.

Note that playing at other resolutions than the 'default' (1920x1080, at a 10:9 aspect ratio) might lead to various visual ugliness, such as the chat windows overlapping, font sizes between menu and dialog changing etc. However, the UI -has- been made to work with varying resolutions, and I tested it in Windowed mode, and you can stretch it quite a bit before it becomes unreadable.

If you experience any trouble with this update, please post in the Official Bug Reports Thread. Much obliged!

Other fixes to the 1.5.1 version include some typo fixes + you can now only actually break into Ches's apartment if you have a crowbar ;)

About This Game

The Bottom of the Well
A visual novel where Alice steps into a different kind of wonderland.

In The Bottom of the Well, you play as Alice who has just had an unsettingly life-like dream about the end of the world. She retells her experience to her friend in a huge, branching narrative. Although a single play-through might take as little as 20 minutes, experiencing all the possible paths Alice can take through the ruins of her city will take you many hours.

The game is intended to be replayed several times with different permutations of Alice: try playing as a survival-minded lone-wolf, or a social butterfly, or why not attempt the difficult but rewarding romantic route? The stats you select for your Alice in the beginning of the game influences everything from small comments to major choices.

This is an updated, reworked and improved version of my entry to the WAG (Write A Game) Challenge arranged by IGDA's Game Writing SIG, where it placed first in the amateur category. The jury had this to say:

- WINNER: THE BOTTOM OF THE WELL
The Bottom of The Well delivers an entertaining, branched story with interesting options. Alice's dream is genuinely terrifying. The conversations feel natural and appropriate. Players can randomize their character stats, but still negotiate the game with deliberate and meaningful choices. The final game stats give The Bottom of the Well immense replay value. One judge played the game several times to find the "best" ending!

When logic and proportion
Have fallen sloppy dead
And the White Knight is talking backwards
And the Red Queen's off with her head...*

There comes another marvelous gem for which it is tremendously hard to write a review and do justice completely without presenting spoilers. The Bottom of the Well is the narrative of a series of staggering experiences concerning a girl named Alice - or many Alices in their different incarnations nonetheless - and her dream of an apocalyptic end. Here, we will participate in her dream many, many times to reach countless endings to try and discover a meaning to it.

The narrative opens with a basic chat screen where Alice talks to her friend M. Hatter and tells him of a disturbingly lucid dream that she has seen the other day. In this unsettling dream, the city is under the threat of getting hit by a series of nuclear missiles due to unknown reasons. The estimation indicates that she'll have a bit more than half an hour to find some kind of protective measure. Here, we become a witness to the before, present and after of a possible nuclear attack and try to lead Alice to some kind of safety while making choices of survival, logic, ethics to reach an ending. We fight against environment, we fight against ourselves; but mostly we fight against time.

Before each playthrough, we are presented the opportunity to choose Alice's stats enrolled into different abilities - consisting of survival, resources, fitness, social abilities, career and dating - to arrange a different incarnation, an alternate dream version of the actual Alice. The combination of different stats change the storyline and starting position for Alice, making different story paths to follow available. In each playthrough different choices and Alice's abilities - and also the exact timing that we chose to take the action during narrative - present countless different combinations, thus different story progressions for Alice. Mostly she dies, sometimes she survives. Yet there is always the reluctant feeling that something's missing is present. As if, that wasn't the way the dream was intended to end... There we go back, start a new game to craft a new Alice and try a new path to follow in hopes of maybe discovering what that was about this time.

We participate in Alice's narrative to make her choices and affect the happenings of her dream past in a frame tale fashion. Alice's narrative of her experience is dreadfully realistic, actualized through an elaborate vocabulary and shockingly pinpoint descriptions. It is confusingly easy to immerse into the narrative experience of Alice, finding yourself trying different combinations of stats and choices endlessly, nearly always discovering some new outcome. The main display consists of nothing much aside some beautiful and appropriately forlorn artwork and the ominous soundtrack lingering in the background to enhance the narrative towards its triumph.

At this point, it should be no secret that the game is inspired from Lewis Carroll's Alice in Wonderland yet it manages to craft the symbolic personifications into a genuine setting of its own; way more realistic and disturbing that Lewis Carroll ever intended them to be so. Aside all of its narrative glory and ever changing gameplay; it is an unbelievable fact that the game is a freeware. Can you even believe it? I'll go ahead and urge you to try this uncanny experience. Maybe this time, Alice will come back from the rabbit hole.

*Lyrics by Jefferson Airplane - White Rabbit

Review:
Visual novel about Alice falling into the nuclear apocalypse Wonderland. With a Sin City's black-red-white graphic style and classic 'Choose Your Own Adventure' gameplay.
I never played visnovels before and never thought I would, couldn't understand the fun of this genre at all... until now. This time Alice-related setting and dark style caught me, I couldn't pass by. And don't regret.
This version of Wonderland is pretty dark indeed - Alice's lucid dream about the end of the world in a nearby future. As deadly as you'd expect a nuclear apocalypse to be - the amount of different ways to die here is extreme. At some point I even got sick of constantly dying :D Few deaths are very cruel and scary too (for a modern setting, that is), and even disgusting to read.
The story is given in a form of chat conversation between Alice and Hatter, who isn't short of bitter comments and accusing her actions, if necessary. Also gets comic shocked when she does something really unfitting in her dream :D
There are also.. RPG elements (?? :O) You can select at the beginning which stats (fitness, survival, dating, etc.) and starting items your dream-Alice will have. Both drastically affect the story and choices you can make. There are also two predefined avatars - default Alice, weak and soft and dying from breath of the wind, and Dark Alice, a true monster, strong and tough and with no moral. Latter one even got black hood covering her face, for everyone who meets her should know how brutal she is :D This variant can't go for the true ending though, as that would be too easy.
An important thing - main storyline is made the way that at some point you can not proceed unless you restart the game several time, going through other branches and with completely different Alices. What a clever way to make people replay the game :D
Narrative (and that is obviously the most important part of visnovel) is so nice, it kept me intrigued till the very end. Explored as much as possible. Even though it would get a bit obvious sometimes, I enjoyed every branch of the story... except the ending, The True Ending. Pretty sudden and short. But devs already said they are working on some kind of epilogue, to expand it a little more.
There's also pleasant minor piano music in the background, finishing off a.. pessimistic atmosphere? You'd never expect a static game to have an atmosphere. But some kind of it is here, yes, and it's good.
Nicely, the game is absolutely free, it's that kind of marketing scheme when "we do first game completely free so everybody can try and see how of a good developers we are".
True little gem for all you Alice collectors ;)
