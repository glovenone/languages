About This Game

Story


Oblivious Garden ~Carmina Burana is a Romance fantasy visual novel. Our protagonist Irell Reis was once a famous general who led thousands of soldiers to expand the land of the Magic Empire on Latoy. However the defeat in Garyalburg, Vaustiya three years ago cost him everything. Fortunately he was not executed, but he was stripped of his rank and forced to live life as a lowly peasant from then on.

Finally, the time has come for Irell to recover his honor and reputation. His uncle the Emperor of Celantu has summoned him. What task will the Emperor give to him? Will Irell Reis be able to succeed in his new mission? He may first need to learn how to get along with the princesses?

Background


Long ago, people once called their land Latoy in awe.
Since ancient times, Latoy has referred to the vast paradise they called their home carried forward by one generation after another. By showing great admiration to various gods and to the motherland, people in Latoy lived in peace for thousands of years.
Now, however, humans have forgotten all of this.
After thousands of years, humans once living in Latoy are divided into the north and the south.
Celantu, the Magic Empire, occupies the southern part with abundant resources.
Vaustiya, the United Principality, occupies the cold northern part.
Though people on both sides were once brothers and sisters, they launched an endless and bloody war, killing anyone holding different flags with their swords and magical powers.

--This is an era when humans lose their admiration for gods and reverence to the land.
However, even in the turbulent time, there exists a care-free paradise like the one a thousand years ago.

--Oblivious Garden
This is a lavish palace built for the seven princesses. It is a “birdcage” glorified with the name “garden.”
And so, the story about love, hate, myth, and forgiveness begins...

I had a lot of money on me, right? I was like "Imma pick two visual novels". I'm glad Oblivious Garden was one of them.

I'll make this a short review by saying JUST GET IT.
It was worth my time. The 4 routes are all unique and have different and fun experiences in each one, and the overall design and style just felt right. Also, the narration and dialogue? On point. But, as you know me well, I must do a thorough and lengthy review so... Read if you want ^^
GO DOWN BELOW FOR PROS AND CONS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Story
For a visual novel, story is everything. It has to be immersive and get you to draw opinions about the characters. This VN does just that: it made me feel emotions, as if I was Irell (main protagonist). The story, in short, focuses on Irell, a former-general who killed countless people in a war long ago. Stripped of his status, he is spared life by the Emperor who tells him to go to the Oblivious Garden and train the 7 princesses for the annual Miracle Play. Only 3 princesses have routes, however all 7 are in the game in various ways. Each route explains a different story that all revolves in the same universe, one princess being vastly different than the rest. Since it is a VN, there are also good and bad endings. All the good endings are logical and are not really confusing (minus Dyanna's ending). Overall, it has a simple yet large amount of lore you can derive from it, and I just eat that stuff up! Other than this, the endings all have a trend, usually. It kind of bothers me that it can be done easily, but the emperor gets killed. Is he not guarded well enough or something? lol

Characters
Characters... VITAL to a VN. Characters HAVE to be likable and serve a purpose. Oblivious Garden does this well. The only complaint I have is that there are a few characters I would've loved to know more about, but sadly did not get the joy of seeing much. However, the main route characters (Dyanna, Lin, Leira and Finn) are each very unique and have rather long routes, Finn's being the shortest. I really like how the dev team made each character have a unique issue with Irell or themselves, and the unique features/traits that everyone had (I want to explain but it would spoil a lot). But overall, excellent character design and personalities. I loved them!

Gameplay/choices
VNs don't generally have much gameplay, however this is not a kinetic visual novel: there are choices. The choices in this game are generally challenging as well (especially with Leira), so you have to actually pay attention and try to pick the best thing to do. Choices go from 2 to 4 choices, and are not THAT frequent BUT they are at the beginning and middle of the route. Ending choices get very crucial to your ending status.
A unique feature this game has in an in-game mini-game called "Finn's Teatime" where you use your ingredients you have gotten to make new recipes and get the correct amount of everything to make the best tea. It sounds very simple, and it is, but it's also quite fun to get the achievements associated with it. Tea makes a frequent appearance in the routes (as this is a formal atmosphere).

Writing
The writing in this game, hands down is GORGEOUS. Storytelling at it's finest, ladies and gents. While there are a few flaws (from translation) overall it is very well-written. I didn't notice any sort of plot holes within the writing, and the dialogue for each character was pretty good. Their inner-thoughts and such fit very well with their personalities. The only awkward thing was when the dialogue box switched over to one of the princesses, and it was difficult to tell who was talking until they said "I, Leira, think...". I knew Irell was not talking, but when multiple princesses were involved, things got a little confusing. But this is no reason to dis the game, and this is only a small flaw that occurs maybe 3 times in the entire game so I would not worry about it.

Pros
  • Excellent character design and personalities.
  • Interesting main character.
  • Depth in general to things.
  • CGs are great.
  • Complex story with a simple outer-shell.
  • Fun mini-game.
  • Good amount of gameplay.

Cons
  • Re-used backgrounds.
  • Not a logical ending sometimes (nit-picking).
  • DLC achievements.
  • Finn's route was a little short.
  • Leira's ending was rushed IMO.

Final Opinion
If I was to score it, solid 8/10. There were not that many flaws, and the ones that were weren't huge and contributed to any gameplay. I cannot wait until the sequel comes out, and I will absolutely purchase it! Thanks for reading, and consider buying this game if you are into VNs!

Product received for free
Playing Oblivious Garden ~Carmina Burana is a beautiful and romantic experience. Set in a far-off magical land, you step into the shoes of Irell Reis, who has been ordered to help tutor seven princesses. Each story route brings with it new adventures and dangers, this simple garden isn’t all that it seems. Become immersed in the lives of the princesses and the rich history of the Magical Empire of Latoy, learn about gods and war as you romance the lady of your choice.

Each route gives you different parts of the story, but all you know at the start is that you are Irell, a disgraced general defeated in an important battle that cost the lives of thousands of soldiers. The Emperor sees fit to give Irell another chance and he is sent to the Oblivious Garden to be a fencing instructor. The garden is so named as its residents are oblivious to the outside world, and with good reason: The garden is protected by a magical barrier making it more of a prison. On the surface the game might seem like it’s simply about romance but there is a lot of magic in this world and a complicated backstory that is touched on in pieces in each different route. Despite being in the garden with seven princesses you can only romance three of them as well as a maid.

  • Irell - One of the greatest warriors serving under the Emperor, he is a skilled fighter but not so skilled with women. After a devastating loss he is stripped of his titles and banished for three years, only to return for a new mission- to tutor the princesses. He wants to atone for his failure, and help the souls of his fallen soldiers find peace. Due to these burdens, he spends a great deal of the game depressed. Through his experiences in the garden and his relationships with the princesses, he finds peace of mind and a new future to fight for.

  • Leira - Leira’s route is the simplest, it doesn’t delve too far into magic nor the long history of Latoy, instead it plays more like a traditional dating sim as you work to pursue her. Her backstory is the one most connected to Irell himself and because of her harsh attitude she takes the most work to romance. She is the classic “tsundere” character type though getting to know her explains her personality much more. She is also the focus of the free White Day DLC.

  • Lin - She is the quiet and reserved type, at first seeming standoffish and dull until you get to know her. Of the princesses, her route is the shortest and has the least amount of choices. It does, however, have a good story and I found it to be the most romantic. She is a very skilled swordswoman but rejects the sword because of a deep darkness hidden inside of her. Following her route uncovers some of the history of Latoy and a bit of the Emperor’s true nature but not much.
  • Dyanna - While Dyanna’s route has less choices than Leira’s, I found it to be the longest. I played this route first because I found her very beautiful but in hindsight it should have been last because of its length and heavy backstory about Latoy. While she and Irell grow close through the story, after you’re done making choices the story continues on explaining her history, her magic and more about the garden and Emperor and Irell himself. It is a pretty heavy story and can seem confusing at first, but the climax is pretty epic.

  • Finn - Gentle Finn is the head maid in the garden and tends to all of the princesses except for Leira who has a personal maid. She knows everything going on but isn’t the gossiping type. She is friendly, kind and adorable throughout every route. Her own route, however, is a bit confusing. I really can’t go into details but it made Finn seem bipolar, it also didn’t seem to accurately portray Lin either but it is the shortest route with the fewest choices.

  • Intelia, Ereface, Charlotte and Sherle - The other princesses in the garden that you cannot romance. Intelia serves as the headmaster of the garden. Ereface prefers to stay in the library, and her magic helps keep the garden beautiful. Charlotte is the daughter of a general and a great fencer. Sherle is the youngest in appearance and in her attitude, usually playing the role of a kid sister. You only really learn more about Ereface and that is through Dyanna’s route. The others get little face time in the game save for Intelia, who is present in all routes but is by her nature very mysterious.

Since this is a visual novel, the interface is exclusively point-and-click. You read through the story and at times make choices to help move the story in a certain direction. The game has the standard features for this genre; an auto text option, a skip previously seen text option and the ability to scroll back and see past text. You can save, load, remove text from the screen to see pictures fully and the game also auto saves. A nice feature I liked was the dictionary, which is available once you finish any route. It contains facts about Latoy and all of the characters. Another neat feature is the tea-making mini game. Since this game is about refined young ladies, tea takes a high priority, especially in Leira’s route. Through the mini game you can brew different types of tea with ingredients you gain through the story and also through raising your tea-brewing level. The only downside is that the game gives you little or no help on how to figure out how to brew good tea, without a guide you’re probably going to be lost.

Artwise there is nothing negative that can be said, the art in this game is beyond gorgeous. Every princess looks beautiful, the backgrounds are lovely and the menus and user interfaces are very elegant. The music is also amazing, every melody is beautifully orchestrated and I highly recommend picking up the soundtrack as well. Where the game falters is the voice acting. The main game has voices for the entire female cast while the DLC also adds male voice acting. I found the acting to be fine but the audio quality was inconsistent. At times some voices were loud, some very muffled and no one was at the same level or quality. What was worse was that at times there was no audio where there should be. Lin was apart of Finn’s story route but had no voice acting through most of it, she also missed lines in her own route. There were also times when what the actress said didn’t match with the text on the screen, though not many that I noticed with my very limited knowledge of Chinese.

Despite some flaws I personally loved playing this game. It was quite lengthy, easily offering 10-20 hours of gameplay to complete everything. There are many Steam achievements to get and also trading cards as well. While at times the story may feel contrived or confusing, I fell in love with the princesses as much as Irell did. My least-favorite princess, Leira, became one of my favorites. The game also has a side story that is free DLC about Leira though her personality is a bit different from the main story and there is added fan service but also more achievements. If you enjoy visual novels then I highly recommend adding Oblivious Garden ~Carmina Burana to your library.

What an oddity.

I have a deep love for everything victorian and regal, and was instantly sold on this game by that fact alone. I was not dissapointed, and yet; I cannot recommend this game at full price.

The art is gorgeous, and all of the characters are beautiful and a pleasure to take in. The backgrounds and scene images are also beautiful. If I had any complaint it would be how they are used. Your bedroom is also the princess's bedroom, the fencing hall is also the stage, and the a destroyed city is also the same city you visit in present day as another city. They reuse assests in such a way it is quite easy to get confused and you rely heavily on context to figure out where you are. A minor complaint but one that does get your attention when you are suddenly enjoying a festival in an identical city as one that no longer exists.

The audio is varied. There are voice actors and you can choose to disable any or all of them, they aren't terrible but they aren't great either. All dialogue is spoken in chinese and the audio sounds compressed when compared to the music. I turned off all the voices personally since I find it easier to get into a character when I hear the voices I gave them in my head as opposed to the ones the game gives you. The music is of higher quality and fits the mood, however I was dissapointed by how forgetable and generic it is with the exception of a few tracks. I was hoping to spend hours enjoying the sound of violins and pianos long after I stopped playing the game, but not once did I feel inclined to listen to the soundtrack outside of the game.

As a choose your own adventure novel gameplay is a minor point but there are some major issues I have here that I need to speak for. There is a mini game in which you make tea and the quality of tea you make requires you to know recipes which you pick up throughout the game FROM INSANELY VAGUE CLUES. I settled on a walkthrough to create some of these teas and from the comments on the walkthrough (found in steam guides for this game btw) I am not alone in the confusion I had with this mini game. It feels forced into the story and it really pulls you out of the story to have the crude and sparactic ui (menus that shift around the screen randomly as you click on them) took away a lot from the story the game wants to tell. I will discuss my other major gameplay issue when talking about story.

STORY. It starts out a little slow but quickly you become excited as the game unfolds from some dark text, I am talking body parts and the like. That is my first issue, the game is fairly light hearted and when dark elements do arise they are heavy handed and paint a disgusting picture (Babies being pulled from crying mothers and then stomped upon, removal of limbs, etc) which may work in something like higurashi, but when you are having tea with a stubborn princess in a light hearted way moments prior it feels disgusting and out of place.
I can only speak for Leira's path since it is the only one I have ( and want to ) finished at this moment. The Characters are deep interesting and I did smile on more than a few occasions. Nothing made me laugh out loud or cry but I did feel enjoyment early on for the characters I met. However as the game goes on, some characters personalities completly change for one or two scenes AS PLOT and then turn back without ever really being mentioned again. Some really cute and clever plot twists are thrown in and never developed and the writing goes from being this beautiful old english that had me looking up a few words to writing that felt of a far lower quality. It is jarring and quite noticable. This is a rare occasion where the story could have been three times longer and meaningful had they taken more time to flesh out these plot twists and ideas. The first few hours (minus the intro) is proof of how well they can actually write. So many curve balls and clever twists wasted by trying to tell a complex story in the length of a pulp fiction novel.
Without spoilers the ending doesn't know what it wanted to be, tragedy or happy. It hits this odd place of unsatisfying and frustrating but still ok. It pulls the "everything is ♥♥♥♥ but its okay!" approach and with so little time spent on it, it is almost cringey.
HOWEVER MY BIGGEST COMPLAINT. It commits a huge sin that sadly many games (not just vn's commit) when giving the player options. The lack of clarity and understanding of choices will lead to failures and trial and error. Do you like the sunset or do you just like being 'here'(earth,garden,alive? who knows). choose carefully or you will be cut short on what seemed like an otherwise great relationship. There is no build up or understanding of what you are choosing half the time, and when these choices can lead to failure (sometimes after many other choices leading you into trial and error) it becomes less of choose your own adventure and more of something skin to playing dragons lair minus the onscreen cues. I would have enjoyed this vn 100x more had the choices been removed entirely instead, not ideal but far better than trial and error.

This is not a great novel nor is it focused however it was enjoyable enough that I felt the need to finish it to completiton, and while I have zero desire to play the other routes, I am still glad I gave it a chance, as it scratched my love for all things victorian itch.
If anything the game offers seems interesting consider grabbing it during a sale, but at 15 usd I just can't give it a thumbs up.

The writing in this game, hands down is GORGEOUS. Storytelling at it's finest, ladies and gents. While there are a few flaws (from translation) overall it is very well-written. Pros
  • Excellent character design and personalities.
  • Interesting main character.
  • Depth in general to things.
  • CGs are great.
  • Complex story with a simple outer-shell.
  • Fun mini-game.
  • Good amount of gameplay.

Cons
  • Re-used backgrounds.
  • Not a logical ending sometimes (nit-picking).
  • DLC achievements.
  • Finn's route was a little short.
  • Leira's ending was rushed IMO.

If I was to score it, solid 8/10. There were not that many flaws, and the ones that were weren't huge and contributed to any gameplay. I cannot wait until the sequel comes out, and I will absolutely purchase it! Thanks for reading, and consider buying this game if you are into VNs!

You are a proud and successful military general, you have won many wars and conquered many kingdoms. But during the deciding battle you make a grave mistake, and you lose everything. Shamed and shunned, you return home to your emperor. How can you ever redeem yourself? As it turns out, the emperor already has a new task in mind.

Oblivious Garden ~Carmina Burana is a romance-themed visual novel set in a post-medieval fantasy world. You are a humiliated ex-general and your new job is to teach the art of fencing to the seven princesses of the kingdom.

The first thing you'll likely notice when you're looking at Oblivious Garden for the first time is the unique and stylish artwork. The character portraits and CGs are gorgeous without exception and the gothic/baroque-inspired character designs mesh surprisingly well with the pastel-focused watercolor art style. The VN has a refined visual identity that sticks out of the crowd and will secure it a second look from many prospective readers. The backgrounds are unfortunately of lower quality on average and range from nicely painted landscapes to crude photo manipulations of rooms. The soundtrack is fitting and atmospheric, but a little repetitive. This would have been counteracted by either a bigger selection of tracks or at least less obvious loops (they're not jarring but very apparent after the first few times). There's Chinese voice acting available that complements the character designs pretty well and provides fitting voices to go along with the faces. It has a spiffy opening animation and the technical aspects (controls, save system etc.) leave little to be desired.

The story is set in the titular "oblivious garden", where the emperor has sealed away the seven princesses. The many parallels drawn to the royal courts in 17th century Europe extend not only to the opulent garden itself, but also to the complex social rules and codes governing the interactions between Irell Reis, the ex-military main character, and the princesses residing in the area. Don't expect much in the way of historical accuracy though, I got the impression that the social aspects were influenced at least as much by the developers' Chinese background as by their impression of past European nobility, which is not a problem at all considering it's a fantasy world. While this sort of setting has been attempted before in fiction, it deserves recognition for originality in the realm of visual novels, even though admittedly I am generous with my praise for any setting that is not a Japanese high school.

In another contrast to the visual novel archetype, the main character is a young adult with a sizable amount of life experience as a military general. Thematically, in accordance with that choice, Oblivious Garden does not shy away from the heavy-hitters: Recurring topics are the justifiability of war and violence, the vicious circle of revenge, the guilt of enabling and orchestrating mass slaughter versus "just following orders", and of course the dichotomy between fate and free will. The VN uses these issues quite expertly in the interactions between the central characters.

Rounding out the package is a charming little mini game about brewing different kinds of tea that maybe could have been slightly more forthcoming with its solutions (or maybe I'm just bad at it), but offers a fun break from the plot and an entertaining experience nudging you to perhaps make a nice cup of tea for yourself as well.

I would have loved to love Oblivious Garden. I saw almost all the building blocks needed for a fantastic visual novel, I really liked the art and the concept, the setting seemed unique and interesting. Yet, I can't love it. I can merely bring myself to say that it is pretty good all things considered. That is because Oblivious Garden commits my one cardinal sin, the one thing that a visual novel can do that I really cannot forgive: The writing is bad. Or rather, it has its good and even great moments (lots of them in fact) but it just can't sustain a consistent level of quality throughout the whole story. And for a visual novel, that is a huge problem.

To be clear, I am not talking about the low-quality English script that is rife with grammatical errors and vocabulary mixups. I know this can be a dealbreaker for many of you, so it deserves pointing out, but personally I can usually gloss over shoddy translations easily if I set my mind to it. In any case, it is something that can be fixed by putting the script into the hands of a better qualified translator or at least a native English speaking editor, which I have reason to hope might still happen. There were only one or two spots that I straight up didn't understand for which I would blame the lackluster translation. My main gripes run much deeper.

But if the writing isn't good, then why do I still give Oblivious Garden an overall "thumbs up" rating? There are reasons for that, I like to think they are good ones, and I will tell you all about them, but first I'd like to elaborate on the specific ways in which I consider the writing lacking and the ways in which it actually succeeds, so you can draw your own conclusions about whether or not your opinion would differ from mine and whether the things that bothered me might be non-issues to you.

Intro
I’m not an avid reader but I do enjoy reading a book from time to time (it is rare, though). Visual novels are a little different but they’re easier for me to relate to because you actually see the characters and don’t have to visualize everything. If you’re a fanatic book reader you probably don’t understand this reasoning because interpreting and visualizing is probably what makes reading a book so magical in the first place. The upside to reading a visual novel is that your choices can effect and influence the story. This differs per game though. But Oblivious Garden is such a game. Did I enjoy it ? Let’s find out.

Gameplay
Reading a book – it doesn’t matter if it’s a physical one, digital one or interactive one – requires a lot of patience. You could miss parts of the story just by not paying (enough) attention, which is actually a shame. The story of Oblivious Garden caught my attention and it seemed very interesting to me. That’s why I was eager to give it a try.
You play as Irell Reis, a once famous general who is given the task of hosting the next fiesta, where the God will forgive the people who sacrificed themselves on foreign land during the war. You will prepare yourself in the garden of the Emperor, where seven princess live who are going to help and work with you. That’s the short version and it might not be entirely accurate as I usually forget smaller details. So forgive me if I’ve made a mistake.
I think it was clear that playing this game is like reading a book, but one that requires interaction from you, the player. You’ll be clicking through the conversations, occasionally making a choice that might affect the story and you’ll even be playing a little minigame where you have to brew tea.
You’ll be talking with the seven princesses, along with some other characters, and well, that’s basically all that you’ll do. I’ve played the game two times now but haven’t actually left the garden even once. It’s just non-stop conversing with the main characters. This frustrated me a little because the bigger story was the thing that caught my interest in the first place.
Also, it isn’t entirely clear what’s expected of you. As I said I’ve played through the game twice, but every time the game ended prematurely because I seemingly didn’t make the right choices. Both times I’ve selected entirely different answers but that didn’t seem to help. It’s very frustrating if you don’t know what to do and it insults my intelligence by simply ending the story so soon without being able to set things right. I don’t want to replay the entire story over again and mouseclick through a story that I’ve already read. This could’ve been handled in a better way, I think because I’m most certainly not going to play through the story for a third time, without knowing for sure that I can complete the story ‘correctly’. It might actually have been the tea minigame which didn’t make much sense to me (I was just randomly adding ingredients, for example), but I doubt the game counts things like that as a failure.
Unfortunately, the game’s typos and occasional bad grammar didn’t help either. For a game that consists of large pieces of text I actually expect something to proofread it before releasing it to a larger audience. I still understood most sentences/conversations so it’s not that I didn’t make sense of it all. It was just… distracting.
Other than that the writing is pretty good and the story is somewhat interesting. I just expected a little more here.

Audio & Graphics
The one thing that’s immediately noticeable is how loud the music is when you first start the game. It can, of course, be easily adjusted through the Options menu but it surprised me a little.
The music is actually very good and the Japanese voice acting – even if it’s useless – seemed accurate but since I don’t actually speak Japanese I’m just guessing here. Still, voice acting isn’t something you see very often in visual novels (I haven’t played that many) so it’s absolutely appreciated.
The game uses mixed art. Some environments look beautiful and others seem to use a different kind of style, like your own room. Nothing ugly, but, again, distracting. The characters are competently made and what you can expect from a visual novel. This means ‘childish’ Japanese girls, with big eyes, small mouths and perfect bodies. If only something would try something new. I usually can’t really relate to Japanese/anime characters. Still, it’s all very solid and I can’t really complain here. It’s a ‘love it-or-hate it’ kind of thing.

Length & Replayability
I’ve already spoken a bit about this in the Gameplay paragraph but the shortest playthrough seemed to take about 2 hours of my time. That is with normal settings so no uberspeedy text. I’ve been told that a ‘good’ playthrough will take about 4-5 hours of your time so that’s pretty solid. Choices do seem to make a difference, but not much. This means you can replay the game and you might get a different outcome but in my two playthroughs I got the exact same ending. I did notice small variations in the story though. Considering the price I think you get enough gameplay out of it, but to be honest I would wait for a sale. That’s from a person that doesn’t play a lot of visual novels so it might be a different story if you actually do play a lot of these games.

Closing
Oblivious Garden pulled me in with its interesting story but unfortunately didn’t keep me interested for long. The story stopped short fairly soon and I had no clue what I did wrong. Even during my second playthrough I couldn’t for the life of me see what I did wrong in the first place.
The game can absolutely use a little (more) direction, or improve things to make a second (or third, fourth) playthrough worth your time.
I have played better and more interesting visual novels during my career as a gamer and therefore I can only recommend to wait for a sale or to just grab one of those others. Please take note that I don’t think this is a bad game per se but it just isn’t worth the current asking price in my opinion.

To be short
Let me sum all of these things up for you, for those of you who don't have the time to read it all.

Pros:

■ Okay visuals
■ Voice 'acting'
■ Multiple endings

Cons:

■ Lacks direction
■ Story isn't as deep as I hoped it would be
■ Been there, done that 'visual novel'-style
■ Typos & grammar mistakes
