About This Game

Neo-San Francisco, 2064 AD. Your life as a struggling journalist is interrupted by the world's first sapient machine, a ROM (Relationship and Organizational Manager) named Turing. Together, you and the quirky robot will encounter a colorful cast of locals and overcome challenges as you uncover the city’s secrets behind the overlapping futures of technology and humanity.

Read Only Memories is a new cyberpunk adventure that will take you on a journey of puzzle-solving and exploring as you investigate the mysterious world and characters of Neo-San Francisco. Inspired by classics like Gabriel Knight and Snatcher, ROM is a retrofuturistic take on the point and click adventure genre that features a rich mature narrative and unique gameplay challenges hidden within the beautiful interactive pixel scenes. The deeper you delve into the life of your missing friend, the further you get entangled into this tale about the dark side of technology.

Can you survive in Neo-SF? Or will the mystery go unsolved?

Key Features:

  • Dynamic narrative defined by player choices, with over 10 hours of dialogue, exploration, and gameplay challenges with multiple solutions.
  • BAFTA-nominated voice actors Adam Harrington and Dave Fennoy guide immersion in the aesthetics along with the exclusive synth-driven original soundtrack by 2 Mello and the retro futuristic pixel art style of JJSignal.
  • A vibrant cast of characters populate the world, as the rich interactive encounters with allies and enemies alike will unearth world-changing revelations.

Note: Cloud Saves currently only support same platform. Multi-platform support coming soon!

A pixel-art point'n'click with a cyberpunk setting? Yes, please, should be right up my alley. (I simply love most of what Wadjet Eye have done, for example.) I even liked the demo.

But no. I found myself having to force myself to play this and always had enough after a short time -- it just did not grip me.
One reason might be that it's more a visual novel than a point'n'click, there just isn't much gameplay.
The text appearing letter by letter (plus sound effect) is cute, but it gets old really quickly as it's tiring on the eyes. Same goes for the emotion sound effects -- nice idea, yet after a while, they grate.
Lastly, it has a pet cause that it relentlessly gets in your face with. I love games that make you think, I don't mind games that have a message, but I loathe games that hit you over the head with their message again and again. Towards the end I flinched at every ... creative use of language.
Speaking of language: The script was functional, but not beautifully written by any standards and there were a couple of mistakes that made me wonder if English was the writers' native language. (But take that with a grain of salt, I'm not a native speaker either.)

So, as a free promotional game commissioned by an NGO, wonderful, but I wouldn't pay for it (again).

To preface, I enjoyed the game. There's a lot of good going on here and I don't regret playing it. But I don't think I could really recommend it to someone else.

Read Only Memories is a quirky, good looking game filled with several endearing characters and some brief dips into philosophy tied together with a good-enough plot. At times the music, visuals, and writing are excellent, but there’s a disappointing lack of consistently throughout the (very short) game. It’s also very keen to represent minority groups, either directly or through allegory, but how the narrative does it ends up feeling ham-fisted on several occasions. The game is quick to chastise you if you are not nice enough on certain topics which ends up severely taking away from the message it’s ultimately trying to put forward.

The biggest flaw of the title is that gameplay is a chore. Dialogue comes in droves and is painfully slow to get through; the two mini games are clunky, and it quickly becomes obvious that you are almost entirely on rails. I think that presenting this as a choose-your-own-adventure interactive novel rather than as a full-fledged game would have been a better choice.

I also can’t agree with this game being categorized as a cyberpunk title. It has many of the staples you see in cyberpunk—crime and counter-culture, dredging through the messes that corrupt corporations make, questions about what it means to be human—but the tone and visual aesthetic of the game are too light to really fit the genre.

Overall, I wish that the narrative had been treated with more subtlety—more nuanced characters and less clear-cut morality on the issues presented—and that the game wasn’t as frustrating to play. Fun way to spend an evening or two but not worth the full price tag.

It's a no for me.

For some context, I am a gay, nonwhite cismale and when I first heard of this game some time ago I was enamored by the idea of a game that presented a more nonwhite, nonbinary world. Something that is so rare in all media, and especially in video games. Imagine my feelings of luck at finding it dirt cheap in a bundle. But having played through it now, I realize that if I were to buy it full price I would have regretted that choice pretty much within the first hour of play, and even now that I've slogged through the bad parts to the okay parts, I can still only say that what I like the most about this game is how it gives me the hope that other, similarly inclusive games can break onto the scene.

I have so many issues with the game that I'm not sure where to start. On the level of mechanics, the game is a point and click with very little puzzle solving and more running through dialogue trees which are less trees and more bamboo poles. You go in a straight line and the only thing that your choices affect seems to be whether or not characters like you, which affects certain events in the story, yes, but it seems to come up with ways for the plot to resolve itself anyway, making many of your choices utterly meaningless exempting the endings, which are apparently based on whether or not your annoying robot friend likes you. And that's fine. It's a visual novel. I like visual novels. But what I hate is that it puts you at the helm and lets you make choices and then takes them away from you at the moments where you have the most reason to protest. What if I don't want to trust the cutesy bot that just broke into my house and threatened to rip my arm off? What if I think explaining my situation to the associate of the character who has explicitly been stated to be the least trustworthy out of all the characters introduced is a bad idea? What's that? Only one option here? Then the characters act as if I'm the one who came up with this nonsense: "Oh he's so stubborn... he's so dogged and determined... I'm not surprised with your track record hahaha." For a game about identity, ostensibly pushing for the freedom to express certain identities, this game really goes out of its way to pigeonhole who you are so that it can keep the plot running.

Then there's the lore dumping. I get it, the setting is colorful and vast. And truth be told, a lot of it is interesting. But not every single character has to be an excuse to exposit on something. Turing is fine. You don't have to have TOMCAT going on and on about the same technobabble. You don't have to have a coffee shop manager explain to me what moe means with a dissertation about Japanese culture. I got so pleased when it turned out one of the characters I was looking for was dead because it meant I didn’t have to through another 20-30 incredibly slow (even at max speed) text boxes of some minor character explaining something that had nothing to do with the plot.

And what a plot. Every attempt has been made to sugarcoat real world dilemmas in pretty metaphors and good intentions, and you know what, it’s insulting. It’s insulting to have the analogue of oppressed peoples being a subculture that gene splices to become furries. Being born into a world that treats you like less of a person because of something out of your control (being born female, nonwhite, LGBTQ+, disabled; having to abort an unwanted pregnancy that might have been forced upon you) is not the same as choosing to have bunny ears or whining that rocket fists are illegal. That is not the same. It’s insulting to be told the world of tomorrow might be instantly changed by the sudden appearance of a new sapient species and literally the next in game day (your new game plus) having your characters discussing wages for the new robot race like nothing has happened. One of the things real people are still fighting for today and this game believes that the people in power will just hand it over like it’s chewed up gum if you ask nicely. This game belittles the suffering of so many people and it’s gross and it’s stupid.

I want to like this game. I think it’s full of charm and good intentions, and in the end, despite their shortcomings, I came to love the characters. How could I not want a game with not one, not two, but an entire cast of nonconforming characters, to be good? But it really is just a wish-fulfillment sugar plum fantasy wrapped in an overly verbose box with a predictable villain and many minor irritations along the way. And I wouldn't recommend it.

Missed it by THAT much!

There's a lot to like in this game. The art is very well done, the setting is intriguing, the characters are mostly likable and the plot is very engaging. Unfortunately, there's just not very much to DO.

It starts out seeming like a classic inventory based adventure game, but puzzles are very few and far between, to the point will you will soon find you stop clicking on anything since almost nothing is actually useful. The player is often railroaded from one location to another with no input on what to do next or where to go. In the rare instances a puzzle does present itself, they are disappointingly easy and simple. Instead, what you spend most of your playtime in this game listening to NPCs talk to each other while occasionally getting a word in edgewise.

This may not sound like much of a chore, but the text types out while annoying sound effects play. The text is also overly pixilated and difficult to read, Even hitting the "skip" bar as fast as you can doesn't help due to the slow pace at which the text appears and the often unskippable animations that go along with it. A lot of the dialog IS interesting, but most of it is circuitous and repetitive. A good editor could probably cut it down by about 50% and not lose anything.

Even with relatively minimal puzzle content I could have recomended it as a more narrative experience, but wading through the text gets so grating it largely ruins the majority of play time. I can't say it's a must-have at the $20 full price.

I bought this game as part of a Humble Bundle deal and started playing on a whim because I liked the art and what semblance of tone/plot I could gather from the screenshots and product description. Every day I take about an hour of my free time to finish up another chapter of the story. I haven't finished the game yet but this has been a very charming experience thus far! I'd play again, I think, just to see what effect being a total jerk would serve, but I think I know the answer to that already. (But mostly I'd play again for Turing.)

Do you like AI, especially AI that have prominent roles as characters? This story is for you.

Do you like being able to see queer people represented in story? This story is for you.

If you like both of those things, the story is doubly for you.

A brief snapshot: The story starts with the introduction of Turing, a chatty little robot who seeks out your help after the disappearance of your old friend Hayden (Turing's creator). This is Turing's first time being independently apart from Hayden - basically, an important formative stage for them, so the majority of character growth you see is from them.
The main heart of the story is Turing, and your feelings on them are going to probably majorly affect how you feel about the rest of the story.

The story tells of AI development from a somewhat kindly perspective - one that is a bit more focused on the development of an individual than most. It's pretty rare to have stories centered around AI actually focus on them as characters and not the problems of the human who is dealing with them, so I thought that was a really enjoyable aspect of the story.

The game has a nice soundtrack,; it sets the mood for the era the story is in. The characters are pretty fun, and you get to meet a myriad of them, all of who can form different opinions of you by the end game. They range in positions too, from law-abiding detectives to criminals to well meaning people who are somewhere in-between.

If any of those elements sound appealing, I do recommend it. It could do with more options in some places, but I was really happy with the elements it handled that I enjoy, and if you like the kinds of story elements the game advertises I think you'll ultimately be pretty happy with it.

The year is 2065. The streets of New-Angeles are riddeled with cri- actually, everything is mostly alright. The world is at the crossroads of three technological singularities. The flames of conflict engulf the peoples and the warring sides settle their differences though democracy and peaceful process.

Needless to say, the author has a very idealistic view of the future..

Read Only Memories is an old school Point and Click adventure, drawing heavy inspiration from Snatcher and Policenauts in terms of its story and presentation. You take the role of a faceless reporter and blogger who wakes up to find a sentient robot sitting next to your bed. Unfortunately, the game doesn't permit you to throw your pillow at them and scream at the top of ones lungs, like one would do in real life.

What follows is a six hour long journey of conspiracies and espionage to uncover the fate of your mising friend with a help of a sillicon companion.

My first compaint with the story is its initial linearity, you are dragged out of your appartment and are nailed to the floor to sit and listen to ten minutes of exposition, followed by another ten minutes of exposition. Though the writing is fairly adequate, one cannot but feel that it could have been compressed or layed out more elegantly.

After that tunel of love, the game finally opens up, both in terms of exploration and the lore. The story takes a turn and introduces three subplots, simply though the player's exploration of the enviorment. Survelance state, radical gene splicing therapy and naturalist movement. Unfortunately, only one of those paths ends up having a propper conclusion, which makes me think that at least some of the content has been cut in development.

However, what carries the story are its characters.

By the time the little bobble head robot asks you about your prefered pronouns and gives you a three page list, you begin to understand what kind of message was trying to get across. The writer was aiming to present a cast of stark diverse characters and achieved this goal quite well. A character isn't defied by their skin colour, orientation, but by their.. well.. character.

Identity politics on the internet are quite a touchy subject, though the creator manages to handle the issue quite well. Most authors tend to get so hang up on creating an inclusive cast that they forget to actually write it, which leads to a room filled with multicoloured department store mannequins or Skyrim NPCs. My personal favourites are the pair of bartenders you meet at the pub and the splicing activist.

After solving a series of puzzles, the game begins holding your hand and then clunches it, like a paranoid mother. After the second act, the exploration ceases and "puzzles" become way too linear. Most players are upset when an adventure is impossible to complete without a guide or pilgrimage to GameFAQs, but this is taking things way too far in the other directions. Usually the puzzle solutions are a few pixels from the puzzle itself. Calling them puzzles would be like calling putting on tooth paste on a toothbrush a "puzzle".

A few minor story branches after, the game kicks you down a corridor for about 40 minutes before the end. There is one scene where your companion mentions where we could get a fake passport and hijack a car. They begins to describe a slum located away from the city, populated by outcast spliced human-animal hybrids.

Following a descrimination bill, many spliced individuals lost their homes and jobs and became vagrants. In order to defend their new home from extremist groups, many established backalley gene splicing clinics and began selling black market military grade mechanical augmentations. Having lost everything, they have shed their humanity in order to become something greater.

So, can we go there? No, instead we just talk to a few NPC's that we have already met and get the key items anyway.

The last ten minutes the game goes full on action, with stealth and shootouts, which are handled fairly well, despite not being central mechanics.

There are multible endings, each doing a pretty good job of wrapping up most of the loose ends. Some are just left hanging there..

All in all, I recommend Read only Memories or ROM (I get it now). A flawed, though fairly endeering adventure that managed to remain short and punchy, despite its linearity and pacing issues. My only wish it that it dug deeper in to the societal implications of radical transhumanism. A sequel, perhaps?

Anyway, I'm off to a gene clinic to get a cool new lizard tale.
