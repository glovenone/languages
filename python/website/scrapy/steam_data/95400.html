Update for Mac - Fix/Workaround for Friend Invite overlay missing.

Multiple players reported on friend invites not working on Mac. More precise, the Invite screen would not pop up after selecting 'invite friend' from the game menu.

In the long run we hope to have a proper solution as we understand this is not ideal.

Designed for Two Player Co-op


ibb & obb is a cooperative adventure designed for two players. So find a friend for some true co-op puzzle solving fun.

Can I play online?
You can play co-op locally or online. Online you can either invite a Steam friend or match up with a random other player. For a local game you can make use of keyboard and controllers, or a combination of those.

No single player?
Technically you can control both characters simultaneously on your keyboard, but the whole game is really designed around the interaction between two players. On your own it'll be very hard and simply less fun.

Steam Big Picture

About This Game

ibb & obb is a two player cooperative game set in a puzzle filled world where gravity goes both up and down. You can only succeed by working closely together.

Find a friend for some true local co-op couch fun or match up online.

Fall up and jump down through 15 levels filled with double gravity puzzles and discover 8 hidden worlds that will test your new non-Newtonian skills to the maximum.

All levels have their own unique music, composed by Kettel, known for his warm melodic electronica.

Key Features

  • Local and online co-op
  • Warm electronic soundtrack by Kettel
  • Steam achievements, leaderboards, multiplayer, cloud saving and trading cards
  • Secret levels that will test your skills to the max

*I didn't try playing single player. I played co-op with my best frenemy.

ibb & obb is a delightful co-op puzzle platformer for 2 (players) that plays around with gravity. The puzzles are well thought out and paced very well. The controls are very basic, all you can do in ibb & obb is move left, right, and jump. Despite this, the devs have managed to include a decent mix of gameplay mechanics that are never overused. ibb & obb feels fresh the whole way through and it never feels as if any part of it is overly repetitive or drawn out. It can be challenging at times, but the solution is almost always clear (the introduction of bubbles -if you play you'll know when you see- is a little bit unclear), and always rewarding to beat.

If playing through multiple levels at once, it seemlessly ends one level with a scoreboard display on an in-game sign as you run right(literally, the direction right -->) through to the next level. The downside of this is that it is unclear when it autosaved, or indeed if it was saving progress at all. Don't worry though, because it turns out the game saves at the end of each level and the "restart in limbo" option goes to a level select area where you can start where ever you want.

I would recommend playing local co-op or online co-op with a voice call. The game includes a drawing mechanism that traces the mouse, or traced by the right analog stick (on an XBox360 controller which I used). Presumably this is for helping with co-op communications but I just used it to doodle. I would just set up a voice call and use that for co-op. Laughter is contagious, so you won't get that experience unless you're either playing local or in a call anyway.

There are very few negatives I can pick out about ibb & obb. The options are a bit lacking; the sound options are missing entirely and there are no resolution options but these issues I would argue aren't major. One of the aforementioned mechanics could have been introduced or better designed (the bubbles). They don't really pop out so sometimes it is easy to miss them. For the most part though, ibb & obb is just a little bundle of co-op joy.

I had a bunch of fun and laughs with ibb & obb and would gladly recommend it to anyone with a co-op partner.

One of the most enjoyable co-op experiences I've had. Great puzzles, beautiful art style, cute characters, good music. Get a whole lot of mileage out of a handful of simple gameplay mechanics. You'll find yourself screaming in frustration, shouting and pumping your fist in triumph, erupting in cathartic laughter as it finally seems you and your friend will accomplish the intricate, difficult procedure you've spent so long figuring out and attempting, only to see your friend make a stupid mistake and ruin everything at the last second.

The game only lets you communicate with your online parter by drawing blobby, imprecise lines onscreen. This could be seen as a drawback, but in fact it becomes its own meta-puzzle: how do I explain this detailed series of actions that will involve precise timing, without using any words? You come to develop a rudimentary syntax; you start saying yes and no by moving back and forth and jumping up and down, respectively, or you draw frantic scribbles on the screen to indicate frustration. It's so much more satisfying to solve puzzles this way; you really feel like you've accomplished something beyond the game itself. Of course there are also those infuriating moments when you give up trying to use the in-game language and resort to Steam chat, or even make a phone call and talk through it in real time. And then there are transcendent moments when you and your partner both know exactly what to do without attempting any communication at all.

It's also great fun to be a jerk and push your partner over cliffs or into enemies.

If I have only one criticism it's that if you're playing with more than one friend (say, one online and one IRL) there's no reliable way to keep track of which levels you've finished with each friend. Like, I beat the game with friend A while I was only a few levels in with friend B, and then I'd start up the game with friend B and some of the puzzles would start to seem mighty familiar, and I couldn't be sure if it was only because I'd already played them with friend A, or because I'd also already played them with friend B. You can reset ALL of your progress, which would have made it easier to kow where I was with friend B if I'd started with them AFTER beating the game with friend A, but I had two games going at the same time so deleting my progress with friend A would have deleted the friend B's progress as well, and it wouldn't have been fair to friend A, either, if we ever decided we wanted to go back and try to get all the crystals on certain levels without replaying all the others. The option for separate play "sessions" could have solved this (I always thought having three save slots in the Zelda games was overkill, but now I see the reasoning behind it). But, lengthy complaint notwithstanding, this is really a minor problem.

I was lucky enough to get this in a double pack as part of a bundle, but It's definitely worth the full price.
