About This Game

Unleash the pilot within with Dovetail Games Flight School!

Flight School, from acclaimed simulation software studio Dovetail Games, puts you straight into the cockpit with compelling, interactive missions and challenges that will get you flying like a veteran in no time.

Choose from two highly-detailed aircraft which are individually tailored to different in-depth experiences. Meanwhile, your unique Pilot Profile tracks your achievements and licenses throughout your career, while acting as a log book so you can watch your hours stack up on your way to becoming a fully-fledged pilot.

Dovetail Games Flight School Features:
  • Two high performance training aircraft: Piper PA-18 Super Cub and Piper PA-28 Cherokee
  • A unique pilot profile which keeps track of your progress
  • 15 fun and immersive lessons covering the basics of the LAPL, PPL, and night flying
  • 9 challenging missions
  • Free Flight mode, so you can try out your newfound skills anywhere in the world

Introduction
I have been a flight sim enthusiast since Falcon 3.0 back in the days. I have countless hours in FS9 and FSX as well as in DCS and X-Plane 10. I have basically spent time with every flight sim released since the early 1990's. Today I get most of my civilian flight sim needs satisfied with Prepar3D v3.

With that background I know that I am not really a part of the core audience for FSc but I still think it would be wise to listen to feedback from experienced simmers when trying to launch new products on this niche market. So look at this review from that perspective.

Hardware used when playing
I have played on my rig built in 2012 which I also use for all other sims I play. This is the hardware I use:

Motherboard: MSI Z77-S01
CPU: Intel Core i5 3570K running at 4.2GHz
CPU cooler: Corsair Hydro H100
RAM: 16 GB Corsair Vengeance DDR3 1600MHz
GPU: MSI Nvidia GTX 680 Lightning 2GB VRAM running at 1.3GHz (latest stable driver)
GPU cooler: Twin Frozr IV
SSD1: Corsair Force (SATA6) for OS (Win7/64)
SSD2: Samsung 850 Evo (SATA6) for game installation
Display: single Acer 1920x1080 144Hz monitor

Flight sim peripherals
Joystick: Thrustmaster HOTAS Warthog
Throttle: Thrustmaster HOTAS Warthog
Rudder pedals: MFG Crosswind V2
Headtracking: TrackIR 5 + Pro Clip


General
The idea of a cheap, entry level sim is great. Flight sims are a rather expensive hobby since it usually involves having hardware with a rather hefty pricetag and buying addons (I don't like the term DLC in relation to flight sims) that aren't cheap. Newcomers really need a way to "try before they buy" and FSc fills a gap and offers that. Smart move DTG.

Performance
I have no real performance issues with FSc and for the most part I get a stable framerate with everything maxed. Loading times are good for being a flight sim. One annoying thing is the object pop-in and I think that the old FSX engine is to blame here. FSc isn't really designed to look good on modern hardware and unfortunatly that shows. Furthermore, some aspects of the flight models used seems a bit off. Trimming can be difficult at times and sometimes the controls feel unresponsive. I have played with the available settings (not very many) but haven't found a real solution which is why I blame the FM's.

Price vs Quality
$15 for a flight sim is a steal. I often pay three times that money just to get one plane for DCS or P3D. And of course there has to be a tradeoff somewhere and in FSc that tradeoff is spelled GRAPHICS. FSc does not look like a flight sim released in 2016. I know, I know... It is based on the same engine as FSX and it is using the same old landclass etc. That wouldn't be a problem if DTG didn't try to hide that fact by using silly PR stunts on the FSc webpage. FACT: FSc does NOT bring a highly detailed scenery with 2016 standards. It does not offer, and I quote, "immersion, unlike anything experienced before". FSc looks rather outdated compared to other modern flight sims and gamers these days aren't stupid. Market FSc as a re-hashed budget version of FSX since that is what it is. It is never a smart move to use false advertising DTG.

Mission design
Here is my pet peeve in relation to FSc. The missions. FSc is setting out to attract newcomers to the world of aviation and it offers lessons to learn the basic skills needed. Yet basic stuff, like explaining what flaps are, is omitted. In some lessons there is "information overload" coming from the virtual flight instructor since he asks the new pilot to do things that hasn't been properly explained beforehand. The VOR navigation lessons is an example of that. There are also no lessons that properly explains ground handling and taxiing. In my opinion this should definatly be included in a sim titled "Flight School".

Instead of including these very fundamental things, FSc pits you into situations you shouldn't be in as a new pilot. You shouldn't fly over open water in a storm with a plane you know is in poor condition. You shouldn't race the sundown with only VFR qualification just to satisfy customers. I'm baffled that DTG advocates this type of poor airmanship in a sim that claims to be realistic. I also think it is kind of weird that most of the missions involves some kind of catastrophic situations instead of showing how extremely nice VFR flying can be. The nine missions available are also, quite frankly, pretty boring.

Things that need immediate fixing
The autogen objects. What were you thinking DTG? There HAS to be a separate setting for the amount of autogen objects in a flight sim. In it's current state a lot of areas look like Endor and there are trees and buildings where there shouldn't be any. I can't even fly VFR over my hometown without getting lost because of this. So please DTG - patch in a slider to set the number of autogen trees and buildings! At night this gets even worse since there are light sources on the ground where there shouldn't be any.

Light effects. Something needs to be done about the light effects. You can have sun reflections in overcast conditions and you can see the nav lights through the wings when turning your head to starboard. You have all sorts of weird lighting inside the cockpit that totally breaks immersion. This is totally unacceptable in a game released in 2016.

Draw distance. FSc needs a setting to increase/decrease how far you can see when flying depending on what hardware you have. This is fundamental in a flight simulator. The poor draw distance makes it difficult to fly VFR since you can't really see where you are going sometimes. The object pop-in is also pretty annoying.

Summary
FSc fills a gap on the flight sim market. It offers a lot of great features for newcomers. But it shouldn't be marketed as the best thing since frozen yoghurt. Make it the proper flight school it has the potential to be by including lessons on fundamental skills needed to understand how to operate a plane both on the ground and in the air. Provide some theory lessons as well and explain how flaps etc work. Fix some of the annoying issues such as lighting and autogen objects and I am sure you will attract people to the wonderful world of flight sims. FSc isn't quite there yet. Close, but not there.

Final verdict
Do I recommend it? No, not at this point in development. FSc has potential but it isn't quite ready yet. There are simply too many lose ends to make this a good sim even with it's low price taken into consideration. I would suggest picking up FSX on a sale and getting the available flying school for it instead.

I really want to give this game a thumbs-up but I can't. First and fore-most: this is a stripped-down version of FSX. What wasn't stripped-down was given a nice polish (mostly). If you have a good machine, the game looks and handles great at max settings. Though you can largely achieve the same result by installing a bunch of add-ons for FSX.

As the name implies, there are a couple flight school "campaigns." There's one for each plane (2) and they basically just consist of lessons lasting between 3 and 10 minutes. While the courses are no substitute for a FAA-approved training sim or (of course) the real thing, it actually gives you a decent idea of real flight mechanics (assuming you turn realism up to max) even you are just playing with a single monitor, keyboard, and $20 joystick. There are also several missions to play. Much like the missions in FSX, they put you in different situations to overcome. The good unfortunately, ends there.

One thing that they actually got worse in their "repolish" of the game is the voices. I know this is a pretty minor thing; who buys a flight-sim for the voice acting, right? It still bears worth mentioning: While the pilot and control voices in FSX are border-line awful, the voice quality in DFS is way worse. It's like listening to a Mac from the 1980s "talk" to me in an English accent. It's comically bad. It almost feels like the person in charge of doing the voices for DFS had put in his two-week notice and totally checked out. Again, this isn't a make or break issue; just one that needs to be said.

But one serious issue which should steer you away from the game is this: crashing and no save files. That's right; can't save. 'Nuff said. But compile this with the crashing and you get a situation that makes it really hard to make progress and actually discourages you from restarting. The game doesn't crash a lot but it's enough to make me want my money back. The game CAN crash at any time; start up, changing settings, during cut-scenes, etc. But individually, those crashes are rare. Where it crashes the most is when it hurts the most: I'll be coming in from a long free-flight trying to add hours to my profile (only way to unlock Licence-test Flights) and just before the destination airport is in sight it'll freeze (not responding). All my time spent does't count unless I make a successful landing. This doesn't occur every time, but it occurs more often than not. The obvious remedy on the user's end is to save frequently and reload in case of software failure. BUT WAIT!!! THERE'S NO SAVE FUNCTION! It's a situation that makes you want to thrash a keyboard like an angry German kid.

If you've already bought the game and you experience the problem, I've found that doing short hops (no more than 15 minutes or so away) in sparsely populated areas (like the Sahara) are usually (but not always) safe.

I wasn't expecting much when I was buying the game (it's more bare-bones than a sun-bleached steer skeleton in the Mojave), but at least basic functionality should be a requirement when asking real money for the game. Currently, it's beta-level quality. It should be a free game with an easy mechanism for quick feed-back until they develop it more.

So in conclusion, as of the date of this writing avoid this game unless you can get it for free. Do not spend money on this game. However I feel despite the freezing/crashing issues, and the minimal content, the game DOES show promise. It might be worth it give Dovetail the chance to finish the game (I cannot accept this as a finished product) and work out all the bugs. Probably a year from now.

I don't see the point of this release other than a source of revenue for the developers, it brings nothing new to the table and I would suggest that people new to flight simming would be better off buying FSX and working through the flying lessons (Learning Centre > Lessons) and missions.
