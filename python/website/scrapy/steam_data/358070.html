About This Game

A sunless world of fungi and bugs, sustained by a slow rain of nutritious substance, could use your guidance and cataloging.
EarthTongue is a vivarium sim that places in you limited control over a small ecosystem on a fantastical world. You will spend most of your time watching life grow and eat each other to survive. Your subtle nudges will be required to maintain the balance of this world, but it can handle itself much of the time. Player given goals are limited, aside from maximizing the health of your ecosystem. You may choose to try to create a fungal garden to your liking. You may try your best to breed as many beetles as you can. You might use those beetles instead as food stock for the mantises. You might instead just peacefully watch the story of Earthtongue unfold on its own.

This review is an analysis of how the ecology functions and entertains you. It will point out quirks that you will learn as you play for hours on end. It covers the base game-world, but the resources that you manipulate in the game has patterns and limitations, and will appear in any game module.

Working with the game ecology and finding the in and out of sustainable systems, i want to say the ecosystem is flawed, and the 2d nature of the game is part of the reason. The game life finds local maxima to survive and will burn out over time. Life in this game works like wild fire burning dry trees, leaving behind ash for the next generation to regrow. Maybe this fire analogy is an accuracy way to expect how life will NOT continue to thrive without considerable maintenance by the user to 'fuel the fire' of this system.

To support the system there are two resources: water and nutrients. How these resources move in the ecosystem limits how plant-like life grows:

Rules of water is simple and less critical to life, water refreshes at the lower layers as ground water, and very rarely by rain, life and time drains the water, some life depends on water more than others, the top of mountains are dry and only some plants will naturally migrate and survive on hills. There is no way for the user to improve the desperation of water without continuous work of manual rainfalls.

How nutrients replenish in the system, is ideally, by nutrient falls from the air. This is like another rain, but it is governed by rules the user must explore and he can enhance. There is a max cap or nutrients in the system that will fall before it stops and it can be increased by the user, but the amount of time and work to saturate the entire land with even falls automatically is unclear and possibly unachievable when compared to the volume of the land that is slowly increasing.
It is the scarcity of nutrients which will kill your ecosystem overtime because of limits how nutrients migrate. The dead rot and emit nutrients back in the surroundings. The living can move and die somewhere else, then rot and make that new land fertile.

The rules how nutrients, as a particle and in this 2d space, is what limits life in local maxima of good condition. Nutrients will over time sink deeper in the ground, and be inaccessible to all but few plants with the right roots to reach, plants that by chance may never grow above these deep nutrients, and in this a world stacked with deep vertical tiles. Nutrients do not move off tiles on its own, and i am sure, never spread horizontally on its own, this limit of motion is what forces only local healthy sub-ecosystems; be it intended or a flaw i do not now, but do not expect drainage from ground water to help move nutrients along the world, and happenstance to hot spots of life that need it.

Instead to move and fuel nutrient short term and as a buffer, within supporting life the time of random and a slow nutrient fall hits; you need swarms of creatures feeding and rotting above plants. The creature ecosystem has balancing problems. Ideally there are diminishing returns feeding nutrients down the food chain, where the loss appears back into the falls, but there are errors of positive feedback; flies and corpses on top of sticky-like plants was one, but was patched; but know where there are dead-eaters stacking, there is a deadlock of nutrients being looped and disappearing in that mini system and not spreading around and hopefully reappearing in the falls somewhere else.

Likely, when the measure life is high in local spots, there are dead zones somewhere else, and like the wild-fire analogy, plants will burn out without fuel from the falls, life will die, and the unique life count will drop down to zero, without your help.

Don't expect life in this game to be self sustaining , expect everything to burn out because resources that fuel this strange life: water and nutrients, do not migrate naturally in the ground, diminish within food chains, and slowly and randomly drop from the sky. Everything burns down like this dying planet.

A pleasant little "vivarium game," in which your only real task is to watch the rise and fall of various fungus and insect species on a sunless planet. The game encourages you to try to build a diverse, sustainable ecosystem, rewarding the successful preservation of species with detailed journal entries about their behaviors. As you play more and more -- and catalogue more and more species -- you're also treated to some additional journal entries that reveal something like a backstory, showing who/what you are, and why you're watching the game unfold. A charming pixel aesthetic and a soundtrack that seems like it's straight out of the NES round out the package.

Gameplay is a relatively passive affair compared to other "build-and-watch" games, like SimCity. You can reposition bugs and fungus spores at will with a click of your mouse, but for meatier tasks (reshaping the planet tile by tile, introducing new species, summoning rain or lifeform-eradicating meteor showers, etc.), you are charged "intervention points," which build up over time. Consequently, a good chunk of the game consists of making something happen, sitting back and watching the effects, and then making another thing happen a little while later. You can accelerate the rate at which you accrue intervention points by having more biomass on your planet (i.e., more fungi and bugs crawling around at any given moment), and you are also given three speeds (and a pause button) for making time pass more quickly.

The forced passivity of Earthtongue is at once a good and bad thing. On the plus side, it encourages you to observe and strategize, knowing that time is another resource to manage, and your absence from the world another factor to account for as you play. It makes for a far different gameplay experience than you might be accustomed to seeing. On the negative end, it's sometimes irritating to have to sit around doing nothing while waiting to save up enough intervention points to perform several major maneuvers in succession.

Aside from what gripes the game's setup may entail, I've noticed a couple of issues with the game itself. First -- and worst -- is slowdown. When my planet is absolutely crawling with wildlife, the framerate suffers noticeably, and sometimes a portion of the planet won't even have loaded by the time I pan over to it. Second is the slight difficulty involved with clicking and moving things. Some spores are so tiny that you'll click one by accident as you try to pick up a bug, and you'll have no idea you're dragging the spore along as you frantically try to click anything else.

Even with these complaints, I can assure you that I've never once felt frustrated playing Earthtongue. It is as close to a zero-stress experience as I've ever had. It can be mildly irritating to have one of your bugs or fungi face extinction, but you can always re-introduce them later for a few intervention points. If you don't like how your planet is turning out, you can always wipe out its population with a meteor shower, or start over entirely with a single button press. There are no failures in Earthtongue -- only setbacks faced on your way to a goal that you yourself set.

I recommend this game enthusiastically and without hesitation. Play it because it's a neat game. Stick with it because it's an experience.

Earthtongue is a fungal ecosystem simulator.

I bet you've never heard of that before. Well... unless you were a mycologist.

Earthtongue is unique in that it's less of a game and more of a simulator with limited interactions. It still has some minor goals (unlocking journal entries) but everything is largely freeform and open-ended for how you decide on what you want to do with your little fungus world. A fully diverse ecosystem is a thing of beauty and playing Earthtongue will teach you to appreciate the balance such an system requires. It'll show you just how fragile the ecosystem around you -- just like on this simulated planet -- really can be.

While its a great little simulator, and I can easily recommend Earthtongue, I wouldn't recommend it to everyone. Its for people who liked games similar to Mountain or watching Breve 3D Evolution simulations. Earthtongue is for people who like watching aquariums full of fish, or ant farms. People who want more interaction with their simulation games -- such as those who play Dwarf Fortress, or SimCity -- probably won't enjoy this nearly as much.

I originally played this game on Itch.io and really loved it. The pixel art is lovely and the music is soothing :)

There aren't any tutorials or instructions, although there are journal entries with specific information on each fungus/insect that are unlocked after a species survives for a long enough time. This means that the player will need to observe the behaviours of the insects and fungi in order to determine how to properly care for them. Some are herbivores, some carnivores - some fungi even eat bugs.

Balancing the ecosystem is both challenging and rewarding. A species can easily die off if they over-consume their food source, for example. Some insects are bad at climbing terrain and may get stuck in an area with no food.

The player is given Intervention points which replenish over time, and allow them to add insects or fungi, change the weather, or alter the terrain. Choosing a random insect, fungi, or weather effect to add is significantly less expensive than choosing something specific, but of course the player receives less control over what will be added to the ecosystem when it is randomized.

Insects can be picked up and moved by the player, allowing them the option to manually balance the ecosystem. Fungi, however, cannot be moved.

Earthtongue can be addictive to play, but also fun to just leave running while I do other things. It's exciting to come back and see what has grown, which populations have survived, or which species have gone completely extinct ;)

Earthtongue is a game about ecology and discovery. And bugs, which is always good.

It has a slow, meditative feel to it, which is in no way meant to sound bad and in every way meant to encourage the purchase if, whether out of pure curiosity or the serious enjoyment of complexity-within-simplicity, you would like to experience the broad brush strokes of intervention in an active and alien bio-system.

You are an entity lingering over a strange, nutrient-rich landscape with the ability to drop insects and fungi. (Already riveting stuff!) The flora and fauna run on guidelines, within themselves, and with the intention of survival and procreation. Some combinations mesh well, others are at odds, and some develop symbiotic relationships.

The beetles, for example, are territorial and won't hesitate to attack other insects. However, they leave a lot of carcasses (theirs and their enemy's) along the ground, which is useful for flies to munch greedily upon.

The setup relies less on action and more on re-action. You push and pull the flow of creatures at a meandering pace, more intent on watching the What and the How than trying to force your agenda. Like a real terrarium of sorts, the experience is in dropping one or two new objects and waiting, or even just checking back in an hour. As you learn what makes every creature unique, the entity will write in-game logs attempting to detail them in ways that you might not have noticed. This datalog adds a touch of flavor, a pinch of tutorial, and a dash of tangible accomplishment to an otherwise fend-for-yourself style of gameplay.
