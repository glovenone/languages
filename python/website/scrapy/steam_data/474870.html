Ping Ping is the first game by developer, Pinky, and publisher Ping a Ling Studios. It is an interactive novel that has been made entirely with Twine. Twine is an open-source tool for telling interactive, nonlinear stories.

Ping is a name given to Minako when she chooses to venture into the blackness engulfing her world in search of her father who has vanished. She meets a Cutie robot named Tocki Three who leads her through his game data world avoiding The Omega in search of her father.

I appreciate the simplicity of the presentation of the game and that your choice determines the direction of the game and the new experiences you encounter. This game enables the readers imagination to be the portal into the world of Ping Ping. You create the surprises and fears within your own mind and make the world as spectacular or minimalist as you wish. I enjoyed the wrong turns that ended in surprising outcomes and the need to restart like a choose your own adventure novel.

I feel that although the game is simplified in it's presentation more could have been done to give the written world more interest. Maybe text that brought greater interest and impact. I also felt parts of the story appeared disjointed. Particularly the injection of the messages to "papa". These understandably are Ping's thoughts and a reminder of the aim of the journey to find her father. I felt that although the story was entertaining and had me continuing to move through to the next stage I felt it was missing a level that visual interaction could have enhanced this game.

I feel several thing's could be done to advance this game further. Firstly alter the text for further interest. Maybe occasionally surprise the reader with a pop up visual scene of the story, or an overall view of the data world Ping is travelling, with location indicators. (Admin's note: This is a limitation of the Twine game engine something a little beyond Ainslie's knowledge level - she knows how to play games but has never taken much of an interest of what happens underneath the hood).

Ping Ping is game of the simplicity of the written word in the creation of a gaming world. It asks you to embrace a game within your imagination and see the world it's presenting within the game of your mind. If you like a challenge to your patience and ability to visualize that which you read then this may be the game for you.

This is all a bit surreal for me to be writing this review, as I'm currently at university doing a minor in Creative Writing, and I've recently had to "critique" the work of a few young female students as part of our assessment and in-class exercises. I've also been messing about with a certain piece of interactive novel-writing software called Twine, which is frankly a great place to start if you know as little (i.e. absolutely nothing) about video game programming as I do.

So what do you get when you combine the style of the aforementioned female writing students with a newbie to Twine? Something called Ping Ping, apparently. That's certainly how this Pinky person's writing reads to me; and that's not necessarily a criticism either, just an observation. She actually writes quite well - as long as you like your writing to be more "evocative" than easy-to-understand - so that's really not the issue here. The real question is: just how much of a "game" are you getting for your four dollars? Hmmm. Well, first of all, let's take a closer look at this "Twine" thing...

One of the things which Twine offers is a vast variety of font types, colours and sizes. The developer has definitely chosen to be unambitious in this regard, choosing a simple greyish-black background with tiny white writing, with the linking words (which you click on to make choices, or at least move to the next page/passage) being a shade of blue only a couple of tones away from the program's default colour. Again, not necessarily a "bad" thing, but it is tempting to say the writer's choice may have been a little lazy in this regard. Buy hey - it works, it's fine, and really isn't a basis for heavy criticism of her work. It's certainly not as bad as seeing that exact same asset for the umpteenth time in yet, yet another Unity or GameGuru "product", and it's sufficiently aesthetically pleasing to allow the narrative itself to do most of the talking.

Okay, conspiracy theory time, boys and girls. There's a certain claim the dev is making on her Store page, one which I can't authoritatively speak the truth on. But as other reviewers/spiteful former friends with a grudge (seriously, just read the reviews in question) have pointed out, it's a pretty big claim that the story has 777 "choices" which the player can make. Having toyed with Twine a tiny bit myself, I can vouch that to give a player that many choices would be an ASTRONOMICAL effort. Like, many hundreds of hours of work, at the very least. What she more likely means is that there are 777 PASSAGES, i.e. pages of text, a lot of which are linked by a single "choice" of word or words to click on in order to progress to the next page. I may be wrong, but I'm fairly sure a "choice" generally involves having more than one option on display.

Furthermore, a quick glance at the statistics of my own largest Twine piece to date - which I can safely vouch has more frequent "choices" on offer than Ping Ping does - tells me that I currently have 517 passages, and just under 20,000 words. The dev here is claiming that she has written over 80,000 words with 777 "choices", but given the math I've just done here, I do indeed think she may mean 777 passages, otherwise we'd be getting a choice of two or more options pretty much every page, and this interactive novel sure-as-â¥â¥â¥â¥ doesn't offer THAT many decisions for the reader to make. (At least not judging by the two short playthroughs I've managed so far. And I just did a couple of rapid-fire spam-throughs to test this theory, and the longer of the two attempts came out at just under 60 bona fide "choices", while the other didn't even make 30. So in the words of Billy Joel, "You may be wrong, for all I know, but you may be right").

Now, unlike others, I'm not necessarily calling the dev/author up on being a "liar", but I do suspect there may be some crucial inaccuracies on her Store page. Also, in her defence, there's no denying the fact that just one simple choice can take the reader on extremely divergent tangents - indeed, entirely different story paths - so there's certainly plenty of replayability here for those who find it all compelling enough to bother. Just don't come in expecting to make a gazillion "choices" in the course of a single playthrough, as such assumptions are only liable to lead to disappointment.

On the whole, though, if you're in the mood for a rather, ahem, "poetically"-written, reasonably original, and somewhat surreal piece of dark/"cute" interactive fiction, with extremely minimal visual gimmickry and absolutely no sound to speak of, you could presumably do much worse than this (though the four dollar price tag really is taking the â¥â¥â¥â¥, all things considered; 99c is a more than adequate asking price, given what's on offer). By all means recommended to any open-minded gamers who like to read, and especially recommended to any budding writers/devs who are curious about this thing called Twine (though having said that, there's shedloads of completed Twine stories out there for free, on forums and suchlike; and a far superior six dollar game on Steam called "This Book is a Dungeon", which shows off what a more ambitious and computer-savvy Twine user could potentially pull off).

Verdict: 6.5/10.

I've always been a big fan of psychologically-focused stories, and Ping Ping is no exception.
The abstract nature of the writing and the question of exactly how many of the events are really happening leaves a lot to be investigated and reflected on after reading.
Although the core gameplay comes from the choices made while reading, I personally think that the real game comes from piecing together an understanding of the story and replaying through it with different choices to see if your theory still holds up. This aspect of it reminded me a little of Silent Hill 2 without the obvious revelations.

My only criticisms come from the UI, that could have done with some personalization to fit more with the mood of the story (background image, custom font, etc.), the lack of a 'back' button to re-read through previous pages, and one or two typos in the writing.
And while the game actually having 777 choices is debatable, I still believe that there is a surprising amount to read considering the low price.

I would absolutely recommend this game to anyone who loves a good unconventional story and an escape from more frantic gaming experiences.

This is all a bit surreal for me to be writing this review, as I'm currently at university doing a minor in Creative Writing, and I've recently had to "critique" the work of a few young female students as part of our assessment and in-class exercises. I've also been messing about with a certain piece of interactive novel-writing software called Twine, which is frankly a great place to start if you know as little (i.e. absolutely nothing) about video game programming as I do.

So what do you get when you combine the style of the aforementioned female writing students with a newbie to Twine? Something called Ping Ping, apparently. That's certainly how this Pinky person's writing reads to me; and that's not necessarily a criticism either, just an observation. She actually writes quite well - as long as you like your writing to be more "evocative" than easy-to-understand - so that's really not the issue here. The real question is: just how much of a "game" are you getting for your four dollars? Hmmm. Well, first of all, let's take a closer look at this "Twine" thing...

One of the things which Twine offers is a vast variety of font types, colours and sizes. The developer has definitely chosen to be unambitious in this regard, choosing a simple greyish-black background with tiny white writing, with the linking words (which you click on to make choices, or at least move to the next page/passage) being a shade of blue only a couple of tones away from the program's default colour. Again, not necessarily a "bad" thing, but it is tempting to say the writer's choice may have been a little lazy in this regard. Buy hey - it works, it's fine, and really isn't a basis for heavy criticism of her work. It's certainly not as bad as seeing that exact same asset for the umpteenth time in yet, yet another Unity or GameGuru "product", and it's sufficiently aesthetically pleasing to allow the narrative itself to do most of the talking.

Okay, conspiracy theory time, boys and girls. There's a certain claim the dev is making on her Store page, one which I can't authoritatively speak the truth on. But as other reviewers/spiteful former friends with a grudge (seriously, just read the reviews in question) have pointed out, it's a pretty big claim that the story has 777 "choices" which the player can make. Having toyed with Twine a tiny bit myself, I can vouch that to give a player that many choices would be an ASTRONOMICAL effort. Like, many hundreds of hours of work, at the very least. What she more likely means is that there are 777 PASSAGES, i.e. pages of text, a lot of which are linked by a single "choice" of word or words to click on in order to progress to the next page. I may be wrong, but I'm fairly sure a "choice" generally involves having more than one option on display.

Furthermore, a quick glance at the statistics of my own largest Twine piece to date - which I can safely vouch has more frequent "choices" on offer than Ping Ping does - tells me that I currently have 517 passages, and just under 20,000 words. The dev here is claiming that she has written over 80,000 words with 777 "choices", but given the math I've just done here, I do indeed think she may mean 777 passages, otherwise we'd be getting a choice of two or more options pretty much every page, and this interactive novel sure-as-â¥â¥â¥â¥ doesn't offer THAT many decisions for the reader to make. (At least not judging by the two short playthroughs I've managed so far. And I just did a couple of rapid-fire spam-throughs to test this theory, and the longer of the two attempts came out at just under 60 bona fide "choices", while the other didn't even make 30. So in the words of Billy Joel, "You may be wrong, for all I know, but you may be right").

Now, unlike others, I'm not necessarily calling the dev/author up on being a "liar", but I do suspect there may be some crucial inaccuracies on her Store page. Also, in her defence, there's no denying the fact that just one simple choice can take the reader on extremely divergent tangents - indeed, entirely different story paths - so there's certainly plenty of replayability here for those who find it all compelling enough to bother. Just don't come in expecting to make a gazillion "choices" in the course of a single playthrough, as such assumptions are only liable to lead to disappointment.

On the whole, though, if you're in the mood for a rather, ahem, "poetically"-written, reasonably original, and somewhat surreal piece of dark/"cute" interactive fiction, with extremely minimal visual gimmickry and absolutely no sound to speak of, you could presumably do much worse than this (though the four dollar price tag really is taking the â¥â¥â¥â¥, all things considered; 99c is a more than adequate asking price, given what's on offer). By all means recommended to any open-minded gamers who like to read, and especially recommended to any budding writers/devs who are curious about this thing called Twine (though having said that, there's shedloads of completed Twine stories out there for free, on forums and suchlike; and a far superior six dollar game on Steam called "This Book is a Dungeon", which shows off what a more ambitious and computer-savvy Twine user could potentially pull off).

Verdict: 6.5/10.

I've always been a big fan of psychologically-focused stories, and Ping Ping is no exception.
The abstract nature of the writing and the question of exactly how many of the events are really happening leaves a lot to be investigated and reflected on after reading.
Although the core gameplay comes from the choices made while reading, I personally think that the real game comes from piecing together an understanding of the story and replaying through it with different choices to see if your theory still holds up. This aspect of it reminded me a little of Silent Hill 2 without the obvious revelations.

My only criticisms come from the UI, that could have done with some personalization to fit more with the mood of the story (background image, custom font, etc.), the lack of a 'back' button to re-read through previous pages, and one or two typos in the writing.
And while the game actually having 777 choices is debatable, I still believe that there is a surprising amount to read considering the low price.

I would absolutely recommend this game to anyone who loves a good unconventional story and an escape from more frantic gaming experiences.
