About This Game

NekoChan Hero Collection is a compilation of 3 little games:
NekoChan Hero - ninja world 2, NekoChan Hero - ninja world 1, Neko Land.
You can customize the controls of each game by clicking on the yellow button on each game in the game selection screen.

  • NekoChan Hero - ninja world 2


    Neko Planet is in danger, the king has been kidnapped and only one hero can save them, his name is NekoChan Hero!

    NekoChan Hero - ninja world 2 is a tribute to all the greatest platformers of the 80s and 90s.

    Controls:
    • Normal jump: Press the A button (hold to jump higher)
    • Run: Simultaneously hold down the fire button and directional arrows (S-keyboard B-controller) or press the run button (D-keyboard X-controller
    • High jump: While running quickly release the fire button and press the jump button (hold to jump higher)
  • Power: (D-keyboard Y-controller)


Features:
  • Create new passages, hitting the bricks with your head or with your shield.
  • Collect coins to buy fantastic vehicles and powers.
  • Use vehicles to reach inaccessible places.
  • Boss battles.


  • Neko Land


    Neko Land is a minigame set in the map where usually in other games you have to select the level.
    To complete the levels it is sufficient that the character reaches the end of the map. To do this, you have to simply click anywhere on the map, but it is not as simple as it seems, along the way you will find some obstacles that you will have to avoid within a time limit (the time will be reset to the achievement of each new level on the map). The game will be made up of several maps (Composed of different scenarios and new obstacles) that we will have to complete having only three lives.

    Features:
    • Two Game Modes
    • Acade
    • Star collector (in this mode you will have to find the hidden stars choosing the direction to take in the various levels)

    Three Types Of Controls:
    • Mouse (left click)
    • Xbox controller
    • Keyboard (spacebar)

  • NekoChan Hero - ninja world


    NekoChan Hero - ninja world is the first platform game that I created.

    • System Requirements

      Minimum:
      • OS: Windows Vista/Windows 7/Windows 8
      • Processor: Dual-Core 2.0 GHz or better
      • Memory: 2 GB RAM
      • DirectX: Version 9.0
      • Storage: 70 MB available space

    Posted: 30 November, 2015
    NekoChan Hero Collection acts as an odd sort of compilation, due primarily to feeling like an assortment of odd ideas and experiments more so than several fully developed games. NekoChan Hero Ninja World 2 (and its just as poorly named predecessor) are the most straightforward entries, showcasing the familiar nostalgia driven obsession with reconstructing retro classics that seems to cause so many novice developers to create the sort of indistinguishable fluff that comprises most flash game sites.

    Given its immense disposability and abundance of frustration, it’s disappointing that Ninja World 2 seems to be where Cosimo Menga has devoted most of its time. From a completely arbitrary standpoint it would appear to be the most finished of the four games in the collection, but it lacks so much in the way of inspiration or purpose that the energy spent creating its many increasingly tedious levels is wasted on me. Ninja World 2 (and to an even greater extent the original) is so unfocused and indistinct that it begs the question as to why I should be playing it as opposed to the literally thousands of nigh identical platformers available across the internet. If basic functionality was the basis by which games were judged Ninja World 2 might have a chance, but then again I couldn’t so much as exit the game without using ALT-F4 so even that would be a stretch.

    Neko Land however is strangely alluring. Its design is that of a once button timing based obstacle course, which when paired with some appealing (though fairly bland) pixel art proved unexpectedly entertaining. Though it is nearly as simplistic as a game can become, I found myself becoming rather excited with each obstacle passed, allowing me to see a little more of the map and what I was up against next. Sadly this become rather obnoxious when I was forced to completely begin again after each failure, made worse by the finicky precision required to actually progress past many obstacles. Still, it’s the most original game in this collection, and the only one I would say I actually enjoyed up to a point.

    Lastly we have Neko Land 3D, which is odd in every conceivable way. Though it plays somewhat similarly to Neko Land proper, the shift to 3D has brought with it one of the most poorly placed cameras I have seen in a very long time. The act of playing NL3D is even more unconventional, requiring you to jump and slide through obstacles, only you can only change directions when you hit certain points on the map. The problem here is that you’re afforded such a horrendous view of the map and given such little time to traverse it that it becomes nothing but trial and error (with the same aggressively strict limitations as its 2D counterpart). Part of me is impressed that this exists within this collection given how dramatic a contrast it presents to the other games, but it is far and away the least understandable and polished of the lot which makes it little more than an especially bizarre novelty.

    I think that’s where the problem lies. That within the context of itself, the most interesting thing about NekoChan Hero Collection are the parts which don’t work quite right; the parts that try something weird and unique but are abandoned before they become anything more than unfinished tangents. But unless you have an obscure obsession with NekoChan’s development pipeline and overarching creative process there isn’t much point to any of this. I have a fondness for messy, atypical experimentation within familiar frameworks, but there is so little to latch on to here that it seems like any time devoted to it would be better spent elsewhere.

    For simplicity's sake, I'll pretty much only review Neko Chan Hero 2; since it's seems to be considered as the main game in this trio of indie games.

    While I'm at it, Alex Kidd called, and he wants everything people dislike about his games.

    For starters, it's the story of a cat-man/girl (let's call him/her/whatever "Ninja dude", also for simplicity) who need to rescue his teacher.

    Yeah. That's it. That's the whole plot. You need to beat 3 bosses, with no personalities or reasons for their actions. Ugh.

    The game is divided in 15 levels. In each of them, there's a special coin you can collect for an achievement. They are usually plainly in sight, there was only one level which I had a bit more difficulty to find it. (yay for secret passages!)

    There's like 5 of those levels with a special "vehicule" made only for the level. (Gimmicky vehicules like in Alex Kidd) they are no way resistant, a pain to control, cost 200 coins (you get 5/10 coins when you collect them in levels, it's not so pricey, but if you need to restart the level, it's a bit of a pain since you need to pay again)

    At least, every levels are short and sometimes, really easy.

    Now now, let's talk about lives. THEY ARE FRIGGING USELESS. You start the game with 3, and the only way to get them, it's to buy them for 500 in the different shops (not all of them) at the beggining of those "special" levels I talk before.
    The thing is; you need way too frequently money to afford lives. (When I said "The only way to get them", there's actually one free hidden in the middle of the last level.)

    You think you can get a game over to get more lives? Well... No. You die 2 times, and you're left with one life. Don't worry, if you die you get a game over, which put you back to the title screen, where you can select again your saved game with only one life. It's more fast to go back every time to the Game Over screen, then the title screen than to buy lives. They are kind of useless in the way the creator put them.

    The total lack of explanation is a big minus to this game. How was I supposed to know you could run (and jump higher) when you keep the attack button pressed? It was actually "explained" in the first level, but it is no way obvious or anything. I beat 14 out of 15 levels without even running; which make me think the game was a "pixel-perfect platformer" while it's actually a "lots of place for innovations and fixes platformer".

    The hitbox also needs to be a bit better; I don't know the number of times I tried to kill an ennemy, only to be killed a milli-second later because of a ♥♥♥♥♥♥ hitbox. Why are ennemies' hitbox smaller than their bodies, while the character's hitbox is the whole body?

    The music is quite great, just a bit repetitive. There's like only two or three tracks you'll hear the whole game, so in the end you'll be dead tired of them. The title screen, level's track, and the boss 'track. They aren't /that amazing/ but they are worth listening a bit if you like video game music from the last era.

    There's some typos here and there in the game's text, but there's like a total of 100 words in all the dialogs, which isn't quite a great ratio.

    Being a fan of pixel-style game, I really enjoyed looking at everything; except the main character's hair. Totally white, no gradiant, or anything. Almost as bland as the whole game.

    Cons:
    -Game is a bit bland
    -Repetitive
    -Lives system will make you rage
    -Bad explanations of the control
    -Absence of a plot
    -Bad hitbox
    -Every levels are frigging shorts

    Neutral:
    =This game make you feel like a total weeabo when people see it in your game list.
    =There's lots of place for improvements!
    =Pretty much feel like a base for a better game

    Pros:
    +The game is short, so you only get bored after 3/4 of the game
    +The music
    +the graphics are really well done!

    In any way, the creator only started making games, and there's lots of place for improvements. It's not really worth the price, but if it can encourage the author to do another, even better game, then it's totally worth it. There's talent in it, it only needs even more polishing.
    Maybe getting rid of the platformer genre, or even "NekoChan" title for his next game (I would really love if it was the second one)

    Using Japanese terms and style is usually a big turn-off for me, and a lot of people, so targeting a bigger audience wouldn't be so bad, since "NekoChan Hero" sounds like some really bad DeviantArt's anime's style drawing from a 8 years old.

    Typically, when a game is made in the spirit of a classic, it tends to not be as good. Speaking for myself here but I've played a lot of Metroidvanias on Steam. For the most part they've been good, even great games, but they're not on the level of Super Metroid and Symphony of the Night. The inspiration for NekoChan Hero is....Alex Kidd. Even if NekoChan was a better game than Alex Kidd, it'd still be pretty bad.

    Look, I don't hate Alex Kidd, at least not anymore. I pity him. He had one memorable game, got shoehorned in a couple others, was dumped by a freakin' hedgehog, and in 2001 he worked as a retail clerk before disappearing. For a fictional videogame character, he's had a miserable life. One of his last notable apperances was in Sega Superstars Tennis and...okay I take that back, I still ♥♥♥♥ing hate Alex Kidd. Apparently he spent his decades of irrelevancy becoming a Tennis God. So when I play Nekochan Hero Ninja World 2, I think of Alex, repeatedly kicking me in the ♥♥♥♥ at Tennis.

    So let's go back to Ninja World...aka the poor man's Miracle World. This game has all of Alex Kidd's trademark faults:

    -Wonky hitbox
    -Crappy vehicles
    -Poor controls
    -A very ineffective punch

    On the bright side, at least there aren't any bosses that involve a rock, paper, scissors battle to the death. Ninja World 2 is a very short game. Most anyone should be able to get through it in less than an hour. It's not even that difficult, aside from some dumb moments involving the hitbox. Still, your tolerance for it will erode fairly quickly. When this game is at its best, it's just really unexciting. As much as I dislike titles like Miracle World and Enchanted Castle, at least they had some ambition, they had ideas. If it wasn't for the slippery controls and cheap deaths they'd be pretty good.

    All I got when I played through Ninja World 2 was a trip down repressed memory lane.

    Still, I have to respect the developer for using Miracle World as his inspiration for this game. No joke, I totally respect that. That was probably his favorite game growing up, and this is how he's paying tribute.
