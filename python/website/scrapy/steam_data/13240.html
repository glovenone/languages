About This Game

Unreal Tournament is the original King of the Hill in the frag-or-be-fragged multiplayer gaming world. As the undisputed 1999 Game of the Year, Unreal Tournament grabbed the first person shooter genre by the soiled seat of its pants and knocked it around the room with its never-before-seen graphics, brutal edge-of-your-seat gameplay and a massive and varied feature list that gave gamers more than they ever expected.
  • Novice Mode - This Botmatch competition is where new flak fodder hit boot camp. The Novice setting makes the computer controlled Bots in the game move slower and inflict less damage with their attacks. For those new to first person shooters, this is where teeth are cut, skills are born and addiction begins...
  • Hardcore Mode - Strap on extra armor and have a hand towel next to your mouse pad to wipe away the palm sweat. This mode is only for the pros -- all of the bots move like lightning -- dodging, jumping and inflicting A LOT more damage with their standard attacks than do their Novice counterparts.
  • Enhanced Bot AI - Bots in Unreal Tournament play as if they're jacked up on a big old shot of steroids. Their basic deathmatching skills have been virtually perfected, their understanding of the game rules is professorial, their ability to navigate levels is GPS-like, and their threat to your existence is very real.
  • Radio Chatter - Each of the commands at your disposal in the game is accompanied by both a text message on screen AND an audio message. Pleas for back-up or for cover fire are accompanied by pre-recorded sequences communicating your needs. Making this especially cool is the fact that you can hear your opponents' messages too (even if you're playing against bots 'cause bots are people, too).
  • New Command Line Options - For the hardcore server masters, this is the dream way to access features and manage a game. Virtually any option you want to tweak can be accessed via the UT command line. Nice and easy!
  • Improved User Interface - Unreal Tournament introduced a better way to see what is happening during a game. By using a unique heads up display (HUD) for each game type (Deathmatch, Capture the Flag, Domination, and Assault), the game provides ALL the info needed to play effectively WITHOUT bombarding the player with excessive info.
  • UBrowser - Unreal Tournament's robust Ubrowser system provides the easiest way to find a game on the Internet and jump into battle. The Ubrowser eliminates the need to download third-party server querying applications. It's as simple as one-two-frag. By clicking on the "Play Online" button and then selecting a server from the list, the game is on.
  • Spectator Cams - Watching a game of Unreal Tournament has the potential to be almost as much fun as playing it. Thanks to the Spectator Cams, gamers can enter a game and hop around to designated Spectator Cams to view the action on the level. Even cooler, the camera can be "attached" to one of the players in the level to view the action from their perspective. Either way, it's a cool feature and a great way to learn for people new to the world of online gaming.
  • Jump Mode - Jump twice as high as normal. It's sorta' like having a never-ending pair of jumpboots on. The impact these boots have on gameplay is almost indescribable. It changes the dynamic completely.

Short: A must-have classics of videogaming. Still totally worth playing today.

Why to buy it: Historical masterpiece. Perfect for relaxation. It helps to find peace and emptiness after a hard day at work. More efficient than meditation, sauna, or sport.

Long version:

Unreal Tournament has something magical. It has been years since its original release, and still, it's amazingly good and definitely worth playing. For those who do not know this ultimate legend of FPS, let me introduce it in a few lines :

Most FPS have tried to find a neat story to keep the player into the game. Then someone came and said: "Hey, guys, why do we care with a story? Don't we play only to shoot and have fun, after all?" And so came Unreal Tournament. The idea is that you are a gladiator in a futuristic universe, with some interesting background with appreciable ties with the Unreal universe. So you are in an arena with guns spread everywhere, and if you are killed, you just instantly respawn and continue the fight. It means there is no break, no searching for the way forward, for stupid opening buttons. You just fight and fight and fight endlessly, until you win the match. The experience was, and still remains, an extremely intense one. Videogame here is at its greater height, even if it's shear violence and barbarousness.

Now, is it still worth today? The graphics have aged, for sure, but the level design was so good, that it doesn't matter a lot. They did not try to build magnificent open spaces areas in a luxurious nature: Most arenas are confined basements, and this is the kind of decor which cannot be much prettier today than it was seventeen years ago. Gameplay is still pretty much perfect. Weapons are sufficiently hidden so that you have to find out where they are while trying not being killed by those who already have them, and there is a good diversity of them. Everything was well-balanced then, and it has remained so up to now, as equilibrium never ages.

What feels like the twentieth century, however, is the AI if you play solo, especially in Assault mode, where your team keeps following you, backing you up in the most useless manner. That means that if you are almost at the objective, and you die, you return to the starting point, and your team, instead of finishing what you were up to, just left the enemy base to greet you in pack at your respawn place.

To end this review, I will tell you some personal story I have with this game. In my youth years I was often bullied by a big tough guy; but one day we spoke and, I don't know really how, we ended up by him lending me his Unreal Tournament game. I enjoyed it a lot, and now that we had a common enthralling to share, we became good lads and he never bullied me again. Peace among people can be achieved through cathartic videogaming, it would seem.

I have to thank the DOOM 2016 open beta for introducing me to Unreal Tournament. I used to play DOOM 2 deathmatch over dialup when I was younger, and I was part of the Quake Alpha/Beta deathmatch trials back in the day. I loved online deathmatch, but went away from it for many years as FPS maps grew and gameplay slowed down. Playing DOOM 2016 deathmatch didn’t feel “right”, and the STEAM comments kept referring to Unreal as a much better online shooter experience. So I grabbed UT GOTY and the rest of the UT series. How right these commenters were….Unreal Tournament GOTY is one of the best games I have ever played. The graphics are amazing, the gameplay is action packed – so fast and adrenaline pumping. It is a pure Arena FPS, and in my opinion Unreal perfected the formula with UT GOTY. I’ve played the later games, which are fun in their own way – but this is just perfection. I loved the Mortal Kombat style voice over as the match went on…”Killing Spree….MULTI KILL” – and the tirade of smartass one liners from the bots, full of personality each one of them. The science fiction theme allows so much variety in levels, which are all full of colour and life…no brown and drab here. Musically, the tracks are all pumping electronica, that capture the essence of each level and the thrill of combat. The story, thin as it is, is all it needs to be – a couple of lines introducing the character or level, and the fact you are in an interstellar deathmatch tourney run by an evil corporation (cool!). The levels are awe inspiring, and I’d have to say the Facing World CTF map is up there with icons such as the Colosseum and Pyramids in its grandeur and online community reverence. No wonder it appears in all the sequels! Even if the online community dies off for this game (I can still get a match online today (2016) through the game which was released in 1999!) – I could always play with the bots, which are powered by the amazing Reaper technology which kept me hooked on Quake when I was younger. Thankfully I discovered this game late in life, otherwise I probably would have been hooked and not able to study or work or find a relationship. A masterpiece.
