About This Game

Psycho Starship Rampage : Space Shooter + Rogue = Pimp your starship

You play the psychotic A.I. of a warship lost in space which tries to come back to Earth. Build your spaceship from scratch. Load it with weapons. And use your creation to destroy waves of innocent aliens throughout numerous levels. Loot their carcasses, extend your ship and jump to the next sector for more action!

Key Features:

  • Shoot wave after wave of vicious enemies in horizontal scrolling levels
  • Many equipments/weapons designed for interaction and combos
  • Build the ultimate killing machine of your darkest dreams
  • Crazy and unique local coop with up to 4 players on the same starship

Background

You play RSR-648b, a psychotic warship A.I., who wakes up somewhere in space after a very long hibernation. It needs to rebuild itself and will recycle anything it finds in order to return back to central command. In its madness, every unidentified moving object becomes a potential threat that must be crushed. Can you shoot and loot your way through alien territory and back to Earth ?

Hiya Gamers!

This is a review of Psycho Starship Rampage and I gave it a 3/5 because i'm very on the fence with this one. There are some aspects to this game that I really liked and others that I wasn't so fond of. You control the spaceship of a rogue A.I. that's trying to get back to Earth and destroy it. As you go across the galaxy a wave of enemies trails behind you like in FTL, if the wave catches up you lose. The gameplay is fun but I didn't like how rigid the enemies movement patterns were and started noticing a lot of similarities with the bosses at the end of each level. Some more movement patterns are added on as you go, but I didn't feel like there was a lot of difference. There is local co-op in the game, but be warned, you will not get extra ships. Instead, you and your friends can control different parts of the ship so be ready to ruin some friendships. I liked the graphics and the music was very outerspace-like and fit the game, but the most interesting part of the game is customizing your ship. So long as you have the scrap, you can make whatever you want. You have the ability to rotate your guns, bind different guns to different keys to conserve energy, and make guns shoot behind you.

Have Fun Gamers!

PSR is a customization-heavy rogue-like game built on the skeleton of a standard space shooter. Gameplay is divided into two parts. Stages are pretty standard shooter levels with waves of enemies, terrain hazards, and a final boss. But, after each one, you go into âhyperspaceâ where you can use parts picked up in the prior level to customize almost every aspect of your ship. Thus the game cycles between design and battle until your eventual demise.

What makes PSR special is the ship customization. This isnât merely a matter of selecting weapons or some suchâPSR gives you the ability to design a ship from the ground up. You build the body of your ship of basic geometric blocks and give it functionality by installing devices on them. You decide the number, location, and type of engines, power generators, batteries, repair units, and weapons. You have control over the precise angle of guns and, in a very clever feature, over key bindings of individual components, meaning that you can map weapons systems to keys in whatever combinations you like. Your customization is only limited by what you pick up in the levels: technologies are rolled like RPG weapons, meaning that even two lvl 1 blaster patterns wonât be identical. Upgrades must be paid for with scrap salvaged from your exploded foes.

This system is the gameâs strongest feature. Your design decisions are significant, noticeably altering how your ship plays. The interface is clear and easily learned. And there is opportunity to test your customizations out before embarking on another levelâa smart feature I wouldnât have known to ask for. Designing ships is what I most enjoyed about this game.

The actual shooter is well-executed, but unremarkable and a bit plain. Enemies are rather abstract looking and all come in the same color. The only other element of a stage is random space debris such as meteors. Backgrounds are nice looking but simple. One noteworthy feature of play is the variety of ways to fight. In addition to projectile weapons, you can install impact weapons (energy spikes) or tractor beams which allow you to bludgeon enemies with debris. (This is the only game Iâve played in this genre where terrain obstacles hurt opponents as much as you.) My only real complaint with play is that in some stages small debris, background elements, and scrap look enough alike that, in the thick of things, it can be hard to tell which is which.

There is a story mode which honestly Iâve yet to thoroughly explore. (It was not implemented yet for the beta period where most of my play took place.) Itâs mechanics are largely the same as those described above, though. Still, I can say that the gameâs quirky, somewhat morbid humor, present in both modes, actually succeeds in being good for a laugh. Also, parts of the gameâs sound track are quite catchy.

On the other hand I was a bit bothered by the progression mechanic. In typical rogue-like fashion technologies, including fairly essential ones, are to be unlocked with play. Now, right up-front I should say that I dislike grinding. Having to play many games at a handicap while you unlock everything is my least favorite feature of current rogue-likes. In the beta, this frustration was compounded by extreme difficulty. The most recent release seems to have mostly fixed the problem; there still is the need to run survival mode many times to unlock everything though. Also, the devs are listening, and this makes me hopeful that whatever tweaks are still needed will take place.

At this point, the only really major complaint I have is that the game involves insufficient documentation. I had to figure many things out by trial and error, and in a few very opaque cases I had to consult the devs for answers.

I really like the core of this game. Building your own ship and blowing things up with it is good fun. There is still room for some polishing and tweaking, but this is a solid implementation of an interesting concept. Iâve been looking for a game like Pscyho Starship Rampage.

With Psycho Starship Rampage (PSR for short), you always come back for more!
I remember when I was first showed the alpha version (long before Steam, that is), and it definitely has come a long way to become what it is now (in a good way).

I'll try to line up what I think is good and what I think is currently missing. Sorry for the long read, that's usually how I shape my reviews.


At the core

What makes PSR special among shooters is the great degree of freedom that is given to you for building your spaceship :
â¢ Shape it however you like
â¢ Fill it with anything you like, be it power generators, power storage, weapons, shields, armors, reactors, and so on. The balance between all of these is totally up to you. You can load a â¥â¥â¥â¥-ton of weapons or only a few, focus on shielding the front, the back and/or the sides, and so on.
â¢ Make yourself comfortable : Assign any keyboard key to any weapon (or group of weapons).
â¢ Weapons can rotate at any time (assign keys to it).
â¢ Equipment can be merged to keep the bonuses of each and turn it into a more powerful instrument (bad bonuses are merged too if I recall, so read the result before you confirm!).
â¢ Construction menu takes a little while to get used to, but once you're get the hang of it, shaping your ship goes pretty fast.

The possibilities are infinite, and that's the cool part. Get Scrap (the game currency) on each level, and upgrade yourself 'till you're ridiculously badass... if you survive long enough.


Another thing that makes PSR really special is that you can play with up to 4 of your friends on the same ship! By that I mean, there is only one ship but you can distribute the movement and the weapon handling to anyone. So you can have, say, one person controlling the ship, and 3 people handling the weapons. The energy (used by weapons) is obviously shared so this leads to ridiculously fun situations. I remember one evening, we plugged 3 controllers and a keyboard and tried a 4-players session. That was messy, but we had a really good time! Just don't forget to put a ton of energy storage for those trigger-happy friends of yours...
Note that you only need one copy of the game to play with up to 4 friends on local co-op. This is great when you invite people at your home and want to have some fun, without connecting on 4 Steam accounts and all the stuff. Having a TV to plug the PC output is ideal imo.


The levels, the graphics, the soundtrack

Neon ships everywhere. It's actually a pretty clever idea, considering the fact that you need to make it look good with any possible shape. The assets look really nice, be it backgrounds or objects.
If you're a Steam Card collector, I don't think you'll be dissapointed (esp. if you like space stuff).
The soundtrack is great stuff as well, and while it'll definitely depend on the individual, I personally haven't got bored of the soundtrack so far.

No level is the same, as enemy spawns are random, and generated 'on the fly'.
At the end of each level, the boss is also randomly generated, and randomly equipped (weapons, that is). The boss becomes more powerful the longer you drag the fight (rage bar on top), so you have to balance the blows you take and the damage you make. Since the boss always changes, you need to adapt your strategy. Not a really big deal on the lower levels, but at higher levels, the bosses can surprise you... rather quickly.


I see a "Rogue-like" tag up there, what about it?

When you start PSR, you only have basic equipments : Cannons, power generators and power storage. When you progress through the game, you permanently unlock more stuff for your next game by doing specific actions (kill X enemies with a certain weapon, reach a certain level, etc). Basically, the more you play, the more things you can do, and the more fun you have. Some unlocks are easy, some others are not.


Game modes, campaign?

PSR has a campaign, and it has a slight FTL feel to it (Faster Than Light). By that I mean, you need to reach a certain point by going from system to system, and bad guys will steadily catch up on you and block some routes. You have to take some risks to grab some unlocks to make your campaign progression easier, but you can't waste too much time either, or the bad guys may catch up. Again, each person will have it his/her way!
As for the survival... well, it's like its name suggests. If you want to build the mammoth ship of your dreams, that's the mode you want! I always die because I like to take risks on my ship setup, but that's part of the fun.


What about the cons?

IMO there aren't many, but if I had to name a few :
â¢ No way to change the color of your ship.
â¢ No way to change the projectile/lasers colors.
â¢ Could use more game modes. Survival is a ton of fun, but something like a boss rush could work too.
â¢ Even more weapons! The game has quite a number of them, but you can never have enough.

Well, the game just got released, so I suppose there's plenty of time for stuff to happen.


Closing words

All in all, I believe PSR wanted to focus on the "freedom of build" to distinguish themselves in the genre. Did they succeed? I personally think that yes, they did a good job at it. You'll never make the same ship twice, and spending time to craft your ship has never been this fun (it's usually the most boring part).
There are a lot of subtle game mechanics to discover as well, like how your ship handles damage (I'm staying vague on purpose), repairs itself, how you can boost your weapon damage, and so on. Discovering these to push yourself to the farthest levels is another fun thing to do!

Happy rampage, lads.

While Psycho Starship Rampage has some flaws (that could be fixed), it does manage to be a fairly addicting roguelite experience. You essentially manage a modular space ship, which you can upgrade and expand through collecting scrap and various upgrades in levels. Upgrades are everything from ship speed, a multitude of weapons, energy capture, and repairing (I probably forgot one or two, as well). The levels are a top-down bullet-hell shooter. You can become overpowered, if you build your ship right - and get a little lucky. Ship management occurs between levels, and you progress along a path similiar to FTL where you jump through space.

Pros:
+Has an addicing 'one more run' feel that has you trying out different ship abilities
+Controller support is nice, although it can be clunky until you get used to it

Cons:
-Tutorial could be more in-depth
-Boss battles are embarrassingly bad - they need to 1)show a boss life meter, 2)be more than a larger enemy that just randomly moves around creating bullet hell, and 3)have some sort of personality or story point associated with it

This isn't perfect, but it certainly is a fun roguelite that I will be coming back to.
