What the developers have to say:

Why Early Access?

“Johnny Graves – The Unchosen One is our very first PC game. As such, it will benefit enormously from the feedback and suggestions of the Steam Community. We look forward to working with and getting to know our players!”

Approximately how long will this game be in Early Access?

“We will be launching chapters throughout 2016 so that the final version of the game will be ready by the end of the year. Upon completing this game, we will begin working on Part II immediately. We expect to be much better game developers at that stage, thanks to the experience we're gaining during this project.”

How is the full version planned to differ from the Early Access version?

“The full game will have more polished graphics, more balanced gameplay and generally more content than the EA version – meaning more story, levels, monsters, magic and visual effects... more of everything, except bugs!

We’ll publish the game in chapters throughout 2016, and add new features along the way. Some of the features have already been scheduled; others will be based on the feedback we get from the community.

Coming soon in the next update:
  • Checkpoints
  • Blink
  • New playable level
  • Boss fight
  • 2 new monsters
”

What is the current state of the Early Access version?

“We’re only publishing one level for now, but it’s fully playable, and to our knowledge almost bug free. The first Early Access build features include:
  • 1/7 levels
  • 1/6 magic bullets & infinite normal bullets
  • 0/1 blink
  • 0/3 boss fights
  • 2/10 enemy monsters (spiders and zombies)
  • 2/8 storytelling sequences
  • Playtime estimate 20 minutes
  • 10% of the finished game
  • 3/3 languages (English, German and Russian)
”

Will the game be priced differently during and after Early Access?

“Yes! The Johnny Graves – The Unchosen One will be on sale until its completion.”

How are you planning on involving the Community in your development process?

“We hope to develop a great relationship with the Community; one in which there is a lot of fruitful, honest dialogue, and a lot of opportunities to learn (on our part, at least). Bouncing ideas off the Community and doing market research in terms of what features players enjoy and are interested in will be a big part of it. We want to know about any bugs or fix-worthy things our players find, and we look forward to hearing their feedback and suggestions for improvement. We will do our best to accommodate the Community's wishes, within reason, in order to deliver a final product that is at least as good as the one we have planned.

Communicate with us here on Steam via the comment section or Steam Chat, or on our social media.
https://twitter.com/stupidstupidgam: For dialogue and updates.
https://www.facebook.com/StupidStupidGames: For less serious dialogue and more goofing around.

First major update will be published late summer 2016

The first major update for Johnny Graves—The Unchosen One will be published late summer 2016. It will include one more level, two new monsters, one bossfight, and one magic ability. We'll also add the most requested gameplay feature: checkpoints.

We've received lots of feedback about not having checkpoints in the game. Johnny Graves was always supposed to be quite a hard game, and it's clear we've succeeded at that (unless you've found one of the ways to break the game - we're not telling you). However, it is frustrating to start again from the beginning every time you die. After the first update, you won't have to.



After surviving the graveyard, Johnny enters St. Mary's Cathedral. Even though it's a spacious cathedral, he won't have too much leeway; the monsters don't have much respect for personal space. Expect for an intense fight.



Johnny will also encounter one of our favourite monsters: the Puppet Master. This slender giant has been manufactured in the monster factories of Hell. He's not a fighter himself, but has a very useful ability: necromantia. Might explain the zombie horde Johnny encountered at the graveyard.



Luckily, one of Johnny's old magic skills comes back to him when he's starting to remember his past lives better. In Cathedral, you'll be able to use Blink, short range teleport ability. It's the perfect backstabber's ability - bet Johnny used it a lot when he still was an agent of Hell.

We hope and believe you'll enjoy the updates! If you've got any questions or comments on them or anything else related to Johnny, please let us know! The discussion board is at your service.

About This Game

You think your life is tough? Meet Johnny Graves - The Unchosen One.

It’s 1979 in New York. Johnny Graves is a former agent of Hell and Satan needs him back desperately to finish the job he started over two centuries ago. Now the devil sends his beasts with wrath to bring Johnny back to the fold. Johnny has one night to close the gates of Hell and prevent Satan himself from coming across the mortal divide.

Johnny Graves—The Unchosen One is an action RPG set in an urban fantasy environment. It is a story-driven single player game for Windows PC, developed using Unity 3D. The Early Access version offers up to 20 minutes of hack and slash gameplay (although a seasoned player might get through it faster)!

This game is the first part of the Night of the Creatures trilogy. The next two games will continue the action that takes place during the one night in 1979, but seen through the eyes of different protagonists.
Among our many products (TBA) that take place in the Agents of Armageddon universe, Johnny Graves—The Unchosen One is our first PC game. In the Agents of Armageddon storyworld, the secret histories of humankind are revealed. Our various products are windows into its 250 000 year long timeline.

Early Access Review
Posted: 18 June
Product received for free
Johnny Graves—The Unchosen One is a horror themed 3D top down action shooter currently in Early Access. The Early Access part is quite crucial, as what is on offer so far is pretty bare bones. Content wise there is one playable level that can be completed in some few tense minutes, and there is little reason to come back to it once you're done.

At first glance, this looks like a game in the style of classic ARPGs, but there are currently no progression mechanics that are commonly associated with RPGs here - that means there are no experience points to be gained nor leveling of any kind. Combat, however, is not mindless left clicking, but somewhat tactical, albeit simplistic in nature. The character has one conventional attack (an aimed shot from his revolver) and one special attack resembling something of a magic bullet that bounces from one enemy to the other, taking down multiple foes in succession. Aiming is done with the mouse and is completely manual, and your revolver must be reloaded every six shots. In contrast with a more traditional ARPG style of combat, you cannot shoot and move at the same time which can cause some tension. It is easy to get swamped by enemies if you are not careful, as the latter tend to spawn in groups and move in on you fast. In practice you will be running away from mobs of zombies a lot while reloading and waiting for your special attack meter to recharge so that you can take them down before they reach you. In a way, the combat is closer to Resident Evil than Diablo due to this rather basic shoot-and-run-dynamic, and because you control the character's movement directly with WASD keys. Failing a more diverse skill system, some other means of crowd control or maybe a melee option would have been welcome here. Add to this that there is no incentive to kill foes at all apart from ensuring your survival and progressing through the level. As mentioned above, enemies do not give XP when killed nor drop loot, so your best course of action is to just sneak your way around them, situation permitting. (Your guy can also jump if need be). Ammunition is unlimited, however, so the game feels far from survival horror despite the squishiness of your character.

Speaking of which, your character's health bar is tiered and will regenerate by itself up to the last tier it fell down to after receiving damage. Melee attacks by zombies will whittle you down quickly, but fireballs and the few chemical zombies you encounter can have an even more adverse effect on your well-being. The fire is courtesy of giant tree dwelling spiders that will attack you off screen and remain aggravatingly out of sight until you draw in close. Chances are you will die when you encounter these spiders for the first time, meaning you will have to restart the level from the beginning. For an admittedly short level this isn't the end of the world, but I found dying and having to go back to square one pretty annoying. Maybe a checkpoint system would have alleviated this.

The environment in general and the level design, while serviceable, weren't really noteworthy and it seemed I was missing some environmental effects even on max settings (e.g., swaying grass seen in the trailer - I ran the game on an AMD R9 290). The cemetery had a lot of open graves for example, with some of them emitting a yellow glow, but there was no reason to investigate further or explore, as there were no secrets to be found or anything else of interest there. There are some nice details and touches in the scenery, but as a whole I found the level a little bland. A bright point is the writing, however, which was witty and spirited, although the extent of it was a few lines of introductory exposition and some banter between the protagonist Johnny and a second character. I hope we will get to see much more of the writing and story as development progresses.

I received this game for free in a giveaway, but I feel that for the price that is being asked here, there could and should have been a lot more in regards to content, even in full consideration of its Early Access status. It is for this reason that I cannot in good conscience recommend this game in its current state. There are demos out there for other games that easily offer much more content and replay value than this. That said, the premise of the characters is intriguing and the writing (what little of it was on show, at least) is of a high standard, so I remain cautiously optimistic. If the developers put in the hours it is possible that this game will shine, but judging from what is on display so far it will take some considerable work to get there.

Early Access Review
Posted: 17 June
NOTE: This game was given to me by the developer for review purposes. This did not influence my review in any way.

==========================================================
---------------------------------------OVERVIEW-------------------------------------
==========================================================
Note: Johnny Graves - The Unchosen One is currently in Early Access. The version reviewed was the latest as of the date of this review.

According to the information on the Steam page for this game, Johnny Graves is an action RPG and the first game in a planned trilogy of games set in an original urban fantasy / horror setting called the Night of the Creatures.

In Johnny Graves you play Johnny, a former agent of hell with a psychic link to Ivy, a woman who offers advice and assistance in your quest. Your quest itself, it seems, is to rescue someone called Paddy, but this is unclear: in its current state, the game doesn't provide any introduction or background information at all, so it's hard to work out just what the plot of the final product will be. The Steam page offers considerably more information on the game world.

==========================================================
----------------------------------------QUALITY-------------------------------------
==========================================================
Graphics - The 3D graphics are a mixed bag. Most of the environmental graphics are very nice, if touch heavy on the browns and greys. Character and zombie graphics are rather blocky, though well animated. The dynamic lighting and shadows are excellent. Exploding zombies look terrible when they explode, and the fire from the fire-spitting spiders (WTF?) is not wonderfully convincing either, but it's passable. There's no separate torso turn animation, so Johnny's gun does not follow the crosshairs used to aim.

Given that the game is essentially an action game, it also seems to be missing some visual indicators of damage, as well. Without the sound effects it's impossible to know whether a zombie is hitting Johnny or not, and Johnny's gunshots only make a very unsatisfying temporary spray of red pixels that are presumably supposed to be blood. Killed zombies collapse like ragdolls (which is cool), without any visible effects of multiple gunshot wounds (which is not cool). The graphics give no real 'feel' of taking or dishing out damage.

The game so far features a couple of conversations between Johnny and Ivy, and these include quite good 2D character portrait overlays, which is a nice touch.

Standard Unity graphics options are provided in the launcher: resolution, full screen / window, and a graphics quality drop-down allowing a selection between Fastest and Beautiful. I played in 1920x1080 in Beautiful and the game mostly performed well, although some of the larger scrolling sections had noticeable tearing. A vsync option might be effective here.

Sound - Johnny Graves features a well-produced and suitably spooky music track in the background, but so far there's only the one. Sounds, too, are limited: a grunt when Johnny is hit and a very loud gunshot when he shoots were all that I heard, I think.

At present the lack of ambient environmental, movement, and enemy sounds makes the game feel rather empty.

Gameplay - Although the Steam page claims that Johnny Graves is an "action RPG", I currently see no evidence of the RPG part; instead it plays like a fairly standard isometric third-person shooter that I suspect may end up including some platforming elements (given the inclusion of a jump control).

You move Johnny around with WASD and independently move his pistol crosshairs and shoot with the mouse. With a controller I can see that this could be controlled like a typical twin-stick shooter, although given the speed of movement of the zombies included in the game so far, I suspect the added precision of the mouse might be required.

The game is currently VERY short and only includes one level. After finishing this level (which should take around 20 minutes), you're shown a brief message that this is only level one out of seven, and level two will be released "in the summer 2016". As mentioned previously, there's also no introduction or background story provided. You're dumped from the main menu into a graveyard, Johnny and Ivy exchange some banter that doesn't make a whole lot of sense without understanding why Johnny is there, or who Ivy is, and then you start walking through the graveyard and killing zombies. According to the Steam page, there is a fairly clear plan for the rest of the content that will be included gradually as the game goes through Early Access. In its current state, though, it's barely a taste.

The in-game text conversations between Johnny and Ivy are well written so far, with mostly good grammar. I hope the final game includes more of these (with a bit of editing), as they really add some interest to the game.

In its current state, Johnny Graves also has a number of issues that make it a bit clunky to play. The worst of these is that there's no way to rotate the view; camera control is automatic, and often not ideal. Even in this short demo level I found myself being beset by enemies from very close by off one edge of the screen, and with no way to rotate the view to move a tree out of the way of the camera, I couldn't see them until they were on top of Johnny. There's also no zoom, which is a bit sad, but nowhere near as important as the missing manual camera rotation. Finally, it's very easy to get stuck in open graves, requiring frustrating jumps to get out, or up against a lamppost or other environmental object, making positioning Johnny in the heat of battle much more frustrating than it should be.

==========================================================
-------------------------------------PROS & CONS------------------------------------
==========================================================
Pros:
+ Original modern horror / fantasy setting that could be quite interesting: demons, spiders, zombies, and guns
+ Existing level has nice atmospheric graphics and excellent lighting and shadows
+ Good music
+ Clear plan for Early Access progression

Cons:
- No introduction, so although the story seems like it could be interesting, it's hard to tell
- VERY short at the moment: I died once and it still only took me 27 minutes to get through the current single level
- Some clunkiness: it's easy to get stuck on level geometry, making the game more frustrating than it should be
- Character graphics are quite blocky and small, although well animated
- Bland character and enemy textures
- Very few sounds
- No way to manually zoom (minor) or rotate (major) the view; automated camera positioning is not always ideal

==========================================================
--------------------------------------CONCLUSION-----------------------------------
==========================================================
Johnny Graves - The Unchosen One has the potential to be a very fun game. It's not there yet -- it needs some work on the core gameplay and the developers need to finish their planned roadmap for the game -- but it's on the way.

As it stands at the moment, it's more of a demo than a game, and most definitely doesn't pass my personal value test at its current price point. But it is an Early Access title by developers with a clear plan, and I think that's worth a chance. I'd love a "maybe" button in Steam; I'm hesitant in recommending it now, but I'd expect that recommendation to get considerably stronger over the coming months as the game matures.

5/10

- No introduction, so although the story seems like it could be interesting, it's hard to tell
- VERY short at the moment: I died once and it still only took me 27 minutes to get through the current single level
- Some clunkiness: it's easy to get stuck on level geometry, making the game more frustrating than it should be
- Character graphics are quite blocky and small, although well animated
- Bland character and enemy textures
- Very few sounds
- No way to manually zoom (minor) or rotate (major) the view; automated camera positioning is not always ideal

==========================================================
Johnny Graves - The Unchosen One has the potential to be a very fun game. It's not there yet -- it needs some work on the core gameplay and the developers need to finish their planned roadmap for the game -- but it's on the way.

As it stands at the moment, it's more of a demo than a game, and most definitely doesn't pass my personal value test at its current price point. But it is an Early Access title by developers with a clear plan, and I think that's worth a chance. I'd love a "maybe" button in Steam; I'm hesitant in recommending it now, but I'd expect that recommendation to get considerably stronger over the coming months as the game matures.

5/10

Johnny Graves—The Unchosen One is a horror themed 3D top down action shooter currently in Early Access. The Early Access part is quite crucial, as what is on offer so far is pretty bare bones. Content wise there is one playable level that can be completed in some few tense minutes, and there is little reason to come back to it once you're done.

At first glance, this looks like a game in the style of classic ARPGs, but there are currently no progression mechanics that are commonly associated with RPGs here - that means there are no experience points to be gained nor leveling of any kind. Combat, however, is not mindless left clicking, but somewhat tactical, albeit simplistic in nature. The character has one conventional attack (an aimed shot from his revolver) and one special attack resembling something of a magic bullet that bounces from one enemy to the other, taking down multiple foes in succession. Aiming is done with the mouse and is completely manual, and your revolver must be reloaded every six shots. In contrast with a more traditional ARPG style of combat, you cannot shoot and move at the same time which can cause some tension. It is easy to get swamped by enemies if you are not careful, as the latter tend to spawn in groups and move in on you fast. In practice you will be running away from mobs of zombies a lot while reloading and waiting for your special attack meter to recharge so that you can take them down before they reach you. In a way, the combat is closer to Resident Evil than Diablo due to this rather basic shoot-and-run-dynamic, and because you control the character's movement directly with WASD keys. Failing a more diverse skill system, some other means of crowd control or maybe a melee option would have been welcome here. Add to this that there is no incentive to kill foes at all apart from ensuring your survival and progressing through the level. As mentioned above, enemies do not give XP when killed nor drop loot, so your best course of action is to just sneak your way around them, situation permitting. (Your guy can also jump if need be). Ammunition is unlimited, however, so the game feels far from survival horror despite the squishiness of your character.

Speaking of which, your character's health bar is tiered and will regenerate by itself up to the last tier it fell down to after receiving damage. Melee attacks by zombies will whittle you down quickly, but fireballs and the few chemical zombies you encounter can have an even more adverse effect on your well-being. The fire is courtesy of giant tree dwelling spiders that will attack you off screen and remain aggravatingly out of sight until you draw in close. Chances are you will die when you encounter these spiders for the first time, meaning you will have to restart the level from the beginning. For an admittedly short level this isn't the end of the world, but I found dying and having to go back to square one pretty annoying. Maybe a checkpoint system would have alleviated this.

The environment in general and the level design, while serviceable, weren't really noteworthy and it seemed I was missing some environmental effects even on max settings (e.g., swaying grass seen in the trailer - I ran the game on an AMD R9 290). The cemetery had a lot of open graves for example, with some of them emitting a yellow glow, but there was no reason to investigate further or explore, as there were no secrets to be found or anything else of interest there. There are some nice details and touches in the scenery, but as a whole I found the level a little bland. A bright point is the writing, however, which was witty and spirited, although the extent of it was a few lines of introductory exposition and some banter between the protagonist Johnny and a second character. I hope we will get to see much more of the writing and story as development progresses.

I received this game for free in a giveaway, but I feel that for the price that is being asked here, there could and should have been a lot more in regards to content, even in full consideration of its Early Access status. It is for this reason that I cannot in good conscience recommend this game in its current state. There are demos out there for other games that easily offer much more content and replay value than this. That said, the premise of the characters is intriguing and the writing (what little of it was on show, at least) is of a high standard, so I remain cautiously optimistic. If the developers put in the hours it is possible that this game will shine, but judging from what is on display so far it will take some considerable work to get there.
