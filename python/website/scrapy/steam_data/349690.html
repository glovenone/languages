About This Game

Story:
The year is 1989. 12 years have passed since the Gogoh Army’s invasion of Earth. Exposure to alien technology during the war enabled Earth’s own technologies to take massive leaps forward. International conflict that was previously rife in the history of mankind fell to an all-time low, and the Earth Federation was established as a unified Earth government. The stage was set for an era of unprecedented prosperity.

However, the period of peace proved to be short-lived and was abruptly broken. The underground militant organization ‘Neo Loran Order’ appeared, hell-bent on establishing a new world order. Utilizing weaponry stolen from the Earth Federation, the Neo Loran Order launched a global assault. Now, the fate of the newly-formed Earth Federation hangs in the balance as it begins to fight back against this global menace.

Features:
• 7 stages of side scrolling mecha-shooting action!
• Customizable weapon load outs!
• 4 difficulty levels: Easy to INSANE!
• New for Steam: achievements, online leaderboards!

ARMED SEVEN is a mecha shootemup by Astroport. Wait, really? It's another one of these games... Pshaw. Why can't these guys develop a visual novel about a catgirl witch school or something? Instead, this is yet another tale about giant robots and explosions. The world is doomed, only one pilot can save the day, blah blah blah. When is Astroport going to make a game that speaks to me? I can't relate to mechas, but I understand catgirl witches.

Seriously though, this is something that I appreciate about Astroport, they make games that play to their strengths. ARMED SEVEN is a 16 minute game. You start it up, choose your weapons, and attempt to save the world. Aside from being easily-digestible, this shooter is very entertaining.

What I appreciate the most about this game is that it's so accessible. Upon starting the game, you have a choice of weapons. The capabilities of the main gun, secondary, and charge weapon are clearly explained. While you're liable to have better results with certain weapon combinations, the best measure of your survival is being able to dodge bullets. Each of the four difficulties changes things up nicely, with enemies implementing new and more difficult attacks.

Since this is a 16 minute game, you probably won't fret as much when you lose. There aren't any continues, but the lives and shields are fairly common. However, mastering this game is a very difficult endeavor. Your play has to be close to flawless in order to get the high-score achievements. For example, when your Mech is at maximum shields, you get a huge influx of points over the course of each stage. However, if you take a hit, you're actually knocked down two levels, which means you'll need two shield power-ups to max out again. Most of the stages in this game only have three power-ups.

The scoring system also revolves around destroying enemies as quickly as possible. Whenever a large battle-cruiser or exceptionally tough mech appears, you'll want to focus on them to get a larger multiplier bonus. Depending on your weapon set-up, this can lead to a multitude of situations. You might even have to put yourself in a disadvantageous position by leaving weaker enemies alive. However, you can't allow yourself to take damage because of the max-shield bonus. These conundrums, alongside the unique weapon-system, give ARMED SEVEN a lot of replay-value.

And...well...that's it. Once again, this is a 16 minute game, there isn't much to talk about. ARMED SEVEN is a very good shootemup, and is sure to entertain fans of the genre. Give it a go sometime.

There's a few things I really like about Armed Seven, not least the concept. Armed Seven is a side on, old-school, doujin shoot-em-up, where you play as a mech defending Earth from the alien hordes laying waste, before leading Earth's armies on the counter attack. It's all set to some bombastic Metal Slug esque music, it's a very assured game.

While Armed Seven isn't a great looking game what's there is well done. The backgrounds remind me of old Mega Drive games like Streets of Rage, all blurred skyscrapers and distant fires. There's touches of flourish happening in the distance, companions being shot down, an upcoming boss wiping out a few city blocks. Said bosses are creative and themed, the way bosses used to be, they split and change, again harking back to the 16bit era without feeling like yet another indie retro game.

Gameplay wise Armed Seven isn't quite a bullet hell shooter, nor is it particularly gimmick laden, it's something in between. Your hit box is tiny, particularly in comparison to the size of your mech, and there's certainly something approaching a swarm of bullets on screen at any one time, but it's not that frenetic. The gimmicks come on the form of selectable weapons, before the game starts you get to kit out your ship as you see fit (to a point). I never found much to suit me beyond the defaults, but I do like the homing missiles as a special. The reason to switch and try new things is because you can alter your aim, not unlike Zero Gunner. You can tilt, lock yourself in to shooting upwards or downwards, and if you don't want to deal with that pick a build that does it for you.

While we might not be talking Dodonpachi levels of bullets there's certainly enough that you'll be pleased your hit box is so small. That said I still took hits from bullets I couldn't see, lost in the background, my outgoing fire and exploding war ships. The health system is interesting. There's pickups to increase your weapon damage and shield, lose all your shield and you'll lose a life, which drops your weapon damage down. It means that your first life can last quite a long time, but staying alive after that requires all the concentration you can muster

On my first at easy mode I completed Armed Seven on a single credit. It’s not insultingly easy, but it’s certainly eminently doable, especially if you play shooters on a regular basis. The step up to normal is certainly more of a challenge, all of a sudden you’ll be put on the back foot, using everything you’ve got to wrestle back control. It’s where my lack of proficiency at the genre makes me doubt things. I can’t go in to any great detail about the scoring because I simply don’t understand the minutia of it, I can’t comprehend it. It makes me wonder about the game’s difficulty too, is the step up in difficulty evidence of a flaw, something not quite right in the high end of the game, something that is nigh on impossible to get right. Or maybe that I hit a wall with the game on the harder settings less about any tightness of control and refinement and more my lack of ability.

Whatever the answer I do know that I like Armed Seven, Without being overbearing it reminds me of my 16bit childhood in the Sega camp. It’s a fun game, with good ideas, some interesting enemy patterns, and memorable bosses. It lacks something though, a (vague) tightness, something to really mark it out. It’s fun though, and cheap, and considering it’s heritage as a doujin game it’s very well done

A solid indie shoot-'em-up by Astro Port, makers of fine mecha games such as Supercharged Robot Vulkaiser, Gigantic Army, Steel Strider, and this. They also made Satazius, which... *cough*

Anyway, Armed Seven takes a lot of visual cues from Assault Suits Valken, which was released here as Cybernator for the SNES. However, it's a horizontally scrolling shoot-'em-up instead of a run-and-gun shooter. Unusually enough for a Japanese shooter, you have a "life bar", but it replenishes very slowly and gives a fairly long invincibility period after you're hit, making it essentially a retheme of the one-hit-and-you're-dead mechanics found in much of the genre. As with most shooters, it's fairly short at 7 stages long, but it's fairly difficult and very replayable.

Probably the biggest point of difference this game has from the rest of the genre is the ability to choose your weapons from a choice of four main guns, four subweapons that fire along with your main gun, and one special weapon that requires charging. "Better" main weapons cause the special weapon to take longer to charge, so you have to choose whether you want a more effective normal shot or to be able to use your special weapon more often. The major problem I have with this is that many of the weapons seem fairly useless to me, and I generally just end up using the submachinegun as a main weapon, either the rockets or the cluster shot as a subweapon, and either the beam cannon or the guided missile launcher as a special weapon. This isn't quite so bad for normal gameplay, but several of the achievements require you to beat each difficulty mode repeatedly using each of the 12 weapons at least once, so be warned if you care about that.

All in all, it'd be hard for an Astro Port game that isn't Satazius to not earn my recommendation, so Armed Seven gets a thumbs up from me.

(Note: I originally owned this game on Direct2Drive when it was released by Curious Factory, so my total playtime is somewhat longer than my Steam playtime.)
