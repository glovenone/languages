Posted: 21 March
Portal of Evil opens with Vanessa Helsing discovering that her grandfather Abraham has gone missing, and quickly thereafter learning that her family is part of an ancient order tasked with protecting the world from a demon invasion through the titular doorway. This quest takes her first through the bowels of the fake Vatican, then to a series of ancient times, where the door's seals have been spirited to by a mysterious villain. It's a great premise, and the game makes good use of it, switching from time to time and location to location, ensuring that the player will never have a chance to get overly familiar with a given location.

Now, how about some Hidden Object Criteria?!

Criteria 1: To what degree do the puzzle screens look like a thrift store vomited on my monitor?

Oh god, it's bad. Really bad. The first screen I came across was set in the garden of the almost-Vatican, and it was just a mess. Random, out-of-nowhere objects tossed on every surface, hanging off of walls, seemingly hovering in mid-air. Throughout the church location, there's screen after screen of items that have no reason to be together in places that shouldn't be messy, dropped on the screen to create a completely arbitrary obstacle. The game does much better with its historical locations, keeping anachronisms to a minimum and developing much more logical item placement. Still, it's about as mixed a bad as one can get.

Criteria 2: Are the searches justified by the premise/story?

It's all standard 12:1 gameplay, I'm sorry to report. The screen might be well-drawn, but the developers haven't done a great job of giving me a reason to go scrounging through them. Even worse, its features an object placement screen for every HOS. While I can understand the appeal of these screens in theory, they just don't make sense within the world of the game. Where did I get this armful of items I'm placing with others on the screen? Why on Earth am I doing it? Maybe someday a game will combine these two types of puzzle screens, and have me pick up 12 random items on one screen and then use them to complete 12 broken items on the next, but until they do, I'll keep judging these screens harshly. It's called the HOG genre for a reason, people, and it's not because I'm supposed to be putting angel statues together.

I don't know why, but the angel statues turn up a lot.

Criteria 3: How well do the various puzzles and object searches meld together to form a coherent whole?

It's actually a fairly well-structured narrative. While I may have been hard on some of the HOS design, it's all in service of a good story that moves relatively quickly. Likewise, the puzzles all fit fairly well into the story - Vanessa moves exclusively through ancient hideouts and secretive enclaves, so the elaborate traps and locks all don't come anywhere near stretching believability. Also, the game features two of the easiest puzzles I've ever encountered in the history of Adventures. They're so easy they barely count as puzzles. It's actually quite remarkable.

Portal of Evil offers a strong story with acceptably solid gameplay to support it. The HOSs could have been tighter, and a couple of the puzzles are a little more finicky than I'm used to, but the hint/skip button recharges quickly enough that it's never really a problem. The problem arises in a puzzle that turns up in the bonus chapter - which by the way, isn't really a bonus chapter at all, it's really just the end of the story. I can't stress enough that this game's story, without the bonus chapter, is not complete, and releasing a version of the game without it would just be insane. Back to the problem - there's a keypad-based puzzle that's flat-out broken. Vanessa encounters two halves of the code, but when she enters them into the pad, they don't do a thing. I entered them half a dozen times, but couldn't get a reaction - sure that I'd done something wrong, I finally skipped the puzzle, only to discover that I'd had the right numbers in the right order - the game just refused to acknowledge them for some reason.

It absolutely a decent game and I have no problem recommending it, but don't be afraid to use the skip button here and there.

For the most part, "Portal of Evil: Stolen Runes Collector's Edition" is a decent casual adventure Hidden Object Game (HOG). The graphics and actual HOGs are fine, and the voice-acting is only moderately bad. I found the game to be weak in two areas. First, the story is senseless. Sure, our heroine has to find the stolen runes or bad things will happen. So, that's reasonable. The problem is that there's no reason for the bad guy to have stolen them in the first place (I can't see that he'll get anything out of the situation) and once stolen, since only a van Helsing can travel the portals, there shouldn't have been any way to put them where they were. The second issue is that the puzzles swing wildly between ridiculously, trivially easy, to bash-my-head-against-the-desk hard. I'd have much preferred more HOGs and fewer puzzles. But, that's a personal issue. Anyway, I got 7.9 hours of decent gameplay out of it (6.3 hours in the main game, 1.3 hours in the bonus chapter, and an additional 0.3 hours getting my final Achievement). So, I'd say the game is worth it even at its current list price of about $6.

I haven't been playing a good HOG, without the signature of Artifex Mundi for a while. Following the advice of a friend, I decided to give Portal of Evil a go. Portal of Evil: Stolen Runes is a HOG from 8floor Games - which I've never heard of, before - and one with the most beautiful artwork that HOG genre is capable of presenting us. Yes, I am pleased.

It all started with an ominous nightmare and a knock on her door... Vanessa Helsing was pretty much oblivious to the world of mythical beasts, powerful artifacts and mysterious doors leading to other realms, just a couple of weeks back. Her grandfather, Abraham Helsing disappears mysteriously and Vanessa receives an invitation from an archbishop Benedict, to the St. Peter Cathedral in Southern Italy. Growing up with the major mystery of her grandfather's job, Vanessa accepts the invitation and hits the road to discover her family's legacy. Unbeknownst to her, she is about to be the sole hope for a perilous matter that centers a certain door which connects the Hell to our mortal realm.

The first thing that I'd like to declare is that the artwork is marvelous! I usually try to restrain myself from using such adjectives for this genre, but when I found myself taking a cartload of screenshots and just taking a pause to watch the light moving over the scenery more than once, I make an exception. Developers really gave their best in environment design, with a strong renaissance theme. Cinematics weren't exceptionally good, but they carry a quality that we stumble in adventure game genre, not HOG, so even that is a bonus.

Gameplay didn't carry any revolutionary elements. It is your millionth HOG, with generic but beautifully illustrated puzzles. HOG sequences were okay, and wouldn't give you much hard time. Your map is interactive with a teleportation feature, and an available action indicator, so the obscene amount of backtracking wouldn't cut you off from the story. Story itself is pretty much generic also, with some pretty details scattered around. I'm not going to spoil it for you, but the ending - a common downside for the HOG genre at this point - would leave you with a "seriously?" face and wonder if the game really did end there. Oh, and voice acting is horrendously bad.

Nothing award winning, Portal of Evil would give you a lazy evening with some really beautiful scenes to watch. Considering I got the game in sale, as part of a bundle, I got my money's worth and added some backgrounds to my wallpaper collection. Win-win.

Story: Vanessa Helsing, grand-daughter of Abraham van Helsing, receives an invitation to come visit Father Bendict in Viva, Italy. Since her grandfather is missing Vanessa goes there for information. She is also having terrible nightmares and day-mares and hopes Father Benedict can answer those questions too. Father Benedict explains her family and ultimately she is a part of the secret organization, the Order of Light. It is the organization's task and hers to prevent demonic evil from entering this world through a gate door. Vanessa embarks on a quest to return the four runic seals stolen form the door and to find her missing grandfather who was/is chasing the traitor thief.

Gameplay: This plays very well with amazing cutscenes. There are ultimately 5 areas with subareas which break down into 8 chapters, plus the epilogue bonus chapter. The first area is the cathedral from which 4 other areas open via a portal, and another area for the bonus chapater. Warning: while searching for the grandfather's letters (scrolls) make sure to find all of them in a given area, since once the runic seal is found Vanessa is instantly teleported to the gate door and there is no longer access to that area.

The game is quite a bit point and click, with some different puzzles types from the standard fare (several quite difficult slides in that "keeping track of where the pieces are supposed to go type" of way). The HO scenes are of two types: 1) the normal list with a use object (highlighted in red on the find list) and a sub/hidden HO scene with more objects (highlighted in blue on the find list). 2) A reverse list called "It goes where?" In this HO the gamer puts the clue items (pictures) on their "list" into the HO scene. For example: A glove on the list would be placed next to its mate to make a pair. A very different type of HO perspective and the reverse of a standard find. Note: the reverse HO scene (placing objects into a HO scene) does count toward the Eagle Eye Achievement (find all clues in 1 minute or less). {Much discussion about this achievement in the threads, and I am unsure whether or not all achievements are possible in a single playthrough. I personally cannot do this the first time nor do I try to ever get this particular achievement the first time.} I think every scene gets played twice first as a reverse scene by adding objects then secondly as a straight clue list find.

I think the cutscenes are excellent. There are quite a few, especially when meeting and talking with the characters. The game is slightly interactive in that regard since the characters give exposition as well as clues. If a character is there click on them, and in one case where the character does not leave click on them at least twice to get all the information and clues needed for that particular area.

This is fairly difficult and relatively long Hidden Object game. The only real difference when playing on "expert" mode is the lack of sparkles on the HO scenes and the skip feature on the puzzles. There is an in-game strategy guide so there should be no problems if the gamer gets stuck. The no-penalty fast travel map comes in handy when looking for missed non-sparkling HO scenes. There is back and forth travel between the portal world and the real world while figuring out how to get that area's runic seal.

The only extra find are grandfather's letters in the form of a scroll, 15 in total. The scrolls are easy to locate and are not "hidden" in the least. As I warned before find all the scrolls in a particular portal area before leaving by taking the runic seal since portal areas lock at completion and cannot be reentered.

All in all an excellent game to play.
