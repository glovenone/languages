A Rollercoaster Ride of Storytelling

Hidden Object games live from the stories they tell, some of them take a more "adventure" - Light stance in their task to tell a story. Weird Park Triology does a lot right, while only minor problems rise their ugly head during gameplay.

Graphics: + The three games have good graphics, only the third supports widescreen fully.

Sound: ++ Original and fitting soundtrack to the game, some of the tunes catch the mixture of mystery, crime and actual horror or circus setting.

Playability: + Not a lot of surprises here, some of the riddles are challenging enough not to let a gamer run through this game like a madman, and you should take your time to try out the riddles. If one particular riddle is too tough it can be skipped, or you can quit playing and continue later. The graphics of the hidden objects are quite well, especially the third part with the assembly HOS section is a real nice and relaxing part.

Now for the Genre-typical Scores:

Story: ++ I hope you don't suffer coulrophobia, since this story is about... Clowns, one Clown and a mischievously evil little entity that is. The story suffers some insignificant lapses during the three parts, those might be reasoned away with developing the villain and his victims (when you play the last part you'll finally notice why).

Technical Realisation: ++ If all three parts would have had the technical standard of the last part here would be three plusses, my personal max score. Sadly the first two episodes are neither CE's nor are they fully compatible to widescreen (yeah, turning them into WS actually is changing them into uglyness). The game is on par with some of the other good HOG's.

Extras: + There isn't too much to explain, only the last game has a lot of extras and a bonus chapter, which actually deepens the story behind the Series. Sadly, the first two aren't so well off with extras. Nonetheless, the Extras in the third part do some compensation.

Overall Rating: 9 + Keep in mind, HOG's are casual games, they don't have much of a replayability, aside some sentimental value. But this triology includes three quite nice and well told stories, one which you actually wish to get deeper and deeper into. It has no fear factor - if you don't suffer coulrophobia - and is quite entertaining.

Last but not least: Value for Money: Moderate 19,90 â¬ doesn't seem much for three games, but they don't have a really long playtime. And replayability tends to zero. Nonetheless, all three games are actually good HOG's with a lot of story to cover. So the price is acceptable. If you have a chance to buy it on some sale for cheap, do it. You won't rue the purchase.

Recommended

I recommend the game on reasons of my given explanations, rarely was a recommendation so easy to speak out.

Weird Park Trilogy are three games revolving around Louis Gauche, a clown that died during a representation. But there is also another recurring character, the real antagonist, which is Loki/Mr. Dudley.

Weird Park: Broken Tune was the first one to be released but I've played it in the Hidden Object Bundle 5 in 1. I enjoyed the dark settings and the soundtrack. But I had an hard time to find my way sometimes, relaying on the hint buttons. Besides, the ending was promising a real good sequel. In this opus, you're searching for a journalist that vanished and that was investigating an abandoned park that closed following accidents and the death of their clown.

But this is where Weird Park: Scary Tales happens. And it's not even a sequel to the story. You're on the road and you're suddenly distracted, sending your car somewhere you didn't know it exists: an old house and a little girl needing help... in another world. You have to save her and her friends, by re-acting some tales.

And to conclude Louis Gauche's story (which isn't really coherent at this point) with The Final Show, you're investigating the disappearance of another child and is sent to another world, where Louis Gauche, the clown, is finally understanding that he was manipulated and that he wants to save the boy that showed him that he could be a good man.

I hate when a trilogy is not making sense. I mean, Loki/Mr. Dudley is either a servant of Louis Gauche or his boss, we don't really have Louis Gauche's background coherent and I don't understand how he could have survived as you defeated him in the first opus. Also, the hero isn't the same each time, so, while you, the player, know more or less who to trust and the story, the character is completely unaware of everything.

If the graphics are very good and very representative of the ambiance of the story, the soundtrack is also good. As for the gameplay, it doesn't change: hidden object games and puzzles.

I would buy the bundle during a sale, mainly because I think that the price is not justified when you already own one of the game as part of the Hidden Object Bundle 5 in 1 - they did the same with another good game, Mountain Crime. Notice that I will buy the upcoming 4 in 1 though I already have the House of 1000 Doors. I don't want to miss the other games in it. But, yeah, for me, Weird Park Trilogy is worthy to be bought and played.

The Weird Park games tell the story of a spoopy ghost clown and his sinister little jester buddy, operating out of an abandoned amusement park. Each installment casts you as some kind of investigator looking into a part of the park's past, and having to assemble crests, collect socks, and shoot crossbows to solve the mystery. They're all hidden object games in that grand European style where the English and the logic are just a little bit off, but you can see the polish get layered thicker and thicker as the series progresses. Honestly, there's a lot of difference between the three games, so I'll talk a little about each one.

Broken Tune is the first in the series, and is about as straight-forward a hidden object game as you can get. You pick up inventory items, you use them on things, you find 12 doodads in hidden object scenes to get another inventory item. There are a few puzzles, but no gimmicks, no item combinations, and no map. However, the game itself is very well laid-out, with items arranged so that they will send you back to areas that have new points of interest. The uses of items are crystal clear, and the hidden object scenes are clean and detailed. One interesting feature of the game is that it is split evenly into two acts, with no backtracking or items shared between halves. The graphics are good, the voice acting is goofy, and it'll last you a good two hours.

Scary Tales is the middle installment and a huge step up from Broken Tune. This one adds more puzzles, cutscenes, and areas to explore. The game is also WAY longer, clocking in at nearly 4 hours. In terms of design, there's more variety to the different areas. The first third of the game is extremely open, allowing you to travel to many different rooms right from the start. This does make the opening act a bit harder, however, because you get a crapload of items at once and no real clear direction for using some of them. After that the game goes into some really creative places and plays with fairy tale concepts. It's a great game to follow the first with, especially since it's more difficult overall.

The Final Show is, unsurprisingly, the last in the series. Again it improves over the previous game, this time adding new variations of hidden object scenes, interactive items you can manipulate, an upgrade system (!), achievements (in-game, not Steam), and a map for ease of travel. Final Show is just as long as Scary Tales if not longer, and is split into convenient chapters with no backtracking between. It also goes full-on crazy with the setting, having only the tutorial set in the real world. Oh yeah, it has a tutorial, too, for some reason. The graphics are fantasic, and there are quite a few voiced NPCs in this one as well, as opposed to the mostly barren games before it. The upgrade system might just be cosmetic, but having something else to find in addition to the necessary items was a treat. There's even a bonus chapter you unlock after beating it that fills in some of the backstory, and is honestly one of the best-designed chapters in the series. My only gripe is that Final Show is harder still than Scary Tales, which pushes it into slightly unfun territory sometimes. In particular, some of the item logic is really questionable. Beyond that, though, it's easily the most polished and deep game of the three.

Taken as a whole, Weird Park Trilogy feels like a great way to get into hidden object games because each part of the series is so different. They form a spectrum from bare-bones to feature-rich, which lets you discover precisely what your taste is. There's even a complete story told across them, though it's perfectly goofy and predictable. It's also hard to beat the value, since you're getting 10+ hours of game in a genre where most will only get you 2. My one piece of advice if you do get into it, though, is to play on the lower difficulties. Whereas the "Expert Modes" in other hidden object games make the puzzles and searches more challenging, all it does in the Weird Park games is eliminate the highlights around hidden object scenes. It's an annoyance made worse by the amount of backtracking you have to do sometimes, because each scene has to be done multiple times at different points in the game. Remember that if you ever get stuck, too... go back and look for a scene that's reactivated. With the hints and the improvements of the later games, though, it shouldn't distract too much from the overall experience. No matter if you're new to hidden object games or an old hand at them, you won't be disappointed.
