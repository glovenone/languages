About This Game

Using a combination of your Test Suit abilities, interact with and manipulate trial elements to reach the goal door. Refine your skills and earn ranks based on time then post times against your friends on the integrated Steam Leaderboards.

JUMP JETS
The Jump Jets are your main source for navigation. With a short cooldown and subtle directional response, time them correctly for the best approach, adjustments, and angle.

TETHER SHOT
When you hit an Orb with your Tether Shot, it will yank you in its direction. Be accurate and precise, your position when linked will make all the difference. Your gun takes a few to recharge, so try not to miss.

REMOTE
Your Remote will activate receivers which, in turn, manipulate an element within the trial. Launch an orb, open a ledge, and unlock a door just to name a few. Don't forget, you're usually flying through the air.

If you've been longing for a game that really pushes you for success and the glorious feeling of self accomplishment, then APT will be right up your alley. Earn ranks based on your trial times-- go for the diamond!! If that isn't enough, race the clock and post against your friends on the integrated Steam Leaderboards.

Features

  • Challenging first person puzzle platformer.
  • Intuitive and responsive movement and controls.
  • Use the abilities of your suit and interact with elements in each level.
  • Earn ranks based on your trial times.
  • Steam integrated Leaderboards.
  • Mini-game Challenges
  • FREE CONTENT UPDATES!

I'll start with the tl;dr version:

It's kind of a fun game, but know that it's exactly what it reads on the tin--a very literally shiny, well made, physics intensive game where you try to reach the doors at the end of each room faster than the people on the leaderboards. That's it. Nothing more, nothing less.

The game feels like a solidly finished, 3D platforming framework for somebody to fill in with substance, except somebody forgot to expose the API. Whoops.



Now for the longform:

It's my fault for making assumptions. For some reason, maybe the people mentioning Portal when talking about the game, I just figured there'd be a little more to it once you got going a bit. You know, some sort of further interaction between the character and the disembodied voice of the researcher that initializes them, or maybe a backstory. A plot thing to keep me interested.

To be honest, this game was kind of an impulse buy for me because I liked how shiny it was and I wanted to listen to the music and see the rooms designs, what have you. Plus it was on sale.

My experience so far has been that the chambers, and therefore the game itself, are little more than a novelty. They're 3D obstacle courses with fancy mechanics (and some not so fancy, like basic platforms) that have you racing against the clock to figure out how the chamber works.

These chambers are indeed quite challenging as you progress. It's mostly because the basic physics of the game itself are just so particular, which is good. However once a chamber's novelty wears off, they just grow tiresome due to the long, unforgiving chains of rapidfire maneuvers required to complete most chambers. That means that when I screw up I have to start all over again in chambers that just seem to keep getting longer and longer. It's just arbitrarily time-consuming, and that leads me to the real problem.

The problem is that the devs dangle no carrots to keep me on the hamster wheel. There's no cheese at the end of the tunnel.

I get virtually no satisfaction from completing a chamber other than being glad that I've finally gotten it out of the way. All of the chambers are just disconnected rooms with no purpose or structure, no story.

As shiny as the game is, there isn't even anything pretty to look at due to their choice of visual aesthetics. (All the chambers look exactly the same as you see in the trailer.) I'm not progressing toward any sort of goals other than to complete the entire list of chambers, but I'm so bored now that I don't even have enough curiosity left to manage that. Scoring diamond on the leaderboards may mean a lot to some people, but I don't personally give a damn.

If you want a better game of the same type, I highly recommend DeadCore instead. Sadly it's not nearly as shiny, but it's a much more engaging experience overall. Not to mention it's cheaper and includes dank memes.

All in all, this is a game for people who enjoy a fast-paced, challenging, first-person 3D puzzle platformer that allows them to get in quickly and game.

For a puzzler of its type there is something to be said about the difficulty. As a casual gamer you may find that most of the levels are easy until you get to that one level which requires an impossible jump. An hour later you might get lucky and start patting yourself on the back for that silver/gold medal you finally claimed; and on you go to the next level. However, if you do decide to retry for diamond, you have to be extremely precise, and perhaps gravity-defying. For those who want to compete, this is a fun game to play.
The game is not without its flaws, but I am confident that the developers are going to fix them very soon. For nearly two hours after installation the game performed horribly at 1920x1080 on an i7-3770k and Radeon HD 6870 (even with all settings on low). By modding the assets of the game and removing a couple materials the game instantly became playable again. As it stands I am convinced there there are some necessary optimizations that need to be done, because removing the materials is only a short-term solution and also subtracts from the game's polish. If you have a more powerful rig than me, then you probably won't have any graphical lag.
Another issue I have with this game is the camera shake. Almost anytime you perform an action the camera will shake violently which makes it very hard to land precise shots while rocket jumping. I have a hard time keeping my mouse still while under pressure and the camera shake only makes it worse.
All that being said, this game is pretty hardcore (and fun).

APT is a cool puzzle platformer with a nice feeling of achievement when you perfect levels.

The game's puzzles mostly revolve around momentum, and traversing around the map as fast as possible.

After your first jump, you can launch yourself incredibly far with a jet boost, and swing from floating balls with a tether tool. Like others have said, this game is a puzzle platformer, somewhat in the vein of Portal.

Like Portal, it has an understated graphical polish that reinforces the central theme of the game -- a kind of apathetic hostility evoked by the sterile test chamber setting that jives well with a game that's hard for the sake of hard. Also like Portal, APT's deceptively simple mechanics unfold to reveal a real depth of gameplay hidden within just a few straightforward actions. Coupled with the visual minimalism, the core simplicity of this game is the perfect setting to showcase the punishingly well-crafted level design.

Unlike Portal, the difficulty in this game is the kind that requires quick reflexes, adroit handling of the controls, and a quick, intuitive sense of the physics in order to do well. It's not the kind of thing that will appeal to everyone, but for many -- myself included -- the merciless, impersonal taunting of a tightly-tuned ranking system is the kind of thing that engenders a genuine sense of satisfaction when you finally shave a few thousandths of a second off your best time to eke out diamond rank on a particularly challenging stage, hours later.
