About This Game


Please, Don’t Touch Anything is a cryptic, brain-racking button-pushing simulation.

Covering for a colleague who is taking a bathroom break, you find yourself in front of a mysterious panel which only component is a red button. And since you’ve clearly been instructed not to touch anything, the only thing you want to do now is push that damn button.

Push it and expect to be held liable for any unintended aftermath. And there will be many.

Unicode Snowman

Posted: 8 July
I had fun solving puzzles and finding a bunch of endings for about an hour. Some of the puzzles are very logical and you're clued into the solutions in clever ways, and you find different endings by solving some of the puzzles in specific ways. You spend the entire game in front of a single desk that changes depending on which puzzles you solve in what order, and it uses music and sound effects really well. It just feels cool, right when you start playing.

But getting all the endings and solving some of the puzzles get in the way of each other in some pretty frustrating ways. Plenty of the puzzles have less logical solutions, and require that you repeat the same series of actions before you attempt to solve them. Restarting the game to make another attempt is quick, but actually figuring out how to get a lot of the endings stops being fun and starts being tedious. It's something like pixel hunting, but it isn't an issue of graphical limitations, as the game looks very nice. The issue is that you're hunting for clues that you only get from following a specific sequence of events, and to actually use them to solve a puzzle, you need to reset the game and solve a series of puzzles that gets progressively longer as you try to unlock later endings.

One ending, for instance, requires that you find one way to beat the game, make note of some symbols before the game closes itself, reboot the game, complete a specific series of tasks that you can easily lock yourself out of if you haven't trial-and-errored your way into finding the specific puzzle that this clue solves, and then input the symbols. This is not the only puzzle you solve like this, but it is one of the simpler ones in this vein. Two of the endings are unlocked through the exact same series of puzzles, but since you can only get one at a time, you need to do it twice. PDTA is an incredibly repetitive experience and, after a pretty short time, stops hiding clues that make logical sense in clever places and starts being totally impenetrable.

It's just disappointing for a game to leave a really great first impression and then to just totally fall flat.

Ever played a free web-browser flash game that was really good, and then thought to yourself 'Wow, they should make a full game out of this. I'd even pay for it'?
Well, this is THAT game. Take it as a pro or con, your choice.

Please, Don't Touch Anything is a stellar game. It has simple controls, simple visuals, and no instructions at all, but the game design is top notch, the puzzles are creative and really fun, and the lore/overall theme of the game is enthralling.
This is one of the most mysterious and unnerving games I've ever played. It always puts me on edge, and I never know what to expect.

Still, the game is arcade styled - it has relatively hard gameplay, but short playtime. Without a tutorial, this may take you days to beat. With a tutorial, you can do it in 15 minutes.

If you want a minimalistic 'different' game, with clever design, thought puzzles, and great atmosphere, this is for you.
If you want a long, lore heavy experience, or are looking for more 'gameplay', this is not for you.
Do note this game has some unnerving parts and at some points is downright terrifying. You have been warned.

TL;DR Review:

If you like puzzle games, grab it now. Exploring and thinking outside the box is key to enjoying this game, and there are some very nice, funny little secrets that you will love. There is also some replay value in it with the "Perfectionist" Achievement, where you must complete all in-game endings without pressing any unnecessary buttons. My challenge for you is to try to get all of the endings without looking up a guide ;)


Detailed Review:

'Please, Don't Touch Anything' is a small, exploration puzzle game where you play around with a panel filled with neat gadgets - all of which start from the press of a single red button. You get to explore the secrets of a room with a large panel, where you must use hidden clues to solve puzzles, play tiny but fun mini-games, and beat up a blood baby with a hammer. The game relies on you using exploration skills and calls for not only a deep-thinking ability, but also the ability to think outside of the box.

If you are the sort of player who is patient, enjoys discovering all sorts of clues and secrets, and has a deep passion for anything to do with 666 or the illuminati, then you will love this game. However, if you are the sort of person that gets bored or frustated very easily when trying to solve puzzles, you might have to pass on this one (or look up a game guide to follow, but to me that spoils the fun.).

For replay value, it isn't awfully powerful in this game, but it is still there. Once you have discovered everything and completed the final ending, you have a final achievement titled 'Perfectionist' to obtain (there is also another achievement to get all of the other achievements though, which, funnily enough, is titled 'Uninstall Me'), where you must complete the game without pressing buttons that are either unnecessary to the game's completion or haven't contributed to any ending (if you accidentally press a button twice when you don't need to, or something like that).

At the end of the day, it's a fantastic game. I bought this game when it was on sale for only a dollar, and to be perfectly honest, I would've paid the full price for such a great game ($4.99 USD). My challenge to any of you guys if you decide to get this game is to try and complete all of the game's endings without looking up a guide (I didn't use one).

P.S. I played this before Markiplier came along. If you want to play this, buy it and play it before you watch him play it. You are ruining the experience by watching Mark play it, or anyone for that matter.

You will not know what to do in this game until you get frustrated and start doing strange things - or read a guide, of which there are plenty available.

Once you start to understand some of the early learning (basic unlock codes, for example), playing with the various levers and buttons that can be found, and looking for secret hints that lead to questions you've not yet encountered that lead to still more puzzles to be discovered becomes an involved pursuit.

If you like puzzle games and enjoy the click-interface (as opposed to precision pixel-placement or rotating puzzles to see where pieces fit, for example), then this game may suit you.

$5 is about the highest I'd go for this one and that's a bit high in my opinion, but if you can catch it on sale (right now it's $.99) it's a great evening's worth of entertainment that you can enjoy with one hand on your mouse, and the other on your soda/beer/cat/other.

Very nice work, Four Quarters & BulkyPix.

I was heavily and pleasantly surprised with this game. I can't say for sure what I was expecting, but, well, it wasn't that.

Which is a good thing! The element of surprise works wonderfully with the game, that always creates new and interesting things to do in the room you're in. I really liked how all the elements of the interface in the game are utilized - you have to pay attention to the smallest clues and details, and this investigation was very rewarding.

The puzzles are very interesting and challenging, and I loved the meta-like style - using paper, taking notes, making calculations and logic deductions, the game has kept me engaged throughout with a diverse set of actions. It was challenging and ultimately, a excellent experience.

I think the only problem I have is that there could've been... more. Not in content or in lenght, but some semblance of narrative or a bigger theme, which some similar games have. As it is, the game is more like a puzzle box, a way to explore a single environment. It's great at that, mind you, but there could be more to make the experience more fullfiling and memorable.

Still, extremely recommended, even more so if you can grab it on sale.
