Posted: 8 July
Mass Effect is an amazing game.
I first played it years ago when I was a young teenager, when it was the only ME game out there and it shaped what I expected of gaming from then on.
Everything from the atmosphere of the game (the music in particular is something to behold) to the narrative and how it plays out all feels well thought-out and engaging.
Some of the plot and side-missions feel like they're plucked from generic sci-fi plotlines and remuddled a bit, but in ME's own way, it owns those factors and you no longer think "That whole alien queen speaking telepathically to someone has been done before" and rather think "Mass Effect did it and did it well."
Your rampage across the galaxy to find this villain who's become a harbinger for the end of life as we know it is fun, and you care about it. Is it the same as every other game where you chase a world-ending villain and his/her catastrophes? Yep.
Still fun.

While, at this point, the graphics are a little outdated, you occasionally see something jaw dropping in Mass Effect. Smoke drifting diagonally away from lava-coated vistas (that you're DRIVING THROUGH) or a blinding, lilac sun on the horizon can, once-in-a-while, take your breath away.
The dialogue is meticulously thought-out and well acted (while sometimes sounding a LITTLE dry) and it makes for a great dialogue-system where you know what you choose to say can and will have an effect on the outcome of a mission. Although, while you have choices in ME, all those anastamosing plotlines end you up in the same place. That's not a downside (insert a philosophical quote about inevitability and fate here.) You still feel in control of your character and your future though and that's as much as you can ask for in a game.
Gameplay wise, it's fun, if a little simple. There aren't any puzzling moments (unless you count getting temporarily lost in one of the many hubs.) The game repeats a little when you have to play the same fight a few times to win. That's not a criticism. I'm all for old-school, 'git good' gaming.
There are really only three or four main things you'll be doing in this game; shooting at and ducking from a wide variety of clever enemies, wandering around hub areas looking for answers to life's questions (and convincing people to do things), driving around in the MAKO (amazingly fun for something so simple) to explore planets, and zipping around the galaxy map to go do one of the three aforementioned activities.
The combat is FUN and simple, but your two AI counterparts are fairly stupid and don't like to use cover like you do, so will get shot ALOT. Ordering them about feels like you're having to baby two other people whilst not dying in a firefight yourself, but you're Commander Shepherd. You can do it.
Wandering back and forth in hub worlds like the Citadel is fun for about five minutes as it's all rather pretty, but eventually, talking to one character then running back to talk to another can get a bit boring and you wind up wanting to leap into some action again, which you can't without finishing your conversational wanderings. And prepare for ALOT of elevators. It's M.E.'s way of loading parts of maps while you're travelling but it takes you out of things a bit and they make you use elevators a whole lot. Boring, sadly.
Planet exploring is fun and scenic (did I mention this game is beautiful?) especially in the MAKO. The vehicle is fun to drive around, even if it does have some crazy oversteer, but what do you expect when you're on the surface of some low-G moon driving over dunes and dustscapes? The guns feel powerful but don't bother trying to shoot anything near you as the thing can't aim down and the MAKO is quite tall. Just run over the enemies, dammit.
They've put so much effort into fleshing out the characters and histories in this game; of you, your squad mates and beyond and it's all well voice acted. You can feel sparks fly not only between you and your teammates, but you notice which squadmates like each other and where rivalries lie in all the tiny ways.
And everybody has their flaws.
I love Ashley. No denying it there. I love her go-gettem attitude and her out of nowhere attraction to poetry. But she's also super racist against aliens, although she denies it. These aren't character tropes that come out of nowhere. Ashley's lived a life before you knew her and she's willing to tell you about it if you get to know her, from her gung-ho attitude she cultivated with her sister, to her father's love of poetry handed down to her, to her stoic patriotism towards Earth and Humanity leading to her distrust of alien races. Like her or not, she's human (irony intended); so much more than just a 3D rendered cardboard cutout. Now apply that detail to the dozens of characters, locations and races in Mass Effect. (All except Saren, the main villain of the game, who seems to just be a laughing, evil face with no deep backstory other than a foreboding 'he used to be what you are now.')
They've sat down and thought this universe out. We NEED more people to do this when making games. It's probably the biggest selling point of Mass Effect. The galaxy has existed long before you arrived and will go on long after you leave. You can ignore all of it; skip past the expositional dialogue or ignore informative text boxes altogether. But if you want the lore, it's all there and it's beautiful. Plus, they make a GOOD attempt at sciencing everything well. It's what makes for 'science-fiction' rather than 'space-fantasy.'

So, to sum up:
+This game is STILL beautiful after all these years.
+The people and locations are so detailed in lore and history that you can't help but love them all.
+You feel in control of yourself in a vast universe where everything seems to be going wrong.
+The gameplay is fun and simple, and there's a lot of it.
+ Hit the jets on the MAKO while driving up a mountain and try to do a backflip!!!
- The gameplay can get a little repetitive.
- Slightly generic (but foregivable) plotlines, particularly in some side missions.
- Ultimately, you're choices don't change the ultimate outcome of the game, but it shows how small you are in this huge story anyway, and serves to drive home that point. (So, secretly a positive?)

The negatives are small and are hugely outweighed by the positives.
9/10.

My first 'real' RPG and my introduction to the trilogy as a whole. Mass Effect 1 is an excellent game that's aged pretty well, though there are still some hiccups here and there.

Pros:
-An immersive story where most choices have long-standing consequences. These range from fairly obvious galactic-scale choices to something as seemingly inconsequential as chatting with a particular NPC. While not all of these are terribly important, it really gives a sense of connectivity between games. People don't just forget how you treated them in the past.

-The planetary exploration system. While it's unfortunately coupled with the horrible Mako controls (talked about in more detail below), this is one often-derided part of the game that I actually enjoyed. By having a planet in nearly every system that you can get down and explore (rather than just scanning from your ship, as in later games) makes the galaxy seem that much more massive.

-The universe itself. Like most Bioware RPGs, the universe of the game is incredibly fleshed out. You'll discover hundreds of 'codex entries', which are little articles which describe how things work in-universe. Everything from spaceship design, to FTL travel, to how certain aliens function is covered at some point or another. This is all optional, of course- you don't *have* to know any of it to play the game, but it really enhances the experience when you can see how much thought went into creating this world.

Cons:
-The inventory system is... not great. I've seen worse, even in AAA titles, but this one is pretty bad. Your inventory will quickly become cluttered with mostly useless junk, with only a few things worth keeping. This does a good job of fleshing out the world somewhat (most things have a small description or at least a logo, making the galaxy feel bigger), but at the cost of convenience. You practically have to micromanage your squadmate's armor and guns for the first half of the game or so. Once you get access to the really good stuff, everything else becomes pretty much obsolete. Credits become kind of superfulous at the end of the game as well- I maxed out my bank on my first run without even really trying.

-The dreaded Mako. It's essentially an armored vehicle for planetary exploration. Sounds great, right? The problem is that the handling is utterly atrocious. The thing lumbers around like a drunken rhinocerous, until it gets stuck on a rock outcropping and flies halfway across the map. It's all right to drive on a flat surface, but most planets are at least somewhat mountainous. There are even a few that are almost nothing but hills. Once again, this adds character to the planets at the expense of gameplay.

Everything else:
-The combat's nothing special. Not bad, mind you, but it doesn't do a whole lot that's really unique. Third-person shooting from behind chest-high walls with some different powers to liven things up. These range from hacking robotic enemies or weapons to 'biotic' powers which are essentially Force moves. The AI for enemies can get a bit stupid at times with characters running straight into your ambushes.

-The graphics are good. A lot of the planetary environments are still beautiful, but some of the characters can get off-model mid-cutscene which makes things pretty jarring.

Overall, I'd rate this a solid 9/10. Good combat, if nothing new, coupled with a wonderfully rich environment, engaging characters, and long-lasting consequences that will follow you through the trilogy.
