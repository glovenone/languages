About This Game

King’s Quest is a collection of five different chapter releases, each focusing on an untold tale in Graham’s past. In each chapter, an aging King Graham reflects on his life of adventure with his granddaughter, Gwendolyn.

  • King's Quest brings adventure gaming into a new era while staying true to what made the original games beloved; the unforgettable storytelling, wit, puzzles, and of course plenty of ways to die!
  • Select between the noble pillars of Bravery, Wisdom, and Compassion to see what rippling effects these choices will have on Graham’s journey as well as later chapters.
  • Puzzles will offer multiple solutions, encouraging players to experiment with their inventory and environment.
  • Befriend the most unexpected and peculiar of companions throughout Daventry!

Future chapters can be downloaded from in-game upon individual release throughout 2015 and 2016.

How to get the full game even if you've played the free episode:
Rightclick on King's Quest and choose settings.
Choose local files and Browse Local Files.
Copy the folder King's Quest, this is the folder that has opened when you clicked browse local files, to another place, for example your desktop.
Click (in the Steam app) on Help and then Steam Support.
Find King's quest, should be clearly visible if you just finished it otherwise just browse.
Open the meny for King's Quest and choose "I want to permantenly remove this game from my account".
The free episode has now been remove and you can purchace the full game. Do that.
Install it and afterwards open the local files again like described above.
Go into the "common" folder, the one the King's quest folder is located in, and delete King's quest.
Drag your copy of King's quest into the common folder. The folder has to be named King's Quest exactly, it might have - copy added to it from earlier.
Go back into Steam and rightclick on King's Quest, open local files and choose validate game cache. It will now install the episodes you removed.
You're done! You now have all the episodes for King's Quest.

Score: 9/10

Kings Quest! It's a lovely game that stands on it's own despite apparently being the most recent in a long-running series of games. I didn't know this when I bought it and it stood on it's own perfectly well.

Pros
+ABSOLUTELY STUNNING. I'm a sucker for amazing art direction, and dang it, King's Quest killed it. Each texture is apparently hand painted, and it's gorgeous.
+The story is well thought out and plays smoothly no matter which direction you take while playing.
+Characters are charming and, while a bit one-dimensional sometimes, still engaging and memorable.
+Challenging, but not super hard.
+Good use of character and plot development before (part 1 spoiler) ripping your heart out and killing your new friend. Like, damn guys. Good job but OW. I'm still hurting.

Cons
-Some stuff is a bit too over the top. Like it goes beyond charming into cringe territory. Not much, but it's enough to make a full point about it.

Super Minor Cons
~It's episodic, which I know is a nitpick but I still don't like that style of game-release. It's better than most episodic game releases, since each part is basically a small, but full-fledged game on it's own, but still.



I was completely unaware how popular King's Quest as a series was when I got it. Part 1 was on sale so I thought "Why not?" I played it, and loved it. Graham was adorable and clumsy and everything I love in a protagonist, the narrative was sweet and funny, the story as smooth, and I wanted to take my time learning and seeing everything and talking to people. I wanted to do more in this world. Then Part 1 was over and I quickly bought the seasons pass and waited for more.

Overall, the game stands on it's own 100%. When I bought it I knew nothing about the series and still had a blast. Afterwards I learned more about the universe it was set in, watched some let's plays and then when I played it again, it was still just as fun - i just knew more about it.


Part 2 eventually came. However my attention span lasts -2 seconds so I had forgotten I owned the game and didn't play it until I noticed that Part 3 came out. It was a nice moment of forgetfulness because that meant I got to spend more time playing intriguing new content.

Now despite being, in my opinion, a bit less fun than Part 1 was, Part 2 is my favorite, and for 1 very shallow reason:
I adore Graham in this chapter. He's adorable in Part 1, and cartoonishly rugged in Part 3, but he's just? Wierdly cute. It makes running around a cave doing chores for a whole chapter more fun. If it weren't for that, Part 2 would be at the bottom.

Part 3 was interesting. It was nice that you got to make a real choice that shaped the world outside the story in choosing your queen. All the other choices made smaller ripples; different dialogue, small changes in Gwen's actions to resolve conflicts, but nothing that changed an entire character based on your actions?? It was nice to see.


To discuss major plot spoilers I enjoyed, and make the game a wonderful story and worth the buy;

Part 1:

-Achaka my poor sweet angel. My subsequent playthrough were SO SAD after realizing that the bow you get in the opening tutorial was Achaka's. (Something I didn't realize when he died. I wasn't paying attention to the broken helmet.)
-Manny being awful from the very start. A subtle detail, one most wouldn't notice, but after playing part 1 a bunch to get (almost) all the achievements, and getting a savestate for the 3 main routes, I did: Manny is the one who tells Achaka about the Dragon. He can be seen talking to him during the Test of Chivalry, when they say the Itch-two-an word for Dragon, which Achaka repeats again when you two are going through the caves.

Part 2:

-Not being able to save EVERYONE was a great part of making sure that Graham's near obsession with losing people so much more prominent. You really felt for the poor guy. Despite not truly being at fault for Achaka's death, it still sticks to him so much and peppers the narrative.
-The entire chapter was one HUGE puzzle that you had to do in order to get the optimal ending, which is saving everyone but Mr. Fancycakes, since he basically does nothing but be impressive the whole time. It was both VERY ANNOYING but also really cool. It took a while to get the perfect savestate, and I enjoyed learning and making mistakes, though it took me an embarassingly long time to figure out how to get the music box song.
-No matter who you choose to bring with you to confront the goblin king, there's always going to be someone who doesn't approve. (ie. Taking Amaya instead of Bramble and Wente will make Neese not like your choice in Part 3, despite the fit blacksmith being the OBVIOUS CHOICE over a pregnant lady and her pudgy husband or a pair of VERY ELDERLY potionmakers.) You can't make everyone happy and there isn't one correct decision for everything.

Part 3:

-I ♥♥♥♥ing called Manny being a goblin the second he made that comment about "Goblins being smarter than they look" during the Battle of Wits in Part 1. I knew it I knew it I knew it.
-Hagatha made me so sad. Like sure she's ♥♥♥♥♥♥ for doing what she's doing but you feel for her. She's been trapped her whole life too. I hated the fact they all called her Hagatha too, like that couldn't REALLY be her name.

TL;DR - The game itself is great so far, but the way Sierra cheats some of it's customers out of the last part of the game is downright despicable.

What is it?

This re-imagining of king's Graham early adventure is a definite departure from the early text adventures or the later point & click titles. Those familiar with Telltale Game's adventure titles will probably feel comfortable right away as the control scheme is somewhat similar. The only time you will be using your mouse (unless you're playing with a controller) is to select items in your inventory or to aim with your bow.

There is a number of action sequences that will require you to dodge obstacles or respond to quick time events. Fortunately they're sparse enough that they don't distract too much from the adventure.

You play the role of young Graham, acting out his past adventures as the old king narrates the story along with your progress or failures, as he's telling the tales of his youth to his granddaughter. If you get yourself killed, the king will mention that's what would have happened if he had pursued that course of action, then you're brought back moments earlier and can pick back up.

How does it fare?

The game look pretty good and the graphics have a certain charm to them. The voice acting is top notch and really bring some depth to the characters. There's a lot of humor and some charming puns along the way.

The first episode gave me about 6 hours of game play to completion. Most puzzles are logical but a tad on the easy side. Don't expect to be scratching your head much at what to do next. Most puzzles can be solved in more than one way and there's no way to put yourself in a situation where you can't complete the game.

The story is somewhat linear and achievement hunters might have to go through it more than once as there's a lot of hidden achievements and many of them are easily missable with no way to get back to them once you've moved past the part of the storyline where you can get them. You can still expect a bit of back tracking as you search for items or for clues about what to do next.

So far, the game stands pretty well on it's own and is a decent addition to the King's Quest franchise. Die-hard King's Quest fans may be disappointed to see the game strayed a bit from it's roots and that it's not nearly as challenging as the olden titles.

The good

  • Beautiful graphics
  • Top-notch voice acting
  • Multiple ways to solve puzzles
  • The game acknowledges your choices and adjust dialogues accordingly
  • Interesting story with a lot of humor
  • Decent episode length
  • Can be played just as well with a controller than with the keyboard and mouse

The bad

  • There is no way to skip the dialogues and animations
  • Some of the action sequences somewhat break the pace of the game
  • When you die, or fail an event, you have to go through way too much story before you can try again
  • The interface might feel a bit dumbed down to experienced adventure players

The ugly

Wait! Didn't you just said you liked the game? What's with the negative review then?

The reason why I can't give a positive review for the game at this time, is because unless you bought The Complete Collection, there is no way to get the game's last part. Sierra has confirmed on Twitter that the epilogue is exclusive to The Complete Edition and they have no intention to release it separately.

Furthermore, from the moment you have Episode 1 in your library, you can no longer purchase The Complete Edition as you're considered to be owning it already. And adding the Season Pass will not provide you with the full content. Unless you bought the whole thing right away, you get cheated out of the last part of the game.

Should I buy it then?

If you're a fan of modern adventure games, this one is definitely worth it. However, if you care about getting the whole content, wait until you can afford to buy The Complete Collection.
