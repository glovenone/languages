About This Game

Prepare for a mind-bending first-person adventure, as you enter the world of the Odd Gods and discover the magical power of the TRI! Master abilities that let you create your own path through an immersive universe full of unknown wonders.

With every twist of a corridor, every raised gantry over your head, every new floor of towers and dungeons in the Odd Gods’ world, TRI challenges you to think outside the box. Empowered by the TRI artifact, you place anchor points on the walls around you and conjure triangular platforms which can be climbed on, walked over and otherwise employed to negotiate the levels ahead. They’ll help you solve puzzles, reach otherwise impossible ascents, reflect light beams and even walk up walls… once you discover how to do it, that is!

A real test of spatial awareness, TRI is a mix of first-person and puzzle gameplay that will push your mind to the absolute limit!

Game Features

  • The Power Of TRI – Create your own path through each level by conjuring triangles out of thin air, then use them to solve puzzles scattered throughout the world of the Odd Gods.
  • Go Anywhere, Everywhere – Careful planning and thought can see you go almost anywhere using the triangles; think about where you place them and hunt for the mystical fox statues that allow you to move on!
  • New Abilities – There’s always something new to learn, always a new power to be earned. Want to walk on walls? Redirect light rays? Use teleportation? It’s all waiting to be discovered!
  • Replay Value – Finding your way through each of TRI’s 16 stages is taxing enough, but you can also hunt for hidden idols, beat the best time or meet fewest triangle targets, against other players on the in-game leaderboards of the Challenge Mode.
    The hidden idols also unlock bonus content like making-of pictures and developer commentaries!
  • First-Person Puzzling – TRI is a thinking gamer’s FPS; there’s no violence or killing, only the joy of exploration, puzzle solving and triangle conjuring. It’s a true test of skill and spatial awareness!

Also worth mentioning is the extremely appealing, simplistic artstyle and truly memorable soundtrack that accompanies this game. They really nailed TRI on the presentation side of things. A cute fox will follow you as you solve progressively harder puzzles while this light hearted chanting goes on in the background, and there's just something really enchanting and relaxing about that.

At it's best, TRI stands out strong in the puzzle genre right now. Even at it's worst, its still worth playing for any puzzler fan.

Tri Of Friendship And Madness is yet another puzzle game shown from a first person perspective. I love these kind of games. But is it as memorable as the Portal games or Q.U.B.E. ? Maybe. Let’s find out.

Tri drops you into the world of the Odd Gods where you have to complete 16 stages in order to find out what happened to two of the foxes that once inhabited the world. You’re being guided by a ghostly figure that gives you cryptic advice and messages. In each of the levels presented your goal is to collect the three hidden fox statues and to put them on their pedestal. Once all three statues have been placed on the pedestal you’ll be able to exit the current level and progress to the next one.
The game has a soothing and relaxing atmosphere. You can take your time; there’s no rush, but if you want you can beat your previous ‘records’ by replaying a level as quickly as possible, by collecting all of the hidden idols or by using the minimum amount of triangles. These triangles are an important part of the gameplay, so let me tell you a little more about it.
Your character carries a device that allows him to create triangular platforms. You can do this by pointing and clicking the mouse. The game will automatically complete the platform once three points have been placed but you have to take into account that there are many rules that make it impossible to ‘just’ create a platform. No, that would be too easy. For example, you can’t create platforms on wood. You also can’t create platforms in ‘corrupted’ spaces, and since the platforms created are in 2D (remember that you can only place a maximum of 3 ‘entry’ points) there’s no real depth or thickness in them. There are more factors to take into account, but I’m not going too far into this. It’s something you’ll experience and see for yourself.
Each level also has an amount of golden idols to collect. These are usually hidden in places you normally can’t reach so you have to carefully set up your platforms in order to be able to collect these. They don’t add anything to the experience, but it’s a fun side activity nevertheless.
A new ‘power’ will also unlock every couple of levels. The first one obviously allows you to create the platforms themselves, but the second one will allow you to walk on walls by carefully placing your triangles. There’s also a power that will let you redirect light and teleport.
The first couple of levels are very simple and can be finished in a matter of minutes. Halfway through the game becomes a bit harder and completing a level can then take anywhere between 15-60 minutes. Since you’re able to walk on walls after just a few hours of playing you can imagine it can get quite complicated; the camera will rotate often and some people might experience motion sickness because of it.
The puzzles are actually very fun - and sometimes also very challenging – to solve. You can’t die, so you can experiment as much as you want. You won’t fall to your death and you won’t get killed by enemies (because there are none). The mysterious story and characters make you want to keep playing, just to see what might’ve happened. The story isn’t 'in-your-face'; you have to think for yourself here, so it makes for a good incentive. A job well done, if you ask me.

The graphics are simple with relatively basic geometry. Graphically it reminded me of the game Kairo; it uses the same kind of soft graphics. But there certainly are more colors to be found in the world of Tri. Some of the levels also have a Japanese atmosphere, because of the lighting, use of colors and some of the objects in it (like lanterns). The visuals won’t blow your mind, but they definitely get the job done.
And the music: well, the music is… different. I thought that there were a lot of ‘world music’ influences in the game, with music similar to African and Japanese ‘tunes’. It’s subtle and not ‘in-your-face’, but it’s definitely something you’ll notice while playing. There are practically no sounds, with just a ‘hnnng’ sound for jumping and sounds for creating triangles. It’s all very basic stuff.
The voice acting is also limited (so far) to only one character. But this person does quite a decent job at pulling off a convincing, yet mysterious, character.

The length depends on your skill and intelligence. Some might finish it in 10 hours I think, others (like me) might take a little longer. Some levels are harder than you might think, and even though I’ve been stuck on the same puzzle for a few hours now I’m still enjoying it. Maybe because there’s no time limit or the fact that I can do what I want, trying to figure things out at my own pace. In any case, it’s a good sign. The length is perfectly fine for a game with this price.

Yes, yes; Tri is yet another game that can be added to the list of already excellent first person puzzle games like Portal and Q.U.B.E. It introduces a new gameplay mechanic that’s original and one that works surprisingly well, even in puzzle format. The mysterious atmosphere makes you want to keep playing and the increasingly difficult puzzles are(mostly) fun to solve.
All in all this is a great game for puzzle enthusiasts.

[Rating: 82/100]

I was lured in by all the positive reviews and the beautiful art style. Once I started playing, I really tried to love this game. I really tried. I got thirteen levels in before I couldn't escape that I hate this game more than any game I can ever remember hating.

There are a lot of reasons why. Many are nitpicks, but there is one overwhelming one: This Game Is Almost Never Fun. I started out enthusiastic, but it wasn't long before all the enjoyment drained out. I went from looking forward to the next level, to only wanting to complete the current one just so it would be over and done with. I could not help contrast Tri with games like Portal or QUBE where, most of the time, the challenge is in figuring out the puzzle. Once you know how it's done, you can do it. In Tri, this is not the case. Realizing the solution just means understanding how much tiring, precarious, exacting, chore-like work you have to do before you can DO the thing you figured out how to do. This is a game where there is no such thing as a simple solution. If solving a puzzle can be made more difficult, it has been. Not in a mentally challenging way, but in a way that feels like having to walk for miles over uneven rocks. (Especially once they introduced having to direct light beams by reflecting them off triangles. This requires innumerable microscopic adjustments each time.)

And even that would be fully forgiveable, if the game's difficulty was FAIR. For me, half my frustration came from the increasingly difficult puzzles, the other half came from inconsistent mechanics. Sometimes when I had to run up walls, a triangle that showed I could walk on it would suddenly let go of me and I'd plummet. Sometimes I got trapped in places I should have been able to jump out of. Sometimes lasers would bleed right through triangles I put up to block them (In one case, I had to layer down twenty-four of them). But the worst was the jump. At first I thought it was a nifty idea that if you were jumping towards a high ledge, you could keep pressing space to haul yourself up and over. I grew to hate this mechanic because of how often it failed. Jumping up a simple step could take five or six tries; impotently bobbing up and down going "HUHHP! HUHHP! HUHHP!" each time. There were also plenty of times when it seemed like random chance whether a given platform could be jumped to or not. Plus there was the tendency of jumps to send you up and over a thin ledge you were trying to reach, instead of onto. I quicksaved OFTEN.

Tri feels like it lives up to its name: they tried. They really did. They had ideas with a lot of potential and the level design is frequently gorgeous. But too often these ideas were implemented poorly, or were not conveyed at a reasonable difficulty level. I quit. I don't want to, and I am massively disappointed to, but I'm giving up and uninstalling this game. I know I'll never see the end cutscene, but honestly, not playing anymore feels like more of a reward to me.

This, is what I define as a "Hidden Gem". By 8th Jan 2016, the game only received 150+ reviews on the steam store page, which is far from a satisfactory number compared to its excellence. What a Pity.

The game, TRI: Of Friendship and Madness, is possibly the best First Person Puzzler I have played in 5 years (since Portal 2). Of course, as to artistic merit, the game falls far behind titles like Talos Principle or Antichamber, which is clearly not the main focus of the game anyway. However, when it comes to gameplay, especially level design, the game is definitely among the best ones in the category (way better than the previous two).

Like every other hidden gems, this game is flawed, every outstanding aspect of the game, is paired with something not correctly done, which I'll break into details in the following paragraphs. Nonetheless, as I always believe, with flaws come the personality and sincerity, which are the criterions I used to define good works.


【Mechanism】

Goods: The core mechanism and most puzzles are nicely designed, both logically and aesthetically. It's a rare one in the First Person Puzzler category that really nailed the 3D thinking, I mean, seriously into a 6DOF level. In fact, let's take a look back, aren't most puzzles in other First Person Puzzlers can be easily recreated in a 2D game? (Yes, I'm referring to Talos Principle here.) But not in this game, the refreshing experience you would have when walking upside-down on the ceiling, is as inspiring as Portal provided years ago.

Bads: While the core mechanism gives you a large range of freedom, it can also be easily exploited and overused. In more than one circumstance，I accidentally used some tricky/cheaty way to break the puzzle, which I'm pretty sure is not the intention of the developers. It might be welcomed by some players, but I personally do not like it, for it makes me feel as if I broke the game, along with its immersion and gaming context. It makes me feel ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ too, especially after I discovered the legit way to do so later on.


【Level Design】

Goods: Levels (after the first few ones) are huge and imaginative, full of secrets and alternative paths, the most important of all: WITHOUT ANY NAVIGATION (or a minimum amount of). This might sounds like a downside for modern gamers, but a diamond for nostalgic gamers like me. The game has all kinds of surprise in full stock waiting for you here and there , which simply gives you a sense of adventure and exploration, in your full control, unlike any other roller coaster experiences modern games would provide. It's hard not to relate this game to those old school classic FPS of the 90s with these elements, such a sweet déjà vu.

Bads: Although I speak highly of its overall level design, the first few levels of the game are rather tedious and uninspiring, which I'm afraid is the major reason that prevents more people from knowing the game. But trust me, if you are patient enough to make it through the first quarter of the game, you will be amazed by how ambitious and imaginative the levels are afterwards.

As you step through the portal you've been led to, this is where the game takes a twist from the relaxing to the oddly enchanting and mystic world of the Odd Gods.

Your objective for each ancestral act of the game seems simple enough; collect the three shining fox statues scattered throughout each sprawling temple of dream-like obstacles and bring them to their pedestal to activate the strange portal. As you'll soon discover after only a couple short levels, these fox statues all seem to be out of reach. Luckily, you quickly come across the very free-form and useful triangle creating ability. Lost in these ancient maze-like structure feeling like some kind of wild animal wandering throughout a huge human contraption. As you move through through each esoteric structure that acts as a separate stage you'll come across your spiritual masked guide again who pushes you in the right direction and fills you in on the rich lore and history of the world you've become enshrouded by. Is it the elusive and playful fox god we give chase to that plays these tricks on us as we navigate the puzzles of the world of the Odd Gods?

This is exactly the kind of memorable sleeper hit I would expect Rising Star Games to take notice of and pick up, and having another strange, beautiful and otherworldly title in their growing library of classics is why they're at the top of my list of favorite publishers right now.

TRI is a beautiful game, both in visuals and in mechanics. You place triangles that allow you to climb, walk on walls, and reflect light beams. When bending light, minute changes in a triangle can greatly affect where the beam lands, and I often placed as many as 30-50 triangles to get the light to land in the goal. (Note: if you use the tab button to fix your head and make your triangles exactly the way you want, you end up with much fewer triangles, but you still often spend a great deal of time making tiny changes) I did not discover the quick save (F5) button until very near the end, and there were times where I would die and get greatly set back, especially when I collected statues since the save. It was not uncommon in the later levels for me to wander around aimlessly for as much as 20 minutes trying to figure out how to progress. Some times I wouldn't pick up on a new mechanic being introduced and end up placing dozens of triangles to brute force my way through the puzzle. Triangles would also place oddly, either refusing to create for no apparent reason, or placing prematurely, requiring more work to get the exact triangle you needed.

And then comes the idol statues. In each level there are around a dozen little statues hidden in tiny nooks and cranies. As an achievement enthusiast, I had to get every one in order to 100% the game. This process would have been excruciatingly painful had I not used the guide (which is awesome). I would have wandered around, spending insane amounts of time backtracking and searching every square inch for fake walls or hidden coridors, constantly worrying about missing something. The time I spent in the game would likely be three times what it is, and it wouldn't have been fun. It was this feeling of banging my head against the wall to find the last idol (sometimes difficult even with the guide) or trying to cross an acid pit that took the fun away from the game and make the last few levels feel like a chore.

I spent a lot of time deciding whether or not to recommend the game, and I finally decided on recommending it, but with this warning: play casually. Don't sit down and think "I'm going to get all the statues in the last two chapter", because you won't enjoy it, it will be hard, painful work. Think instead, "I'm going to enjoy this game" and play through what you get through, and do it calmly. Try not to get overly frustrated at a problem, and if that does happen, take a break! If I had done that, my experience with the game would have been SO MUCH more enjoyable.

Tri is an atmospheric puzzle-platformer, mixing lovely visuals and immersing music with varied and inventive levels. It's beautiful, and the story gives a reason to press forward through the game, but the level design and the tough but fair puzzles are where the game truly shines.


Initial gameplay is straightforward. Explore the levels, place triangles as platforms, look in side areas for the hidden idols. The game quickly moves into spatial reasoning, with the triangles aligning your gravity with them, giving plentiful freedom of motion. Levels progressively grow more open-ended, giving full use of that freedom of motion; anything you see, you can explore, as long as you can figure out how to get there.
While the game mechanics remain simple, the world itself (and its playful nature about the laws of physics) allows for numerous applications, giving the puzzles a great variety. The levels aren't too difficult to complete, but the idols hidden throughout the world give a reason to really explore, leading one into the most clever puzzles the game has to offer.

Tri is a very relaxing game; the peaceful world, soothing music and calm Monk companion give the game a lovely atmosphere. As for the gameplay, timing-related puzzles are few and far between, nothing can kill the player except static environmental hazards, and the level design encourages exploration and taking one's time.


Personal assessment: Tri is absolutely fantastic. The open level design and integration of the puzzles into the levels as a whole makes the entire game feel like a cohesive package. It's not super long, but towards the end the puzzles are so different from one another that it stays fresh the whole way through.
My favorite moments are the puzzles that violate natural laws to the point that they look literally impossible at first glance, and the puzzles that are so mind-bending that even when past them you don't know if you actually solved it or if you missed the point entirely.
Your Mileage May Vary; many of the puzzles don't have well-defined boundaries or completion rules. While I very much enjoy that aspect, some may find it hindersome when going for full completion. The game is very much worth an initial playthrough, though.


Bottom line: if you have the time to let your mind be melted by a fox and a bunch of triangles, Tri is a great experience.
