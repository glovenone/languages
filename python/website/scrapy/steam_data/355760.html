Drizzlepath: Genie is Released!

After almost a year of working, I have finally completed developing the sequel to Drizzlepath. The title is Drizzlepath: Genie. It has got greenlit in just 8 days.

Drizzlepath: Genie is even better than the first Drizzlepath in every way. It has a captivating story, subtitles (with multiple languages), again very nice soundtrack but this time with much longer duration, it offers a much longer gameplay time presenting a fantastic world to the player.

About This Game

In Drizzlepath you are set on a journey to the top of the Mountain of Fire. This game is what you'd call a "walking simulator". It aims to be an atmospheric, relaxed and enigmatic experience. It was made in CryEngine 3 to achieve an immersive graphic quality. If you enjoy games that are about the experience of discovering of the world around you, you will probably love Drizzlepath.

This is an interesting experience and its worth your time.
It is a one man project and the first one of that Developer.

Yes, you walk along a path and get narration and music along it, it is a "Walking Simulator", and not a game in the usual sense. It does have fall damage, so you can die, but thats about the only thing that will prevent you from finishing Drizzlepath.

You will find beautiful sights along your way, and you can explore almost anywhere you wish to, but for the most part its pretty linear. The details get less when you decide to walk off the path, as its designed to look great along it. The main path will take you maybe about 30 to 60 minutes to reach the end, depending on how fast you take it, it is not a very long experience. But is an idyllic one for sure. The music highlights certain parts of the way, and there could have been more of it.

There are also things that could be improved, Subtitles for example would have helped me greatly to understand the voice of the female narrator, which has a Spanish accent that made me sadly unable to follow much of what she said. I didnt think the narration held any deeper significance and served more as a tool to accompany the character on his way.

Also there where graphical oddities when i wandered off the path, and the waterfalls didn't look right for me, but that might be an issue with my hardware. The game dosent offer much in terms of options for graphics or other settings, but it did run well for me. All in all Drizzlepath looked stunning while following the main path, I think it is a great use of the CryEngine.

You most likely will get value out of this experience, its relaxing, even if it doesn't offer much in replayability. Especially with the very reasonable price point this is offered. Just don't expect something that will keep you busy for a long time, its about taking a short walk along a beautiful path, and thats probably something we all don't do enough =)

TL;DR

Drizzlepath is a subpar "walking simulator" that essentially fails to accomplish its goal and is fraught with issues.

But it did slightly increase my motivation to actually go outside, and it does have very nice music (about 15 minutes total).


A Lazy Knock Off

For those familiar with Dear Esther, this is something vaguely similar, but much more poorly executed, albeit using a newer, fancier engine.

That's the thing. This game basically consists of what seem to be stock CryEngine assets with a few musical narrative interludes interspersed (you just walk along a path up and down some hills). The amount of effort and resources that went into this seem to be minimal.

(In fact, glancing at the game data, the package seems to include a large variety of stock and example assets that are never used in the game. The package is MUCH larger than it needs to be. Sloppy! Furthermore, after an hour of playtime, there was a 50MB log file full of errors and warnings.)


Pretentiousness Simulator

The female narrator's thick accent makes the abstract poetry even more difficult to understand than it would have been had there at least been subtitles to help you out. What little of it I was able to make out sounded pretentious or simply nonsensical.

This is the impression I had from the beginning, but when I heard the phrase "void of finite infinity", I said to myself, yep, definitely pretentious and nonsensical.

(Just in case, I actually looked up "finite infinity" to check whether anybody had managed to use it in any acceptable manner. Turns out, Emily Dickinson used it in a poem once. I read the poem. It worked for me. But nothing in this game did.)

Since I actually set out to write a review, I thought I'd double check so I fished out the voice files from the game data (Drizzlepath/GameSDK/GameSDK.pak/Sounds/wwise_project/Originals/SFX) so I could transcribe it. Here's the full version of the verse where that phrase comes from:

She was damned since the moment she ate that frog from the swimming pool.
She just couldn't stand [a/the?] chance to lock tight and look right for another [course/curse?].
Just like dwarfs from a shiny little town, she disappeared into a mist formed by droplets of nothingness that precipitate upwards into a void of finite infinity.


Buggy, Too

The few graphics settings that exist seem to be a bit glitchy (you must always re-apply the current settings, otherwise they apply... incorrectly).

There are also quite a few obvious geometric problems that break immersion.


Steep Hardware Requirements

Even running at essentially max quality settings, I wasn't particularly impressed. If you have anything less than mid-high end hardware, I suspect the game would look even worse.


Great Music

The one aspect of the game that is actually good is the music. Alejandro Padilla Coronel deserves credit there. The OST is included with the game. You can hear a selection of it here: https://soundcloud.com/alejandro-padilla-coronel/sets/drizzlepath


Just Watch a Playthrough

The full game takes about 30-60 minutes, but the parts with music and narration comprise only about 15 minutes, which you can see and listen to here:

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xy4wMe4mVNY

Gameplay Tips

If you do decide to play it anyway, there are a few important things you should keep in mind:

* Follow the path and do not look back. The game is designed to only look good along that rail. If you deviate, you will merely harm whatever immersion there is. Also don't look down at your feet or your dorky shadow.
* You can't save, so do not exit or close the game until you are done. If you need to play with the graphics settings, do it in the very beginning - the resource requirements are consistent throughout.


Developer

This is the developer's first title. For what it's worth, he seems like a decent guy and I'm interested to see where he goes from here. I hope this review does not discourage him from working to improve his craft.


Disclaimer

I received the game as a gift. Thanks again for that! Even though I ended up not recommending it, I do appreciate it as I was curious about the game.

Drizzlepath is a little game of exploration (many would call it a "walking simulator") completely developed by one single man, Tonguc Bodur. Though that's by itself an impressive feat, it should not cloud any appreciation, which needs to focus on the merits of the game by itself.

The game may be small, but the world in which you can walk (not run!) around certainly is not. Using the CryEngine, Bodur has created a world of exceptional beauty, with far-reaching views over sea towards towering mountains in the distance. The world feels rather tropical, but not in a lush way: animals are scarce, and the mountains are extremely steep and imposing. Sometimes it felt like I was walking like somewhere in the Andes (the setting of Macchu Picchu came to mind at a certain point), at other moments I felt like being in a completely unreal world out of a fantasy setting. The game allows for a fair amount of exploration off the beaten track (a path one can follow across the land and up to a mountain top), but there are hardly any incentives to do so.

Rather, staying on track is more rewarding as every couple of minutes the game offers a quiet, beautiful sound snippet and an enigmatic piece of text read by a female voice with a heavily accent. The "format" of the game, so to speak, has clearly been inspired by Dear Esther, but the finished product is nowhere as polished or as inspiring as that game was. The hard to understand spoken texts let the game down, as they prevent the player from experiencing any kind of story. I made up my own story while playing, feeling like someone who went up the "drizzlepath" (drops of rain drizzling on my face all the time) in order to perform some initiation rite while experiencing a growing sense of danger, and maybe this kind of "fill it in by yoursefl" is what the game is all about.

Graphically, although the landscapes are impressive and the lighting effects stunning, some of the textures are very bland and in some areas graphical errors abound. This makes the game feel almost like a study on "how to use the Cry Engine", but a very impressive study nonetheless and certainly one which I enjoyed playing a couple of times.

Recommend, but only to those who enjoy a relaxing game of exploration

This was a very pretty game. Really quite ebautiful at times, although at other times, the scenery was somewhat washed out. Strange disparity there.

The music was fantastic. I would definitely buy an OST for this game. Lots of kick ♥♥♥ guitar tracks and some other cool piano and orchestral pieces as well (and even a harmonica piece at one point). Anyway, my actual play time was right at an hour, and I was pleased with the experience overall. Would walk again.

7.5 mountains traversed out of 10 possible. If you like exploration games/walking simulators, I think you will enjoy this.
