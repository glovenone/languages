About This Game

Darkest Dungeon is a challenging gothic roguelike turn-based RPG about the psychological stresses of adventuring.

Recruit, train, and lead a team of flawed heroes through twisted forests, forgotten warrens, ruined crypts, and beyond. You'll battle not only unimaginable foes, but stress, famine, disease, and the ever-encroaching dark. Uncover strange mysteries, and pit the heroes against an array of fearsome monsters with an innovative strategic turn-based combat system.

  • The Affliction System – battle not only monsters, but stress! Contend with paranoia, masochism, fear, irrationality, and a host of gameplay-meaningful quirks!
  • Striking hand-drawn gothic crowquill art style
  • Innovative turn-based combat pits you against a host of diabolical monsters
  • Narration system to celebrate your successes...and failures
  • 14 (and counting!) playable hero classes, including Plague Doctor, Hellion, and even the Leper!
  • Camp to heal wounds or deliver inspiring speeches.
  • Rest your weary, shell-shocked characters in town at the Tavern or the Abbey to keep their stress in check.
  • Classic CRPG and roguelike features, including meaningful permadeath, procedural dungeons, and incredible replay

Can you stem the tide of eldritch horrors erupting across your family’s ancestral estate?

Descend at your peril!

This game is what RNGesus prays to.

I am not recommending this game because I don't believe that it is for everyone. At least the very least, I recommend waiting for a decent Steam sale.

I currently sit at 9.2 hours clocked into this game which is not a lot, I know, but every one of those hours were played separately after having to step away for breaks from the grinding. Not solely because of the grind, mind you. I have an affinity for reptative and tedious tasks in general. BUT I draw the line when said tasks result in absolutely no personal skill improvement or in-game reward.

As far as personal skill is concerned: all of the meticulous memorizing and party/skill combo experiments are thwarted against the game's RNG.

For in-game progress: I've yet to complete a dungeon. Most of my time has been spent so far purely farming for curing characters enough to continue farming. The game has a very minimalistic approach to leveling and upgrading. There's no stat or trait selections, no large armour, weapon, jewlery, trinket, and silk panty customization. It seems that a little is meant to go a long way when upgrading which should make it exciting when you finally get the cash and/or materials to upgrade...

But that just has not been the case. Not only is it very hard to gain the cash/materials needed for upgrading when you can only complete 3 or 4 rooms per run, but I've also realized quite early on that spending money on upgrading characters is pointless. They WILL die. I believe I have only 1 original character left and that is mostly by chance. Not to mention that the skill upgrades seem very low. Buffing helps a lot but when enemies are throwing out an average 10 damage per hit (not including dot damage like bleeds), sometimes raw healing is needed. But the healing skill I have is upgraded from 5 to 6 points for one character per turn. No matter how I spam, it is impossible to keep up with the damage output of many enemies across all of my characters.

Not being able to upgrade characters has lead to a real stagnation in any progress. I have spent about 9 of the 9.2 hours just grinding the same enemies in the same dungeons which is just getting dull. Yes, I have been able to upgrade my little Hamlet place a bit, but saving a couple hundred gold on curing afflictions has not made a significant dent in my ability to keep up with curing. Most of my cash goes to this, recruitment, and food that doesn't do anything anyway except negate occasional 20% damage hits in the middle of crawling.

I get what the developers were trying to do here. The game has cool visuals, the audio is great and the idea of dungeon crawling taking a toll on the average adventurer both physically and psycologically is an interesting concept. I just don't believe that the execution was very good. The story, or what little there is of a story, has really been stamped out by the mechanics and I would prefer a more in-depth, story-driven look at the affects of taking on your nightmares than this. As it stands, I don't care if my characters go mad. The only reason it bothers me is it messes up my combat strategies. At that point, I'd rather just let the person die because it's cheaper to just recruit a carbon copy than care about the psycholical stresses and recovery from them for that character.

I will likely continue playing this game for many hours into the future just to see if I can progress anywhere and learn any sound strategies as I simply enjoy the environment and ambiance offered by the visual and audal work that has been implemented.

With that, I did have a pretty funny experience at the time of losing most of my starting characters. And this might contain some spoilers if you can consider a cautionary tail about one of the game's surprise prostate exams a spoiler.

I approached this strange, glowing stone that was wrapped by a twisted tendril protruding from the floor. There was a bit of information that popped up on my screen--perhaps as one of my character's dialogue boxes--that mentioned I should use an item on this cute piece of contemporary art. Upon doing so, my entire party was sucked into some vortex and trapped in an abyss with quite the impressive-looking boss which I could best describe as a wonderful rendition of an actual purple people-eater. After a couple of love taps to the monster's belly, I realized my quest to loot this guy was going to be futile. Even the game itself was quite aware of what a s**t storm I had gotten myself into. My first tool tip informing me of the "retreat" mechanic popped up on screen. "Thank God!" I thought as I began spamming that bad boy before the monster could lay another finger/tentacle/whatever on my party; the same party that was now looking more like that box of raisins that have been floating about at the bottom of a juniour high kid's backpack for an entire semester. That is to say, there wasn't much left of my party at this point. My relief was quickly dashed, however, when the message that the attempt to run away had failed! With a well-recorded "slap" from the boss (love the audio), one of my party members fell dead...permenantly. My next couple of turns were spent just trying to run away with the message that the attempt had failed popping up on my screen before it could even completely dissolve after the last click. Just before my party was about to be completely wiped, the game had the audacity to pop another tool-tip up for me to REMIND me that the retreat function exists and that if my party wipes, it will be entirely my fault for not timing the get-away properly. I would have been livid if I weren't laughing through my face-palm. The next couple of minutes were spent by me sipping mineral water and enjoying the high quality recordings of people dying and the monster blurgeling while the narrator chastised me in his low, sensual voice. Needless to say, I've since avoided all future art school projects that I've come across in the hallways of the Darkest Dungeon.

I love this game. I've nearly beaten it, it has been very fun. But I can't recommend it in it's current state. It has obviously had a lot of work put into it, but it still needs a lot of work. My main complaint is that for a game that was created with the idea in mind of introducing the player to a world of realism(with fantacy Lovecraftian Eldritch horror themes), of the human mind and body being put to the test and the results of doing so, for some reason they decided the way to do this was with RNG deciding the mechanics they so heavily wanted to emphasize: permadeath and afflictions.

PROS:

+Very well done visually.

+Sound effects, narration, and music are great.

+Strategy based gameplay with many party and skill combinations to use.

+Difficult in a satisfying way (usually).

CONS:

-Too heavily dependant on RNG. Mechanics that are the main focus of the game, such as permanent death (Deathblow at Death's Door) and how heroes react to stress(Affliction/Virtue), are entirely up to chance. I'm fine with RNG when it's used to create the quests, but the combat and progress in general is too heavily dependant on it. To add on to this, the game actually becomes more RNG dependant as you progress. Critical Hits become more common and effects decided by RNG (bleed, blight, disease, debuffs, move) become more common.

-A total grindfest. Seriously. It takes SO LONG to raise heroes to be able to have any chance at surviving the LVL 6 Darkest Dungeon missions. Heroes come with, and gain throughout their quest through RNG, various negative quirks that render them useless against the extremely unforgiving stress and health damage of the late game enemies. So you have to grind low level dark runs, to gain enough gold to afford the treatments to cure their quirks, to make your higher level heroes even usable.

Excellent presentation and concept marred by a core gameplay loop based on grinding for gold and disposing of your characters. Progression is slow and unsatisfying, loot is rarely wanted. Treatment subsystems are essentially meaningless due to expense.

To get the things you need to upgrade the hamlet and the few good trinkets that exist, you need to go through higher level dungeons. To go through higher level dungeons, you need higher-level characters. To get higher level characters, you have to put them through the degredation of leveling up, which gives them all sorts of detriments that you can't get rid of because you don't have the money for treatment because you don't have the characters to beat the appropriate dungeons.

So you just hope for an Antiquarian and grind out apprentice dungeons.

Which is for the better, honestly: the apprentice dungeons are where the game is the most fun. Difficulty spikes on veteran dungeons are nothing short of grotesque and will appeal only to a certain subset of players.

It isn't anything like Dark Souls, and more's the pity.

Darkest Dungeon has become the unfortunate example of "The game that the devs messed up". Not everyone obviously agrees with that statement, but the almost infamous interactions between Developer RedHook and the most hardcore parts of their community have put the game in a rather unhealthy state.

So what is Darkest Dungeon? It's a Cthulhu-inspired turn-based roguelike-like in which the player controls a party of four not necessarily very brave adventurers on their journeys through the horrible parts of the family estate, which has been overrun by corruption after the now deceased ancestor opened some door to a hellish place in his crazy search for power near the end of his life. The story is very well narrated, but paced poorly, as only boss-kills actually reward the player with new lore-bits, and those boss-encounters are not only rare, but repetetive, as each areas 3 bosses are literally the same with various stat-upgrades.

Graphically, this game is absolutely amazing. The artstyle, the very distinct animation style really make the turnbased and thus not exactly hectic combat feels heavy, impacts and crits sound and "feel" devastating. And a large variety of classes with customizable skills, as well as a vast variety of quirks (both positive and negative) that they acquire until their semi-inevitable permadeath is highly entertaining. It's one of those games that writes its miniature stories through its heroes and gameplay.

Unfortunately, that's where the glaring balancing problems come in. Your party members need respite, otherwise the stress they accumulate in combat will make them freak out and lead to them doing stupid things, before they instantly die of a heart attack once their stress meter fills a second time. This change alone has been highly controversial and makes the game VERY difficult to complete unless you had a highly leveled bunch of characters before the patch in question was implemented. If a character is struck by lethal force in normal combat, they will teeter on "Death's Door" and can be healed, or even withstand some more blows with lucky dicerolls, or die immediatly. This creates tense but also VERY rng-heavy scenarios, especially if your characters are suffering from damage over time effects like poison or bleeding. It can be quite literally impossible to save someone who suffers an unlucky crit and then gets to take your first go while bleeding.

To prevent this, the pretty much only available tactic to the player is to constantly recruit new sets of heroes, to burn them on a run that basically no supplies are bought for, and to then discard them and recruit the next bunch (for free) while investing the gained gold into the actual "main" team they're trying to progress with. This kind of grinding can be incredibly tedious.

Put on top of that the fact that some ridicolous rare enemies which are by far mightier than bosses might just randomly walk into you on any mission and you have a recipe for disaster, as your entire savefile (namely your main team) might be screwed at any moment if you simply roll a 1-5% chance badly. Sure, it's not really possible to permanently lose, but having to start 40+ grinding runs all over again to flesh out a new alpha team is more tedious than entertaining.

Overall, If you want a REALLY hardcore roguelite dungeon crawling experience, this could be your game. But do yourself a favor and turn off the added changes in the options menu. And while you're doing it, be happy the devs even added an option to turn them off, something the most hardcore fans of the game declared to be heresy, even blasphemy. Trust someone who enjoyed the game a lot in its launch state, you neither want Heart Attacks, nor Corpses blocking you from attacking in combat, nor permanent debuffs for fighters you rescued off deaths door if you have any intentions of finishing this game in under 500 hours of mostly grinding on throwaway freshmeat parties.

The problems simply outweight the enormous potential and atmossphere that the game did a great job delivering at the start. If there's anything to be taken from this game as a long term example, it's not to listen too much to a small group of your audience unless they're forking over hundreds of dollars into your budget, and to get an awesome narrator into every indie game ever. He's truely great, even after all the changes.
