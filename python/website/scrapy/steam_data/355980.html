About This Game

Dungeon Warfare is a challenging tower defense strategy game where you become a dungeon lord to defend your dungeons with deadliest of traps and insidious contraptions against greedy adventurers and wanna-be heroes.

As you successfully purify your dungeons throughout the world from the pesky invaders, you will gain access to more powerful traps and devious utilities. Gain experience from killing the invaders to permanently upgrade your traps.

  • Launch a huge rolling boulder to crush everything on its path.
  • Reroute enemy path towards a busy minecart track to smash the invaders away.
  • Strategically place traps with enormous physical force to mercilessly push them into pitch black abyss.
  • Gather a force of your own by installing summoning portals to instigate a chaotic brawl.

TL;DR: If you like tower defense games, this is an enjoyable TD with a twist. But it has its problems.

The graphics and music are well done in the 8-bit era style, nothing to complain here.

The gameplay reminded me of Orcs Must Die (or Dungeon Keeper even). You place traps that finish your enemies in various, graphical and delicious ways. In addition to the traps, the levels can have boulders and pits which you can use to your advantage.

However, due to the nature of traps, the game lacks some depth. Why? Well, it all boils down to fact that traps have very limited range of operation AND the path is one-way (i.e enemies just run from start to end). Some traps operate on larger area, but the trigger area is usually quite small, like one tile. This means that the path can actually be considered a single line on which you place the traps, and each trap can only affect one short part of the path. And because different traps benefit from proximity, the most efficient solution is usually a small "death alley" where you grind everything to pulp (or toss/drag the in the pits).

In many TD games you can use the tower range to your benefit by placing towers in such places that they cover as large area as possible, and areas as early AND as late as possible (to make use of giving your tower more time to work on the enemies). In Dungeon Warfare there is simply no such thing. (Well, there is, but it's more like a special case).

Once you realize this, it eats a bit of the experience. Even the most fancy and complex levels can be solved with concentrating on a small space, effectively ignoring much the level.

The game does get a bit more complex in the end. And for the record, I haven't completed the game yet, I think I'm at 70% or so.

Some random issues:
* Start of the game was quite heavily tutorialized, but nothing annoying
* Trap tiers and upgrading traps was NOT tutorialized, didn't learn this until later on when wondering what the "tier" means
* Going back to old levels to finish the optional gem levels is a bit stupid as all your upgrades make the game super easy (why not limit traps and/or slots per level?)
* Music is slightly repetitive... I cannot understand why many indie games have such a short soundtrack. Easy way to add value, folks!

Despite all this I'm still enjoying the game quite a bit, and wholeheartedly recommend it. It's just that with some design changes the game could be even better. Maybe for DW2? :)

A great tower defense game with a huge variety of towers, enemies and possible strategies.

This is a hidden gem of a very good tower defense game, comparable with Defender's Quest and Gemcraft, but ten times less known. What can you expect from the game?

- A huge variety of upgradable towers, unlocked gradually so you can learn about each one
- Respeccable skill points that you can (and must) distribute according to the map you're going to play
- Challenge modes that make the maps more difficult in exchange for better rewards
- An endless mode with ever harder waves
- User-created maps and a map editor

Most of the fun comes from the well designed towers and traps. In their most basic form, TD games have usually three types of towers - the reliable peashooter, the slow firing group damager and a slowing tower. In Dungeon Warfare you can of course use these basic towers and combine them with traps such as:
- Spikes shooting from the ground
- A harpoon pulling an enemy towards the abyss
- A push trap that pushes whole groups in the direction you wish
- A blackhole that sucks nearby enemies onto a single tile, making them a better target for the following AoE attacks
- A lich that turns any enemy it kills into a zombie fighting on your side
- A rotating spintrap rising from the ground and hacking everyone who comes near into minced meat
- A hex trap that turns an enemy into a frog

There are many effective combinations you can find and there is no set way of finishing a level. For example so far my go-to killzone consists of a chessboard of spintraps and spikes with a blackhole at the beginning and a spring trap at the end. The spintraps and spikes deal most of the damage to groups, blackhole pulls enemies periodically back so they have to travel through the kill zone again and the spring trap throws any straggler back into the fray. Sprinkle with a few liches to further slow down enemies and a gold enhancer and put a grinder at the end for any heavy units that make it through.

Another favourite strategy that some maps support is a using lot of push plates and harpoons combined with a slow trap, disposing of enemies by pushing/pulling them into the abyss. This is especially useful for large groups of slow, heavy enemies. It doesn't however work on thieves and horsemen who are too fast for the push plates and run through. Those must be disposed with a spintrap/flamethrower or spikes. The abyss also doesn't pose danger to flying enemies, those are better killed with a lightning trap and bolts.

All enemies have their weight and pushing/pulling traps have their force, so light enemies will get pushed further/faster than heavy ones, something you have to take into account. You can upgrade your push traps to have more force, but if there is a walkway behind the abyss, light enemies might get pushed too far and land safely on the other side, while if the trap is too weak, heavy knights and golems don't even move. Of course this can also be used to your advantage, as you can create endless cycles forcing groups of light enemies to go through your killzone multiple times...

You get the idea.

Graphically the game looks very simple, but unfortunately the screenshots don't make the game any favor as they look very chaotic. When in motion the game is quite clear and you know what is happening.

Don't let the screenshots dissuade you.

All in all Dungeon Warfare is a very good entry in the TD genre, well worth playing.

Early Access Review
Posted: 17 June, 2015
Shortest review: Orcs must die in 2D

You like Tower-Defense? And you liked "Orcs must die!"? Then you'll love "Dungeon Warfare".
It's basically OMD without Orcs but with the usual fantasy-dudes.

Sometimes very challenging at first, at least if you want to "3-Star" every map from the start, which
gets slightly easier the more traps you unlock and the more gems you've earned. Sometimes
you'll just lack the more fitting trap for some maps.

And even if you've 8-runed (each rune adds another difficulty like faster/harder/tougher mobs etc) all maps with 3 stars, you're not done. There's a very nice and
easy editor to make your own maps. In not 1 hour i've put together a very nice map with lots
of rounds that was actually fun to play. You can also publish your map right from the game.

But...early access?
This is by far the most complete, balanced and bugfree Alpha i've played in a while. I have
not much doubt the dev will deliver a fully polished product.
But even if he'd stop right now, i would not feel scammed, as it was a lot of fun for not much bucks :-)

The good:
  • Free placement of traps, no stupid fixed points (which are usually favoured by the lazy devs because it's easier to balance)
  • Thanks to barricades: Mazing!
  • Nice soundtrack (so far) and fitting SFX. Kicking mobs into a pit is very satisfying, even after 100 of them :-)
  • Many more or less different enemies, each with its own strength and weakness
  • All the usual OMD-traps and some more, all upgradeable
  • ONE godly power you'll choose before a map. Like traps, spawns or a general recharge-reduction...
  • Zoom-out-function for the quick overview
  • You'll earn gems for which you can make traps better/cheaper/faster. Like the skulls in OMD
  • You're leveling. Each levelup yields some gems and more importantly it raises starting gold for each map!
  • Good balancing, considering it's still alpha, has leveling and upgradeable traps! No easy job to balance this. You'll need an asian for that ,-)
  • A nice, friendly and dedicated dev who even publicly posts his email.
  • A "re-setup" button after the first retry of a map. Never setup your traps manually all over again just because you've failed :)
  • Even though it's 2D you'll never loose the overview. Even when it gets hectic.
  • 3-Star-System (none reached portal etc.) and on top of that: 8 runes you can activate that makes maps harder. Restrict traps, make mobs tougher/faster/more aggressive, increases cooldown etc.
  • Easy to use editor included. Make your own maps!

The bad:
  • The sounds can become a bit annoying after some hundred dead troops :-)
  • AI seems even dumber than the orcs. If, e.g., mobs have the choice of 3 roads to take, they always choose the shortest way. Which means they choose the heavily trapped way instead of those free roads.
  • Totally no story at all (yet). I personally don't care, but some may very well care.

The ugly:
  • Nothing!

Recommended? YES! Definately a pearl that needs more attention!

Early Access Review
Posted: 22 March, 2015
Before to start the review, I’d like to say that I’m really big TD lover from the age of Warcraft3.

As you know, most of the types of TDs that you can find in the market today, has been defined at that time.

Personally, I suppose there are two categories as a big picture, and those are like below;

1. One – way TD
You can only install the tower along the path which is fixed, the only thing that you can arrange is ‘when’ you install ‘which’ tower.
This type of TD is really easy to build for the developer, because of easiness of leveling the difficulty.
It is really predictable, and there are no many options for the players to get the goal.

2. Maze type TD
You make your own path with the barricades or obstacles, the only thing which is fixed is entrance and the exit of enemy.
This type can be provide wide range freedom to the player, because this TD is all about “how”.
But it’s really hard to get a proper leveling.
Because depends on the players’ skill, it can be too easy or too hard. And also, it’s really difficult to design vary maps.
Usually in this case, the differences between maps are only types of enemy or types of tower that you can build.
This limit is also connected to the difficulty of the leveling itself.

Actually, I prefer the Maze type TD, but it’s really hard to find in the market recently. Because of the limit that I’ve mentioned above, it’s almost not proper as the product to sell.

Because its natural defect on the volume of the game.

If you want to sell a TD to the gamer, you have to put “reasonable number of maps” in the game. But not like one way TD, maze TD’s maps can be really similar each other in the different meaning. Because for one way TD, developer can set only one path in one stage, but in the Maze TD there are so many possible ways to play in one stage. So to say numbers of map set itself, one way TD has really big advantage.


Sorry for so long prologue, but I just like to explain why I like this Dungeon Warfare this much.


This TD is ultimate-proper hybrid which is mix of those two types,


DW, it’s not fully maze type, but it is providing maximum level of the freedom for the player with the aspect below.

1. There is no tower type limit for stages, except very beginning of the game.

2. Always there are bypass to make a way.
There are many kinds of obstacles in the map tile set that player can use. it can help player to make a maze or can be irritating.

3. But as a design of map, each map has its own character because of fascinating unique features that you would never find in another TD.
(You never miss the mine cart)

Not only the map design, but also there are many attractive in many aspects.


1. You can set the difficulty very specifically. Normally TD has very simple difficulty level, but in DW, you can set many kind of abilities of enemy.
Especially what I love is the “weight of enemy”. This thing can affect hugely to the game.
This is a really good example that simple physics how could help make game fun. Bigger unit is slower, heavier ,and harder to throw into the air.
It’s really natural, but I couldn’t stop laughing when I saw this.

2. There are many kinds of towers that you can get.
I played only 14 hours, and I’m really bad at gaming unfortunately, even I love so much.
So I couldn’t go that far. But already I have 9 types of towers, and each of them are very fresh. Especially Harpoon, Push, and the spring.
I will not mention the specific function of those here. Because if you see the towers in the game that I’ve mentioned, you would know what I mean “fresh”.

3. User-friendly UI.
Except mini-map, which I want to complain about, There is all the needed user interface. Very comfortable to play, and very noticing.

4. Volume of game.
I wonder why this game has the early-access tag. This game seems already finished to build, well defined, and has enough volume to play not like other early-access.
I heard there are 30 maps, and with the difficulty set up that I’ve mentioned, I think it can guarantee more than 30 hours of playing time.


But also there are a few things must be improved further.

This is one of the best, if not the best, pure tower defense games in a very long time.

Depth:

There are numerous enemies with lots of different abilities, defenses, speed, etc.

20 towers, each customizable by placing points into them. Most of them have 3 upgrades in game, a few only have 2.

I counted 47 levels, but the map is kinda a mess so I could have easily missed one or two.


Challenge:

You can adapt any of the levels to be as easy or difficult as you'd like. Need to just beat a level to get the rewards (new tower or gems) but having problems? Tone it down a bit with the "Breeze" rune.

Looking for a challenge? Load up to 10 different runes increasinng the difficulty greatly, but also the reward!

Looking to go nuts? Turn on the "Endless" rune and get ready for an increasingly difficult grind.

With all of the customization available, you can replay this game until your mouse breaks, buy a new mouse, then keep playing some more. There is no end to the fun.

Posted: 19 March, 2015
Dungeon Warfare is the game where you kill or you die.

To more accurately describe the game, Dungeon Warfare is a tower-defense game focusing on installing traps in pre-defined dungeons in order to kill waves of enemies. The game starts you out with access to only two basic traps: The floor-mounted spike trap, which periodically juts up deadly spikes to damage the enemies above it, and the the wall-mounted dart trap, a device that continuously fires darts at whatever direction it is pointed at. As you progress through more of the levels, more traps types become available. Want to push enemies into a bottomless abyss? The pusher trap's got you covered. Want to burn them? The flamer will take care of that. Want to summon demons to fight for you? You can do that too. Each trap type is unique and varied, with over 13 different types as of this writing and more on the way. The fact that you can only take a limited number of trap types with you into a level means that you will need to plan out your defenses ahead of time. Often I've found myself having to decide just how I was going to deal with that massive wave of peasants in wave 5, for example.

Speaking of enemies, the enemies that you are fighting are more "good" than most things usually found in dungeons. In Dungeon Warfare YOU are the big bad evil guy, defending your portal from the legions of good. From the humble peasant, slow and weak but deadly in large numbers; the rough and tough warriors, able to take a beating; the theives, quick enough to bypass most of your traps; the mighty knights, that push your damage skills to the test; to the haughty nobles, that have more gold than they know what to do with; with over 12 different enemy types, every enemy in Dungeon Warfare is unique and presents a different challenge to the player.

The game deals with progression well, allowing the player to learn how to deal with each new enemy type over the course of levels. The game utilizes an experience system to reward the player with bonus starting gold for all levels, as well as "gems". In this game, gems are the currency used to upgrade the trap mastery of each trap, with increasing cost per level. For example, raising your spike trap mastery to level 5 takes 10 gems, as 1 (Level 2) + 2 (Level 3) + 3 (Level 4) + 4 (Level 5). The effects of trap mastery are twofold. Immediately, each increase in level affects the stats of a particular aspect of each trap type: For spike traps, it is a percentage increase in damage, for dart traps, the cooldown time between shots is decreased by a percentage, etc. There is no need it be worried about spending your gems on the wrong upgrades; at any time between levels the player can reset their gems at no cost, allowing them to mix up their play style for the next level. The second effect is that for every 5 levels in a trap mastery the player gains, the player unlocks a new tier of upgrades for that trap. These are straight upgrades that the player can spend gold on during a level to upgrade a single trap, and the upgrades often cost less than it does to place a new trap, although sometimes more can be better; again, depends on the playing field of the level.

On the topic of levels, for that matter, the game boasts a count of 29 unique maps as of this writing, with more levels being added each week. Also, while not currently implemented, the game will include a level editor that will allow the player to make their own custom levels and share them through steam workshop. Level layout will determine your strategy, forcing you to adapt. Some levels have natural defenses in them, such as bottomless abysses, thin corridors, and minecarts running through the map; figuring out how to best use what you're given is up to you.

In Dungeon Warfare, your experience total is the sum of all of your best scores across all levels, meaning that it pays to go back and revisit a level to boost your score. What seperates this from most games is the Modifier system that the game uses. In each level, you can choose from nine different modifiers that make the game more (or less!) challenging. Each modifier changes different aspects of the game: The "Nil" modifier decreases the bounty you get from killing enemies by 10%, forcing you to be frugal; the "Anchor" modifier increases enemy health by 5%, and enemy weight by 100%, making shoving them into the abyss much harder; the "Breeze" modifier is for those that are having difficulty, increasing the spawn times between waves, lowering enemy speed and health, but also cutting your score by 40%. All the modifiers affect your final score with multipliers, and up to 8 modifiers can be stacked (with the exception of the breeze modifier) to provide a massive challenge that, should you emerge victorious, gives you a outrageous multipler of x5.23 for your final score for the level. Be warned, this challenge is not for the weak; you will need all the skills you can muster for this monster difficulty.

All in all, Dungeon Warfare is a promising Early Access game. With more content being continuously added, massive replayability value, and a creator that is vocal and very visible to the public, this is one game you don't want to miss. Now get out there, and wage some DUNGEON WARFARE!
