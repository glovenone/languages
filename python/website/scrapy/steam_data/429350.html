Early Access Game

Get instant access and start playing; get involved with this game as it develops.

Note: This Early Access game is not complete and may or may not change further. If you are not excited to play this game in its current state, then you should wait to see if the game progresses further in development. Learn more

What the developers have to say:

Why Early Access?

“Satellite Rush is a game with a lot of content and we want to build it in a modular way. Right now we have a playable and fully beatable game, but we want to progressively add new enemies, weapons, bosses and everything else with feedback from the community.”

Approximately how long will this game be in Early Access?

“About 4 months.”

How is the full version planned to differ from the Early Access version?

“The amount of content will be the main difference. Right now, the game is a complete experience where you beat enemies and bosses, collect upgrades and weapons and unlock additional content. But all of those things will be increased in number, and the interaction among those parts is what will make Satellite Rush a richer experience.

The way the player interacts with the crowd should also get more interesting, with more exciting ways to make them cheer for you and a variety of bonuses and penalties depending on how they feel about your performance in the arena.

The game is supposed to be replayed a lot, each time being a different experience and having new content unlocked, like most roguelikes.”

What is the current state of the Early Access version?

“A complete experience, where you beat enemies in randomized rooms, face bosses to get upgrades, please the crowd so they throw weapons for you, and unlock additional content. The randomized elements make your run unique every time.

The final version should have a higher variety of this content, and the combinations among this variety will lead to more complex and engaging gameplay.”

Will the game be priced differently during and after Early Access?

“The price for the final version of the game should be higher.”

How are you planning on involving the Community in your development process?

“We want to constantly show what we're working on, and progressively add new enemies, bosses, weapons, upgrades and gameplay mechanics.

All of this should be slowly but constantly added into the game, and we'll prioritize and iterate future additions depending on the feedback we recieve. We also want to reach out for the community for ideas and have them be part of the creation process, all with due credit of course ;)”

About This Game

Welcome to the Satellite!

Satellite Rush is a sci-fi top-down shooter with pixel art graphics and Roguelike elements.

Take control of an abducted office worker and blast through Satellite Moebius, an intergalactic space arena filled with vicious aliens, robots programmed to clean (or kill), and a (not always so cheery) audience.

It is one part Super Smash TV, one part Binding of Isaac (Asimov?), four parts Douglas Adams, shaken - not stirred. Serve with a sci-fi imaginarium garnish.

Please the audience!

To survive in this vicious arena, you better make the crowd happy. Keep shooting stuff and giving them the bloodbath they've paid to see! They will throw valuable weapons for you when you do so.

Features

  • Randomized corridor generation, because nobody would buy tickets for the same bloodlust twice;
  • Permadeath, with a few persistent effects to trick you into thinking you have a chance - yes, we mean unlockables;
  • A demanding audience, and a lax security. They can throw stuff to you. Will you be able to keep them entertained?
  • Boss fights and Upgrades, because what sort of entertainment wouldn't involve blowing up bosses and getting neat rewards?

Early Access Review
Posted: 2 April
Satellite Rush is a tricky arena shooter with a cheerful, colourful setting that disguises the fact you're a random bloke who's been abducted and has to fight for his life!

It's a fairly typical action-roguelike set-up. You choose from a red, green or blue door, then have to clear out a series of randomly allocated arenas, and finally a boss. Repeat until you've won all three doors.

The crowd thrill meter at the top is a clever way of adding urgency to the game. If you string together kills, the meter goes up into the rewards zone, and cool temporary weapons will spawn in the arena. But it will continually tick back down again, forcing you onwards. I'd like to see them do more with this concept.

Gameplay review video, including two bosses:
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v5vILZ3g9Nc

I liked how the aliens have a variety of attacks to learn, encouraging you to come up with strategies such as who you'll need to kill first. But there needs to be more enemy types - some of the existing ones are a little dull.

The persistent unlockable system is a familiar way to encourage you to play again, and the door choice at the start allows you to focus on the upgrades that suit your play style, which is cool.

But the runs behind each type of door don't feel hugely different, which is a shame. A much wider selection of arenas and level styles seems crucial for long-term replayability.

Also, the control of the player isn't very slick, with eight directions of movement (and only four to shoot). He can feel lumbering and unable to dodge stuff. It helps that you run faster when not shooting, but getting out of a tight spot is often just not possible.

As is often the case with action-roguelikes, the game starts off slow and gets more exciting as it goes, so once you've played the game a few times the early parts can feel like a chore to get through. The game takes off once you've got the crowd meter high enough for weapons, but if you get the wrong arenas, this might take a while.

The bosses are tough, so they're satisfying to beat. Some of the bullet hells are a little silly, given your character isn't that manouverable, but bosses do become much more beatable with practice.

So overall, it's got a few problems - one of the major ones should be addressed by new content being added over the course of early access, but it's the basic control of your character that might put people off. I enjoyed the game enough for it not to bother me too much, especially given its value price point.

Satellite Rush is a tricky arena shooter with a cheerful, colourful setting that disguises the fact you're a random bloke who's been abducted and has to fight for his life!

It's a fairly typical action-roguelike set-up. You choose from a red, green or blue door, then have to clear out a series of randomly allocated arenas, and finally a boss. Repeat until you've won all three doors.

The crowd thrill meter at the top is a clever way of adding urgency to the game. If you string together kills, the meter goes up into the rewards zone, and cool temporary weapons will spawn in the arena. But it will continually tick back down again, forcing you onwards. I'd like to see them do more with this concept.

Gameplay review video, including two bosses:
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v5vILZ3g9Nc

I liked how the aliens have a variety of attacks to learn, encouraging you to come up with strategies such as who you'll need to kill first. But there needs to be more enemy types - some of the existing ones are a little dull.

The persistent unlockable system is a familiar way to encourage you to play again, and the door choice at the start allows you to focus on the upgrades that suit your play style, which is cool.

But the runs behind each type of door don't feel hugely different, which is a shame. A much wider selection of arenas and level styles seems crucial for long-term replayability.

Also, the control of the player isn't very slick, with eight directions of movement (and only four to shoot). He can feel lumbering and unable to dodge stuff. It helps that you run faster when not shooting, but getting out of a tight spot is often just not possible.

As is often the case with action-roguelikes, the game starts off slow and gets more exciting as it goes, so once you've played the game a few times the early parts can feel like a chore to get through. The game takes off once you've got the crowd meter high enough for weapons, but if you get the wrong arenas, this might take a while.

The bosses are tough, so they're satisfying to beat. Some of the bullet hells are a little silly, given your character isn't that manouverable, but bosses do become much more beatable with practice.

So overall, it's got a few problems - one of the major ones should be addressed by new content being added over the course of early access, but it's the basic control of your character that might put people off. I enjoyed the game enough for it not to bother me too much, especially given its value price point.
