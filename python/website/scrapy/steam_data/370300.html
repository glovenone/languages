About This Game



8-Bit Adventures: The Forgotten Journey Remastered Edition is a retro-styled JRPG with polish and personality, that focuses less on references (although there are plenty!) and more on telling a story...



Stop me if you’ve heard this one before.
A Warrior, a Thief, and a Mage go on a journey
to save the world by collecting three elemental treasures.
What’s that? You’ve heard it all before?

Tell me then…what happens next?

Join 3 unlikely heroes on a quest to save their world from a threat unlike any other!



Featuring:

  • Classic Story with a Unique Twist!
  • Vibrant, 8-Bit Inspired Graphics.
  • Genuine NES-like Music with Retro Charm.
  • Explore a vast world, packed with monsters, treasure, and more!
  • Fly an Airship, Pilot a Snowcraft, or Travel by Whirlwind!
  • Colour-based Element System adds an extra layer of Strategy to Battles.
  • Discover Engaging Characters, and People with Personality.
  • Enjoy an old school adventure!

Credits:
Created by: Joshua Hallaran
Music by: Carfonu
Character Art by: Jerram Fahey
Promotional Artwork by: MingLuke

System Requirements

    Minimum:
    • OS: Windows (XP or higher)

    • Memory: 512 MB RAM
    • Graphics: 1024x768 or better video resolution in True Colour mode
    • Storage: 100 MB available space
    • Sound Card: DirectSound-compatible sound card

If you are looking for an RPG that is mostly story over grinding this is the game for you. I quite enjoyed this game with all it's references to other games and familiar scapes and tropes. This game takes the aesthetics of different games and platforms even and adapts them quite nicely. I'd say it accomplishes this quite naturally, better than something like Evoland which I happened to enjoy as well.

This is one of the best RPG's I've played in a while in terms of pacing. I'm unsure if it was just my approach to the game but it seems like the game is just well thought out in that respect. I played the game mainlining the story and quests and occasionally chose to grind near a generously placed healing spring. I decided to run away from battles more than I normally would in most turn based RPG's since there is no pesky beastiary to deal with. I could be wrong but I also noticed the thief's steal function only seems to get you restorative items and never equipment. I quickly lost interest in stealing things from enemies which is something I usually get somewhat obsessed with in RPG's. I feel this approach simplifies this game in a very positive way; while leaving enough battle & breadth of story to keep things interesting.

The battle system is set up so that certain colored characters have an advantage vs. certain colors of monsters. This essentially means that you have to pick your skills more selectively in some battles. You can circumvent this a bit by running away from most battles that have several different colors of monsters, and finishing all or most battles where all the enemies are a single color. An example would be using strong water attacks or blue abilities in a battle with all red monsters to do extra damage.

If you are extremely frugal with money in RPG's you can always return to a certain green spire once you've completed that section to heal instead of paying for an inn. It's an odd thing to think that neither the Soldier nor the Mage has a bed they can sleep in to restore the groups health. I suppose that might make the game far too easy though.

I mention achievements quite a bit in reviews as they are something that's important to me. I really feel this game approaches them well for a RPG. If you hop onto the discussion forums the developer has pinned a topic referring to missable achievements. I found most of to be them earlier in the game which is good for an RPG. There's nothing quite like playing 100+ hours and realizing you wont be able to fully complete a game you wanted to the first time just because of the nature of what your playing. Instead this game gets that right.

There are however (as always) a few things to pick apart.

The game doesn't allow you to take a screenshot through the Steam UI. This wouldn't be as bothersome if you could change the key binding or it wasn't replaced with a load function. If you hit F12 in this game you're screwed.

I used a wireless Xbox 360 controller to play the game. I found it odd that only the left analog stick could control character movement and the D-Pad was simply not functional. I feel like a game with a retro aesthetic should always allow for the option between a left analog stick or more importantly the use of the D-Pad. The left analog stick ends up controlling quite sloppily and at times would go where I didn't intend. This is a minor annoyance I suppose, considering this is an RPG and not a platformer or something.

I also noticed you can explore narrow areas it seems like you shouldn't be able to. An example would be in between rows of bushes or in between the edge of a cave and a line of bushes\trees. There are also tons of open barrels everywhere. I mention both things because exploring randomly and clicking on open barrels or randomly on bare ground is simply useless in this game. This seems to defy the tradition of RPG's hiding things in unlikely places. Simply put if it's not in a chest in this game its not an item you'll be finding.

I never saw an accessory the entire game even though there is a slot to equip them on each character.

It appears (due to spacing) as if you could have four characters and you only ever have a maximum of three.


Now that those last two thoughts are randomly strewn in...
I have to conclude that this is one of the best turn based RPG's I've played on PC period. I love RPG's and would consider myself less forgiving of them than other games. The end credits of this game give thanks to RPG Maker XP. If this game was actually made in RPG Maker XP I would consider it a shining example of being able to overcome it's often criticized engine.

If you like turn based RPG's play this game.

A delightful RPG recommended for anyone who wants to play a game that celebrates the 8-Bit era of RPGs. If you liked games like Phantasy Star II or Final Fantasy II, chances are big that you will enjoy this game.

I love it when a RPG has an actual story to tell and this game does that; through the mixed world that you'll get to explore and through the colourful inhabitants that you'll get to meet. Your party members will share many happy, sad, and wonderful things together and their bonds and personalities grow stronger during their journey.

Every place, action, and scene has an original music score that accompanies it; I found the music very fitting for every setting, and found myself humming along to some tunes hehe.

What I also liked about this game is that there's no need to grind! If you don't skip *every* battle you get into, you'll gain enough EXP to continue your journey. That is not to say that the mobs are pushovers. The random battle encounters are turn-based and require some strategic moves. Mobs can be of 3 different elements. I think the best way to handle a battle is: Discern your enemies' element and exploit their weakness, but other strategies are also needed.

For the achievement junkies out there: this game has them ;) After gamers asked the Dev to add them, he listened to them and actually implemented 40 achievements after only a short period. (This while adding achievements was not as easy as counting to three, due to the nature of the software which was used to create the game).

By the way, I can honestly say that the Dev is the most friendly, helpful and respectful developer I have ever spoken to. He really cares about this game that he created, and he genuinely listens and responds to any suggestion that gamers make. Also makes the quickest bug fixes I dare say, so don't be afraid to give this game a try :P

I wholeheartedly recommend trying out the demo if you're not sure wether you'd want to play this 8-bit inspired game :)

In mid-June, I played the same game for three consecutive evenings/nights. I thought 8-Bit Adventures was that good and I was very seldomly bored with it, so it must have done something right. Yet it was difficult to capture that feeling in a review.

I grew up playing classic NES/SNES era JRPGs like Dragon Warrior (abb. DW, my favourite) and Final Fantasy (FF). The best ones of those keep the story flowing fast and smoothly so you never get bored. In 8-Bit Adventures pacing has been done extremely well. There's no point where you don't have stuff to do. Just get over NES-style graphics and music, which by the way is rather nice.

Story is both original and classic at the same time: naturally, you are controlling the heroes who got to save the world. You get to name the heroes (mine were called Elric, Melissa and Garland) but not choose their classic classes: warrior, thief and mage.

Okay, I said it was original but it does copy some features from some of its role models: e.g. it begins with you having to save the mystical crystals that somehow control everything in the world except not anymore (surprise!) and you have to fix it. At first you only control one of the protagonists at the time but it will soon become a traditional game with a party. After a while the world opens up when you get the usual, better transportation capabilities.

That said, there was something odd about the game in the beginning. I had all those peculiarly familiar feelings, like I've played this before but still don't remember what's going to happen next. Then it subtly drops a few story and background hooks and keeps you going. Story gets more interesting the farther you get.

Gameplay is traditional JRPG with towns for talking to random people (mind you: some of which your protagonists personally know), buying stuff and healing at the inn. Goody goody as they are, they will promise to save every whatever periphery they roam at the time. Wilderness is obviously for random encounters and exploration, and dungeons are for (more) random encounters and questing. Dungeons are almost never too big to get bored and every traditional helpful trinket possible is virtually given to you to help you progress (so many potions) and keep the story going. I'm particularly looking at you, Escape Amulet (thank god for that trinket!).

Thankfully, you can save anywhere and game helpfully warns you if "something really bad is up ahead". This unanimously means a boss monster. I don't think I grinded too much but I never lost a battle in this game, not even in the weak and initially difficult beginning. Even so, some bosses and even random encounters were nasty and felt challenging nevertheless. The colour combat mechanic keeps every character useful most of the time: every character and enemy is assigned a colour that is stronger against one colour and weaker against the other, similar to rock-paper-scissors. An observant or well-guessing player may use this well to their advantage.

The main characters are likable and grow along the story that spurts out surprises, funny references and both happy and sad moments. Why, I seldomly become moved by some random NPC's storyline but heck, even that happened (small spoiler: it was the man whose kid disappeared into the woods).

Possibly the only chime I have is the rather cheesy ending and it felt forced (though oddly fitting), to use as neutral word as possible to save you from the spoilers. Overall the game was just really good. Okay, it's done with RPG Maker XP, so taking screenshots is a chore (F12 resets the game but Print Screen / paste to Paint is a start) and you are stuck with one resolution option. I played with a gamepad so I managed to avoid that keyboard thing that traditionally the keys in RPGMaker games are weird, nonrebindable and need getting used to. Also, one in-game thing to be noted: there was no log to check what you're supposed to do but I never faced this problem due to playing it through in just a couple of sessions. If I had taken more breaks, it might have been different.

To wrap up, it was good and lasted 14.5 hours / 3 evenings with 100% everything. The achievements are fun too and there are no truly grindy ones! So if you enjoyed classic DW or FF, you will certainly enjoy 8-Bit Adventures. If you didn't ever try them, I dare say this is a good JRPG to start in the genre!

This is one of the best RPG's I've played in a while in terms of pacing. I'm unsure if it was just my approach to the game but it seems like the game is just well thought out in that respect. I played the game mainlining the story and quests and occasionally chose to grind near a generously placed healing spring. I decided to run away from battles more than I normally would in most turn based RPG's since there is no pesky beastiary to deal with. I could be wrong but I also noticed the thief's steal function only seems to get you restorative items and never equipment. I quickly lost interest in stealing things from enemies which is something I usually get somewhat obsessed with in RPG's. I feel this approach simplifies this game in a very positive way; while leaving enough battle & breadth of story to keep things interesting.

The battle system is set up so that certain colored characters have an advantage vs. certain colors of monsters. This essentially means that you have to pick your skills more selectively in some battles. You can circumvent this a bit by running away from most battles that have several different colors of monsters, and finishing all or most battles where all the enemies are a single color. An example would be using strong water attacks or blue abilities in a battle with all red monsters to do extra damage.

If you are extremely frugal with money in RPG's you can always return to a certain green spire once you've completed that section to heal instead of paying for an inn. It's an odd thing to think that neither the Soldier nor the Mage has a bed they can sleep in to restore the groups health. I suppose that might make the game far too easy though.

I mention achievements quite a bit in reviews as they are something that's important to me. I really feel this game approaches them well for a RPG. If you hop onto the discussion forums the developer has pinned a topic referring to missable achievements. I found most of to be them earlier in the game which is good for an RPG. There's nothing quite like playing 100+ hours and realizing you wont be able to fully complete a game you wanted to the first time just because of the nature of what your playing. Instead this game gets that right.

There are however (as always) a few things to pick apart.

The game doesn't allow you to take a screenshot through the Steam UI. This wouldn't be as bothersome if you could change the key binding or it wasn't replaced with a load function. If you hit F12 in this game you're screwed.

I used a wireless Xbox 360 controller to play the game. I found it odd that only the left analog stick could control character movement and the D-Pad was simply not functional. I feel like a game with a retro aesthetic should always allow for the option between a left analog stick or more importantly the use of the D-Pad. The left analog stick ends up controlling quite sloppily and at times would go where I didn't intend. This is a minor annoyance I suppose, considering this is an RPG and not a platformer or something.

I also noticed you can explore narrow areas it seems like you shouldn't be able to. An example would be in between rows of bushes or in between the edge of a cave and a line of bushes\trees. There are also tons of open barrels everywhere. I mention both things because exploring randomly and clicking on open barrels or randomly on bare ground is simply useless in this game. This seems to defy the tradition of RPG's hiding things in unlikely places. Simply put if it's not in a chest in this game its not an item you'll be finding.

I never saw an accessory the entire game even though there is a slot to equip them on each character.

It appears (due to spacing) as if you could have four characters and you only ever have a maximum of three.


Now that those last two thoughts are randomly strewn in...
I have to conclude that this is one of the best turn based RPG's I've played on PC period. I love RPG's and would consider myself less forgiving of them than other games. The end credits of this game give thanks to RPG Maker XP. If this game was actually made in RPG Maker XP I would consider it a shining example of being able to overcome it's often criticized engine.

If you like turn based RPG's play this game.
