About This Game

Space Run Galaxy is the sequel to Space Run, the game from one-man studio Passtech, which seduced hundreds of thousands of players with its dynamic and original gameplay. By twisting the tower-defense game style, Space Run offered an addictive real-time spaceship construction strategy experience. Space Run Galaxy expands and refines the original’s unique gameplay, introduces a persistent online universe and takes-off again with plenty of new features!

Set 20 years after the original game, Captain Buck Mann has now set up his very own intergalactic courier service. You’re a member of Mann’s team of space runners, racing to deliver precious goods from planet to planet! Reinforce your modular ships with armaments including laser turrets, missile launchers and energy shields, while leaving enough room for your cargo. Fly fast – the quicker you arrive at your destination, the better the rewards!

Venture into the depths of space spanning an entire galaxy, with 4 solar systems and over 50 zones to visit across 100+ runs. Space Run Galaxy offers limitless gameplay, while fresh new features expand the original experience:

  • User-created contracts – Take on contracts from other players for better rewards and unlimited replayability.
  • Intergalactic player market – Trade hard-earned goods with your fellow space runners to sell off extra supplies, or snap up a tidy bargain.
  • New corporations offer riskier jobs – Carry the extra fragile cargo of XenoPrime, or risk transporting The Force’s highly dangerous weapon prototypes (which may even endanger your own ship!)
  • Fresh dangers await – Face off against brand new boss-type enemies, such as deadly pirate ships and huge space monsters…
  • Deep customization – Customize the shape and size of any ships you acquire during your adventure. There’s no restriction for what ship you use in each mission, letting you get creative with every run!

Adeon Hawkwood

Posted: 6 July
I really enjoyed the original Space Run and I think this does a good job of iterating and improving on the game play of the original.

As with the original this is essentially a tower defense game but with a lot of emphasis on micromanaging your constructions and careful activation of special abilities. A common complaint I see in reviews is that the lack of a pause option makes this overly difficult. I can't disagree with this, personally I enjoy the somewhat chaotic nature of trying to manage everything but it is a valid criticism and something to watch out for from an accessibility viewpoint.

Compared to the original this does offer a lot more in terms of replayability. Unlike the original where each run had a specific combination of ship and cargo here you can choose what ship and cargo you carry on a specific run (with the ships coming from a pre-set list and cargo gained from NPC missions, other players or personal cargo). Additionally the routes themselves are slightly different each time, with variations in what opponents you get and what angle they come from. The actual algorithm for generating the random nature is actually quite good, it varies it up but ensures that enemies don't come out of nowhere within a run by sending successive waves on the same vectors while increasing the strength.

The general changes with how items such as upgrade modules (which replace power generators), shield generators and other types of buffing modules work with connector modules as well as the restrictions on repairs are good. It takes a bit of getting used to compared to the original but overall I think it leads to a more flexible building environment. The use of a separate resource (Focus) for paying for special abilities (instead of using one currency for building an abilities) was definitely a good choice by the developers. It means you can afford to use special abilities more at the start of a run and also cannot spam them as much near the end, compared to the original game where you couldn't afford them early in a run and had more than enough hexnuts to just spam them at the end.

The multiplayer features are a nice touch with the downside that they do require an internet connection to the central server in order to benefit. The actual implementation of player contracts is very well done. Since the transporting player gets a reward from the game for carrying another player's cargo you are incentivized to help out other players as opposed to just rerunning the NPC missions or moving your own cargo around (if I move your cargo and you move mine we both end up better off than if we'd each just moved our own cargo). If I need to do a run, either for an NPC mission or just to improve my score on it I'll generally check to see if there's a player contract I can include in the same run to maximize my rewards.

I am somewhat split on the crafting system. On the one hand the limited number of each module build you can do during a run adds a strategic level to your construction and increasing your module counts gives a solid feeling of progression. On the other hand the need to gather and move crafting materials as well as get space credits for upgrades can feel a bit grindy at times. One thing I think is good is that mechanic level limits not just which modules you can build but also how many of them you can build before you need to find a higher level mechanic. For me this encourages me to move on with the game rather than hanging around farming materials in low level systems to build a ridiculous number of modules.

Overall I would say that if you enjoyed the first game then you will probably enjoy this. If you haven't played the first then I would still recommend this over the original. While the original is worth playing, the price difference is small enough that Space run Galaxy is the better deal in terms of entertainment time.

Temporary shorter review, I'm very tired.

If you liked the first game, you'll probably like this one too.
I loved the first game... except for one glaring omission in features which is missing here too.

There's one feature that many people have said they want (ever since the first game), including a paid reviewer of SRG mentions it very early in his video, and that is the ability to pause and be able to view information, to place turrets, sell them, rotate them, and use/aim their special abilities. There was a thread about this subject in the forum, several in fact, but they have been deleted. It's only a matter of hours before the same thread(s) pop up again.

The first game you could slightly mitigate this issue because the missions were so very focused and polished; each had a lovingly crafted preset enemy pattern so you could mostly memorize which types there were and exactly where they'd come from. In SRG the enemies are the same if you restart a run, but where they come from is often randomized. (which I like)

But what was a problem for me in the previous game is a big problem for me in SRG.
Many people against this feature were of the e-pen swinging Pro-MLG "git gud" 360 no-scope variety who think this game is about twitch reflexes. Others think this game is a strategy tower defense title about thoughtfull planning and adapting to incoming enemies. Check the store page tags to see what this game is labeled by the dev himself even though he refuses to acknowledge the issue.

There were two people in the thread I posted in, one of them myself, that don't just want this feature... It's something that would make the game more playable. Both of us, it seems, have been injured in the past and those injuries will affect our gaming forever. I cannot perform super fast twitch accurate mouse clicks which is why I never bought Nuclear Throne nor Enter the Gungeon as much as I'd like to play them.

If I'm having this trouble in the begining of the game with smaller ships and fewer turrets, I can't imagine the nightmare I'l be facing later with many more I'll need to micro-manage.

When the first game was out, the dev basically said he was never going to add this feature because it goes against his artistic vision for the game, claiming it would make it "too easy". Well mister dev, that's simply not true, and any false difficulty brought about by lack of this much requested feature is just due to poor control-ability not well thought balance.

All of the RTS (Real Time Strategy) and Tower Defense games I can remember playing since Command & Conquer and Warcraft 20 years ago have had this basic tactical pause feature.

I'll do a full review later once I've played more of the game.
I've been awake WAY too long, but I couldn't get to sleep so I wrote this.
If you can ignore this glaring omission, then I recommend the game. It's fun.

The initial game launch problems (due to under equipped servers) appear to have been fixed. Hooray! The game is running smoothly again.

If you liked the original Space Run, it's a safe bet you will enjoy SRG. It improves on the original in nearly every aspect. Gameplay is once again solid. The missions are difficult, yet not overly brutal (especially during the first hours of playing - but it gets beautifully hectic before too long). The multiplayer function is a nice addition, and the graphics look crisp.

The ability to run missions in the order you prefer helps make SRG's progression feel a little less linear. The crafting system is well executed, and upgrading your ship is now more tactical. Advancing in Space Run Galaxy feels more rewarding than it did in the orginal game.

The addition of ship designing is another excellent feature. You can buy pre-existing ship blueprints and build on them, and you can also design your own ships from scratch. Want to build the Millennium Falcon? A Star Destroyer? The USS Enterprise? You can, if you want to.

A tactical pause function would be a helpful choice for people who want and / or need it. I prefer not to use one, but this option would be nice for those who enjoy racing through less intense cargo runs, and would like more time to set up a firm tactical strategy. I love the frenzy of it all, and how I always feel like my current run is a just few seconds short of utter destruction, but again, a pause feature would be a beneficial option to have for those who would like to ultilize it.

Space Run Galaxy is a lot of fun to play, and it looks sharp. It certainly has that addictive "just one more run, and then I'll go to bed" aspect going for it. This game takes a compelling approach to the "tower defense" genre, and provides a good amount of replayability. When you get to the point in the game where you have a large ship and lot of different weapons, it's exciting to watch the entire screen light up with massive amounts of firepower eye candy.

Giggity.

8/10
