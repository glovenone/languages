About This Game

The Hope Lake Boarding School was abandoned long ago, when one of its teachers drowned by accident. Many years later, all of the female students began to disappear, one by one. Visit the place where it all began. Try to pick up the maniac's trail and put a stop to his crime spree.


Game Features:
- 48 locations
- 8 hidden object scenes and 26 unique minigames
- an interactive map
- a flashlight, an essential item for every detective
- two game difficulty levels

-Short Review-

+ Pros:

*Good art style

*Good atmosphere/scenery

*Interesting story

*Good gameplay length
(I got a fair 5 hours out of the game, and it felt like I was playing longer than that)


- Cons:

*Incessant music
(There was only one song playing for the entirety of the game, and it got annoying after an hour)

*More HOG scenes than puzzles

*Poorly translated HOG items
(Example: I'll be looking for a key but it's actually a wrench, etc.)

*Ending was too abrupt
(The story was a little drawn out, a lot of it was pieced together by reading documents, and it was pretty obvious to guess the culprit in the end - but the end result was rushed)


**Overall: This game was enjoyable with the eerie abandoned school in the forest with bouts of rain, and some of the puzzles were fun as well. Play the game for the atmosphere and puzzles, HOG scenes will get tiresome, however.
Definitely buy it on sale if you're unsure.

+ . + . + . + .

I saw Hope Lake on Greenlight awhile back and supported it because I am a fan of HOGs and I liked the game's concept.

You play as Detective Wells, who travels out into the coastal town of Hope Lake to get to the bottom of a series of cases involving young women who are kidnapped and murdered. Driving through the woods, Wells chases a mysterious cloaked figure towards an abandoned school with a tragic history.

It has somewhat of a noir feel to it, which also drew me to this game.

It's a pretty game with it's dark, foggy, cob-web covered backgrounds that stretch from the beach to an abandoned all-girls boarding school. The puzzles are familiar, they're not so different from classic puzzles and mini games that can be found in other HOGs. There's even a nifty gimmick where you can use a flashlight to make it easier to find hidden items and search for light switches in dark rooms.

Also, there are various photos, trinkets, and journal pages to collect that give more depth to the story, these were some of my favorite features. Also, surprisingly, there were a lot of places to explore and I strongly suggest that you use the compass to fast-travel and get around.

One of the gripes I have, in particular, is the obnoxious title music that never switches to a different track, it can make the game tedious to listen to. Also, some of the items in the HOG scenes were mistranslated, or mislabeled, which made it really tiresome to have to use the hint function for the final two or three items on the list (and waiting for the hint bar to fill up again).

And yet, I personally enjoyed this game for what it is, despite some of its flaws.

It was just fun to go through all the beautiful, and sometimes creepy, scenery and imagine how life used to be back when it was inhabited. The puzzles were relaxing and I liked the symbolism behind them, they added a unique charm to the game and gave it a little more personality.

I'll admit, it's not perfect and it may only appeal to a niche crowd, but if you're a fan of detective stories and/or HOGS, you might want to give this one a try. But, if you're uncertain of how much it's worth its merit compared to its full price, I recommend buying it on sale.

I was so looking forward to playing this, and I ended up being fairly disappointed.

There is a lot of good in the game in terms of artistic value and plot; it's exactly the sort of dark, mysterious story that I like. Some of the imagery was spot-on wonderful. Sound and voiceover work wasn't terrible comparatively speaking.

However, that said.... I cannot make a recommendation on this.

The puzzles are the usual fare for this style of game but seem to be far easier than most. I feel like puzzles should be...well, puzzling. At least a little bit. Some were also tedious to complete even though the puzzle itself was easy.

The worst thing is the rampant mislabeling of items in the hidden object scenes. There are a LOT of scenes in the game (which is great!) but there needed to be a lot more quality control before they released the English version, at least. A few randomly misnamed items or typos don't bother me in the slightest, but this was in almost every HO scene and sometimes more than once per scene. It was too much even for me, and I'm generally pretty laid-back about things like that.

Examples include wrenches labeled as keys (and keys as wrenches!) and a slingshot labeled as "forked stick" but there are far more examples and I could fill a page with them. Some are just irritating but some make it almost impossible to finish the scene without getting hints (i.e. a piece of pipe labeled as a "trumpet" is where I gave up and started hitting the hint button).

It's not an expensive game and it has a pretty long playtime compared to comparable games, but the rampant errors and the boringly simple puzzles make it a "no" for me. That said, if the type of story appeals to you and you don't mind hitting the hit button constantly (or random-clicking and hoping for the best), you may still enjoy it. This is a title that could really use the "meh maybe" option for recommendations rather than yes or no!

I was lucky enough to win this from a promotional giveaway, and I figured the least I could do was complete Hope Lake promptly for a timely review. It was exciting for me to get to dive into it so soon after its released, especially since I love hidden object games. That being said, this fortunate win has no affect on my review, and I am going in as a neutral player.

For those who don't want to delve into the details, here is a one-sentence summary: it has flaws, but is ultimately worth playing. Want to know more? Look below!

Let me start by saying that this was a surprisingly long hidden object game. Most that I play take four to five hours to complete on a first run. This one took me seven hours. On one hand, it was nice to have the game fleshed out. On the other, some of that came from running around tediously, along with some puzzles that took way too long.

The setting is an abandoned boarding school its landscape. It's an interesting location, but I admit that after a while, I wanted to move to somewhere else. This isn't the same case for me in most games confined to one area. For whatever reason, being inside the boarding school became a chore later on, as if to say, "Really? There's another three rooms to visit now?" Still, I appreciate the developers for choosing a setting like this, length aside. It brought variety to the table and set up the plot well.

Speaking of the plot, it's fairly good. Nothing revolutionary here, but it's a change from most hidden object games that have magical elements to them. Mind you, I like magical elements. I just think we could use some more hidden object games that don't rely on them for story, and this is a good example of one. The plot kept me interested enough to want to see what would happen next and how the tale would end. That's generally what I want in hidden object games, and this one succeeded there.

The characters are not dealt with in much detail. The protagonist is bland, the antagonist is Generic Evil Guy #2948, and the only other person you meet is in all of two scenes. What is especially grating about this is the complete idiocy the protagonist displays. There are so many times I had an easy solution to a puzzle, but of course no, she didn't want to do it that way. She was more interested in taking the long, difficult route that was ultimately more unrealistic than what I wanted to do. Now don't get me wrong, a lot of hidden object games have flares of that, and it's a difference between story and gameplay. I have a reasonable suspension of disbelief for this sort of thing. But Hope Lake had SO MANY instances of this that it really made me wonder how this woman ever became a detective.

We also have an unfortunate generalization of certain types of people. The villain in particular is wince-worthy. His evilness is explained away as him being insane and not getting the treatment he needed. It is such a gross understatement of how mental illnesses work that it made me quite irritated. But really, it's not trying to tell some grand tale or make a sweeping moral statement, so I'm probably looking too deeply into it. It's just worth noting that the story could have been a lot more interesting with a few touch-ups.

The graphics look good overall. There is one amusing part near the end with some silly-looking animations, but other than that, it has nice art and decent cutscenes. The music, on the other hand, is about what I expected from a hidden object game. It's the same track the entire time, and while I liked said track well enough, it's nothing special. Fortunately for me, I don't get sick of music I like. For other players, this might be a problem. Keep the mute button ready if that sort of thing makes you cross.

So how about the gameplay? Let's cut it into three parts: standard, hidden object scenes, and puzzles. The standard part is straight-forward and well executed. There is a casual and expert mode. You have a hint system if you need it, though being the stubborn donkey I am, I refused to use it. The map is nicely laid out and marks what areas have new tasks to complete. This is good because sometimes hidden object scenes will need to be revisited, and that is the only clue you have of when that happens. Hidden object scenes are easy to find, loading time is quick, and the text is formatted for easy reading. The one complaint I have is that sometimes you have to click in a very specific location to get an item to do its job. I got stuck on one door for a while before realizing I was a few pixels off the section I had to click on.

The hidden object scenes... Whoo boy. In terms of the objects being hidden well, they are. Some of the choices are clever, and there is a good mix between easier and harder items to spot. The problem comes with what the items are called and how many there are. I can't begin to count how many times you have to find a "Cup" in a scene, only for it to really be a mug instead of the cup-like object nearby. Calling a pipe a "Trumpet" really threw me off in one scene. And calling a crushed soda can a "Bottle" (or whatever the term was, but believe me, it wasn't "Can") is really misleading.

But the worst part about that? The developers messed up the names in four different scenes. In two of them, they say you need to find a "Key." Nope, turns out clicking on the wrenches in both scenes marks off "Key." In the other two, it's vice versa. Click on the keys instead of the "Wrench"es that you're asked to. Otherwise, you're stuck there. This is pretty darn inexcusable. I am hoping the developers will fix it, but if they don't, consider that a major strike against this game.

The puzzles are good for the most part. There is one puzzle I found a bit frustrating, one that I found infuriating, and all the rest were at about the level I hoped for. A few are ridiculously easy, but most have just enough difficulty to make your mind work without getting irritated.

Let me talk about the book puzzle for a moment. The book puzzle is rearranging books to fit into a certain space. This is not a bad puzzle idea. What IS bad is that the books are awkward to move around and put down, meaning you'll spend tons of time fumbling with the dumb things. You can only move them if there is nothing already stacked on them, which means you waste your life dragging the top ones off just to get to the bottom tome. There are several of these books, so you have a lot to juggle. The rancid icing on the stale cake is that it is difficult to tell whether you are taking up as much space with the books as you need to. I'm sure some people will find it far easier than I did, but I wouldn't be nearly as annoyed at it as I am now if it had better controls. Having to constantly drag out ten books or so and try to put them on shelves just so I could start over again got old. It got old really fast. At least there's a skip option if anyone wants to use it.

Hope Lake does have trading cards and achievements for those that care about that sort of thing. The achievements, all thirty of them, are easy to get. You'll probably wind up with all of them from one playthrough of the game. Heck, I'm not even sure if you can miss them. I liked earning them well enough. The trading cards are silly in terms of what they chose to make cards out of, but they're nice if you want them.

Oh right, I didn't mention the voice acting. It's flat and easily forgotten. There, now I've mentioned the voice acting. To the game's credit, there aren't too many spelling or grammar errors, though some snuck in. Proofreading would help.

Despite all the negatives I listed, I ultimately enjoyed playing Hope Lake and was glad I got to. That is why I am giving it a thumbs up. However, it is far from perfect. Hidden object game fans might find it worth picking up on sale. I recommend that people new to the genre look elsewhere for a better first impression, then perhaps come back to it later.
