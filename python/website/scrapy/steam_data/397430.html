About This Game

Play Zoombinis and guide the little blue creatures through fun and increasingly challenging puzzles on their way to a new home.

Zoombinis is a re-creation of the classic, award-winning 1990's puzzle game Logical Journey of the Zoombinis. The small blue creatures, each with distinct personalities and appearances, are escaping imprisonment by the evil Bloats who have taken over the Zoombinis' home. Players take on the role of guide and help the Zoombinis reach the safety of Zoombiniville.

Use logic, analyze data, spot patterns and hone problem solving skills. Players face a series of increasingly difficult challenges that stand between the Zoombinis and freedom. Twelve perilous puzzles each with 4 levels of increasing difficulty, a great story, and many fun and memorable characters make for an engaging and challenging experience.

We challenge you to get all the Zoombinis to safety!

Overview
Zoombinis is, for I'm assuming just about everyone who is planning on playing this a game, a memory form their childhood. It was initially released back in 1996 as a disc for the PC, and has now been recently remastered for modern OS's with this version. So, just how does this new edition shape up when compared to the original, and on its own merits? Let's take a look at the presentation, gameplay, and... sadly, bugs, as well as my final thoughts, to see.

Presentation
In relation to the original, there is no comparison in the visuals department. This reboot of Zoombinis is beautiful, mostly due to the scenes painted in the background on every stage. This remaster truly realizes the artistic aesthetic that the original was never able to, and it really adds to the game by making each level a scenic view for you to rest your eyes upon. As for the sound (as far as I know), they took it all from the original release of the Logical Journey of the Zoombinis, and that's what really packs the largest punch of nostalgia as you play through the game; the outdated and yet still fresh sound effects as well as the shoddy recording of the wonderful narrator. Pair the beautiful level artwork and nostalgic audio with the new design of the Zoombinis themselves, and you have the cute, and yet still logical, journey that Zoombinis always was. As far as the presentation goes, I'd say this version did quite well.

Gameplay
This is where the real meat of Zoombinis is. All the same classic puzzles from the original game can be found here, from the Pizza Trolls to Mudball Wall, from the Stone Cold Caves to Bubblewonder Abyss. The puzzles themselves are just as challenging and head scratch inducing as before, which I was happy to see because I am much older than when I first played Zoombinis. Get to a high enough difficulty and it doesn't matter how smart you are, you're going to have to put a fair amount of effort in to solving the puzzles on each and every stage. Despite the gameplay being admittedly quite repetitive at times, the pacing of how often you replay a level is done very well, just enough to keep you from knowing exactly what to do on any given level, thus forcing you to think logically on each stage. Now that you've heard me sing my praises on every aspect of this remaster thus far, I regretfully say that it's not all perfect. That's why I'm writing a section on...

(The specifics of the below section will likely not make sense to you unless you have already encountered these bugs, or if you have gotten to very very hard difficulty in the Mountains of Despair).

Bugs... WHY?
I would like to prefix this by saying that there has not been a patch in 6 months, despite constant outcry from the players. I did recently get word from one of the devs that he would check up on what I'm assuming is the team that has the job/choice to patch this game as needed, but I'm not sure if he meant that a patch was in the works, or if he is separated from the patching team and has no idea if a patch is actually being made. With that said, let's get on with the bugs I'm aware of. Just two. Sadly, one of them is near game breaking, or rather, takes the logic out of a particular puzzle. Both of them occur on the crystal mines stage. The minor bug is that the reflecting mirrors used on this levelâs puzzles do not place sometimes, the only way to fix this being uprooting all of the mirrors you've already placed and resetting them. Nothing more than a minor, yet persistent inconvenience. The near game breaking bug is that, on very very hard difficulty in the Mountains of Despair (activates shortly after you've guided 200 Zoombinis to Zoombiniville), the crystal mines levelâs puzzle is essentially just plain broken as far as I can tell. You can get Zoombinis across, but it comes down to pure guesswork, rather than any sort of logical process. The way I've been able to get around this is through another bug which functions as an exploit, making you only have to match the second Zoombinis in the mirror to get both across, but this feels quite cheap, and doesn't always work. This bug has caused me to stop playing the game, and I only plan to return to it on rare occasions when I have nothing else to do or play. Other than this there are some other bugs being reported, but what I've stated is all I've encountered.

Final Thoughts
When all is considered, this remaster of Zoombinis does a great job, as long as you don't plan on doing a full playthrough. The further you get the more bugs you will encounter, until they end up wearing you down; at least that's what they did to me. It's a shame, but not a large enough one to take all of the fun out of the experience. It was still a journey that allowed me to relive some dear childhood memories, which I thank the devs for. I'd absolutely recommend this game to anyone who has memories of it in their childhood, just be aware that completing it may not be realistic in its current state. Based on Zoombinis' own merits, itâs just passable, and then a bit more due to the overbearing nostalgia factor.

Personal Rating: 7.5/10

Note: Should a patch that fixes my qualms come out, I will heavily edit this review.

I've played some strange MS-DOS games in my lifetime, and Zoombinis was always one of those obcure oddities that will always have a soft spot in my heart. I remember playing this game on the computers in my third grade classroom whenever it was recess on a rainy day. Zoombinis a puzzle strategy game where you must guide a heard of blue innocent creatures called Zoombinis to a safe haven called Zoombiniville (great name). Each puzzle is a point-and-click as you spend the game thinking about how to apporach each puzzle without putting any of your Zoombinis in harms way. Think of it as a kiddie watered-down version of Lemmings without the stress of keeping track of every single member in your heard. Each puzzle gets more complex as your progress and after carrying one heard all the way to their safe haven you must direct the next group down that same path. To avoid repetition and boredom the game has branching paths where you can choose the sets of puzzles you want to complete and this makes solving these puzzles more worthwhile. The game is also nice enough to increase the difficulty by the time you've done at least one or two runs of a certain path. There's even a practice mode to help train you on the puzzles that give you some trouble. The game's graphics have also been enhanced from the original game's format. I like how some of backdrops looks like paintbrush strokes. This game can help anyone improve their critical thinking skills if they spend enough time learning the ropes. $15 isn't a bad price, but if possible, wait until the Steam sales for a price cut. The game provides enough otherworldly, but relaxing atmosphere that makes brings out that springtime joy. If you haven't played this game before, try it out! You might like it.

Childhood classic of mine, but the puzzles are for all ages. Most of the stages are puzzles based on the properties of your Zoombinis--hair, eyes, nose, and feet--and you have to get as many of them through a stage based on the "rules" of the stage. Other stages include variations on Mastermind (Pizza Pass), Battleship (Mudball Wall), and Frogger (Titanic Tattooed Toads). There are four difficulty levels for each stage, and as you move more Zoombinis through, the stages get harder. Great game with endless hours of fun, and lots of replayability.

Oh, and the Zoombinis are cute too.
