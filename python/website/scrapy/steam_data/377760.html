About This Game

There is a Fox in the ballroom!

Or so the whispers from the other ballroom dancers suggest. Can the Flock trap the Fox before it is too late? Or will the Fox systematically eat all the other dancers before they even realize he is in their midst?

Based on the puzzle game Fox & Geese, Fox & Flock is a dark, modernized version of Queen Victoria's favorite classic!

Storyline, local multiplayer, and single player modes supported.

This game is an odd combination of stylish visuals, cute presentation, lackluster content, and minor glitches that makes it hard to chose yes or no. I'm going with an overall thumbs down because I am not very enthusiastic about it in the end, but with the low price and its "exactly what it says on the tin" presentation makes it worth buying if you're interested and want to give it a try anyway. You don't have a lot to lose if you want to try it.

Actually, if you don't mind the diluted blood stains (as seen in screenshots, nothing worse) I'd even give a thumbs up recommendation as a family game, since I could see my 8 year old self having a lot of fun with it, and there's no bad language.

I played through everything a few times while waiting for card drops. Here's my pros and cons:

Positives:
+ Interesting art
+ Pretty good narration
+ Board games are fun!
+ Story Mode/Tutorial/Local Co-op all work
+ Steam cards sell for a good amount (for now), you could probably break even if you sell them all.

Negatives:
-Can't skip the sloooow narration in story mode
-Making moves is slow too
-A little goofy, +/- depending on your tastes
-Very little content. Fair for the price, but not a lot to do.
-Story mode glitches sometimes and the character art freezes, annoying but not unplayable
-No settings, I'd have liked to change the volume in-game
-Local co-op didn't hold my interest. Kids will probably have a good time playing story and against each other.

So it wasn't engaging or very entertaining, but nothing searingly wrong with it. Maybe it could have benefitted trying to add new gameplay gimmicks, adding new types of players besides fox & flock (like letting the lion move uniquely perhaps, larger board, idk). If you think it looks cute and want to try it, I'd say go for it, support the devs. Ain't expensive. But if you're feeling wishy washy, it's entirely skipable.

Old board game with story tutorial.

While you will probably enjoy playing this old game more against an actual human on an actual board (or perhaps via Internet and the Tabletop Simulator), this game teaches anyone (and thanks to full voice-overs, that means children, too) the basics of this game. There's the "tutorial", and then there's a short campaign with poetic narration; it took me half an hour to complete.

If you have no one to play with, you can challenge the AI to a quick game. Its difficulty ranges from "dolt" to "aristocrat". I played a classic game as fox, and the aristocratic AI not only happily repeats the same moves forever (which should ordinarily be a stalemate), it also did some fairly dumb mistakes in the endgame that gave a certain victory away. So there seems room for improvement.

Fox & Flock is a fun way to learn a classic game, and priced accordingly. Recommended.

The game rules are fairly simply. I am not going to try to explain them in detail here, it would be a waste of your time but if you are a fan of games like GO, Chess and Checkers you will be on familiar ground. If you are not a checkers pro, fear not; there is a quick tutorial game that teaches everything you need to know about the rules in about three moves.

Throughout the Story mode you play as both the Fox and the Flock. Each round is tied together by voice acted narrative poetry. Which is good, the voice acting and poetry, both. The Story is to-the-point but interesting, not that it takes much to push an hour of gameplay. The Music is excellent, as it is time-appropriate Classical that really sets the mood. The orchestrated score contrasts nicely with the subtle blood stains that accumulate each time a fox eats (jumps) a member of the Flock. The simple hand drawn art style also lends well to the overall Ball-Room Era theme of F&F. It really looks like you are playing the game on the page of an old weathered book or something of the like.

The real challenge is to be had in the Quick-Game mode. The Quick Game mode is the place to go if you are looking to crank up the difficulty or play a two player match, locally. The Story Mode was quite short, to be sure. None the less, I found Fox and Flock rather enjoyable... and the story mode even has a twist ending... which was more than what I was expecting. Well worth the two Dollar Price tag.

I'm a huge fan of board games for my computer. Anything I can fire up quickly, learn how to master, and just keep for those days when my more heavy duty games aren't keeping my interest is always something I'm willing to add to my list.

I've never played the game that inspired Fox & Flock so I made sure I played close attention to the tutorial. This is a very quick game to learn and I have a special love for things that come from the 1800s so color me already interested.

The music is very relaxing and puts you in the ballroom mood while you'll be very entertained with the charming place markers of the flock and foxes. (Go foxes, uhm color me biased!)

There is a bit of a storyline during your adventures if you choose not to indulge in quick games and there is a lovely little narration with voice acting that I did enjoy. It was something that most people overlook in games like this so I enjoyed it. (Fur standing on end... Thank you for keeping in character the entire time, too. People really do overlook things like this.)

There's no flashy graphics to give you a migraine. There's no complicated keystrokes. This is a game you can unwind with and include within your big titles just to have to wind down with...of course until the AI figures out what your game is and you're left cursing at the screen. Nothing painful unless we count your self-inflicted pain when that turkey in a ballgown has blocked your path to the noms. Multiple times. Accept your fate, turkey. Just accept your fate.

I guess that would be my gripe. Much like tic-tac-toe, there's more of a "let's go for deadlock" than a winning combination. There weren't too many risks the AI was willing to make in order to win, making it so difficult that I had to frequently restart...

Which leads to one HUGELY GLARING DETAIL. This game has a major bug. If dialogue fails to intiate between matches to tell you if you're foxes or flocks(es haha rhyme) or to announce who has won, you will freeze and you will not proceed. The game will not save what has happened. This has occurred a few times to me so far. Now it's not frustrating as I'm fine to replay the level. No harm, no foul! (Or should we say fowl? haha flock, haha animals, oh I'm way too tickled with this one!) But that is a little annoying so I hope that's fixed soon. This COULD deter people from downloading because if you spent a long time on a particularly difficult match, you're not going to want to replay that again.

Bugs aside, this game really fulfills a lot of things that I do love and I find myself selfishly saying to download the game despite the buggyness and just play through it because it really is fun to waste an hour just moving those animal pegs around.

Also, a fox does deserve a good meal, after all. How dare they look down upon the foxes? You all taste good with ketchup and ale in the end!

Fox and Flock is a lovely adaptation of a classic strategy board game that has been around for centuries. This version plants the core game itself inside a dark tale rich with comedy and tragedy ~ uninvited Guests at a ball.

I greatly enjoyed the production value of the game; the artwork was quite well done and the choice of baroque period music fits very well with the story.. My only minor complaint was that the perspective of the board at times makes it difficult to see where pieces are or where they are not, and it would have been nice if the board had been rotated on a slight angle to prevent this.

All in all I Highly recommend this to strategy lovers and anyone else who might appreciate something a little more casual than your typical action-oriented game.
