9/10 - DarkZero

From the creators of the hit webcomic Brawl in the Family comes the delightfully musical adventure game Tadpole Treble.

As Baton, you’ll literally be “playing along” with the game’s music as you dodge past predators and obstacles who take the place of the notes for that level’s song. Avoid enemies and hit percussive notes on the beat to accumulate points and fill your “treble meter” to blast through anything in your path.

Tadpole Treble features 30 original music compositions, 13 levels, and 5 boss battles. In addition to the story mode, the game includes bonus content such as unlockable music, a large bestiary, game commentaries, and online leaderboards for every level. Best of all is the robust Composition Mode which lets players create their own original levels and music.

Early Access Review
Posted: 8 July
Disclaimer: Backed the kickstarter, fairly long-time reader of Brawl in the Family. So I might be a bit biased!

One of my favourite things about videogames generally is the music, and while some of it took a little while to grow on me, the more I've played this game the more I love its soundtrack. I guess that's the nature of game music - it really helps to hear it in context, at least at first. And since this is a game where you have to really think about the music to play the levels well, that probably goes double. I've had a soft spot for Chiptune Lagoon ever since it featured in the demo, but for me there's also a great stretch of level music for the levels from Snowfall Lake to Thunder Creek. Then again, ask any fan of this game and they'd probably pick out their own faves. I dunno. Music. It's good stuff.

The gameplay is solid, with a good difficulty curve. I'm not generally a fan of rhythm games, but the decision to make it a game about dodging the notes (for the most part) rather than hitting them allows for a lot more freedom for the player - and creativity for the level designer. The gameplay varies nicely, with some levels focusing on gimmicks and others just focusing more on hitting you with increasingly complex songs. There are even boss battles every third level or so! The main game is quite short, but there's plenty of replayability if you want to gun for challenges or high scores - which also adds to the good feel of the difficulty curve. The game is easy to learn, reasonable to beat (heck, I've beaten it, and that's saying something) and pretty darn hard to master.

The art style is simple, cute and effective, and the game's writing has some nice humour, as you might expect from Matthew Taranto - make sure to chat to Etude whenever you get the chance :).

As it stands, the game's only real weakness is with Composer mode. It's a brilliant idea - a toolset that allows you to write your own tunes and, in the process, design your own levels for the game. The way that you transfer your saved levels to other players - QR codes - seems really clunky though, and if you try writing a long or high-tempo piece of music you quickly exceed the game's ability to generate QR codes for sharing. Steam Workshop integration would be a huge deal if it could be implemented.

Other than that, the game's a pretty great deal. I don't often write Steam reviews, but Tadpole Treble feels like it could use some more publicity, so I'm poking my head over the parapet for it.

Early Access Review
Posted: 19 June
Tadpole Treble puts a fun twist on the rhythm game genre. Typically, in rhythm games, the goal is simply to time you're button press correctly. Games like Guitar Hero, Rockband, and Rhythm Heaven are great examples of this. The other way these games usually play is having you move around. That encompasses your DDRs and Just Dances. Tadpole Treble doesn't fall into either of those two categories.

In every level of Tadpole Treble, your goal is to dodge the notes as you swim down the river. You can ascend and descend the scale to avoid danger, nab collectables/health, or simply to position yourself. It's an incredibly fun way to play, and every stage has it's own way to keep things fresh and interesting. A few worth noting are travelling in reverse, changing the theme to 8-bit, and giving you a tadpole partner who serenades you as you swim. These gimmicks give every level its own unique feeling.

The aesthetics to this game aren't overly complicated, but they're pleasant to the eye. Colors are well used to contribute towards the mood of each area. Everything you need to know is quickly communicated to you, with enough time to react accordingly.

The music is excellent. If you've seen Matthew Taranto's work on Brawl In The Family, or the Ace Attorney Musical, then you'd definitely see this coming. I don't know much about his brother Miechal, but I'm sure they both contributed greatly to the final product. The songs have very nice rhythm, and match their areas perfectly. My personal favorite is Midnight Bayou, as it has vocals by Matthew (He has the voice of an angel).

This game is definitely worth purchasing, even in the early access phase.

Early Access Review

Product received for free
FULL VIDEO REVIEW:
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=F-uEsP1a1Nc
PROS:
  • Frankly some of the best music I’ve heard in an indie game. Each song is catchy, fun and makes the soundtrack alone worth the price of the game.
  • A ton of hidden objectives and unlockables that make the game have a surprising amount of replayability. Additional game modes and developer commentary for each level just to name a few.
  • Free demo of the entire composition mode available at: http://bitfinitygames.com/composition-mode-demo/

CONS:
  • Composition mode songs are shared through QR code which may make it virtually impossible for those without a webcam or smart phone to share songs in addition to being a general pain overall.
  • You can only save up to custom 12 songs locally, meaning it is impossible to build up a big library of user created music.

SUMMARY:
Tadpole Treble is the epitome of what an aesthetic and music focused game should be; simple gameplay made fun by good game design and an outstanding soundtrack. Each world encompasses a completely different genre and theme that throws a new challenge at you keeping each level fresh even though the gameplay is simplistic. Backwards levels, giant turtles, angry piranhas; I’m hard pressed to find even a single level that is the same as another. But what really separates the levels is the music. Almost every song in the game is of some of the best written music I have seen in an indie game. Most of the songs are extremely memorable, so much so that I’ve actually downloaded a few of my favorites for personal listening. My only real issue is that the composition mode could be more user friendly, but gameplay wise for 10$ the game is definitely worth a play.
