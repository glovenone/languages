About This Game



Money Talks. Silence Pays. Prepare to Make a Killing. When assassins from Agent 47's contract agency, The ICA, are eliminated in a series of hits, it seems a larger, more powerful agency has entered the fray. Sensing he may be a target, 47 travels to America, and prepares to make a killing.
  • 'Blood Money' system: the cleaner the 'hit' the more money you receive which can be spent on bribing witnesses and police to reduce your notoriety, weapon customisation, specialist equipment and information.
  • Customisable weapons: modify Agent 47's custom weapons in a variety of ways including sound, recoil, rate of fire, damage, reload speed, accuracy and zoom.
  • Strong narrative: who is wiping out the ICA and what is their motivation?
  • New engine: the world of the assassin has never been so interactive or looked so good!
  • New gameplay techniques: including disarm, distraction, accidents, body disposal, human shield and decoy weapons.
  • New control and camera system: Agent 47 now moves independently of the camera.
  • New moves: Agent 47 can now climb, hide, scale ledges and automatically pass low obstacles.
  • Improved AI: guards will follow blood trails, investigate suspicious items and behaviour. New pathfinder engine provides improved tracking and movement with realistic enemy behaviour and interaction.
  • Soundtrack by BAFTA-winning composer Jesper Kyd.

This game is the reason I couldn't really like Hitman: Absolution. I propose my thesis: this game is really fricking awesome.

Exhibit A: there is a level that is just you in your hideout. You are not some kind of traveling salesman hitman, peddling your stabby-shooty wares door to door in a car you probably stole from a developmentally disabled man. You have your own little underground hideaway where you can try out your sniper rifle on pop bottles, tell your pet canary he is a pretty bird, murder rats, and sing Ave Maria without the menu screen music reminding you of your tin ear.

Exhibit B: Diana! I mentioned in my Absolution review that Diana failed to live up to my expectations in that game, so here's why she's better in this. First, she's her own character. She's busy doing her own thing, scheming, organizing, conspiring with the powers that be to create the bizarreness and awesomeness of that final level. She isn't just stealing some girl and then waiting to be horribly murdered on the toilet. Second, she's mysterious. She's always in shadow, you never see her face, you never know what she's planning. I think maybe we were supposed to instantly feel wrong in Absolution where you see her face on her file, but it's just thrown down in a picture on a file in a cutscene while another character is talking, so it doesn't really have much weight. Also, the only time we see her in Absolution she is naked. So there's that. Finally, she's a really good handler. She's calm and detailed and you can tell she and 47 work well together.

Exhibit C: the levels are fun and colorful. Absolution was kind of grey and dreary at times with lots of industrial levels in factories or labs, and even when it was somewhere fun like a barber shop or a tiki hotel everything is sun-faded or blown the heck up. In Blood Money the levels are set in abandoned amusement parks, over-the-top stereotypical suburbia, a steamboat paddling down the river. Everything is very distinct and self-contained.

Exhibit D: In this game the cutscenes tell one story, a kind of serious one, but because the cutscene world of the two plot-framing doofuses and the level world of the doofus who got murdered by a scantily clad woman at a Christmas part are so very distinct it doesn't feel like the tone is just very confused.

I can't really think of any more exhibits at the moment, so here's a list of fun things to do in Blood Money.

1) Drop a piano on a bird-lady.
2) Throw a cake on the floor, then pick it up again and use it to blend in the kitchen.
3) Run around in a bath robe blowing up cook stoves.
4) Snipe a dude off a Ferris Wheel.
