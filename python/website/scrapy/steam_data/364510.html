Part I + Eternity Port

The special edition of Transcendence includes two full adventures:

The Stars of the Pilgrim

The Stars of the Pilgrim is Part I of the Domina & Oracus saga, in which you play the titular Pilgrim. You must journey from the safe environs of Starton Eridani, through the wondrous yet treacherous systems of Human Space, all the way to the edge of the alien quarantine zone.

This adventure, containing over 24 hours of content, will be continued in Part II: The Vault of the Galaxy, currently scheduled for release in 2016 (sold separately).

Eternity Port

With a fast ship and the skills to fly it, you earn a living running dangerous missions for any sovereign who will hire you. But when a digital hyperintelligence threatens the entirety of Human Space, the only hope lies with the reclusive cyber-citizens of Eternity Port.

About This Game

Transcendence is an open-world, single-player game of space combat and exploration.

In the year 2419, humanity has colonized hundreds of star systems, strung like beads on the network of stargates built by a dead alien race. But now a god-like hyperintelligence is mentally compelling people to travel to the Galactic Core, and the Ancient Races of the galaxy have decided to quarantine all of Human Space. Alone in your ship, you must acquire whatever powers you can, and hope to survive long enough to unravel a secret that threatens the entire galaxy.

  • Adapt your strategy to survive the journey through dozens of randomly generated star systems. Take advantage of scavenged weapons and devices. No two games are ever alike, so adaptation equals survival.
  • Blast your way through the swarming tactics of Anarchist battlepods; steel yourself against the EMP attack of the Dwarg masters; and pray to Domina that you can withstand the terrifying archcannon of the Phobos dreadnought. Transcendence includes over a hundred different kinds of enemy ships and stations, each with distinct abilities.
  • Loot wreckage to obtain more powerful weapons, armor, and shields, or purchase them at dozens of different friendly stations. Earn unique high-tech weapons by completing missions for the Commonwealth Fleet or by helping the Corporate Hierarchy with their research.
  • Earn money by escorting the giant freighters of the Korolov Corporation; sell exotic ores extracted from asteroid fields with seismic charges; compete for glory at the gladiatorial games of the Battle Arena Maximus; or get an introduction to the Black Market and sell illicit goods for profit.

Continuous Development

Transcendence is a living game, constantly being improved through core updates and DLCs. The next major release will focus on Part II of the Pilgrim's story, in which we visit the rest of the galaxy, and the realm of the Ancient Races. But we also plan several minor releases before then. For Steam, in particular, we plan on supporting the Workshop and Achievements.

Visit the Ministry of Records for a list of reported bugs and future enhancements.

You're here to figure out whether this game is worth playing.

I'm here to tell that it is. Here's the tl;dr: you don't have to believe me, you can try the base game (which contains a good 75% of the content for the main storyline) for free, to your heart's content, while also enjoying any modifications available for it, which can be accessed through the community forums, and all future updates. Yeah, for free -- no, not free2play, but free as in free to have fun.

What will you be buying?

So, Transcendence has a long term development plan. New versions are going to be released with no end in sight, as far as I know. Transcendence, the base engine, allows you to play adventures in the Transcendence universe. This base engine is free.

The primary adventure (yes, there are others, well explained by this infographic) is that of a character who hears the voice of a hyper-intelligence calling them to the centre of the universe, and listens to the voices in their head -- this adventure is called "Domina and Oracus", and is also free. Currently, Part 1 of the story has been released, along with a content expansion pack called Corporate Command, but the expansion pack is not free.

There is another official adventure that has been released, and this one is called "Eternity Port". It is about a character who is left for dead in the depths of space after an attack on their ship, only to be saved by a selfish (I saved you, so *obviously* you owe me) entity representing the virtual citizens of Eternity Port. They want you to do something for them, starting off the adventure. This adventure isn't free. If you want to see how Eternity Port fits into the lore of the Transcendence universe, check out the infographic I mentioned earlier.

You're buying Corporate Command (the expansion content for the main adventure), together with Eternity Port (the other official adventure); in other words, you're buying all the content that exists so far, and costs money.

How are basic things like the graphics, UI and sound design?

The graphics are pretty, as you can see from screen shots. Starry space backgrounds, mixed with the foreground composed of ships, planets and stations with textures that fit in perfectly. Even though this is an independently developed game, it doesn't skimp out on graphics by pretending to be "retro", or trying to get into the good books of old-schoolers with trashy UI design. The game's graphics are modern, and suit the gameplay.

The game has a soundtrack that's original and beautiful (I am humming the music that starts you off on an adventure right now), and has sound effects that aren't archaic. While allowing for some mouse interaction in menus, the UI is mostly designed to take keyboard input, but isn't painful to use because of that decision, and most gamers will feel at home right away: the arrow keys move your ships around in interesting ways, and major buttons like the spacebar or control keys handle the firing of weapons. Keyboard shortcuts usually make sense etymologically (e.g. "D" for Dock, "J" for jump), and there is an excellent help infographic that is accessible any time in the game that lists out all the keyboard shortcuts.

So, space adventure game. What will I actually be doing?

The storylines, depending on which adventure you are playing, give some background as to why you are setting off in space, and then some occasional guiding structure throughout. What do you do in between? Well, it's up to you.

You can trade: there are minerals to be mined, the ore sold or forged into useful armour, which can be sold for an even higher price. Or, you can go about simply buying low and selling high, making a living for yourself that allows you to buy fuel and the ammunition needed to stay safe. Maybe you decide you're interested in also running a side business in the black market. Well, as long as you have a murky sense of morality, the connections to get started, and the know how to hide your goods from the authorities, maybe you'll be successful.

You can sign up with the Commonwealth Forces, or some of the other regional powers (depending on which adventure you are playing), and rise up through the ranks. You can meditate in the halls of the Sisters of Domina's temples. Maybe you can figure out what this voice in your head wants. Maybe you are interested in donating items to the Sisters' cause, and helping the needy that you meet in beaten down habitat stations that are nothing more than cargo containers strung together.

Maybe you free slaves held in living stasis inside coffins by the Sung Slavers, as you attempt to make sure you aren't squashed by them. Maybe you get involved in story of the Sung and the Huari, the Aztec/Maya-esque space culture that was decimated by the Sung's relentless ruthlessness. Maybe some zoanthrope buddies tag along because you beat their masters, and thus are next most powerful thing they know to follow. Maybe you willingly submit yourself to the nasty experiments of humans who have genetically modified themselves to such a degree that they might as well be aliens.

Maybe you actually bump into aliens, and other creepy-crawlies. Maybe. It's your call. The game is detailed enough to waste hours of your time, with nice flavour text to spice up every piece of the universe. Bring your imagination along, and go for a ride.

What about mods?

Transcendence is heavily modifiable, and has an active community of modders: everything in the universe can be described and programmed with XML files and a custom LISP variant (TransLisp). The C++ guts of Transcendence are completely open as well, and available for viewing and participation on GitHub. Modding documentation is sparse, but community participation makes up for it.

So, if you're into modifying games, Transcendence will give you lots to do. If you like playing with other people's modifications, Transcendence won't disappoint you there either. Popular mods include a complete revamp of the mining system, AI based modifications that allow the game world to be even more dynamic by randomly generating conflict and rebuilding stations, content packs that add new weapons or features such as the ability to craft new weapons from components, and so on.

I can't help but quote something really relevant from one of the prominent modders, pixelfck, on why he loves the game:

#Discussions_QuoteBlock_Author
The storyline is quite deep, with promise for even more; There is a lot of content (devices, armour, misc-items), many of which hook back into the background universe; Its open source (Â© George Moromisato); I can mod it (step 1: initial notion); The modding system has a quite powerful scripting available (step 2: the promise); All vanilla content has been made with the modding options available to me, no hard-coded 'magic' (step 3: the realisation of full potential);

Final words.

My recommendation is that you try out the free content. Get yourself into the community and modifications, if that interests you. If you love it all, and want more, buy the Steam version that contains all content, or buy the content piecemeal as you want to using Kronosaur's "Multiverse" infrastructure. Like pixelfck, start dreaming, and think about how you can contribute too, because I want to play your mod!

An excellent game! I've played this since the 0.95 version came out, and it's been great ever since. The base game is actually free and will take about 1-2 weeks to complete. The Corporate Command and Eternity Port expansions add lots more ships, systems, enemies, missions and plot-lines to follow, and are well worth the 8 bucks. This is like Escape Velocity, but better. And a lot more difficult. One caveat though - you can't switch ships, so you're stuck with whatever you picked at the beginning. There's a mod called "Playership Drones" I remember installing some time ago, it allowed you to buy new ships and even use them as wingmen.

This is really an open-world sandbox, where you can make your own decisions and do whatever you feel like doing. You can escort freighters, mine asteroids, work for the military, engage in piracy, smuggle drugs, even become a gladiator. Be warned though, your actions and choices will always have consequences both good and bad. Gameplay can be very challenging at times, especially the combat which requires good reflexes. Pick the Wolfen gunship and you'll see what I mean. There can be quite a steep learning curve cliff, and you'll get blown up. A lot. You can resurrect and pick up from where you left off, with no penalties apart from a lowered high score. On my first play-through I died about 25 times.

Some tips: Learn the trade routes, and how to profit from the black market. Don't hesitate to use exploits like sitting on a planet (they're invulnerable) while waiting for your shields to recharge, or luring enemies to a friendly station to be killed. When about to die, press (I>1). You can attack salvager nomads (big brown ships) and smugglers (gray transports with small escorts) with no penalties. They are easy to defeat and carry good loot. Don't attack the Huari. And definitely don't feed the Teraton fabricator radioactive waste :) Also, remember to have fun and don't get discouraged by the amount of times you get blown up. It's very rewarding when you complete the game at last. Just resurrect and try again. And again.

10/10 would die again.
