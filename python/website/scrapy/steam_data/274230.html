About This Game

Ronin is stylish a turn-based action platformer following the exploits of a vengeful heroine determined to strike down five prominent figures of a powerful corporation. Move with precision by meticulously planning out each silent step, soaring leap, and devastating swing of your blade from the shadows as you seek your revenge.

Turn-Based Action: Carefully plot your movements to setup the perfect strike before retreating back into the shadows or boldly confronting your opposition head-on with your katana.

Decisive Strikes: Dispatch all that oppose you with your lethal blade and razor wire or avoid their attacks with a holographic replicator and grappling hook to find a more advantageous position.

Path to Vengeance: Battle through over fifteen complex missions as you stalk and cut down five closely guarded figures within a secretive conglomerate.

I recommend the game with some qualifiers. First off, I received the game on sale for around $4.00 and for that price I think the game is worth it.

The gameplay is interesting, but a bit confused at what it wants to be at times. The turn based combat is fun, it's a bit like a puzzle, but the game is much easier at times if you play this as a stealth game. In larger rooms I found myself just hopping around like a crazy person until I was shot or on occasion I would catch a break and be able to kill a guy without getting shot a moment after. If you approach the situation with more stealth it becomes much easier, but the game wasn't really built for a purely stealthy experience so it's not as smooth as you'd want it to be playing that way. I found the game turning into a largely trial and error affair. Sometimes that's fine, but other times you'll be half way through a level and you'll get stuck in a situation like my example above where there's no real solution except jumping around hoping that you can get lucky. If you die, you're back at the beginning of the level.

Nothing really stands out in other aspects of the game (graphics, story, music) either positive or negative for me, so the game largely stands on its gameplay. It's good enough for me to recommend if you can get it on sale.

I really want to like Ronin. Its concept is unique and its presentation polished. But its gameplay leaves something to be desired. Its controls lack precision, and communication with the player is imperfect at best.

One of the ubiquitous "helpful hints" the game gives you continuously is "The jump arc is a lie," which is really the sort of thing that ought to be established up front and in detail rather than as a one-line random hint. Sometimes the game will not let you project a jump arc onto a surface, obviously preventing you from jumping at all. Other times it will let you project an arc, but when you attempt the jump you will be interrupted by the corner of a platform or an enemy.

In hindsight these limitations make sense, but in practice they are enormously frustrating, and representative of the sort of difficulty, rather than challenge, that the desigers of Ronin programmed into the game. Because it is obviously intentional -- this is not a game that is rough-edged because of oversight or laziness. Ronin is a game that is rough-edged because its developers wanted it to be rough-edged.

By the time you are ten or so levels into the game, you will probably begin to understand how to do what you want to do in Ronin perfectly every time, using the tools the designers have given you. But if you have the will to get that far you are a more patient gamer than me. Ronin is a whole lot of stick and not nearly enough carrot. It's not that I hated the game -- I just don't care enough about it to keep trying to play it successfully.

This game started off well. It introduced me to mechanics and techniques that felt intuitive and fresh, despite being heavily inspired by the likes of Gunpoint. It put forward a nice challenge and trial and error gameplay where I still felt like I was learning. The plot seemed interesting, with targets that had personality and a backstory. Enemies were varied and interesting to approach. I enjoyed grappling most of all, swinging out of a line of sight remained thrilling throughout. Even the bonus objectives were fun to achieve and lead to good rewards that assisted with later levels.

However, my opinion on this game has drastically changed as of late due to one level in particular. The Officer level is actually the worst thing I've had to experience in a video game. I have not been so infuriated in my life. From my hours, I could guess that about 50% of those are from this level. Every once in a while I boot this game up to try again and leave more frustrated than before. Allow me to explain.

This level starts off normally, with clever platforming required to dispose of three or four guards. The second checkpoint is where this game loses its "innovative" mechanics and its "gripping" gameplay. Featuring upwards of ten guards, this courtyard has almost no grappling points besides the buildings on either side. This whole middle section is prone to being covered in bullet lines, with at least four being double turn shots. The amount of times I have been trapped in this part is almost impossible to count. It also features the "boss" of the level, the previously mentioned Officer. His attacks are telegraphed with a red line as per usual and yet he can only travel a set distance. For this reason, he may only travel 3/4 of the line before stopping and becoming vulnerable. This leads to a large amount of guess work as to whether you will die or not. Allow me to clarify, I have killed the Officer multiple times, opening up the exit to me. Yet, the bonus objectives prompt me to kill all the enemies, something I have yet to achieve. Even worse, this game has a Lockdown counter that starts when out of range or if a specific enemy is present. While this may not be such a big deal normally, the other bonus objective is not to trigger Lockdown. At this point I am seriously considering never playing this game again, and I feel anyone willing to unlock all the skill points will feel the same way after trying to finish this level.

Final Verdict:
The game is sound intially but later levels reveal its shortcomings in an infuriating way. People who fly through it without needing these abilities/skill points may have more fun, but completionists will likely find it difficult to perservere.

Game: RONIN
Genre: 2D Turn-based Action Game
Developer: Tomasz Wacławek
Publisher: Devolver Digital
Copy supplied by Steam

RONIN is a 2D turn-based action game developed by Tomasz Wacławek and published by Devolver Digital. This game looks very similar to Gunpoint, one of my favorite indie games of all time, so I was instantly intrigued. However, this is a very different beast, and that isn't necessarily a good thing. This is the first Devolver Digital published game that I am not going to recommend.

STORY
Kill Bill meets Hotline Miami. In order to get revenge, the main character goes on a rampage to kill all those who wronged her. Simple, straight-forward, and (unfortunately) not the main focus of the game.

GRAPHICS
The 2D art style is pretty good, the animations are polished, and the level design is very well done. Unfortunately, there is little variety to the environments and you will face the same types of enemies over and over again.

GAMEPLAY
Similarly to Gunpoint, you move around mainly by holding a button to jump in the air and go on walls. However, the focus is on fighting rather than stealthing your way through, and when you enter combat, time stops and you must carefully plan your next move depending on where the enemies are going to shoot. The more hits you get, the more ability points you unlock to perform special actions like throwing your katana or teleporting.
There is a serious issue, and that's the controls: they are very awkward. Aiming your jump is very buggy, and you can't always hit the enemies even though you are standing right next to them.

CHALLENGE
The game is not really challenging, as much as it is frustrating: because of the afore metioned buggy controls, dying is usually the fault of the lack of escapes or the inability to kill an enemy. I died over 60 times in my walkthrough, which is even more than the times I died in Dark Souls III. But I have to say that finally clearing a room full of hostiles feels very rewarding and satisfying.

SOUND
A mediocre soundtrack and lackluster effects don't help raising the enjoyment that this game might give you.

REPLAYABILITY
In order to achieve 100% completion, you have to finish each level killing every enemy and avoiding a lockdown. Accomplishing this tasks will give you ability points to spend in the skill tree. Other than that, there is little to no reason for coming back. There aren't even achievements that aren't story related!

Overall, the game had great potential, but its awkward controls and frustratingly punishing difficulty stop it from being enjoyable. I can't recommend it in good faith.

Story: 6.5
Graphics: 7.5
Gameplay: 7
Challenge: 8
Sound: 6.5
Replayability: 5

ENJOYMENT: 5.5
FRUSTRATION: 9
