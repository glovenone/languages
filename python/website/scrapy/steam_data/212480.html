Sir Plus Income
( 9.0 hrs on record )
Posted: 7 July
The stages are vibrant, dynamic, and well-themed. Each course is based off of classic Sega worlds and areas, with fitting music to match. I felt so damn pleased when I started up a race in Shibuya and heard "Let Mom Sleep" come on. I mean, I was almost sold right then.
The vehicle swap mechanic is horrendously fun and introduces interesting aspects, particularly with some shortcuts in the stages causing you to swap vehicles and adapt your playing immediately. Or run face-first into a wall because you forgot how planes work.
The game has clear progression and unlocks, with levels for characters unlocking mod kits that tweak their stats, in case you love a character but find their playstyle impossible to deal with. It's also great if you love seeing bars fill up and being told you did something good and earned a reward, and don't even care what the reward is.
It runs smoothly and looks fantastically stylized. Also, it has local multiplayer, which, while common on console games and their ports, is not something I ever take for granted. That is a selling point.

But, to be honest and a little glurgey, it also brings back a lot of good memories. Regardless of how one feels about some of their mistakes, Sega has been around a long time, and has touched on a lot of innovative and fun ideas and characters in their years of making games. Some of them were expanded upon, some of them have collected dust for a long time. But this game addresses them. Cynically, I guess one could say it's just milking their old franchises for cash, but with the care and detail put into the characters and stages, it feels different, different from the random, purely visual cameos in some more forgettable games. It feels like this is someone's way of saying that even if these worlds and series never get a new game, even if they only exist in rerelease compilations and cameos, they haven't been forgotten. Sega has fans on the inside.
