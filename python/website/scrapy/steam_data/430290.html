About This Game

Some say you’re only as old as you feel. Shmups Skill Test says you’re only as old as you shoot! Shmups Skill Test is a clever way to evaluate your shmup skills in the form of a "gamer age". Challenge and unlock shoot-’em-up-based minigames in a gauntlet of shmup nostalgia goodness. When it’s over, you’ll be given a score and gamer age. The younger your gamer age, the better! Bring up to three friends along for the ride and feel young (or old) again together.

Features:

  • Keep on your toes with quick-hitting skill challenges
  • Practice individual challenges or go for the randomized test
  • Receive a strict score and “gamer age” based on shooting, dodging, judgement ability and more
  • 1-4 local player support
  • Replay support
  • Rockin’ soundtrack sometimes bordering on the bizarre
  • Includes Steam Achievements, Leaderboards, and Cloud saves

Are you ready to know your gamer age?

A Historical Shmup

Crafted so that anyone can pick it up and enjoy it, Shmups Skill Test features Triangle Service CEO Toshiaki Fujino's nihilistic and surreal humour and parodies guaranteed to put a smile on the face of any fan of the shmup genre. The Steam version is based on the Shmups Skill Test included in the arcade game Shooting Love 2007.

The arcade version is a long running hit, still to be found in arcades across Japan, despite many of its peers from the same time (2007) having been long removed. Its arcade sequel, included in Game Center Love. ~Plus Pengo!~, is also still going strong.

Furthermore, despite having been developed as an “examination” or “training” game, the versus play proved exciting enough to create the demand for competitions being held, something of a rarity for a shooting game. The Steam version takes this one step further, adding new tests (games) and the ability to play with up to 4 players. You can also focus on practice with the newly added ability to choose specific tests.

Of course, no shooting game would be complete without music. “Mr. Shooting Love Sound”, NAOTO returns but he's not alone. With him, we have two guests in the form of Go Sato (of Raiden series fame) and WASi303 (from the Psyvariar series).

Ever since the dawn of humanity has man been attempting to quantify his skill in order to prove superiority over one another. In ancient times primitive metrics were used such as who can hold a large rock over their head for the longest or who killed the most mammoths that day. As time progressed we became more materialistic and began using possessions as an indicator of wealth and power, leading to concepts such as class. Soon the need for validation became so great, ways to test your reaction times, your handwriting and even your personality type emerged.

These tests all pailed in comparison to one, however. A test surfaced that could not only determine a person’s skill, but one could argue their suitability as a mate. I predict within two years all major companies will use this test as screening for interviews, Grindr will have a space to input your score on your profile and the worst performers will receive disability benefits. Soon, you will be asked for your Shmup Skill Test rating.

Originally appearing on the arcade shoot ‘em up compilation Shooting Love. 2007, Shmup Skill Test is a collection of one shot shoot ‘em up mini games all of which are designed to test difference facets of the shoot ‘em up player’s skillset. For example the manoeuvring games test your ability to dodge incoming projectiles, aiming games determine how precise you are, and the shooting games test your ability to mash a button. You partake in tests to gauge your proficiency at shoot ‘em ups, you play 5 or so games and your scores in each are averaged out to an “gamer age”. The younger the better.

Shmup Skill Test is yet another straight port of an older game directly to Steam, save for the addition of achievements. The fact that it looks and sounds exactly like a Playstation 1 game is no longer surprising, but rather disappointing. There is also some of the seldom seen vertical letterboxing on offer here that blights the game, bloating what could have been a small window on the screen into a monstrosity that fills 4/5ths of the game with filler. It is possible to rotate the display in settings for vertical monitor setups and stretch it to fullscreen, but I don’t propping my monitor sideways against the wall to try it.

Turning to actual gameplay for a second, the majority of the mini games available are fun and varied as they can be while still being able to claim they are a shoot ‘em up. My personal favourite of the lot, “8-bit”, harks back to games like Galaga and is by far the most fleshed out of the games. The two “Tanks” games are surprisingly satisfying for a game the object of which is the endless mowing down of artillery in pursuit of points. Also on offer are some good old fashioned, tough as nails bullet hell levels for the masochists among you. Most of the games on offer are very enjoyable, if slightly vague with their instructions. “Nice Bomb!” is a bad culprit for this, where your only instruction is to make a “Nice Bomb”, whatever that is. Which if searched for on the internet will most likely land you on some sort of government watchlist. I have played this game for a while and I still have no idea how to complete it properly.

However, like any box of Quality Street, there are some weird green ones that get left in the bottom of the tin. There are two games (“Hold Out” and “When It Glows”) where once you know the solution, they become trivial to complete and require very little mechanical skill. Like a riddle you already know the answer to, it loses its impact the second time around. Somehow these still come up in tests, which is sort of like being able to write “ROSEBUD” on your GCSE english exam paper for 10 extra marks. The least enjoyable of the games by far is “Empty Can”, where you have to knock tin cans into the recycling, in theory at least. In reality it is nigh on impossible to get the cans to go where you want them to due to them being able to collide with each other. It is quite fitting that this game takes place in space, because much like the universe in its infinite vastness, your actions ultimately have no bearing on the outcome and you are going to die anyway.

Shmup Skill Test reminds me of the smaller games Twitch streamers play while their actual game is loading in the background so they can get their eye in. Fun enough in 5 minute bursts but not much more. Much like a one night stand there is no lasting relationship here. At £4 currently on the Steam store you can’t go too far wrong, but do not go into it expecting the next Ikaruga because it’s not here.

(Note: Owned the game originally on the Xbox360 via the Shooting Love 200X collection. Game is basically the same as it is there minus the TriZeal Remix, Exzeal and Minus Zero titles)

The Shump's skill test is a odd little duck of a game, essentially a mix between the Brain Training series for the DS and more classic arcade japanese shooters, this game pits you up against a variety of different challenges, testing aim, speed, dodging ability and several other factors to generate a final score and 'Shooting Age', the lower ages you geat meaning the better you have done.

Style-wise it is a little basic at times, this isnt a bad thing as it means that you can focus on what is going on easier but the lack of flair does hurt a little. Another complaint is the scoring mechanic, for those who want to focus on leaderboard chasing, the game is a little too random with some games giving out much more point-wise than others meaning you could be playing for quite a bit before you reach the ideal run.

These complains though are quite minor in the long run as the controls work brilliantly, setting up controllers is easy thanks to clear instructions on setting up buttons and the whole game runs smoothly at all times, optimized well for even the more lower end of computers. In addition to the normal test mode, you can also try out most of the different games in a variety of score-rush modes, as well as having a go at them with 2-4 players side by side.

This is more for shump fans though, those wanting a more full story-led game would be better off elsewhere but for the low price and amount of content, its well worth picking up.

As "William Shakesman" mentioned, Shmups Skills Test is essentially a Brain Age Shmup.

There are a collection of minigames that you can unlock that test a few core areas that are required for competitive Bullet Hell Shmup games. The list of skills tested are as follows:

  • Bullet Dodging
  • Obstacle Dodging
  • Accuracy
  • Progression of Enemies

They also have a few more areas, like *coolness* or *judging ability* but these can be distilled down to luck or endurance respectively. You might have also seen the Trash Can with Cans screenshot above. When I first saw that screenshot it reminded me of the 90's with those 100CD packs of games they use to sell at Electronics Boutique. Kind of like "My First Shmup" kind of thing.

Let me tell you, that Trash Can/Soda Can level is legit. It perfectly encapsulates precision and mindfulness of your shots, while having a degree of physics built into the level. To the point if you just shoot like a madman, you'll make the level harder to dodge as well as scoring less altogether because you aren't putting the cans in the trash.

It looks really damn bad in a screenshot, but it's quite effective.

Now for the only thing that is actually bad about the game. The music.

Dear lord.

The music. It's so bad.

One track is a simple arcade techno beat mixed with the opera. I.E. Bass Loop + Fat Lady Singing.

Another track sounds like an 8-bit synthesized whoopee cushion.

There is *one* good track and that is when they are copying the Space Invaders music.

Anyway, the game seems to employ a direct translation, so be prepared to fail initially as the small amount of info they give you during a new mission that is sprung upon you, doesn't really instruct you with anything meaningful.

Ultimately, I think Shmups Skills Test succeeds at it's goal of testing individual areas that are required from a range of Shmups.

I can't think of many shmups that have made me laugh out loud, but this one sure did (in a good way)! If you like zany action, fun Engrish, and shmup parody, this is the game for you. There is plenty here for shmup veterans to enjoy, as new stages or even music tracks can contain references to timeless classics. Plus, you get to feel good about keeping the earth clean by throwing empty cans in the trash.

As for the actual gameplay, the controls could not be any simpler. I used a 360 arcade stick, and it was recognized flawlessly, and all gameplay that I have been seen can be done with just the directional input and only one button. Some challenges have auto-fire implemented, but others require you to button mash and seem to be a test of how quickly you can do so. In addition to shooting challenges, there are also various dodging challenges, bombing challenges, and power-up and medal collecting challenges. Failure is often more hilarious than success.

Clearly this game will provide nostalgia for shmup veterans, especially since some bosses, challenges, etc, will remind them of specific other games. But I also think this game is great for a shmup newbie who just wants to test their skills, or anyone who likes to try to decrease their age as much as possible, like in Brain Age. Don't expect to play long sessions of this, but for the price it is definitely worth it!

Warioware meets Brain Age all in a shmup. One second you're blasting an army of tanks, then you're dodging through some really aggressively spinning scenery, then you're... shooting cans into a recycle bin to save the Earth?

Complete about a half dozen of these challenges and you'll register a score and get an insulting shmup age of 57 or something, then you get back in and knock out a different assortment of games.

It's not a hugely long-lasting affair. The games are certainly nice in the one-and-done quick change format, but they aren't compelling. It fits perfectly as a niche shmup that you can knock out a full session of in 10-15 minutes, but it's certainly not a game you play seriously.

Ever since the dawn of humanity has man been attempting to quantify his skill in order to prove superiority over one another. In ancient times primitive metrics were used such as who can hold a large rock over their head for the longest or who killed the most mammoths that day. As time progressed we became more materialistic and began using possessions as an indicator of wealth and power, leading to concepts such as class. Soon the need for validation became so great, ways to test your reaction times, your handwriting and even your personality type emerged.

These tests all pailed in comparison to one, however. A test surfaced that could not only determine a person’s skill, but one could argue their suitability as a mate. I predict within two years all major companies will use this test as screening for interviews, Grindr will have a space to input your score on your profile and the worst performers will receive disability benefits. Soon, you will be asked for your Shmup Skill Test rating.

Originally appearing on the arcade shoot ‘em up compilation Shooting Love. 2007, Shmup Skill Test is a collection of one shot shoot ‘em up mini games all of which are designed to test difference facets of the shoot ‘em up player’s skillset. For example the manoeuvring games test your ability to dodge incoming projectiles, aiming games determine how precise you are, and the shooting games test your ability to mash a button. You partake in tests to gauge your proficiency at shoot ‘em ups, you play 5 or so games and your scores in each are averaged out to an “gamer age”. The younger the better.

Shmup Skill Test is yet another straight port of an older game directly to Steam, save for the addition of achievements. The fact that it looks and sounds exactly like a Playstation 1 game is no longer surprising, but rather disappointing. There is also some of the seldom seen vertical letterboxing on offer here that blights the game, bloating what could have been a small window on the screen into a monstrosity that fills 4/5ths of the game with filler. It is possible to rotate the display in settings for vertical monitor setups and stretch it to fullscreen, but I don’t propping my monitor sideways against the wall to try it.

Turning to actual gameplay for a second, the majority of the mini games available are fun and varied as they can be while still being able to claim they are a shoot ‘em up. My personal favourite of the lot, “8-bit”, harks back to games like Galaga and is by far the most fleshed out of the games. The two “Tanks” games are surprisingly satisfying for a game the object of which is the endless mowing down of artillery in pursuit of points. Also on offer are some good old fashioned, tough as nails bullet hell levels for the masochists among you. Most of the games on offer are very enjoyable, if slightly vague with their instructions. “Nice Bomb!” is a bad culprit for this, where your only instruction is to make a “Nice Bomb”, whatever that is. Which if searched for on the internet will most likely land you on some sort of government watchlist. I have played this game for a while and I still have no idea how to complete it properly.

However, like any box of Quality Street, there are some weird green ones that get left in the bottom of the tin. There are two games (“Hold Out” and “When It Glows”) where once you know the solution, they become trivial to complete and require very little mechanical skill. Like a riddle you already know the answer to, it loses its impact the second time around. Somehow these still come up in tests, which is sort of like being able to write “ROSEBUD” on your GCSE english exam paper for 10 extra marks. The least enjoyable of the games by far is “Empty Can”, where you have to knock tin cans into the recycling, in theory at least. In reality it is nigh on impossible to get the cans to go where you want them to due to them being able to collide with each other. It is quite fitting that this game takes place in space, because much like the universe in its infinite vastness, your actions ultimately have no bearing on the outcome and you are going to die anyway.

Shmup Skill Test reminds me of the smaller games Twitch streamers play while their actual game is loading in the background so they can get their eye in. Fun enough in 5 minute bursts but not much more. Much like a one night stand there is no lasting relationship here. At £4 currently on the Steam store you can’t go too far wrong, but do not go into it expecting the next Ikaruga because it’s not here.
