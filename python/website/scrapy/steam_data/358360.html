About This Game

Raptor set a new standard for PC shooters. Its smooth VGA graphics and heart-pounding music will leave you breathless.

Discover secrets, find bonuses, and battle boss ships at the end of every wave. With each ship and ground target you destroy, you'll earn more cash to increase your ship's technology for the next wave. You'll be driven to kill and destroy until it's in your blood!

Features


Big-Screen Shooting: Ultra-detailed, smooth-scrolling VGA graphics and explosive cinematics.

Hardcore Harold: Up to 16 ship upgrades can be bought from Harold's Emporium between waves.

Get Ready, Rookie: 27 killer levels, each with at least one unique boss ship to destroy.

Zero to Hero: Four insane skill levels - higher skill levels introduce new opponents!

Controller Support: Partial Xbox 360 Controller support.

Posted: 7 April
Given the amount of janky ♥♥♥ ports of old games floating around on Steam these days, it's refreshing to discover that Raptor: Call of the Shadows seems plagued with none of the usual complications. No window-sizing issues, no compatibility problems, not even the slightest hint of input lag (I'm looking at you, Age of Empires II) - I've read of some people having a harder time with the 2015 Edition, but from my experience, the Classic Edition is flawless from a functionality standpoint.

As for the game itself - well, video games have come a long way in 22 years, and you won't find a much better barometer for exactly how far than Raptor. The levels are ugly - they can get incredibily cluttered to the point where it's hard to tell what is even happening, the colour palette gets very dark and murky in places, sometimes to the point where enemies are difficult to distinguish from the background scenery, and both of these things combined with some pretty poor colour contrast means that the inclusion of the occasional night time mission was a very, very bad idea.

The upgrade system is fairly extensive, but ultimately pointless. You can afford arguably the best gun in the game (which costs less than two-tenths of the most expensive gun available), the Auto-Track Mini-Gun, before the end of the first sector, which is to the games difficulty curve what Ivan Milat was to European backpackers.

But ultimately, nitpicking a 20 year old game aside, if you enjoyed the game way back when and are the nostalgic type, there is enough here to recommend, especially for 5 bucks.

This is how Steam ports of old games should be done.

10/10

It's you against the world. Thousands of planes, helicopters, tanks, boats, and stationary guns, and nothing standing between them and you except an arsenal of ridiculous weapons and your own twitch skill. Elite mode is basically suicide, and your keyboard will not survive. It's played with arrow keys + ctrl-alt-space, which is a little clunky but you get used to it.

Raptor survives the test of time, released more than 20 years ago, and the polish that Apogee put into it really shows. The pixellated graphics are skilfully-rendered and it's very clear what's happening, which is definitely a good thing since the game is basically a bullet hell. Skillfully using the situational weapons you buy and find is critical to keeping down the flood of enemies so that you aren't overwhelmed. Every enemy and building destroyed yields money at the end of the mission that can be used to fix up your top-of-the-line fighter with shields, radar, conventional missiles, auto-tracking miniguns, experimental EMP waves, and eventually my favorite weapon, the laser that fires twin beams of azure fire.

The levels initially follow a simple progression: Fight off a few enemies, pick up a weapon, use it to maul your way past the rest of the level while trying to avoid damage and make your end-of-level repair bill as low as you can. Eventually you'll fight a boss (Known to the devs as a "Battle Cow," strangely), which is usually an immense airship bristling with weapons. If things get too hectic, there's a pricey but room-clearing Megabomb just a tap of the spacebar away. It destroys everything onscreen (Dealing heavy damage to bosses) but takes a few seconds to go off (As it floats to the middle of the screen) adding a bit of strategic depth on timing and being able to expect yourself to get shitcanned a few seconds before it actually happens. A bit later in the game, some of the original bosses make appearances as mini-bosses which always calls for a hectic all-out DPS-dump. Take too long and you'll be fighting off the mini-boss at the same time as the level's standard enemies. Later, the bosses will manifest as humungous, multi-turreted ground-based fortresses, which have immense health bars and require a good amount of timing to cut down.

A lot of the weapons are designed to be worthless in certain situations, picking the right one is always the key to survival. A couple of the weapons are total duds, but the small handful of "always active" weapons are always a safe bet. Save up for the auto-tracking minigun and prepare for a ♥♥♥♥ing good time because its DPS is huge and it shreds stuff to your sides, which most weapons can't do, quite useful if you've only got a pinprick of health left and a giant boss is obliteraing the airspace directly in front of it.

It's not nostalgia, it's just damn good: Raptor is tough as nails and the harder difficulties will probably beat your ♥♥♥. Do yourself a favor and pick this up.

GOD, I LOVE THIS GAME! Brings back so many memories... The only thing is, for some reason I haven't yet figured out, it resets my progress after the final stage of Bravo Sector. Other than that, it's every gamer's dream!

A word of caution for all: This game was developed before screen rotation was introduced, so be careful not to press Fire (Ctrl), Weapon Select (Alt) and movement (Arrow key) all at the same time. If you make this mistake, as I did, the screen will rotate on you and ruin your game. Some windows setups have the option of locking/disabling screen rotation, but the best (and smart) thing to do is save your game after every mission. This will save you alot of headaches.
