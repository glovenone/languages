The story alone is reason enough to get this, but there's plenty more to it.

I won't say too much about the story because it's best to just experience it, but suffice to say it's a Stephen-King-esque horror/mystery story about a lake with a mystical effect on creative works, a dark presence within it, and a writer fighting (and writing) to set things right. It's set in a small town in the Pacific Northwest (as someone who lives in a small town in the Pacific Northwest, the backdrop felt well-captured and realistic) with a wide cast of interesting characters and unexpectedly beautiful environments (every now and then, you're fighting the forces of darkness and come upon a lookout tower or something and you go up, turn on the local radio, stare out at the mountains, and everything is completely and inexplicably at peace, and then you reload and get back to shooting things).

Alan Wake features a few sets of collectibles. The most significant are the manuscript pages - these, without giving too much away, help fill in the details of what's really going on and can even give you information about what's about to happen and what to be prepared for. There are also 100 coffee thermoses scattered through the game, which are a brilliant and hilarious choice of collectible for a writer in the Pacific Northwest and lead to great moments where you sprint through a horde of ghost zombies, grab a thermos, and sprint back to the light. Coffee. It's that important.

The game is formatted as if it were a TV miniseries, with six main 'episodes' and two post-game specials. It works quite well for the purpose of the narrative, and each episode in turn is divided into segments for replay purposes, which is great for tracking down those pesky missing collectibles.

Now, it's not a perfect game. The combat, while the central mechanic of fighting off enemies with both light and gun is unique, gets repetitive because the enemies mostly work the same way throughout the game (though the fact that the flare gun is the most powerful weapon in the game is beautiful in its own right). The voice acting is apparently not great (I played with the game sound off and can vouch that it's plenty gripping without the audio, so if you don't want your internal voice for the characters ruined, that might be worth doing). The over-the-shoulder camera can be a bit wonky at times (though the flashlight generally means you have a built-in sight). But it's absolutely worth it.

It's dark. It's brilliant. I can't recommend it highly enough.

As a life-long fan of the survival horror/psychological thriller genres of games, as well as David Lynch's Twin Peaks, I found Alan Wake to be extremely refreshing, primarily in the complexity and uniqueness of its storyline, combat system and seemingly ambiguous mystery. The unsettling environment, malevolent atmosphere and directives given to the player by the "creator" of Alan's dream, that almost remind one of a "dream god," makes this a game you will want to keep playing in order to unfold the mystery. This has become one of my favorite games due to both its deflection from relying on a pure combat system of brutality and gore, and relying more on the player unfolding the mysterious plot through difficult tasks while encountering sinister forces unlike you've seen before. Alan Waks is a perfect example that "monsters" do not need to be grotesque or disfigured to be terrifying. Moreover, Alan Wake requires thought to understand and its plot is not spoon-fed to the player. Conclusively, this game is fantastic and a must play for anyone who likes horror/survival horror games and/or psychological thrillers.

(Full review follows below)

  • Game Name: Alan Wake
  • Original Release: 2012

  • My Overall Grade: A-

REVIEW
âNightmares exist outside of logicâ â Stephen King

Alan Wake is an extremely enjoyable third person action-adventure/survival game with a very story-driven experience and the theme of a psychological thriller. It is a very cinematic game, with a lot of dialogue and cutscenes. So if you need constant action, you wonât find it here; but if you enjoy games that feel a lot like movies-you-control, this is a great pickup.

The backbone of this game is the excellent narrative, which is well-executed and well-acted. The main story arc is mysterious, intriguing, and suspenseful. And the voice acting is quite convincing, which is critical for a game this heavily story-driven. But my personal favorite aspect of Alan Wake is the development of the various characters. Video games sometimes have great stories, but often they lack the depth of character development one may find in other mediums of storytelling, like books, TV, or movies. The way this game was produced, though, it is easy to feel the connection with the characters and immerse yourself in their personalities.

The graphics, animations, effects, and soundscape all work in concert to create an extremely engaging atmosphere that reinforces the strong narrative. The gameworld looks great and you can feel the heavy atmosphere of the creepy little village that acts as the storyâs setting. This vivid and intense setting allows the entirety of the game to be entertaining; even slow plot points remain amusing due to the immersive ambiance. If you are into immersion, Alan Wake is definitely the kind of game you want to play with headphones on, lights off, and no interruptions.

But looking past the engaging narrative and atmosphere, you will find that the actual gameplay is also pretty decent. The controls are mostly solid, albeit a little clunkyâ but it is the kind of clunky that is purposefully imposed to adhere to the paradigm of the scary/survival genre. And there is an interesting dynamic involving shadows and illumination from which all action/combat is derived and that affords the game a charming uniqueness. There are also a lot of collectibles that provide further lore/backstory and add an additional dynamic to the total gameplay. Be warned however: the collectibles system can break the immersion of the game at times because youâll have to sidetrack to find them during intense moments in the storyline.

In short, Alan Wake is a great experience. It may not appeal to those who want constant action from their games, but for those who enjoy cinematic, narrative-driven games, then this will be great to consider trying if you havenât yet. The dark psychological-thriller theme is easy to find intriguing and the gameplay, while not innovative, has a unique charm that is gratifying to experience.
