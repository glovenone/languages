About This Game

Shadowcrypt is a challenging arcade game of tactical sword & shield combat. Quick reflexes, timing, and keen observation of enemies are key to survive the deadly catacomb! You play as a hero who is the last of an ancient order sworn to battle corrupted sorcerers. Pick up weapon upgrades along the way, find the Necromancer and defeat his horde of undead warriors through a series of labyrinthine rooms and torch-lit corridors.

Features

  • Engaging and visceral melee combat that keeps you on your toes.
  • Authentic hand-drawn pixels and animation.
  • 70+ non-linear rooms.
  • Weapon upgrades and elemental enchantments.
  • Old-school gameplay that will challenge your skill & reflexes.

Shadowcrypt is a semi-challening combat focused platformer that takes obvious influence from classics like Castelvania and Zelda. Unfortunately it is missing a few things that would make me recommend it. The lack of content for the price is the biggest issue here and if the game was a quarter of the price then I would perhaps look at the game differently.

Like arcade games of old, death will put you back to the beginning of the game. The dungeon is not procedurely generated, so death will mean you will be playing through the same dungeon over and over. This isn't a problem if the level you are forced to repeat is enjoyable. Unfortunately in Shadowcrypt, many rooms are empty and encounters are separated through backtracking which becomes tedious very quickly. Most of my playtime was spent jumping through empty rooms that look almost identical. More encounters and some new designs to make areas fresh would have worked wonders.

Combat is quite enjoyable, certain enemies require patience and if you try to hack and slash your way through everything then you will most likely die. Again though, lack of content means that the combat isn't as good as it could have been. Swords and shields are great, but to limit combat to only these weapons with some slight variations based on damage is just poor. I would have loved a staff with limited usage or a slow and heavy battle axe to crush certain enemies under their shields. The lack of content just pops up time and time again.

Repetitive encounters, environments and a focus on backtracking just make Shadowcrypt more of a chore than it should be. At the moment it is too expensive for the content and you will be able to find much more enjoyable experiences for less money. If you are a fan of classic arcade platformers then maybe put this one in your wishlist and wait for a massive sale.

There's two big reasons, two different types of people who play games. There are people who play video games to unwind, because they had a hard day at work and they want to let loose and not have to think, people who want to have fun without the worry of being stuck or presented with obstacles.

Then there are those who love punishment, people who live to play games like Journey to Silius, I Wanna Be The Guy or the more recent Dark Souls series. Diehards who don't want their hand held and want to be beaten mercilessly over and over until they finally learn what they've been doing wrong this whole time.

Shadowcrypt is absolutely a game for the latter, and I'm proud to say I'm one of these deranged masochists. This is a game that presents players with a big question; are you in touch with your roots?

As the torches of the title screen begin to flicker around the ominous skull decorating the entryway the haunting chants of the intro music beckon you to the dank and dark depths of the most brutal arcade platformer I've had the pleasure of playing.

Shadowcrypt is all about the combat. The bleak and claustrophobic level designs of the game only lend to your unbridled focus on the task at hand. All of your attention will be drawn to the beautifully crafted sprites and their precise animations as you slash and bash your way to the heart of the Crypts in pursuit of the wicked Necromancer.

Only 4 rooms into the game my mysterious cowled hero crumples to the floor dead..again..for the third time. "I-I'll just try this again later.." I whimper to myself, tucking my gamepad away and walking away to make a sandwich to hopefully forget about this brutal game that just made me it's slave. Not even a minute later, I toss my condiments to the ground and declare "Eff that, I can do it!" and find myself rushing back to the computer where I give these beckoning crypts another go. I feel that same kind of determined spirit I held in the Famicom days.

The gameplay with it's visceral combat and the insane amount of concentration needed exceed my expectations, it's almost a little overwhelming. I've heard people call other games "the Dark Souls of 2D gaming" before many times in the past but Shadowcrypt is probably the first title I've played to actually match and truly capture the punishing and calculated approach to combat Dark Souls has, where every little move you make has a huge impact and consequence.

Even more-so are the striking similarities to what seems to be the developer's hugest influence, Zelda II, from the candle finding objective needed to progress to the highly memorable and iconic shield-wielding Iron Knuckle battles that the combat here seems to inspire from heavily. It's the refreshing change of pace I've been waiting for since the golden-era of games.

While I can see a lot of less patient players outright quitting in frustration the moment they die and are reduced to the starting 3-7dmg sword prior to a strong foe, the more retro-minded of players will remember this sort of challenge from our earlier and most important days of gaming. We'll stick with it and experience something exceedingly rewarding. You'll die often, but everytime you start again you'll feel better, stronger, and make it a little bit further. And when you do finally beat those rooms? Holy crap, it feels good.

Though the system of losing your only reliable weapon will feel almost too brutal for newcomers, it's what gives Shadowcrypt the refined gameplay it has. To give a sense of real distress in every confrontation and to make death that much more consequential.

The punishing nature is extremely familiar to the first Castlevania titles where dying would lose you your chained whip, sticking you back with the puny leather alternative in the midst of a tough level. Konami in general were masters of this formula, as my other favorite series Gradius was very similar in nature with deaths reverting you back to the almost useless pea-shooter.

That fear of losing what was making me do better in those classics caused a much higher level of awareness and addiction to the game. To succeed in Shadowcrypt you will need to adapt and get into the motions of the combat. You will block, dodge, and bash your way through enemies, you will learn to stand face-to-face with your most feared adversaries. Eventually, things just start to 'click' with the player.

The game is certainly short and decidedly so. It requires a level of focus that if prolonged would just cause player fatigue on unmanageable levels. The short, sweet, and very to-the-point approach gives a lot of breathing room for the hectic and chaotic nature of the player's progression.

Shadowcrypt is composed of one lengthy stage or "level" roughly the size of the original Castlevania, but the amount of practice and dedication required in order to pass even a small section will have most players retrying for days before they're able to conquer nearly all of the evils within.

You will get no breaks nor will you receive any hand-holding, it's a game by the hardcore for the hardcore. Unfortunately that may drive the less strong-willed of players away, but it's that determination in presenting the game how the creator intends which is extremely admirable and is exactly what draws me to the titles I have the fondest memories of.

When video games are developed by people who genuinely love the culture and obviously grew up with a passion for them it's always a breath of fresh air, and Shadowcrypt is the kind of game I and probably many others have wanted to play since the late 80s as kids.

there is very little content. every sword is identical in usage except for the randomly varying damage ranges and the possibility for 1 of 3 elemental enchants that have a 10% chance (RNG? in a game where "quick reflex and timing are key"?) to trigger a minor effect. there are also 3 shields in the game with chances to trigger minor bonuses. that is all of the "gear" in the game.

you will quickly grow tired of going through the exact same basic rooms time and time again, fighting the same limited collection of simple enemies.

Shadowcrypt is niche to say the least. So 8-bit at its core that it takes either a very old person or someone with an aquired taste for oldschool gamedesign to really appreciate the perfect simplicity of it.

Here is the deal: fight through a bunch of rooms and corridors and kick some evil necromancer in the skull. Basically, it is a sword & sorcerey short story turned game. The game itself is about the same level as first and second generation NES titles in terms of complexity. Possible actions include: attacking, jumping, shield-bashing, hanging on ledges and spear-throwing. You use this to overcome a clone-army of undead skeleton warriors and wizards, spike pits and pointy sticks on the ceiling.

Sounds rather sparse, right? It sure is, but being so limited also makes the game feel pretty "on point" in delivering that oldschool flavor that I still crave today, 20 years after these kind of games were dying out. You see, most games today seem to cram everything and then some into their gameplay - so much that they feel overwhelming and I end up growing tired of a game that only does everything and tries to please as many types of gamers as possible. Shadowcrypt on the other hand has super-limited scope (survive till the end, kill necromancer) and the few mechanics implemented in this game feel natural and fitting for the task at hand (basically, they help you kill stuff). There are no hidden secrets or complex mechanics, you can pick this up right away and get it. There is no meta stuff - you fight, you kill, you overcome or die trying. I just love the purity of it - this is a proper videogame! A videogame the way I remember them, not what they are now.

However, there is a price to pay for being proper oldschool. In this particular case, variety is severely lacking and the skill-ceiling is rather low. You basically get a bunch of same looking rooms that you can learn and that won't provide much of a challenge once you figured them out. The same goes for the combat, while the shield bash seems nice - it is actually too slow and becomes somewhat unsafe if you don't have the timing down, as mentioned by other reviewers it is best to do hit and run attacks instead, rendering the shield mechanic somewhat useless. With that in mind, both shields and swords that have the ability to stun or knockback are considerably better and much more worthwhile than the types that only do damage. Yeah, there is a bit of a random element here, you get weapon drops (easily the worst part of the entire design as far as I am concerned) - I would have much prefered to get a choice of weaponry at the start of the game and fixed amounts of swings certain enemies take before being destroyed. The way it is now, you can occassionaly have a bad roll, resulting in more swings needed to take out the tougher enemies. But once you figured that out, the only way to actually lose HP or a life is by getting impatient and not taking it slow. Since the doors of rooms only unlock once you killed everything, you can't really rush through the game either - which makes the the first ten or so minutes of the game REALLY tedious after a while. Which is actually quite horrible if you consider that game lasts about 30 minutes in total.

Still, I couldn't really stop playing it and kept coming back until I beat it (which took about 3 hours in total), it's limited size really helped me getting into that "okay, one more game"-mindset and makes it a perfect oldschool-snack.

To counter the fact that Shadowcrypt looses quite a bit of appeal once you learned most of its tricks and got the layout of the catacombs memorized, the game offers a bunch of challenges/achievements that might change the way you play this game or at least make you try some stuff you wouldn't bother doing like killing wizards with deflected fireballs. Nice addition, but also very grindy and at times questionable (die 100 times in a session? Why would I want to do that!?).

I think you really need to enjoy to master a game and go for the crazy challenges like not getting hit and not loosing a life to get the most out of this, otherwise this quite playable piece of software might turn stale rather quickly. I certainly wouldn't mind this being something of a first part in a series of connected snack-size retro-adventures and would recommend it to like-minded retrogamers. But heed my warning if you cannot live without customization options, desperately need RPG-elements and level ups, hate losing progress or generally do not like the idea of memorizing a game - Shadowcrypt isn't for you.

For me it is a nice addition to other excellent games in my library like Völgarr the Viking or Oniken and something I will probably return to every now and then.

Excellent game for anyone looking for a game that's simple but challenging.

Shadowcrypt feels very retro in all the best ways: Simple controls, a manual with valuable game knowledge, no long tutorial, and the best part: its replay value comes from not the length of the game or a plethura of features and modes, but the sheer challenge of finishing the game (and getting achievements).

To clear things up:

This is a combat-based platformer, meaning that if you don't learn the timing and dynamics of the combat, you won't get very far. Understanding that this is by design and is meant to be adapted to is essential to enjoying and completing Shadowcrypt.

The description may sound like the game has procedurally generated levels, but it does not. This isn't a bad thing though, once you get into the game you learn to embrace it. The levels are technically non-linear, but at the end of the day, they're linear. They just involve exploration and remembering where to go; the crypt is confusing and a bit maze-like, but that's a part of the game. Getting lost in the crypt is also part of the game. You'll see.

You get three lives, and when you lose them all you start over from the beginning. A complaint I hear a lot is people saying once you lose your first life you're screwed because you lose all of your gear (which is an important part to progressing). To these people I say: Don't get discouraged! You can beat this game with the starting crappy weapons, as impossible as that may sound. If you're good at fighting in this combat-based game, you will move forward. And there will most likely be a weapon or shield drop or a treasure chest. Just keep going, don't give up!

Final verdict:

This is one of the BEST games I've played this year. It took me a long time to beat it and I appreciated the mood, the music, the excellent character animation, the sound of the dungeon doors opening and closing, and especially the challenge. It's old school and feels like you picked it up from the SNES bin at the local game store, and I love that.
