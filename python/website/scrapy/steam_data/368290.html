About This Game

Visibility is a platformer with a unique twist. It is completely dark.

That is until you place your lights, of course. Each level has a limited set of lights which you must strategically place around the level in order to illuminate your path to the exit. The challenge isn't over once you've got your lights in the right place, however. The platforming itself is a different challenge of its own, offering awkward jumps and varying traps that you will not be able to navigate safely if you have not lit up the level correctly.

As there are no specific light locations, each one of the 20 levels have their own unique solution that you will create as you play. If you're not particularly efficient with your lighting then you may be forced to run through a section of a level completely unaided with only hope (or your memory) to save you.

Although conceptually wonderful, this game has flaws that spoil the fun.

I almost feel a sense of conflict with the design: the simplicity of placing lights to guide your path is met with difficult level pathing and frustratingly grindy attempts to replace all your lights upon dying or misclicking a lantern.

I'll start with the gameplay and then do a pros/cons section. You're given a small number of lanterns to place in a dark room, revealing parts of the surrounding area. You then manouver your character through this dark room, overcoming obstacles such as spikes and bottomless pits (these will kill you) to reach the black square at the end of the level.

Pros:
- Effective and interesting gameplay concept.
- The music (acoustic strings) is melodious and soothing. It's quite a subtle and nice background noise.
- Controls are simple, easy to use and responsive.
- Shadow and lighting effects are executed fairly nicely.

Cons:
- Level design. Even in clear lighting, there are some jumps and obstacles that are very punishing when they don't need to be. Trying to overcome jumps in clear sight distracts from the challenge of visibility .
- Resets. Upon dying or misplacing a lantern (in a wall for instance) you're forced to reset the level and place your lights all over again. Nevermind how long you spent trying to get your lantern placements perfect. If you didn't die so many times per level, I don't think this would be an issue.
- Too few lights to place. A difficult jump or obstacle is that much harder without lighting- it makes sense that you'll spend time fumbling in the dark, but the levels should allow for that with the number of lights you're given.

I finished this game by taping a piece of plastic over my monitor and highlighting (on the plastic) places where perfect lighting could be achieved. I used a new sheet on every level so I wouldn't get confused, and the spikes still killed me in dark areas.

If you're on the fence, I recommend holding out for a sale. Otherwise, pick it up if you enjoy trial and error or think your patience can endure the frustrations. If you can, I'm sure you'll find more fun in this than I did.

Visibility

Ah, visibility, where to begin... Why not start from the beginning, well of course. Months ago I remember the creator working on some small project, all it was to begin, probably, can't fully remember. But he offered to me the alpha, so I graciously accepted the offer, and boy was I surprised about the depth of the game, there's so much more than just a simple indie game, and it does come across as very enjoyable.

Let's get this pajama party of a review started with an insight into the game itself, then we can work further into it as this progresses. Now at first glance, you may look down on the blocky graphics that could run on a SNES, and the same lighting as a collapsed mine, but looks can be deceiving and in this case their scant nature is what builds the game. It is the deep pitch-dark view you are presented with that draws you in, “what's this”, you think as you remember the controls, “ah yes, all I need to do is add my own light”, begging the question of 'how many rectangles does it take to change a lightbulb?'

One. It seems.

But really, these simplistic graphics are a real powerhouse in this modern era of high-tech graphics, where many gamers focus too much on the initial, skin-level, aesthetics of a game, angrily reviewing graphics alone. No, this game is a true light to me, showing that, like many indie games, graphics are not a huge priority in making a game a game. Rectangles and a broken dimmer switch make this game truly glorious and straightforward in the most necessary of areas.

The general feel and flow of the game and its controls are perfect, responsive and accurate, testing each player's ability to platform, as well as their ability to puzzle solve, with the light placement being the centre point of completing a level. I was honestly amazed by the tuning of the game and the polish the creator has drummed in, a very admirable result from a single man trying to juggle this game with education. It really does have a “Thomas was Alone” feel to it, I find myself with that same voice in my head, 'Troy was alone, but he was good at falling, and this reverse falling'...

'Oh, and dying.'

This happens a lot as you progress, getting to the levels full of spikes, trial and error would apply, but there isn't enough error in that phrase. More like a lot of error, and occasional failing a bit further into the level, and only then, when your psyche is in ashes and you have every death achievement, do you have the creator's permission to finish the level.

Overall, this game is damn near perfect, it doesn't overstay its welcome, it's concise and lasts as long as it needs to. One cannot complain about the game being small, as an indie game it is meant to be a shorter game, not being a hardcore game, though one that is still as challenging as the high end of modern puzzlers. So I do highly recommend this game, and this isn't because the creator has paid me (he hasn't) but because it really is an amazing game to distract yourself from the harsh realities of life.
