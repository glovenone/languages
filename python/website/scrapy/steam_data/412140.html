About This Game

Disclaimer: Only play the game if you're fine with the visuals presented in trailer and screenshots. May not be suitable for people with epilepsy.
The color puzzle at the end is a side quest and is not required to finish the game. There's also no timer in the game.

Electric Highways is a game that's all about experience and exploration.The setting takes place in a surreal, virtual world, made out of abstract 3D graphics, where the player must explore ten levels, each with an individual theme. Some levels contain simple puzzles, but for the most part the main focus is exploration. In some ways, the game is like an interactive conceptual music album, where each level is like a short song.

The year is 2072. Virtual reality has become a worldwide phenomenon, allowing people to sink into the virtual web, while experiencing isolation in real life. A VR engineer has finished his virtual reality project, but before uploading it on the web, he decides to visit it for the last time, plunging deeper into his own surreal world.

The atmosphere of the game has been inspired by early 80's electronic music (Gary Numan, Kraftwerk), visual style of games like LSD: Dream Emulator and the 1984 version of the film Metropolis. The project uses 3D engine from 90's.

Posted: 7 July
This game has grade A atmosphere, interesting back story, and challenging puzzles.

For starters, let's talk about visuals. The developer did an excellent job mimicing the old school "DOOM" style game, everything flowed smoothly visually. Biggest drawback to it was at a few points the textures looked a tad bit off the walls, but given the style, this may have been intentional, and regardless is very much overlookable.

The soundtrack was simply AMAZING. The classic retro techno style could really get you into the game, and also added significantly to the atmosphere. Considering the slight horror elements in the game, the music was a good way to ease your nerves, but also could put you on edge.

Gameplay was exactly as you'd expect this type of game to be. You explore the area, look for things to help you progress to the next level, and occasionally break things. Very simple, but simple isn't bad. Biggest draw back to the gameplay was how long it was, or rather, how long it wasn't. Without loading times, reading text, and "cutscenes", the game is 30 minutes long. Despite the length, the game still followed through with an enjoyable experience, and it's quite possible that it being short may have saved the game from becoming too repetitive and dull.

The atmosphere was a bit of a rollercoaster, but not in a bad way. You would go between bright colors, upbeat sounds, and interesting surrounds, to haunting echos, dark corridors, and unsettling apparitions. This created a bit of suspense between levels, leaving you to wonder if you might be able to relax as you entered the portals. Your solitude adds to this, when the only signs of other humans in the game are ominous messages and camera's that follow your motion, giving you an uneasy feeling of being watched.

Lastly, the story. There isn't much too it, but as I said before, simple doesn't mean bad. You do get to sense the emotions of the protagonist with the isolation in game, which seems to allude to his lonelyness outside of the 'simulation'. His minimal contact with humans shows in his work, and you get to experience it in full. A simple, but interesting look on lonelyness.

All in all Electric Highways is a top notch indie game, one that I would be willing to pay a few dollars for, but that isn't necessary as it is free. If decently challenging puzzles, retro styled visuals, and unsettling atmosphere interest you, or if you want a strange game to fool around with, this is an excellent choice!
