Overall a detective game "with potential" though, that is where it seems to end.
A very poorly done console port leaves MUCH to be desired.
I'd barely got even to the "fourth" possible destination, looking for a particular person, and spent the next 26 attempts trying to get past the ensueing QTE before getting fed up without any option to skip or auto complete.

"Registration" of mouse movement is extremely temperamental. Sometimes it'll work with just the tiniest inch of movement, other times, your virtually having to throw the mouse across the room to get it to detect; and when it does, it often detects the wrong direction entirely.

Baring in mind my limited playtime for this.

PROS
- Seemingly interesting story with plot twists, though thrown into the deep end with a incompetant police officer to start.
- Interesting and varied puzzles, solvable even without having to purchase the hints and requiring a little thought to figure out where those hints are, often looking at objects in a 3D View.
-- Though the above is also highly troublesome due to the interface. Through most of the game even, your constantly changing between using the keyboard and mouse by force, while many things could be accomplished with the mouse alone, such as rotating objects to get the 3D view.

CONS
- No options in the menu to define graphics quality or resolution, pretty much a standardized option for PC users even with years-old games.
- Forced into using both keyboard and mouse, not an optional switch to steamline according to your own preferences.
- Mouse sensitivity randomness when dealing with QTE Events
- No "Skip" option for QTE or Puzzles.
- Forced Tutorial, unable to skip. Invasive at times too when you already have a clue what your supposed to be doing.
- Full Screen doesn't "stick" itself. With a dual screen setup your constantly moving between the screens with your mouse, making QTE even worse to try to complete. In the majority of anything else full screen will "stick" the screen and you'd have to alt out of it to do something on the second screen.

On top of that, does this game even some with a save feature?! I found out eventually that it auto saves, but there was no indication of that what so ever.
Still, I didn't get too far into the game before getting pretty much fed up and mashing to get out of it. To think, I was looking forward to eventually getting around to playing it as part of cleaning up my backlog. May eventually try it again, but not likely.

Red Johnson's Chronicles is a point-and-click adventure that has a certain attitude, with decent graphics and some quick time events (i.e. press button X before time runs out, and then some other button, and then some other mouse move...) to pass the "challenges", and the usual puzzles. While the writing is somewhat fresh, the puzzles are forced and derivative, QTE's are boring and out of place, and the weird interface really turns you off. This game was clearly NOT written for the PC, and it shows.

RJC has you playing the titular character, Red Johnson, who's a private investigator and he's been tasked by Officer Johnson of the metro police to help check out a murder... someone got whacked on the bridge... unfortunately, no body (may have fell into the water). You have to solve a couple puzzles to get access to clues, find more, and solve a couple QTE sequences on the way.

The graphics has a sense of those "noir" detective novels / graphics novels, with brown and gray being the primary colors. The transition between scenes are often animated and the cutscenes are quite nice with good if a bit cliched voice acting.

There are three major problems to this game: forced puzzles, weird interface, and QTE button smashes, all stemming from this being a console port.

The puzzles in the game makes ZERO sense and felt extremely "forced". For example, you find an instant camera, but the eject button doesn't work. Opening the bottom revealed that... "all the resistors had fallen out" Huh? However, someone left enough clues on the board that I was supposed to form one complete continue line of resistors from 'start' to "finish" without violating the "number" (from 0 to 3) which is the number of resistors around each particular square. HUH? Another puzzle involving a different camera requires you to slide pieces of circuit board around to restablish the circuit among a power supply and two power destinations. Yes, the good old 15 pieces in 4x4 board sliding puzzle.

The interface doesn't help either. Most PC adventure games either use only the mouse... or only require arrow keys, space, ESC, and maybe one other key. This game requires the use of the mouse, left AND right click, space, B, V, M, AND the arrow keys. AND with the mouse you have to perform drag up / drag down / drag left / drag right actions, and it's never consistent. Many actions that could be done with arrow keys require mouse drag instead. This makes sense as this was originally designed for console controller with dual sticks AND a D-pad, plus addition buttons (4 primaries, and shoulder and trigger...) but when it came to the PC, new keys were assigned without thought on how a PC gamer would be approaching the game.

Which brings us to the QTE... again, can never tell if you'll be doing mouse drag or actual key tap, maybe both. My first ever QTE was encountering Saul. First I hit space, then mouse drag three times (different directions) to conclude this easy test, and I flunked the first time because I wasn't gettng it.

All in all RJC's developers chose to make a barely adequate port and it shows. It's annoying to play, puzzles are forced and obtuse, which makes playing a chore rather than fun.

Red Johnson's Chronicles is a point-and-click adventure that has a certain attitude, with decent graphics and some quick time events (i.e. press button X before time runs out, and then some other button, and then some other mouse move...) to pass the "challenges", and the usual puzzles. While the writing is somewhat fresh, the puzzles are forced and derivative, QTE's are boring and out of place, and the weird interface really turns you off. This game was clearly NOT written for the PC, and it shows.

RJC has you playing the titular character, Red Johnson, who's a private investigator and he's been tasked by Officer Johnson of the metro police to help check out a murder... someone got whacked on the bridge... unfortunately, no body (may have fell into the water). You have to solve a couple puzzles to get access to clues, find more, and solve a couple QTE sequences on the way.

The graphics has a sense of those "noir" detective novels / graphics novels, with brown and gray being the primary colors. The transition between scenes are often animated and the cutscenes are quite nice with good if a bit cliched voice acting.

There are three major problems to this game: forced puzzles, weird interface, and QTE button smashes, all stemming from this being a console port.

The puzzles in the game makes ZERO sense and felt extremely "forced". For example, you find an instant camera, but the eject button doesn't work. Opening the bottom revealed that... "all the resistors had fallen out" Huh? However, someone left enough clues on the board that I was supposed to form one complete continue line of resistors from 'start' to "finish" without violating the "number" (from 0 to 3) which is the number of resistors around each particular square. HUH? Another puzzle involving a different camera requires you to slide pieces of circuit board around to restablish the circuit among a power supply and two power destinations. Yes, the good old 15 pieces in 4x4 board sliding puzzle.

The interface doesn't help either. Most PC adventure games either use only the mouse... or only require arrow keys, space, ESC, and maybe one other key. This game requires the use of the mouse, left AND right click, space, B, V, M, AND the arrow keys. AND with the mouse you have to perform drag up / drag down / drag left / drag right actions, and it's never consistent. Many actions that could be done with arrow keys require mouse drag instead. This makes sense as this was originally designed for console controller with dual sticks AND a D-pad, plus addition buttons (4 primaries, and shoulder and trigger...) but when it came to the PC, new keys were assigned without thought on how a PC gamer would be approaching the game.

Which brings us to the QTE... again, can never tell if you'll be doing mouse drag or actual key tap, maybe both. My first ever QTE was encountering Saul. First I hit space, then mouse drag three times (different directions) to conclude this easy test, and I flunked the first time because I wasn't gettng it.

All in all RJC's developers chose to make a barely adequate port and it shows. It's annoying to play, puzzles are forced and obtuse, which makes playing a chore rather than fun.
