About This Game

Snow Light is a Visual Novel. Basically, it's a digital graphic novel where you can read while listening to BGM's and sound effects.
In a Visual Novel you won't shoot or kill your enemies. You'll read, listen and hopefully enjoy it.

This game takes place in a futuristic world where the nations are living in a fragile state of peace. Military conflicts have become commonplace, due to the rise of private armies as means to protect the now scarce natural resources.

You'll follow a professional spy, Snow Light, and will travel all over the globe to places such as Turkmenistan, Russia and England.

- Features:
  • A written story divided in 5 chapters, for a total of more than 30 thousand words.
  • Anime inspired art, with ADV-style presentation.
  • Intense action sequences with Quick Time Events.
  • A unique story arc, with various viewpoint changes.
  • 19 music tracks composed by Petteri Sainio and Christoph Rauch.

It is impossible to state how poor this game is, and that's not counting the excellent "translation". (Maybe it reads better in French or one of the other languages, but I somehow doubt the story is saner.)

- As a visual novel, the game is a debacle. The story is an atrocious mess of jargon which is apparently a side-story to the creator's epic "Dragon and Weed" (This is not a joke, it explains this in a heavily abridged form in a submenu you can find) saga that you can bet your â¥â¥â¥â¥ I'm never going to look up. There are maybe 4 "branch points" in the game. They do nothing. Save and click either option! Nothing changes at all. As a result, you're just here to watch whatever strange superpowered wankfest this is play out over a few hours, with the occasional break in the form of...
- Quick-Time Events. No, seriously. Keep your hand on the keyboard and make sure you're familiar with french keyboard layouts, because that's what the keys are bound around. Earlier in the game there are more of these and then they just stop, like even the developer realized how dumb they were and didn't care enough to remove them. At least one failed to register for me until I restarted the game, so don't go in expecting them to be tightly-coded, either.
- The game says it runs in some sort of custom 3D engine! Probably to allow for the QTEs. It's not very good and somehow it crashed twice on me in the process of playing. A visual novel with no branch points crashed on me. Just take in how much of a wreck it is that this is a thing I'm writing. It wasn't a hard thing to test, fella.
- I don't speak the other languages to compare, but the English script is a debacle. Characters will repeat the same line back at each other randomly, some phrases are just entirely garbage (my favorite line in the game: "It's seems that Menkens hathwart someone he shouldn't. Men of the Bad Santass are here and not to joke..."), and more than a few lines just stopped a word in and continued on like the script wasn't matching up with timing somehow. Others were just still in French for no reason. Unlike a lot of VNs, there's no rewind function, so I hope you like being left in the dark or restarting entire chapters to figure out what the hell is going on. It's not going to help.
- The art is terrible. Action scenes don't feel kinetic, they feel like someone on Deviantart just learned Photoshop has filters. One of the climactic moments of the story has a man take off his shirt and then suddenly the screen is a mess of glowing red chains, because "he used to tame the tigers". It then cuts to black and suddenly he's beat up a little.
- I hope you don't want a soundtrack, because the only accompaniment the game has are some three-note "baby's first music" compositions, a lot of generic stock sound effects like gunfire or explosions, and most of the game is silent. Entirely silent.
- I guess the obscene price on here is part of some kind of weird scam by the creator to get you to buy it from him elsewhere directly rather than give Steam a cut. (I got a review key.) It's not worth buying, so take neither route. This thing is a trainwreck, and in that sad "400 passengers dead" style rather than the "oh man it's so bad it's good" fashion.

In no way should you ever give this game the dignity of a thought, much less devote yourself to playing it.

Posted: 2 November, 2014
This specifically reviews the game played in English.

If you are going to sell a game as a visual novel, you should copy edit your text and review your translations. I can not speak for any of the other languages, but the English translation for Snow Light is embarassing. Not only does the vast majority of the script read like it was run through Google Translate without revision, it has enough typos to clearly indicate the writer/translator did not even bother to use spell check.

The terrible writing is not the only thing that muddles this story. The game uses so many acronyms that it can be difficult to keep track of them all. Some exist in the real world, some are made up for the game, but they all run together to combine with the incoherent translation in making the story extremely hard to follow.

The art ranges from appearing decent to looking like something a teenager threw together in Microsoft Paint. In some cases you can tell the artist reused a face asset which makes a character look like a mishapen freak of nature with two mouths.

The text is not always accompanied by sound. During my playthrough there were a number of long durations of text exposition that occurred in either total silence or with just some ambient white noise. The game has no voice acting, and most of the game sounds are just cheesy stock-sounding gunfire noises.

The interactive aspect consists of approximately ten quick time events that appear without warning and involves seemingly random keypresses. You never know when to expect them, what key you will have to press (either once or quickly in repetition), and they have a very short input window. When you fail the game goes back around 5 seconds for you to do it again once you know to expect it. Luckily you only lose those few seconds each time, since you will most likely fail them all at first (they also pop up at the top of the screen while you are reading the dialogue at the bottom). There are also two branching options that the player can choose from, but they appear to only affect a specific scene each and does not have any affect on the larger narrative.

The narrative itself, as difficult as it is to follow, ends as if this is the first chapter in an episodic game. I don't know if this is the developer's plan, but even if it is, do not expect much satisfaction from the story. There are much better visual novels available on Steam. I suggest you exhaust as many other options as possible before purchasing this.
