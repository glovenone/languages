About This Game

The game is made up of stages, with a puzzle on each stage and story cut-scenes in between. The game has auto-save feature in each stage so if you failed in a certain puzzle, you can try again by clicking continue.

On every stage there will be a trial phase and a battle phase. In the trial phase, the player will be given a puzzle that can be solved alone by thinking or calculating. In the next phase, which is the battle phase, the player will have a match against the AI. Battles require concentration and patience.

Calcu-Late was developed by Blazing Fanfish and is being published by Back To Basics Gaming.
www.BackToBasicsGaming.com

Posted: 4 July
Those who try to build a game of one genre, using a toolset intended for another, very seldom meet success. The result is typically clumsy and clunky, built of parts not really meant for the purpose.

So it is with Calcu-Late.

While my impression from the store page suggested that this would be an educational math game, it turned out to be more of a generic puzzler, built in RPGMaker. Though it's interesting to see RPGMaker pushed to create something other than still another formulaic SNES-era RPG, the outcome is more tiresome than fun. Clicking through dull, badly-written dialogue in order to provide a patina of context for the puzzles proved even less enjoyable than clicking through them to establish the clichéd, half-hearted romances and shopworn revenge motivations of the typical RPG.

Similarly, solving puzzles by plodding around with the overworld controls was slow and frustrating, when compared with the mouse interface of a normal puzzler. I don't know whether the developer chose this path as a personal challenge, or simply had only RPGMaker available to work with, but this was an endeavour more noble in conception than in execution.

Overview
You play as a boy who gets help from a maths goddess to pass his finals. You have to solve various logic puzzles to get through the game. There are 4 difficulty levels; the game's extra cutscene is only available if you beat the game at "hard" or "expert".

Likes
  • Nicely coloured pixel graphics - Sense of immersion's there.
  • Something of a short story - Lame, but still fun in a childish, silly way, if you're the kind who likes having a story for your game (I am).
  • Music was ok - Nothing great, but it fit the game.
  • 2 of the puzzles were good - The first and last one in particular required a bit of thinking, though they sadly weren't original in any way (I've encountered puzzles like these many times before).
  • Good puzzle instructions - Despite the poor English (see below), instructions were concisely written and perfectly clear. (Maybe the developers only had enough money to pay for someone to vet/edit the puzzle instructions and not the story dialogue for the entire game!!!)

Dislikes
  • Really bad English - Almost every message box contained a spelling or grammatical error.
  • Very short - Minus the time taken to solve some of the puzzles, I'd say it was 30 minutes max.
  • Very few puzzles - If I'm not wrong I think there were only about 5 in total, and 1 was repeated.
  • Most puzzles were juvenile, unchallenging and very unoriginal - E.g. Touch an array of crystals so that all of them glow; match pairs of cards; etc
  • Puzzles have nothing to do with maths - Other than a few that required basic addition skills, the rest tested more of logic. And as you can see from the example I named just above, one even tests memory.
  • Silly ending - I rolled my eyes.
  • More for kids or teens - Due to a combination of all the factors mentioned above. Don't think adults will enjoy this that much.

Summary
I can only recommend this game if it's free. There are too few puzzles, they aren't original (I have seen them all somewhere), the story is bland, and the poor English really kills the experience, especially when there's a fair amount of story dialogue involved. This game feels on the whole way too unpolished for a paid release.

Tips to developer
You could really improve the quality and feel of this game by getting a native English speaker to vet through and correct the language. It wouldn't take long and you could probably find someone to do it for you for free.
