About This Game

From the creators of Ultima Underworld I & II and System Shock.

Lead an elite fighting team battling for freedom from the sinister forces of the Earth Hegemony. Engage in combat on the bloody battlefields of four unique worlds. Encased in your Powered Battle Armour (PBA) you are an unstoppable fighting force.

Which is just as well, for as your colony prospers a new breed of pirate has emerged. More organized and armed with powerful weapons, these new pirates have prompted the Cantauri government to establish an elite fighting force, the Strike Force Centauri.

Lead your team against the new threat to Centauri peace and discover the dark secret behind the pirates growing military might...

Bonus Content Included: Music from the band Tribe (feat. Eric and Terri Brosius), High Resolution scans of Manuals, Quick Reference Cards and Armour Schematics, Retro Desktop Themes and more!

  • Battle to save your colony in over 30 missions, including recon, search and rescue, night raids and full scale battles.
  • Create your own missions with the Random Scenario Generator.
  • Sophisticated Artificial Intelligence engine allows you complete control over your squad mates and enemy adaptability.
  • Breakthrough outdoor renderer allows for a fully interactive environment.

I've only played this game for a few missions but I'd like to provide a review as a Mac user.

This game has a lot of audio stuttering. I'm running it on a standard 2014 macbook pro running OS X 10.9.5. That's pretty much it. Graphics are fine, controls are fine. CMD+F transitions from windowed to full screen mode easily, even if the game freezes. I personally really enjoyed games like Wing Commander, Descent, Terminal Velocity, and Mech Warrior, and this game seems like a bit of a forgotten gem from the era of real 3D action on CD-ROM featuring an amazing 255 color palette. Maybe you didn't even play these games using a mouse.

Anyway, its a bit wonky but very playable.

The enviroment is reasonably pretty and actually impressive for a game being released in the mid 90s.

The controls sadly haven't aged as well as the rest of the game and are wonky and clumsy but they do work and will not be a hindrance once you have gotten used to them.

I strongly recommend this game. It is a very impressive game for its time. It is still one of the best mech games out there.

Terra Nova: Strike Force Centauri was an immeasurably revolutionary computer game released in 1996.

Terra Nova established the outdoor first person shooter genre with extreme render distances and advanced physics modelling including gravity simulation, water reflections, player movement systems, and weather conditions. Terra Nova also pioneered and perfected squad commanding in an fps environment, with competent, realistic bots. The squad members performed the player’s exact commands, ranging from movement options, tactics, engagement techniques, and special abilities. Terra Nova even pioneered exosuit simulators, inspiring the Tribe series of fps’s. It is a very technical simulation, with the missions requiring strategic planning, in outfitting the squad with weapons, suits, pilots, and, “alternative suit functions.” Alternate Suit Functions, or A.S.F.’s, permitted an operator to perform an ability, including deploying sentry turrets, additional suit shields, demolition explosives, power plants, repair modules, cloaking, and other abilities. On most missions, the player had to rely on the bots to perform tasks for the player, either defending a structure, destroying enemy fortifications, and scouting. The player managed all of these tasks via three multi-function displays, similarly to an aerospace fighter jet. The player had access to a powerful and immersive mapping program, drones, damage outputs, and A.S.F. controls. The player also had access to jump jets. Jump jets were integral to gameplay, allowing for mountains to be scaled with ease and impossible maneuvers to be performed.

Due to Terra Nova having been released on the Microsoft Disk Operating System in 1996, months before ID Games released Quake, technical limitations exist. Terra Nova runs off of a modified version of the System Shock engine, based off of the Ultima UnderWorld engine. To accommodate the long render distances, only a limited area around the player is texture mapped. For extreme distances, voxel rendering was utilized. Because of this conglomerate of rendering techniques, the resolution is limited to 320 by 400 pixels. Based off of the System Shock engine, Terra Nova also inherits System Shock’s controls, lacking mouse look. Due to poor popularity, few mods exist for this game. Joerg Fischer, now a German neuroscientist, wrote a famous mod for Terra Nova known as Operation Stonehenge, available at Rich Nagel’s website. Upon inspecting Steam’s Terra Nova game files, it was observed that Night Dive Studios hadn’t even bothered to extract the game files from the ISO copy of the disc. If anyone intends to modify Terra Nova, the disc’s contents must be extracted. Also notable is the use of DOS4GW instead of DOS32A in the Disk Operating System Emulator, limiting the maximum performance and speed of Terra Nova.

Terra Nova was and is an extremely accurate and enjoyable exosuit simulator. The game had the reputed Seamus Blackley as the lead physics programmer, responsible for dozens of flight simulators, including Flight Unlimited, and was originally planned to be part of the Starship Troopers franchise, hence why the player and his or her squad began each mission by falling from the sky. The character models were based off of a dynamically animated skeleton with bipedal physics simulations that outclass current fps’ movement systems. Similarly to System Shock, Greg LoPiccolo composed and programmed the soundtrack to dynamically react to the player’s status. It was Paul Neurath’s dream, the director of Looking Glass, the team of programmers, artists, and designers responsible for Deus Ex, System Shock 2, Terra Nova, System Shock, Thief, and Ultima UnderWorld. Unfortunately, like their previous titles, Terra Nova received critical acclaim and no popularity. The company went bankrupt and was summarily purchased by Ion Storm. The highly experienced team proceeded to develop the highly regarded Deus Ex and System Shock 2 games. After Ion Storm’s breakup, the Looking Glass team was divided amongst many current developers, with some now returning under Paul Neurath’s new company, OtherSide Entertainment. With the rights to System Shock finally having been purchased by Night Dive Studios, work on remaking System Shock and developing System Shock 3 has finally commenced. If anyone recalls the final scene from System Shock involving TetraCorp, a new Terra Nova might be coming out within the next few years.

I've completed this game before its release on Steam, and it's excellent. From the creators of System Shock, we have a full-scale, totally immersive combat game experience with an extensive array of weapons and gadgets, cheesy full-motion videos, and large, pretty levels. The story would take too long to explain, but in short, you command a 4 person squad (yourself included) in Strike Force Centauri, a special forces group made out of the best soldiers from each smaller special forces group in the Centauri system. Your squad wears heavy Powered Battle Armor (PBAs), which basically means you're wearing a ton of things that can get broken in firefights (including jump jets!). Each mission has a video and text briefing, after which you can set up your team members and their loadouts, based on what you think they should need for what you were just briefed on. The levels themselves are very large, so that you may plan ahead and set waypoints on the map to make a route for a safe sniping distance from enemy camps, tanks, etc.
If you've ever played the classic Rainbow Six games, the squad members work like that in-mission. There are a ton of commands to give them, and you can tell each individual member what to do or where to go. This is NOT for beginners.

My only complaint is that the game desperately needed a sequel. ("Needed" because today's devs would screw it up)

...Gameplay-wise, this game is like Rainbow Six and Battlefield mixed with jump jets, or something. It's a very fun game, and it's well worth the price. I paid about five times more for the boxed version, and I proudly display it on my shelf.


Let me remind you that Terra Nova is NOT a game for beginners. Thank you. Have a nice day.

Highly enjoyable, fast-paced combo of power armor simulation and tactical shooter.

Interface and controls might look overwhelming at first, but they are quite easy to get used to. In many ways, considering its age and amount of depth it offers, Terra Nova is a surprisingly accessible game.

While player is quite limited during the early mission, later on you'll have a choice between multiple power armors and a large array of weapons and equipment. Depending on mission, you'll either go in solo, or command varying number of squadmates. Tactical component offers sufficient amount of depth, with great array of commands that can be issued to your squadmates.

Where Terra Nova relly shines is design and variety of missions themselves. Mission objectives are extremely varied (not to mention that player will often be presented with optional secondary objectives), as are locations they're set in. You'll fight on multiple planets, in environments ranging from hills & forests, trough deserts and mountains, to barren and ominous wastelands.
Missions are all set in sprawling open areas. That, combined with solid amount of customisation, means there is a great amount of freedom and flexibility when approaching mission's objectives.
This is a Looking Glass Studios game, trough and trough.

Story is fun and pulpy, with great amound of background lore. While 'tis nowadays fashionable to mock directing and acting in FMVs from this game's era, in Terra Nova's case they are actually pretty solid and enjoyable.

An amazing-for-the-time squad commander Mech simulator thats fast and fun to play with a lot of depth.

It feels different to most walking robot sims in that these "Powered Battle Armor" suits are a lot more agile and fragile than a Mechwarrior Mech or a Heavy Gear Gear. Success requires good use of terrain, planning your approach, strafing and your jump jets. Background and story are interesting and there are a lot of FMV cutscenes (reminiscent of Wing Commander 3/4) although the writing and acting are pretty cheesy.

Fortunately its aged well and doesn't have issues like a lot of other DOS era games, the interface and controls are OK and there are two training missions at the start that function as a tutorial. Hard to believe this came out around the time of Doom - the rolling hills of the terrain, water reflections and other engine features are amazing for a DOS game.

Make sure you read the quick reference card PDF that is in the game directory - lots of useful controls and shortcuts in there!

Pros:
- Fun squad-based Mech game with more agility than most
- Good variety of missions, terrains and planets (varied gravity, weather etc)
- Detailed but sensible order system for your squadmates (e.g. order a cautious approach or all-out attack)
- Difficulty can be changed before the mission if you're finding missions too hard or too easy
- Random one-off scenario generator for quick action
- Optional tutorial missions to get you started
- Interesting storyline and background with lots of FMV cutscenes
- Bonus Features in the game directory includes scans of the Manual, official Strategy Guide, game art and music
- Controls are not perfect, but reasonable and easy to pick up

Cons:
- Lots of very useful controls buried in the manual (e.g. shift to bound in game which uses jump jets to boost your movement speed, tab in the base menus to see where to click, keys to crouch and look sideways) and especially 'o' to bring up the options menu which includes the difficulty settings.
- Video resolution limited to 320x400 at most (wierd as it seems to be using a modified version of the System Shock 1 engine which supported much higher resolutions)
- Might require some mucking around to configure sound when you first start, the Gravis Ultrasound emulations seems flaky

Terra Nova: Strike Force Centauri was an immeasurably revolutionary computer game released in 1996.

Terra Nova established the outdoor first person shooter genre with extreme render distances and advanced physics modelling including gravity simulation, water reflections, player movement systems, and weather conditions. Terra Nova also pioneered and perfected squad commanding in an fps environment, with competent, realistic bots. The squad members performed the player’s exact commands, ranging from movement options, tactics, engagement techniques, and special abilities. Terra Nova even pioneered exosuit simulators, inspiring the Tribe series of fps’s. It is a very technical simulation, with the missions requiring strategic planning, in outfitting the squad with weapons, suits, pilots, and, “alternative suit functions.” Alternate Suit Functions, or A.S.F.’s, permitted an operator to perform an ability, including deploying sentry turrets, additional suit shields, demolition explosives, power plants, repair modules, cloaking, and other abilities. On most missions, the player had to rely on the bots to perform tasks for the player, either defending a structure, destroying enemy fortifications, and scouting. The player managed all of these tasks via three multi-function displays, similarly to an aerospace fighter jet. The player had access to a powerful and immersive mapping program, drones, damage outputs, and A.S.F. controls. The player also had access to jump jets. Jump jets were integral to gameplay, allowing for mountains to be scaled with ease and impossible maneuvers to be performed.

Due to Terra Nova having been released on the Microsoft Disk Operating System in 1996, months before ID Games released Quake, technical limitations exist. Terra Nova runs off of a modified version of the System Shock engine, based off of the Ultima UnderWorld engine. To accommodate the long render distances, only a limited area around the player is texture mapped. For extreme distances, voxel rendering was utilized. Because of this conglomerate of rendering techniques, the resolution is limited to 320 by 400 pixels. Based off of the System Shock engine, Terra Nova also inherits System Shock’s controls, lacking mouse look. Due to poor popularity, few mods exist for this game. Joerg Fischer, now a German neuroscientist, wrote a famous mod for Terra Nova known as Operation Stonehenge, available at Rich Nagel’s website. Upon inspecting Steam’s Terra Nova game files, it was observed that Night Dive Studios hadn’t even bothered to extract the game files from the ISO copy of the disc. If anyone intends to modify Terra Nova, the disc’s contents must be extracted. Also notable is the use of DOS4GW instead of DOS32A in the Disk Operating System Emulator, limiting the maximum performance and speed of Terra Nova.

Terra Nova was and is an extremely accurate and enjoyable exosuit simulator. The game had the reputed Seamus Blackley as the lead physics programmer, responsible for dozens of flight simulators, including Flight Unlimited, and was originally planned to be part of the Starship Troopers franchise, hence why the player and his or her squad began each mission by falling from the sky. The character models were based off of a dynamically animated skeleton with bipedal physics simulations that outclass current fps’ movement systems. Similarly to System Shock, Greg LoPiccolo composed and programmed the soundtrack to dynamically react to the player’s status. It was Paul Neurath’s dream, the director of Looking Glass, the team of programmers, artists, and designers responsible for Deus Ex, System Shock 2, Terra Nova, System Shock, Thief, and Ultima UnderWorld. Unfortunately, like their previous titles, Terra Nova received critical acclaim and no popularity. The company went bankrupt and was summarily purchased by Ion Storm. The highly experienced team proceeded to develop the highly regarded Deus Ex and System Shock 2 games. After Ion Storm’s breakup, the Looking Glass team was divided amongst many current developers, with some now returning under Paul Neurath’s new company, OtherSide Entertainment. With the rights to System Shock finally having been purchased by Night Dive Studios, work on remaking System Shock and developing System Shock 3 has finally commenced. If anyone recalls the final scene from System Shock involving TetraCorp, a new Terra Nova might be coming out within the next few years.

So, one would hear about this game, and at first glance think: Well, this game looks interesting, let's give it a try, right? It's made by Looking Glass after all! (sounds a whole lot like me ;)

And then you play it. Holy crap, this game is amazing. Complexity unlike ever seen before, one of the best soundtracks i've ever heard (albeit kind of biased), a story that keeps punching you in the face with plot-twists and the gameplay is nothing short of excellent either.

Sounds awesome right, a game with absolutely no flaws! ...Well, even though i would love to say this, there are some tiny nitpicky things i have to mention. The videos sometimes don't play, but a simple reboot of the game fixes this 4 out of 5 times.
Afterwards, you start a mission, but i screwed up and i want to retry. Maybe i still need to get used to the clunky controls yet (which feel surprisingly natural if you're willing to learn them). But this game has no pause-menu. Aaaaand, i ran out of explosives to kill myself. So i shoot my teammates excessively, and they call me a traitor and kill me.
...Wait a minute, i can do that? How much attention for detail does this game actually have? I am supposed to talk about the negatives now but i can't even stop myself from noticing even more good points of this game! This should really tell you enough, JUST BUY IT!

I've only played this game for a few missions but I'd like to provide a review as a Mac user.

This game has a lot of audio stuttering. I'm running it on a standard 2014 macbook pro running OS X 10.9.5. That's pretty much it. Graphics are fine, controls are fine. CMD+F transitions from windowed to full screen mode easily, even if the game freezes. The only time I have a consistent error is when exiting game from the save screen, this returns you to the start up menu but, on my computer at least, the windows of this screen get all wonky and the best way to exit is just to hit CMD+F and close the game from outside.

Having mentioned all that, the game runs very well. It's fun. The story and acting are interesting and well done for the era. If you grew up playing games like this, but never tried this one, I can recommend it with a clear conscience. I personally really enjoyed games like Wing Commander, Descent, Terminal Velocity, and Mech Warrior, and this game seems like a bit of a forgotten gem from the era of real 3D action on CD-ROM featuring an amazing 255 color palette. Maybe you didn't even play these games using a mouse.

Anyway, its a bit wonky but very playable.
