About This Game

Based on the Koihime Musou visual novel, Koihime Enbu features a reimagining of the Romance of Three Kingdoms where the majority of characters are girls. Originally an arcade fighting game, Koihime Enbu draws upon this pedigree to offer players accessible and exciting mechanics that will draw in beginner and veteran players alike.



Koihime Enbu is an ground based fighting game. Anyone can enjoy it from a beginner to a skilled fighting game veteran. Fighting game veterans will relish the tight controls and fast combat, providing the possibility for huge combos and devastating counters. Explore the Fatal Counter system that creates additional combo opportunities. With almost every attack being punishable, Koihime Enbu strives to provide a cleaner and more precise fighting experience.

Features:

  • Play as 13 different characters and 7 "assist" characters to create a huge variety of strategies!
  • Explore the Fatal Counter system which gives moves different properties that make comboing easier, and longer!
  • Utilise the Tactics Meter with special moves, ex special moves, super moves, and ultimate moves!
  • Save your best bouts for later, or learn from your previous battles with Replay Mode!
  • Ascend the online rankings by battling in ranked matchmaking, keep it closer to home and play with your friends in local versus mode!
  • Steam Achievement, Cloud, Trading Cards

You know, it’s not easy to find an actually bad fighting game; sure, Street Fighter V sticks out like a sore thumb due to its massive mismanagement and being left unfinished, but the common modern fighter always has a well-executed formula. Koihime Enbu joined the fighting game frenzy on Steam last month, but here I am, delivering a review for this site at long last. Better late than never, right?

-Graphics-

Yep, this game is incredibly flashy and fast-paced – Just how I like how my fighters! In fact, it’s pretty darn lush and vibrant, filled to the brim with detailed backgrounds and fluid anime-esque animation. However, as much as I adore this exquisitely clean look, I do wish there were options regarding graphics settings. The game can reach the ideal 60 frames per second framerate, but when I tried it on my computer in Full-Screen mode, the game suffered from slowdown. This was rectified when I resized the game window to something smaller, but I wish I didn’t have to do that.

-Audio-

Audibly, this game makes sure that you’re pumped up! It has a high-quality soundtrack that makes me think of the days of early PC games, where there’s an emphasis on making the OST as suited to a CD as possible. And of course, the sound effects make landing hits satisfying as always, and the voice clips for the game’s all-female cast can range from fittingly anime-like to a tad unnerving.

-Story-

Probably the weakest aspect of the game, the premise is considerably by-the-numbers. There’s a fighting tournament going on, and the gals all want in on the prize. Sound familiar? To every fighting game fan ever, it probably does, although the game at least tries to keep character dialogue kind of entertaining, even if it is just text. Still, it would have been great if that premise was completely different, especially considering this game is a spin-off of a visual novel series. Even I feel like it’s been used too much.

-Gameplay-

But who needs story when this is a game about anime girls beating the crap out of each other? With fighting moves so appropriately over-the-top there isn’t really a need, Koihime Enbu does the genre justice with its ability to provide a fun and quickly paced brawler.

The controls are the kind that can provide beginners with something to work with, but indicate that the player should master the mechanics in order to, of course, be fairly rad at the game. It isn’t necessarily hard to figure out what buttons do what kinds of attacks, but the challenge is figuring out how to use them properly in any given situation during the battle in order to avoid getting wrecked.

I commend this because I certainly wouldn’t want to plow through a game super easily (although button-mashers can possibly have that sort of luck I guess?); I want to be able to learn its ways and conquer my opponents with attacks I knowingly used, and I have no doubt that’s exactly what the game wants players to do as well, and when you do figure out how to play more and more, you get progressively better and further appreciate the game’s design.

The game also has a reasonable amount of game modes to work with. There is an Arcade mode, where you work your way to the top fighting opponent after opponent, a Scenario mode where it’s kind of the same except with a story emphasis, and Online and Local multiplayer Vs. modes, which is ideal for any modern fighting game. The AI opponents aren’t as easy as I thought they would be, but the challenge is indeed fair. Besides, there is at least a difficulty setting to adjust in Arcade mode.

-Verdict-

It all adds up to a great addition in the Steam library, especially for fighting game fans out there. $39.99 is probably rather on the steep side of things, but if over a million people are willing to purchase a $59.99 unfinished mismanaged mess, I guess Koihime Enbu isn’t in too bad of a position anyway. Should you look past that price (and some minor things in-game), however, you’ll find yourself with a gem in all its Japanese glory.

First thing you notice upon booting up the game is the fact the graphics are lovely HD sprites and its presented in 16:9 which is always nice when playing Japanese fighters.

The main roster is made up of a collection of buxom and at times possibly under-age anime girls accompanied by a selection of side characters that add to each characters abilities.

Each character also comes with a selection of colours and as you fight through the game via either the arcade or storyline mode you are treated to a bit of back story that fleshes out the characters.

Gameplay wise Koihime Enbu is a relatively simple fighter taking most of its cues from the Street Fighter series. Most of the moves are done by simple circle combos but the ease of performing special moves means that you can pick up and play immediately. The game is a port of the PS4 release and as such the on screen button combinations are all PlayStation which when using an Xbox controller is very confusing.

Gameplay is something of a cross between BlazBlue and Street Fighter 3. You have 4 special gauges that can be used to trigger a number of special moves ranging from using your secondary character to combo ending anime explosions.

There are a few little issues that I have with the game most notably being the odd difficulty curve. Even on hard the arcade mode is actually very easy with the difficulty only spiking on the last fight.

The other slight issue is that currently unless you are hoping to play locally multiplayer may be out of the question. I tried on many occasions to get an on-line multiplayer game going but alas each time it simply expired.

Overall Koihime Enbu is a decent fighting game and definitely one of the better indie fighters I have played in recent years. For fight fans it is a fun little game with a few little niggles that doesn't spoilt it overall. Definitely worth picking up if you are a fan of the genre and have some friends to play local multiplayer with.

With the recent influx of 2D fighters on Steam, sometimes the key to getting noticed is to have a different approach. Koihime Enbu's angle is "Romance of the Three Kingdoms", but almost every character is a girl. Guan Yu? Cao Cao? Sun Shang Xiang!? All girls. Furthermore, this game is a spin-off of Koihime Musou, an adult visual-novel/strategy game about a young man who travels back in time to this Three Kingdoms era. To be honest, I'm a little surprised that Koihime Enbu is an awesome fighting game.

In terms of game-design, Koihime Enbu is unlike other contemporary 2D fighters. Instead of having its combatants soar into the air and devastate each other with dizzying combos, this game keeps it very grounded. For the most part, combos rarely go beyond two-in-ones, or jump-in > follow-up > special. A lot of matches are determined by fighting-game basics. By that I mean spacing, footsies, pokes, anti-airs, and so on. It's not that these elements aren't present in other fighters, but players mistakenly tend to ignore them as the genre becomes increasingly complicated. If I were to draw comparisons, Koihime Enbu could be considered a faster-paced Samurai Showdown.

Going further, the moves you perform all have wildly-varying degrees of safetyness. Light attacks are generally a good idea for applying pressure, but they probably won't decide matches. Medium and heavy attacks have their uses, and with proper timing and spacing, you can control the flow of the match. However, when you hold forward, or down/forward and perform a medium or heavy attack, everything changes. Your heroine performs a high-risk attack. It's slow, can be countered in many ways, but it can also dramatically effect the outcome of the fight.

Now I'm not sure what to call it. A critical-counter? A fatal-counter? A romantic-counter? Well, it doesn't really matter. Basically, when the opponent is hit with a certain move, they crumple to the floor. This gives the player still standing an opportunity to land a massive combo. We're talking wall-splats, ground-bounces, the works. Off of a single one of these counters, players can expect to lose anywhere from 30 to 60 or even 100%(!!) of their life. It sounds crazy, but keep in mind that the larger combos will require some rather precise positioning, execution, and a lot of meter. To add to this, you can't get hit by these devastating counters if you aren't caught pressing buttons. Obviously the answer to avoiding these situations isn't "don't try to attack", but you have to be wary.

While I've just discussed what can be considered the x-factor of a Koihime Enbu fight, these occurences are few and far between. If your opponent is well-aware that you're fishing for a crucial-counter, they're going to have no trouble picking you apart. In this game, the simplest combos or even single-attacks can do serious damage. A lot of the time, you'll find that the best strategy is to stick to the basics. Aside from those mythical 75-100% combos, the execution barrier for this game is very low. This allows players to devote their energies towards understanding match-ups, looking for openings, and hopefully, winning matches. While I think that the roster could stand to have a few more entrants, it does strike the right balance between depth and accessibility. Every character is easy to use, but they can also offer some unique moves or interesting tactics. Aside from everyone having good and bad match-ups, there doesn't seem to be any truly ineffective characters.

As far as content goes, it's fair to say that this game is a better buy for competitive players. While this tends to be the expectation when it comes to fighters, a sizable number of these games still include extensive single-player modes. Unfortunately, this isn't the case for Koihime Enbu. I'm going to start this portion of the review off by admitting that... well... I got a little curious and did some research. I browsed the Koihime Muso wikia, looked up some other related materials, and now I'm interested in seeing more of the storyline. Unfortunately, the scenario mode in Koihime Enbu is a little disappointing. Each character has their own story, and dialogues with their opponents in-between matches, but the plot isn't there.

I suppose I shouldn't be asking for a storyline that affects the Koihime Muso canon, especially with the omission of a few key characters (such as the male lead and Ryūbi Gentoku aka Liu Bei). Still, as a huge fan of the Three Kingdoms novel, I was hoping for something with a little more depth, rather than a handful of gags and sorta-intimate moments. Still, the scenario mode might be worth checking out, since at least it gives some insight on everyone, and will help newcomers get used to how the game works and the franchise that it spawned from.

The training mode in this game is very well-designed. It covers all of the functionality that fighting game fans are accustomed to, such as a multitude of options, the ability to practice against the CPU, and even allowing for two players to spar with each other. Alongside the training mode, there is also a challenge mode where players can attempt several combos, each more convoluted than the last. Training mode can also be accessed while waiting for online matches, which is always welcome.

As far as the online-play is concerned, I believe that it's satisfactory. There have been some instances of lag, most notably when I (an American) was playing against someone from Japan. Speaking of, expect the competition to be extremely stiff and punishing. Koihime Enbu has been in Japanese arcades since 2014, and on consoles since the beginning of this year. I've already had a few matches, where I put up as much of a fight as a training dummy. This is partly because some of the strategies I've run into, simply won't occur in the single-player modes.

To give an example, one of my opponents was a Sonshōkō (Sun Shang Xiang) player. The first round was a close win for me, my opponent won the second. After the second round, I noticed that my opponent had a full bar of meter. So what happened in the third round? On my end, everything went to Hell. Each faction in the game has a couple selectable strategists. Summoning help costs meter, but they offer some useful techniques, if you know how to employ them. Anyway, Ryomō (one of the strategists), throws three projectiles at the opponent. While this is happening, Sonshōkō has some freedom to attack or defend at will. I wasn't prepared for this. So needless to say, all of my advances were thwarted, I fell victim to some nasty mix-ups, and I couldn't even land chip-damage on my opponent.

That's not to say that all of your fights will be decided by unfamiliar tech. In every match, there is a measurable level of chaos. To explain it better, I'll post a link to a video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VwaDqSMs5iE . Notice how several seconds pass by in the first round without a single hit being registered. As the match progresses, both players start taking risks, and the rounds are decided far more quickly. High-level play can be extremely methodical, so if you don't have the patience for it, you'll get hurt.

Although Koihime Enbu is lacking in terms of single-player content, I have every reason to believe that this is one of the better fighting games on Steam. It has its own style, and offers enough playability to challenge gamers of any skill-level.

While it may be an 'Anime' Fighting game it plays more like your traditional 2D Capcom or SNK fighter. Moves have huge recovery and not everything is cancellable, on block you’ll always be minus, meaning you have to be smart with your button choice. Frame traps are non-existent because of this outside of chaining jabs together. The lack of air dash, air blocking and constant pressure separates this from the ‘anime’ style of game. Don’t worry though you have massive supers that do 40% and show the ladies in all their glory.

The game is a counterhit paradise; if you’ve played SFV it’s similar to crush counters except this whole game revolves around it. Without counter hit you might be doing 15%-20% damage, on CH we’re talking 40-75% damage depending on your resources.

Overall it’s very easy to pick up and play, you can freestyle for the most part and a lot of the characters have universal systems. There’s universal overheads, launchers and sweeps, allowing you to jump between characters and have an understanding with no problem.

The major problem is this game has been out for some time on the JPN PSN and even longer in arcades, you might have a hard time finding games outside of launch. Easy to get into, but hard to master. The combat revolves around the basics, meaning knowing when to charge in or hold back and being able to string in small combos whenever the opportunity presents itself, will earn you victory. Like most Anime-style fighters you are able to combine dizzyingly long combos but it requires to make use of the high-risk, high Reward Fatal Counter system. The Fatal Counter system can be triggered by counter-attacking with Slow/Heavy attacks that give opponents a larger window of opportunity for quick jabs+special moves, showing the need for patience and proper timing.

Like most fighters nowadays, Koihime Enbu has a training mode in which players can test out moves against CPU opponents in a variety of situations (crouching, jumping ect...) The training mode also acts as the challenge mode that goes from basic special moves to longer complicated combos.

Each character has a limited but diverse moveset which can be used with different tactics, giving each character a unique feel. Along with the 13 characters, there is also a choice between 2 tacticians per faction Shoku (Kan'u, Bachou ,Chō'un, Chōhi), Gi (Sōsō, Kakōton, Kakōen, Gakushin) and Go (Sonken, Sonshōkō, Kannei, Shūtai) with the exception of Ryofu who only gets 1. Tacticians can fit a variety of purposes from being able to prolong combos to the ability to create an opening needed to gain ground.

There is a scenario mode that includes dialogue before and after a fight that can somewhat show the relationship between characters, but in order to fully understand, prior knowledge from the Visual novels (or anime at least) is required. By completing a Character scenario you are rewarded with an event and a gorgeous CG that will be added to the In-game gallery.

Overall, Koihime Enbu is a solid 2D fighter thats great for beginners and advanced players, being able to teach the core skills needed in any fighting game and still provide a decent challenge.
