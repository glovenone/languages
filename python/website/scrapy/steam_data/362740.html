About This Game

Paranormal activity and freakish events stalk the land. The dead haunt the living and take possession of their homes….

In 1947, small-town girl Ashley Reeves discovers she has a talent to connect with the supernatural. She dreams about a child whose being held captive in an old mansion. Upon investigation she finds a house full of restless ghosts. Help Jessica release the bizarre spirits to the hereafter, while saving the life of the young girl in her dreams.

-Captivating storyline with a diary of event to trace your evolution throughout the duration of the game.
-Play in “Adventure mode” and “Casual mode”.
-Spellbinding gameplay, combining point & click, puzzle solving and endless mini-games.

Short version: 68%
Age of Enigma is a collection of simple puzzles of little variety, put into an equally repetitive storyline about six ghosts and their incredibly short tales.

Long version:
Age of Enigma: The Secret of the Sixth Ghost is a mostly forgotten smartphone game of a mostly forgotten French independent studio. Like most of its kind, it is extremely short and overpriced.

The game mechanics are similar to a hidden object game, only there are no hidden object scenes: you just gather obviously placed items from the rooms then use them to access very simple, childish puzzles, which quickly start repeating themselves, especially in the fifth chapter.

The story is about a young woman who is lured into a mansion (what else?) to help six ghosts. All six stages consist of a quick one-room segment where you gain the trust of the ghosts, then a 3-4 screen part where you solve their riddle. It is very straightforward, very simple, but mostly nothing but an excuse to draw some pretty backgrounds.

Odd little hidden object game, though it is not hidden object in the traditional sense in having a list of names of objects for which to find. Rather, there are items that need to be constructed at various times, such as finding the six pieces of a spear to give to a ghost. It is almost a point & click game, but while there is definitely pointing and clicking, it is more along the lines of what you typically see in hidden object games that have additional puzzles. For instance, there you might be asked to match pieces of fresco to where they fit in the scene, or count the number of cubes in a set of blocks.

You do have to collect objects and assemble them, but unlike most point & click games, you are pretty much told exactly what to do, and there is little doubt most of the time as to what to do next. If you have any doubt, you can go to your handy guide which has a list of current tasks, in case you are totally brain dead, and you can click a question in which the answer will show that pretty much explicitly tells you exactly what is needed to be done to complete the task. It's unusual to see what amounts to a hint guide (to this extent) built into a game, but this game seems to be designed for a ten year old who might get confused at times.

While the artwork is hand drawn, it is not animated as one might normally expect, but rather you might see a dog running as being indicated as five individual drawings of the dog shown one after the other superimposed on top of the scene. Or another example is when the screen shakes a little to indicate an earthquake. This lack of animation hinders the game at times, since it can be quite distracting.

This game reminded me a little of the hidden object game Mirror Mysteries

http://store.steampowered.com/app/291630/

but unlike that game which I felt compelled to finish to the end, I started to find this game quite tedious after finishing with the first ghost given that it holds your hand the whole way and the puzzles and hidden objects (such as they are) present little challenge.

So is this game worth $10? Definitely not. Is it worth perhaps a buck or as part of a bundle (which is how I got it)? Only if you have a kid who has never played this kind of game before and needs a helping hand.

Age of Enigma is a decent casual collection of diverse minigames/puzzles connected by a linear ghost story. It took me ~3 hours. The game resolution is fixed at 1024x768 (fullscreen or windowed).

There are "casual" and "adventure" modes, I played the latter and still found it fairly casual. The game's decent, the art is decent, the sounds are decent, the voiceovers, hmm, the shadow guy may be a little below par. I couldn't find anything outstanding about this game, either positively or negatively. It is no hidden object game, but in many respects similar to one.

It's ok if you like that sort of thing and can pick it up cheaply (I got it in a bundle), but otherwise, I wouldn't recommend it. If you're looking for a casual puzzle game with excellent art and writing, check out Puzzle Agent instead - it's better, cheaper, and funnier (and also a little mysterious and scary). If you want to be truly amazed by a casual puzzle game, get The Room.
