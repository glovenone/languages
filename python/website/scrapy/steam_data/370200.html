About This Game

Play as Therese, who's somehow found herself unable to leave a cathedral that she doesn't remember entering. Things only get stranger when a young boy, Eldin, who Therese has never met claims to know her. Eldin claims that the two of you are sharing a dream-- a dream that neither of you can wake up from. What can you believe? Who can you trust? Dive in and try to uncover the secrets hidden within, and outside, of your dreams...

This visual novel features:
+Over thirty unique illustrations
+Seven original music tracks
+Fully animated character sprites
+Two full length parts to the story
+Over one hundred pages of dialogue
+Bilingual in both English and German

Wow. This is probably the 'game' I liked least this year.

So, why?

There is exactly one puzzle to solve in this game. The puzzle? The fact that you have to quit out of the first part of the game, to start the second part of the game. With the game giving you a warning that you should 'finish part one' first, even though there's nothing to 'finish' -- aside from getting into the endless loop.

The story started off cryptic and interesting. Honestly, I was expecting something like 9-9-9 / Zero Escape here. The theme could have been used well. Instead, it was wasted.

There are literally no choices in the game. And, realistically, there is no *ending* to the game. Nothing is resolved, because the entire concept is an endless loop. That means that the entire game is one big tease. You're left with questions,

Ultimately, this is not a game. It's not even like 9-9-9 on the iOS, where you make a few branching path choices...because there's no choice the player ever makes in the game. *YOU* are not wanted here, aside from as an observer.

I'm glad I got this game as part of a bundle, because I can't condone paying for it.

So, I am new to visual novels, as most of what I've seen out there doesn't appeal to me. I'm not interested in dating sims / otome, and I'm not interested in hentai or anything explicit. What I *am* interested in is a good story / good storytelling. An emotional journey. Interesting characters. That kind of stuff. So it seems like there should be VNs out there that have the great story/characters, without some of the other stuff that gets a lot of attention. Oh, and I also didn't want a game where you had to play it a dozen times to get all of the endings (and achievements).

So that brought me to this game, which (after being bundled) seemed like a low-cost entry into the genre and didn't have any of the obvious things I avoid.

Unfortunately, this game at least didn't work for me.

For one thing, as some have pointed out, it doesn't have choices, it's pretty much just click-to-read. Maybe if I'd realized that in advance, I wouldn't have been surprised / put off by it. Some of the story was interesting enough. The premise and subject matter was good. The most frustrating part was that there are two "parts" of the story, but they are not connected in-game. It's like you have to know you've done all you can in Part 1, then exit and start Part 2 to continue. There wasn't a way to connect them in the game itself? The result was that I was scrambling around for a LONG time (check out my playtime) trying to figure out what to do to continue the story. The answer, it turned out, was to exit and start Part 2. Meh.

Anyway, I know this game isn't a crowning achievement of VNs, and maybe not the best introduction to the genre, but I can't really recommend it unless, I don't know, you've played all the VNs out there and noticed that you missed this one.

It's pretty enough, and the first part is compelling.

But from the first episode looping on itself, to the animations, there's a distinct feel of lack of polish.

The "ending" of the second part starts falling apart into vagueness, losing the strong flow of the story before then,

There's no actual ending, and no real resolutions or clarifications of note.

It really feels incomplete, like it was something intended for three parts, then the developer got rushed at the end of the 2nd episode, threw down a few tidbits, and wrapped it up.

Also, the gameplay time is maybe 15 minutes, perhaps longer if you're a slow reader.

Which, honestly, the game might have been worth $3 had it been a polished, 3-part story.
As is, it feels like you're overpaying for a demo- unfortunately.

Now, while I can't *recommend* the game for purchase, the fact that it IS fast and has some compelling elements- both on artwork and plot- means that I can easily recommend it for play, if you got it out of a bundle or something.
Take a few minutes, enjoy the art, and don't go in with any expectations of resolution, and you'll at least get a story snippet that, for the most part, is higher quality than that of most VNs.


If this had been a complete and polished experience, I likely could have given it a review of 4/5.
As is, it's more of a 3/5, and even then only if you view it as a free to play experience.
I can't honestly recommend paying anything for the game, given how incomplete it is, but I can recommend playing it.

Really, if you're the type who likes to support indie developers with good talent but incomplete projects, go ahead and throw in when the game is on sale/in a bundle.

If you're aiming for a complete game, or something that'll leave you with something more than a feeling of 'think what this could have been', you should probably avoid the game.

Kimchi Tea

Posted: 29 September, 2015
I like visual novels. I like short visual novels. If Two Steps Back were a free VN, I wouldn't have anything bad to say about it. The art and music are used effectively, and (although I'm not sure if this was intentional) the freakish animations help set the mood. The story and character development are insubstantial, but as a short horror story it works well enough.

But for a commercial release, I expect better effort when it comes to programming and UI design. The first part of the story ends in a repeating loop, at which point you're supposed to go back to the menu and start Part 2. There's no reason for this. Why not add unlockable choices to find your way to Part 2 from Part 1? Why not leave Part 2 locked until you've read Part 1, and then give some in-game indication that you should go back? Why not do literally anything else but leave Part 2 accessible from the beginning with a fourth wall breaking note that you should read Part 1 first? That's not just bad design; it's actively lazy. So is using the default Ren'Py UI.

I want to see more visual novels on Steam, so I hate giving one a negative review. But I really can't recommend Two Steps Back unless the designer gives it some serious polish.

10/10


This is a Visual Novel in which you are told the story from the perspective of one of the main characters - Therese.


Part One opens with death and determination.
Therese awakes in some sort of classical-yet-modern manor, totally clueless as to where, why, how. A young boy named Eldin makes an appearance and - after becoming confused - Therese and he subsequently strive to make sense of their predicament.
There is a confounding mystery to be hashed out, but the majority of the story is of the interactions between two (almost) strangers; of the bonds that can form in no time.

Part Two opens with a promise and a preamble.
Therese awakes in some sort of classical-yet-modern manor, agitated at not knowing where, why, how. A young boy named Eldin makes an appearance and - after becoming disturbed - Therese and he subsequently strive to make their way out of their predicament.
There is a suspicious conspiracy to be hashed out, but the majority of the story is of the interactions between two (almost) strangers; of the love that can seem otherwise.


The art is well-done. There is a stark contrast between some of it; it's obvious that those (background) images that are of lesser quality were made to appear as such for atmosphere. How? "View full-size version in browser" for the first advertised image.

As for the writing, other than a few absent apostrophes and commas, one use of an incorrect homonym, and one misspelling ("breath" for "breathe") - it's perfect English (as I know it at least, fer wutever dats werff). There is a good deal of stuttering ("A-are..." "E-Eldin..." etc.), if that grinds your gears.

The story is great. Will not spoil more'n I already have.

The soundtrack is the worst part of the game. Any collection of sounds confined to seperate "tracks" is owed the title, but I didn't consider the majority of what I heard to be a sampling of a soundtrack; ambience that will either hit you as spot-on or make you wince a bit. To be sure, there is some music and I enjoyed what of it I heard; the music obeys the tone and gestures of the characters (i.e. abrupt or no transitions) and most of the sound will be of the eerie-but-maybe-annoying ambience. And that's just a personal problem, as my ears are sensitive; isn't even the loud noises that bother me, I guess just the pitch of the chanting - crackled in my earholes, yo.

I recommend this game; I think the asking price is fair.

EDIT:
I played Part One twice; Part Two once. Read at my own pace (moderately fast(?)): 57 minutes.
Will probably play through again at least once more.
I would advise saving often, maybe when you hear the music change, just because there are several save slots, and some of the text is skip-worthy after having read twice, but some is worth re- and rereading. And you never know when those bits will come up.
