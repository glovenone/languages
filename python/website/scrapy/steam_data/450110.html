About This Game


Gherman Driven, a middle-aged man, fanatical conspiracy theorist, who has abandoned all joy of his life.
Family, friends, work. Gherman remained without anything because of his obsession with the "Philadelphia experiment", which led him to neglect every other aspect of his life.
11 years after the events of the alleged experiment, Gherman found a document that marked the coordinates of the military establishment in which all involved in the project were taken.
After spending all the savings of a lifetime for trip to Greenland, certainly he did not expect to find so much...

A story-driven horror where the goal is to find out what lies behind the experiment.
All that you have is a flashlight, a lighter, and your intelligence to solve various environmental puzzles, escape from unpleasant situations and hide from what you find.

This game was developed to be played perfectly in virtual reality.
Each menu, object, or text, will be implemented in the game to allow who will use the Head Mounted Display to have a realistic effect.
All you have to do to activate this mode, it's open the game menu and activate "VRMode" in the "Graphic Settings".
I'll hope you enjoy it.

I'm 20 years old, this is my first game and it's a one-man-project.
I learned to use the engine, techniques and everything related to the development of a video game with this project.
I hope you like, and leave feedback if you want :)

Amigdala

DISCLAIMER: I received this product for no cost for the purpose of reviewing.

Amigdala is a first-person horror game that puts you in the shoes of a man named Gherman. I won't go too much into the story to spoil it, but as you play the game, you're trying to uncover the secrets of an experiment that happened in the past. The game communicates to you via notes, memos and experiment logs that are left behind in the building. The game reminds me a lot of some older horror games such as Penumbra or Amnesia, but it still has some of it's own unique ideas. You can play the game with a virtual reality headset, or as is.

Graphics: 8 / 10

The visuals are really well done. The game looks super nice, The animation quality is very good and there are some nice visual effects as well. There's an outdoor setting which looks fantastic. The indoor, dark and grimy areas are very atmospheric, which is what you would expect from a game like this, although that also makes it sort of a double edged sword. There are many games with this sort of atmosphere and environment. This horror setting has been done quite a few times, although as this title is newer, it does also have the nice shine and polished feeling when playing, that certainly adds to the experience as well as make the game look great. The game's graphics add to the atmosphere, and when playing with a VR set, it's even more immersive. The game is optimized very well. As good as the graphics are, it's not a very demanding game, which is a good thing. It should run perfectly fine on mid-tier graphic cards, as well as some lower end ones.

Sound: 7 / 10

The sound assets are very generic and standard "horror-game" sounds. Although that may sound harsh, it's not really a bad thing, as it IS a horror game and the sound assets get the job done. The sound does not get annoying or repetive, and does add to the atmosphere and immersive experience. The sound quality is decent, and playing this with a good headset, as well a VR set up can really suck you in. There is some voice acting in the game, and again, it was decent but not amazing. It got the job done.

Mechanics: 7 / 10

This is a little bit harder to rate as the game does not have many mechanics. It's not a "walking-simulator" but there also aren't many actions or things to do . That being said, I don't believe the game actually needs more mechanics that it actually has, as it is a horror game that is meant to be more of an experience than anything else. You can use your lighter at times, and you do have to be mindful of battery life in your flashlight ELSE IT'S LIGHTS OUT (I thought it was a good pun). You can die from enemies and other certain situations, although enemies tend to move a bit slow (but then again, so do you). Hiding or running away from them are usually the best ways to deal with them as there are no weapons in the game, though it's easy to get lost within areas if you aren't careful.

Final Personal Remarks:

I actually don't play many horror games, but I did like this one. I do agree with most statements saying that the game is a generic horror game, though it looked and sounded great. The background noises and wonderful graphics made it something nice., and yes, because of the atmosphere, I was on edge while playing, and filled with suspense. It's very well-made, and great for people who own VR sets. Its also very impressive that it was made by one person. I do think people who haven't played much horror games, should try it, as well as people who own any virtual reality consoles.

Thanks to the developer for giving me the opportunity to review the game, as well as anyone who took this review into consideration, I hope it helped.

(FD: Review copy provided by developer)

Amigdala just about scrapes a passing mark on account of summoning a consistently grim atmosphere and a solid realisation of a disturbing military facility where skinless terrors from decades gone by go bump in the dark but buyer beware; it is not without its issues.

Based on the real life urban legend of the Philadelphia Experiment, an attempt by the U.S Navy to turn one of its ships invisible that resulted in mass crew insanity and several of them being teleported into the walls, the game plays its survival horror tropes close to home, communicating much of its story through memos and files and leaving you powerless to do anything but flee from the test subjects that lurk around you.

Of course, by 'flee', what I actually mean is 'back away slowly' as you are locked to one ambling speed for the entirety of the experience. While a lack of sprint key in a horror game is not automatically a terrible thing, it does start to feel like a ball and chain when there's as much constant backtracking required as there is here. Even with the sheer amount of save points lying around, death is an exhausting experience as you shuffle to regain lost ground, a particularly irritating example of which is tied to your light sources. Your flashlight requires a constant upkeep of batteries and so you will inevitably come to rely on the lighter to guide you, That is until you wander blindly into one of the game's many gas traps and blow yourself to pieces.

Amigdala looks lovely and, although I played it on the desktop, I can imagine it would be incredibly immersive in the VR environment it was designed for. Mechanically, it's by-the-numbers; competent enough to wave through for fans of the horror genre but not a particularly convincing argument for it.

I got the key indirect from the developer to write this review. It didn't influence my recommendation

I played this game for 2 days now and finished it after about 4 hours. I thought about it a while but finally came to the decision that I can't recommend it to everyone, but to those who are really fans of this genre or want to test their VR-Headsets.
The game isn't bad, but it's just not enough for a recommendation. If I have to give points, I would say it is a 5/10 where everything above 7 would get a recommendation.

What I liked about the game
The overall gameplay and atmosphere was very nice. During the 4 hours I was frightened from time to time. Not by jump scares, but by the overall atmosphere. Noises in the background, running out of battery. And the casual zombie/undead whats-o-ever around the corner.
And I liked that it is a pacifistic game, you don't have a weapon or traps you use to fight enemies, but rather evade them.

If you have issues with the game you can expect an answer from the dev within the same day. Either he supports you with helpful tips or will fix it in a future update.

What I didn't like
The first thing I misliked came right at the start of the game. Well, actually before it even started. You have to manually install the C++ runnables from the Microsoft Website. Why not use Steam to install that. I know it's annoying to wait for them to install, but doing it manually was much more annoying. But ok, this is a one-time process...

Then there is the main menu of the game. Nice idea starting in a cave and walking to the menu point. But to be honest, it took me over a minute to realize, that I have to walk to the floating text "new game" to be able to select it and it took me another 1-3 minutes to research for the button I have to press to interact with the menu item. Would have been awesome to just show the button text or key text instead of the word "interact".

It was very hard to follow the text and voice during the first cutscene, because the background audio was so loud, that I could hardly understand the talking. The subtitles helped a bit, if you look in the correct direction. After the cutscene I went to the menu, I saw a audio settings menu item, which can't be selected...
Sometimes during the game the voice talking and the subtitle are not the same, makes it harder to understand.
There are some errors in the texts, but since I am not a native speaker I won't comment or judge them.

The game itself starts with lowest video settings, not that big problem, but would have been nice, if it starts with your native screen resolution.

Really annoying was the fact that when you interact with a document (pick up and read) your flashlight turns off and you have to manually turn if on again. Same procedure when you have to load from a savegame. It's funny when you sit there, waiting for the game to load but eventually move the camera and you see that you were just staring at a black, unlit location and the game was already loaded.

I found the HUD not good. I misunderstood the battery icon for a long time. For me it fills over time and whenever I pickup a battery it drains my status... Perhaps it's just me, but I was confused in the first minutes.
Next confusing thing was, when there was a earthquake. This made the HUD to dance around, but the camera was stationary. My impression was, that the HUD is some kind of smart goggles, but that wouldn't explain the dancing HUD. But ok, this situation doesn't appear too often.

I guess the HUD is virtually floating in front of the camera. So whenever you are standing next to a wall or door the HUD will disappear in the wall :(

Conclusion
If you are a real horror fan and want to test your VR-Headset, go and grab the game. Maybe you want to wait till it appears in a bundle or it is on sale. But I guess you will like it.

For a one-man project this game is really good. It has really nice visuals and a nice atmosphere. But it is just not good enough for me to recommend it to all users.

Amigdala is a courageously, well optimized, stable running and atmospheric one man project accomplished by a very kind and supportive young developer.

Amigdala

This review was last edited after playing the game for about two-three hours. (The additional play time listed was whilst the game was running but I was not playing)

Short Review

An extremely atmospheric first person exploration/horror game which I found enjoyable for the first couple of hours but became a bit lack lustre after that.

Long Review

Note that although this game supports VR I did not play the game in this mode. I used my Xbox controller for the PC rather than the keyboard/mouse option that was also available. A number of different resolutions were available but if there was an option to run as a windowed application I didn't spot it so I played it in full screen mode, 1400x900 resolution. I found using headphones enhanced the experience significantly.

The idea of the game is to explore an abandoned military base in Greenland that is associated with the Philadelphia experiment and to discover what lies behind the experiment. The game is played in first person and you enter the base armed only with a flash light & a lighter. These become essential tools in underground areas with no lighting.

The opening area set the scene very well. The sound effects of footsteps in snow and the wind along with the spectacular scenery really gave a real feel for the environment. This building of atmosphere continues throughout much of the game.

As you move during the opening scene instructions are displayed on the screen informing you of the various controls.

The controls, whilst not entirely intuitive are easily picked up and work very well.

I found the game to be VERY atmospheric, the audio & visual providing a suitably uneasy feeling. I jumped when a light simply sparked. As for when I met the first adversary I died very quickly but mainly because I panicked, pressed the next use object key instead of the run key chose the lighter, managed to start running and promptly blew myself up running through some escaping gases and so died. Definitely no complaints about that as it was genuine panic I felt and I had a good chuckle about it straight after :)

There is a display for the amount of battery left for your flash light and managing this becomes a key part of the game. You handily come across batteries that you can pick up and use; often enough that with careful management you can use your flash light intermittently to find your way around but not often enough to have it on permanently. You cannot store multiple batteries so you must remember where you came across them so you can return to pick them up when your flash light battery becomes low.

As far as I can tell you can use your lighter indefinitely but bear in mind its a naked light in a confined underground location with fractured pipes .. If you are careful you can often see split pipes and see & hear the gases escaping from them before you get close enough; other times the first time you know about escaping gas is a big BOOM

When you can interact with an item this is clearly displayed on the screen. Typical items you can interact with are documents which you can read and handles you can turn.

There is a run button available which utilises a stamina meter which reduces as you run and once at zero you can't run until it recharges again.

You can only save at set points in the game and you are prompted on screen when you are at one of these points. If when you die you restart at your last save point.

Having good spacial awareness helps in this game, particularly with regards to turning handles which affects objects in the same XY but different Z co ordinates. Maybe having a compass bearing displayed on the screen would help?

For the first few hours the game was enjoyable but then I started to find that it became a bit tedious I'd get blown up using the lighter, return to my previous save point, redo the last 5 minutes again but this time swap the lighter for the flashpoint just before I got to the point where I knew the lighter would blow me up. I'd often find myself periodically returning to a save point which could involve several minutes travelling through empty corridors and then making the same journey back again just to ensure I didn't lose significant exploring time.

In the end I stopped playing because I appeared to have twiddled all the knobs available, explored all the areas I available and yet I couldn't figure out what to do next. I knew where a number of locked doors were but there was no interaction available for them. Maybe I just missed a lever somewhere or even an exit but the sad thing is I just got bored wandering around trying to find what I had missed.

It's a real shame because I was enjoying the game up until this happened and the game certainly has a lot of potential. Maybe when there are some walk throughs available for the game I'll revisit the it and slap my forehead, realising I'd missed something obvious. Sometimes that happens.

I wanted to recommend this game but in all honesty I can't. Whilst I enjoyed it for a few hours I wanted to progress more than I did for me to give it the thumbs up. This is one review where if there was a neutal option I'd have selected it.

Pros
  • Very atmospheric with good use of graphics and excellent use of audio to enhance the experience

Cons
  • When I first went to play this game I got an error message warning that the C++ 2015 Runtime was required. I used the following link to download & install it https://support.microsoft.com/en-gb/kb/2977003. I would usually expect the game installer to take care of this for me.
  • The initial introduction was a little fast for me to read, it could have done with a button press to proceed to the next screen. The issue did continue with dialogues, the text disappeared too quickly. Whilst there is audio at the same time it is heavily accented and the volume compared with the sound effects is low so the odd word can be difficult to make out and so referring to the text would be useful
  • This game suffers from the same issue many games of this type do, namely if you miss an object/opening you can wander around aimlessly for ages trying to find it. This is exacerbated when you are turning handles that affect things not in your immediate surroundings.
  • Once you locate the save points they are reasonably well spaced but if you don't happen to notice one (which happened to me) you can have a long walk back to the previous one.
  • Whilst I could always see escaping gases with the flash light sometimes I couldn't with the lighter which seemed a bit odd - but maybe this was an issue with my graphics card, I'm not sure.
  • I had one area where I could only pass one way through what appeared to be some blocks that were the remnant of the explosion that revealed the passageway into the area.

Marks Out Of 5

5) Must have - stop whatever you're playing now and play this
4) Worth the asking price and considering as your next purchase
3) Maybe purchase if you're stuck for choosing your next game or mildly discounted
2) Only purchase if it is heavily discounted
1) Not worth getting even if given away free
