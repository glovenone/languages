About This Game

Close Combat: Panthers in the Fog details the desperate German counter-attack at Mortain, the last chance of the Wehrmacht to stave off defeat in Normandy.

Can you match the tenacity of the American defenders of Hill 314? Or can you succeed where the Panzers failed, driving through to the sea and changing history?

Play single battles, linked operations, or even the Grand campaign simulating all six crucial days of fighting. With 35 linked battlefields and historically accurate formations and units, strategic choices are as important as tactical prowess. New features of long-range interdiction, strategic high ground and adverse weather conditions bring an insight into the challenges faced by those who commanded both sides in this pivotal WW2 battle.

Command your forces on the strategic level, issuing orders to battalion or regimental-sized units to attack or defend, and allocate air or artillery support. Then fight out the resulting engagements in a highly-detailed, real-time simulation of World War II tactical combat. The results of the tactical battle determine who controls each area, and where they can move at the strategic level.

For the Allies the Normandy landings of June were followed by two months of hard fighting in difficult terrain against a tenacious German defense. But in late July the U.S. Army’s Operation Cobra has finally created a decisive breach in the weakened German line. Patton’s 3rd Army races through the opening along the western coast of France and into the clear, driving for the Brittany Ports. Faced with the choice between retreat and last-ditch counter-attack, the Germans struggle to assemble a panzer force for one more charge. Can the Germans move quickly enough to overcome a desperate situation, push through to Avranches on the French coast, and cut off Patton’s racing spearheads? Or will the thinly stretched Americans troops screening Patton’s flank be able to hold out, regroup, and hold the vital lifeline open?

FEATURES
  • Completely redone and revitalized 32-bit graphics give the game an all-new look and feel.
  • Issues orders to your major units on a strategic level, and then fight each engagement at the tactical level.
  • Accurately depicts World War II tactical warfare and its challenges in real-time.
  • Proven psychological model influences the behavior of your men in combat.
  • Each individual soldier may tire, gain experience, and gain or lose morale after every battle. Manage your forces and preserve your veteran soldiers to field the most effective force possible.
  • Includes a wide variety of historical squads, weapons, vehicles, aircraft, and artillery.
  • Mount troops or guns on vehicles during tactical battles.
  • Accurate and realistic equipment modeling.
  • Weather conditions, including night and fog influence the battle at all levels.
  • Use strategic-level air and artillery interdiction to block or slow the movement of enemy forces.
  • Call in close air support, mortar support, and indirect fire support at the tactical level.
  • Integrated multi-player lobby and match-making forums.
  • Enhanced Scenario Editor - Create your own "what if" Scenarios.
  • A host of adjustable game settings including speed scroll speed, sound volume, and UI layout.
  • Enhanced to run well on modern DirectX systems
  • Enjoy the huge variety of modifications available from the Close Combat Community providing years of additional gaming pleasure.

I'll start by saying that this game is seriously under-rated, when you leave out the price tag. Me, I paid the full price because I was excited. I know there's a few people on here that had purchased the game and were disappointed. There's a few points I want to hit on:

1. The Price

There's two sides to this coin. It really comes down to whether or nor you'd want to spend the money on the game. If you're a fan of the franchise, then yes you should buy it (especially if you're a die hard like me). If you don't want to spend that money, then don't. Who cares about the price - it's your money, dude.

2. The Engine

This is where I get kinda irritated about some of the existing reviews. Honestly, man, it's a 2D top-down tactical game. How good can the engine actually get? Yes, Gateway to Caen was graphically better, but they're using the exact same engine as Panthers in the Fog. They added 32-bit into the engine because it allowed for better graphical animations and transluscency. It's really not that big of a deal, guys. It's Close Combat, and anybody that's been playing the series for a long time will tell you the graphics have pretty much always been the same. I re-iterate: IT'S NOT THAT BIG OF A DEAL. Close Combat has always looked like this. The upgraded engine has slightly better AI and better stability, as well. As far as I'm concerned, this is not a game-breaking issue. Hell, when I play it I don't even think about that. Close Combat was never about the engine - it was about the gameplay.

3. Less Content

Let's take a look back into the majority of the series. Each game, with the exception being the re-releases like the Longest Day, have all been unique in their setting. In this case, it's the German counter-attack known as Operation Luttich. Me, I dont gripe about the content when it comes to Close Combat. Historically, those are combat elements that took place in the battle. That's the way it is, so you really don't have a position to complain about it. Personally, I wouldn't place that among the ranks for a review. Yes, it has less diverse unit selections, and yes, it has slightly fewer maps. Unless you're expecting them to cover ther entire war's range of the unit types and the majority of the Bocage, this isn't an issue.

So, that's the rant of this review. Now to core of it:

It's the classic simple interface I've come to know and love about Close Combat. It's 2D bird's eye view, and I think that's awesome. There's alot of depth they've put into this particular perspective, and all it would take is a time or two through the tutorial and you're ready to rock. Also, there is a level of depth in tactical decisions in this game that I think a good majority of the existing reviews have overlooked, from elevation all the way to effective ranges of period weapons and the fact that a mortar team has to zero in on a series of coordinates rather than the round magically landing where it needs to be. If you make a mistake in a battle, you pay for it. That's strategy, and unless you learn tactics you're going to get your ♥♥♥ kicked. 'Nuff said. The graphics and gameplay is simple enough to master pretty quickly, yet with enough depth to become a good simulation, simulating morale, cover, etc.

My conclusion is that if you're a big fan of strategic, tactical gameplay and you're a history buff about world war 2, this is definately a game you should consider.

Close Combat has always been my favorite wargame series. I cut my teeth on the game with Close Combat 5: Invasion Normandy, went back and bought all of the previous ones, and even bought a few of the later entries into the series. For a while the series fell off of my radar, but I recently noticed this game on Steam and decided to purchase it.

I can safely say that Close Combat is still amazing. Panthers in the Fog feels like the better installments of the game, yet is upgraded, with more realistic reinforcement pools and better graphics. The game is still very detailed, with each soldier's mental state, health and supplies tracked, but never in a way that requires micromanagement of any kind. One of the things I always loved about Close Combat, thanks to these features, is how an unintentional narrative can unfold around squads of men under your command. The ability to rename squads allows you to give them nicknames based on this unintentional narrative.

The maps are not 3D, which may turn some newer gamers off, but really, they're beautifully painted maps with a ton of detail (remember that holding the right mouse button allows you to see the elevation and type of cover under the moused-over area, which is important for keeping squads alive).

The game is much slower-paced than more recent WWII wargames. I just did a battle that lasted a good 45 minutes because the AI and I were trying to outmaneuver one-another's tanks without really firing any shots. But that is part of the appeal of this game and other Close Combat games: the level of tactical depth is hard to find these days. Strategy will make or break your victory chances.

There are some things I wish they would move into 2015 though: why are tanks still silent? We should be hearing them echoing across the battlefield. And where are my weather effects? Still, these are all such minor details compared to the things that make Panthers In The Fog and other Close Combat games so good. This game is a worthy entry into the franchise.

10/10.

I love Steam, but in this case I hate the simple review system that limits the final review score to recommend yes or no and the aggregate that determines the final rating.

The original Close Combat was a game changer.

No longer were "realistic" wargames limited to turnbased hex gameplay with complex computations, but basic visuals. Not only did Close Combat look good, but it was real time and easy to play.

With an emphasis on men and their morale you experienced the thrill of getting your men through the fight in one piece.

In short you really cared and were excited about what happened on the battlefield.

That was back in the mid nineties.

Close Combat Panthers in the Fog (2012) does a reasonable job in extending the original experience, but doing so with the original engine, even when it is tweaked to include 32bit hires graphics and an improved strategic layer, does limit its appeal to gamers that can look beyond the age and structural limitations it has inherited.

Here's when I'd like something more than a simple yes or no recommendation.
This game can easily be recommended to war gamers who enjoy the Close Combat-series.

But I would never recommend this to someone who's into more dynamic game play and up to date visual experience.

Of course the developers are equally aware of the situation, so work continues on Close Combat Bloody First with its new 3d Unity based engine. This will finally allow the basic Close Combat game to progress beyond the limitations of a 20 year old game engine.

I'd describe Close Combat as a light company level war game. Fun for a quick round, but without becoming too casual or a RTS click fest.

The good thing about Close Combat in general is that it is easy to play, but with enough depth and a good sense of history to draw in the more advanced war gamer.

The continued (niche) success of the Close Combat series is probably caused by a lack of alternatives: RT, WW2, top down, easy to manage and historical.

Close Combat still stands alone in its particular niche.

To sum it up.

Plus:
- It is Close Combat
- Easy to get into
- WW2

Minus:
- It is still the old (albeit improved) Close Combat engine
- AI pathfinding can (still) be terrible - btw not unique to CC
- price

The old engine combined with the high price are imho the most limiting factors in recommending this game to anyone but fans of the series.

As an old fan though I am enjoying my "relapse" into the series and I will certainly buy Bloody First and any spin off for some time to come!

If you make a mistake in a battle, you pay for it. That's strategy, and unless you learn tactics you're going to get your ♥♥♥ kicked. 'Nuff said. The graphics and gameplay is simple enough to master pretty quickly, yet with enough depth to become a good simulation, simulating morale, cover, etc.

My conclusion is that if you're a big fan of strategic, tactical gameplay and you're a history buff about world war 2, this is definately a game you should consider.
