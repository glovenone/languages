About This Game

To Burn in Memory is a free, ahistorical and atemporal Interactive Fiction work released for IFComp 2015. Explore a city that never existed, and uncover its secret history through the memories of those caught in the chaos of a Europe spiraling ever faster into war.

Featuring a fully original soundtrack by the author, with illustration by graphic design maestro Johannes Poell.

I recommend this interactive story if you enjoy reading light novels. I don't recommend it to people who want to "play" a game because it's not really a game. There are elements of puzzles in here, but it's so easy to figure out, and it goes along with the story that helps you unlock more story. But this isn't the reason I'm not recommending it.

This game is pure text and clicking. There is no music, and its a 2 hour game. There is only reading. But I like that you can see the items you pick up on the right side. I don't mind the text being white on black, but I understand some people find it irritating. I feel like the black background is important just because the black helps us focus our mind more to reading what the author is trying to show us.

But here are some things I found that made it rather 'annoying,' and why I'm not recommending it: first off, my visual memory is way better than trying to "picture words," so a lot of the times I'm reclicking back and forth to the same 2 or 3 areas. I think this has a lot to do with visual memory and also UI. I understand its text based, but it's easier to see that you can go 'back' into an area if the words are on the left, or forward in an area if the next place to visit is on the 'right.' Instead of the words being on top of each other -- which is a little bit unintuitive if you're entering into a room's door, but there are words at the bottom you don't realize at first that when you click makes you go back to the previous room if that makes any sense...

I'll be honest though, the story itself can be rather difficult to follow, and I have a hard grasp of actually understanding the ending... I feel like the elements and fancy wording detract from the story -- there were areas that I felt like the background was just kinda there and some stuff made no sense and didn't connect well with the overall story. It also seemed like its based on reality in the 1900s but with advanced technology and a group of people called, "Provost" but to be honest, I don't know what the provost is even after finishing because there wasn't a clear explanation on it. There wasn't any part of it where I was like, "Oh yeah! I totally see/didn't see that coming!" This story has drama, but there's no conflict -- you're just trying to find out what happened, but I find that I don't really care for it. The only thing that kept me going to the end was, I knew the ending was near and that I wanted all the Steam completions.... I hadn't tried a visual novel since Cartoon Network's Space Ghost Coast to Coast text best game, and it was soooo long ago. But I'm definitely game to see more that are imaginatively engaging and had a bit more time put into it.

TL;DR: A surprisingly well-written text-based experience! While the game's design lacks some fundamental elements found in other titles of its genre, To Burn In Memory overcomes this by presenting a rich and in-depth story. 8/10.

Long Review:
To Burn In Memory is an extremely well written text-based adventure. Unlike the numerous poorly written click-through adventures on the Steam Store, this title is reminiscent of both traditional MUDs as well as interactive adventure novels of old.

To simply compare this to other interactive adventures is to do it a great disservice. The writing style and overall quality is above average. The author paints a perfect picture of a city devastated by warfare: riddled with death and destruction and yet maintaining the notion that it was once great. The use of imagery through describing colors and the city's architecture make game's setting well fleshed out. Even the selection of quotes in the game's introduction are well selected.

While I don't want to give too much away, To Burn In Memory seemingly takes place in alternate re-telling telling of our own history (yet I'll let the narrative speak for itself as some things aren't clearly explained). The few technological innovations illustrated seem to be almost steampunk-ish, plausible in their "old world" existence but dissimilar from our own (at least my grandparents never gave me an Oroboros trinket). This story is told through "memories" that you'll experience along the way, learning of the city's hellish past and the lives of its denizens.

Telling the story is one of the game's strengths but there are a few glaring weaknesses. There's no Steamworks integration at all: no cloud saving (or any other kind of saving), achievements for seeing the title through to the end, or screenshot capability (if you have a favorite moment or like a well written segment of the story, you can't store it for later).

Another weakness - why I can't give this a higher rating - is that the in-game navigation is terrible. With the exception of Free Roam mode, you're supposed to collect items around the city in order to advance through locked areas. But losing your way is easy: there's no compass and the descriptions do not always match up with the directional options. What's the point in putting "east" or "south" in the choices if we don't know what's in the north or west (just an example)? Many MUDs had better directional control, many interactive stories do as well. I get that there are only a few options for movement but it probably could have been handled better.

There's also a negative that can actually be taken as a positive: the story is extremely linear. This isn't "choose your own ending." Because "north or west" aren't defined, the story's path is usually pretty clear. The narrative is actually tight because of this clarity. It also makes the game less "game-like" and more like reading a story (though the inclusion of background music probably wouldn't have hurt). Some think this is a negative, I don't. But the game should also be tagged as a text-based adventure since some gamers obviously don't read the reviews before installing.

I'm a huge fan in the way in which the story unfolds on screen. The story's elements present themselves as memories: initially you start with little paragraphs of text and to see the whole history of a thing, you need only click to "experience" it. Additionally, you can rush the entire game in Free Mode by not reading any of the memory entries (I'm not really sure why anyone would do this!)

Collectables needed to unlock areas of the game to advance are listed on the right-hand side of the screen. The only collectable I had diffculty with obtaining was the "crescent moon key" but I associated this with poor directional control rather than defect or error.

Overall this was a great experience and - if in novel form - I'd purchase it in a second. Keep up the good work. /wall of text over :)

This is an absolutely gorgeous piece of interactive fiction. Yes, it's a game! No, it's not going to let you shoot things. Sort of.

If you've played Fallen London, this is pretty similar, though it's built on something like twine rather than storynexus. Lots of reading, lots of vivid descriptions of fictional places in historical settings (seriously the memories are some of my favorite things). I've only played it the once so far, but I'm pretty sure it's joining Anchorhead and basically anything by Emily Short in my replay bin.

I think it's only got the one ending (it feels like it's got only the one ending, if that makes any sense), and since the plot is wandering around the ashes of a once-thriving city, learning about how it came to be ashes through the memories of the people who lived and fought in it, there isn't anything to change, any more than wandering Rome with a history book would let you change the past.

Because it's built on something twine-ish, it's a great introduction to IF as a genre, because it skips out on a lot of the frustrations more 'traditional' IF can run into (guess the verb and blocking yourself into a no win corner, in particular), and the writing, as I said, is astonishingly gorgeous. The whole game sort of sneaks up on you, where one moment you're exploring an opera house and the next you're crying because everything is beautiful and everything hurts.

I didn't find any of the puzzles particularly tricky, but I have a background in the era it's set in (art history making itself useful, finally), which definitely helped. I'm 98% sure that a little googlefu could get you the answers, though, as there wasn't anything particularly obscure about the answers.

tl;dr It's interactive fiction, read everything, pay attention, enjoy yourself.
