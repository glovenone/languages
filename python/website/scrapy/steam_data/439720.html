About This Game

Unending Galaxy is a unique mix of space-sim and 4X strategy.

Explore a vast and dynamic universe in which the different factions conquer new territories, colonize planets, and conduct diplomacy. Start as a simple pilot and amass wealth through trading, mining, and fighting. Turn this wealth into new stations and warships that you will use to carve the frontiers of your own empire. Or do none of that and become a powerful pirate and war profiteer by turning other factions against one another.

  • Multiple maps featuring hundreds of sectors and thousands of NPC
  • Includes multiple hand-crafted maps and a procedural map generator
  • Sandbox gameplay: mine, trade, build, colonize, fight, destroy, scavenge
  • Control multiple ships and switch between them at will
  • 10 factions, 90 ships and 70 station types, dozens of weapons
  • No in-game loading screens, real time gameplay with active pause
  • Powerful modding toolkit included

Unending Galaxy is a fast-paced 4x real-time empire builder. there's a few issues, but it's got a LOT going for it, not the elast of which being the active developer who is constant working and updating the game, something that is unfortunately rare in the current climate of early access and greenlight trash that permeates the Steam.

down to business then:

Pros:

-Many (positive) similarities to the X series of games, specifically X3 - Terran Conflict, probably the best known of the franchise. This is mainly due to the developer being a serious X3 modder - he brought in all sorts of fancy empire management options and automation that is common in the X games.

-sophisticated mechanics that allow for deep gameplay

-variety of ways to play. you don't have to build an empire or colonize worlds, you can make plenty of money and get huge fleets going just by trading, mining, hunting bounties, and pirating.

-game explodes in complexity once you finally colonize a planet and get around to building industry infrastructure (for some this is a con, but if so, you're playing the wrong game bruh)

-game graphics are simple, 2d sprites, but this actually helps in terms of being able to clearly identify what is happening on the screen

-so many different maps, scenario starts, and game world customization options, that you could tool around with the diffenret starting options forever.

-Race/faction ships all play and feel genuinely differnet, races have well-established strengths and weaknesses.


Cons:

-UI is sometimes difficult to parse, there is a LOT information to be juggling in your head all at the same time, and those who aren't veterans of the X games or of large-scale 4x/real-time strategy games in general are going to feel overwhelmed.

-selecting ships and giving orders is much harder than it needs to be. too many menus to go through in order to give simple instructions. Dev has acknowledged some of this criticism and is actively working on parsing menus and generally improving the UI.

-it's not entirely clear what strengths and weaknesses certain weapons have, and which weapons might be more effective for certain situations.

-combat is simply too fast - large battleships can be reduced to cinders in seconds by a well-coordinated swarm. ships movement as well, is gnereally speaking very very quick given the size of the individual sectors - if the sectors in space were larger, the speed would be fine, but as it is, you traverse sectors and die in combat entirely too quickly. if you really want to keep your ships alive, you'll have to engage primarily in small-scale maneuvers and take personal control of your battleships so you can get them out of harms way quickly

-diplomacy is basically non-existent - this is the only place where the game really falls down, IMO. There are no real permanent alliances or trade options, everything is on a timer and will eventually run out. the ai has no real sense, it seems, of where it's fleets are at and what kind of force distribution is going on - generally speaking, when it's time to go to war, the ai throws everything it has at you (and each other) and whoever happens to win the first major engagement will then proceed to (more or less) steamroll the loser. beyond this, the ai is EXTREMELY aggressive. their only long-term interest is war, and when you are just beginning a game, attempting to colonize a planet and build up your empire, you can easily get smashed before you can even get off the ground.

Most of the cons amount to simple fixes or adjustments in the way the game mechanics work. I'm confident that the dev will get things figured out.

I would reccommend this game for anyone who liked the X games or large scale space 4x games with a real-time twist. now is an especially good time to buy, seeing as there was just a big content update as well as a superb graphical update which really improves the overal look and feel of the game.

This little game actually surprised me.. Quite a bit so.. It's very simple, yet complex enough to be fulfilling. It has done an excellent job of finally satisfying that craving for a simple space empire/fleet building game.

There are a few things that I feel could have been done better, and i'll cover those second.. But first, onto the positives!

1- Simple and effecient!
This is where this game surprised me. I am really impressed with the control and management given to the players in this form. Want to play rpg style with a single ship as your own? Or have a small fleet of escorts? You can do that. Want to play rts style where you tell a few ships, or large quantities of ships to perform specific actions? You can do that too. Want to just sit back, and stare at a spreadsheet and operate your vastly growing empire without directly controlling any ships? You can do that too, All the while not being hassled to mine/farm repetatively for 20 hours before you start your own empire. You can do that right off the bat. Additionally, while you do have control over your own faction ships and structures, civillian activity is present aswell. The structures you build will automatically spawn civillian ships which will act like free-willed npcs to participate in the growth of your empire. Whether that be mining, trading, or even bounty hunting. You do not need to manage them on your own, and there is no loss when they get their little pea shooter box-car bounty-hunter blown up by a dreadnaught pirate.


Unending Galaxy has managed to mash all this together in one simple and efficient design where one method of play does not clash with another, all in the same game session. Nor are you required to do one over the other. The controls are simple and straightforward enough, without the hassle of selecting gamemodes or setting things up. Simply generate your world, and off you go.

The AI works. You can assign different roles and goals for all your ships individually, or in groups, and the optional automation is self-sufficient.

Now. There are a few things that you need to be wary of though. They are not greatly impacting in terms of gameplay, but they may be unappealing to some.

1- The graphics. While this does not hold a huge impact on my judgement for this game (as i've all but been able to see past it all), the game does not use original assets, and the most beautiful visuals are the planets. (Like, really beautiful!).. Some assets are open source assets, which included a ship i used to make my first mod in Endless Sky (That was amusing to see!)

2- Spaceship movement. First and foremost, while I can't confirm this, I feel like the ships use sprite sheets rather than a single sprite rotation system. This means, that while there is a 360 degree turning and piloting mechanism, the sprites do not support directional visuals for all 360 degrees. It feels more like there is a sprite every 15 or so degrees, so rather than seemless turning, it is jolty. This was a huge turn off when i first started playing, but it didn't take long for me to forget it was there.

3- Lastly.. Crashes..
That dreaded thing that no one wants to see in a review of a full release game. On the bright side, the dev(s) are still actively patching or otherwise working on improving stability.. As things are now though, I believe i've experienced a crash every 2-3 hours of gameplay.. Admittedly though, i'm extremely tolerant of crashing issues, provided they don't happen every 15 minutes...

One final note, is that when I first got this game, I was not able to launch it. I was having issues that was ultimately determined to be my AV.. But before that was figured out, the dev was very helpful in trying to get my issue resolved, and even offered that if my attempts to make the game work went past the refund policy for steam, he would send them an exception to allow me to refund it. I'm glad that it was not necessary, as I've had a lot of enjoyment with this game.

Unending Galaxy is a fast-paced 4x real-time empire builder. there's a few issues, but it's got a LOT going for it, not the elast of which being the active developer who is constant working and updating the game, something that is unfortunately rare in the current climate of early access and greenlight trash that permeates the Steam.

down to business then:

Pros:

-Many (positive) similarities to the X series of games, specifically X3 - Terran Conflict, probably the best known of the franchise. This is mainly due to the developer being a serious X3 modder - he brought in all sorts of fancy empire management options and automation that is common in the X games.

-sophisticated mechanics that allow for deep gameplay

-variety of ways to play. you don't have to build an empire or colonize worlds, you can make plenty of money and get huge fleets going just by trading, mining, hunting bounties, and pirating.

-game explodes in complexity once you finally colonize a planet and get around to building industry infrastructure (for some this is a con, but if so, you're playing the wrong game bruh)

-game graphics are simple, 2d sprites, but this actually helps in terms of being able to clearly identify what is happening on the screen

-so many different maps, scenario starts, and game world customization options, that you could tool around with the diffenret starting options forever.

-Race/faction ships all play and feel genuinely differnet, races have well-established strengths and weaknesses.


Cons:

-UI is sometimes difficult to parse, there is a LOT information to be juggling in your head all at the same time, and those who aren't veterans of the X games or of large-scale 4x/real-time strategy games in general are going to feel overwhelmed.

-selecting ships and giving orders is much harder than it needs to be. too many menus to go through in order to give simple instructions. Dev has acknowledged some of this criticism and is actively working on parsing menus and generally improving the UI.

-it's not entirely clear what strengths and weaknesses certain weapons have, and which weapons might be more effective for certain situations.

-combat is simply too fast - large battleships can be reduced to cinders in seconds by a well-coordinated swarm. ships movement as well, is gnereally speaking very very quick given the size of the individual sectors - if the sectors in space were larger, the speed would be fine, but as it is, you traverse sectors and die in combat entirely too quickly. if you really want to keep your ships alive, you'll have to engage primarily in small-scale maneuvers and take personal control of your battleships so you can get them out of harms way quickly

-diplomacy is basically non-existent - this is the only place where the game really falls down, IMO. There are no real permanent alliances or trade options, everything is on a timer and will eventually run out. the ai has no real sense, it seems, of where it's fleets are at and what kind of force distribution is going on - generally speaking, when it's time to go to war, the ai throws everything it has at you (and each other) and whoever happens to win the first major engagement will then proceed to (more or less) steamroll the loser. beyond this, the ai is EXTREMELY aggressive. their only long-term interest is war, and when you are just beginning a game, attempting to colonize a planet and build up your empire, you can easily get smashed before you can even get off the ground.

Most of the cons amount to simple fixes or adjustments in the way the game mechanics work. I'm confident that the dev will get things figured out.

I would reccommend this game for anyone who liked the X games or large scale space 4x games with a real-time twist. now is an especially good time to buy, seeing as there was just a big content update as well as a superb graphical update which really improves the overal look and feel of the game.

I bought this game a year ago way before it was on Steam. It was a lot of fun then, and since then it has had numerous improvements and fixes and extra features added.

It's a sandbox top down space shooter with both manual and RTS controls and empire management. You can pilot your ship and go bounty hunting, fight pirates, or trade. As you get money you can buy more ships, set up automated traders, build stations of your own, and even start your own empire that can compete with and conquer the AI empires. You can fly your ships manually, or just tell them what to do and watch. In combat you can just give orders to your fleet, or hop around controlling ships willy nilly if you like. Or play a single ship and have the rest following your orders.

What I really love about it is what you do actually affects the universe.

If you're a bounty hunter, you aren't hunting randomly generated quest opponents - you are actually hunting threats to that empire - pirate and other ships (like Swarm) who have been destroying their ships and earning a bounty. You also have competition - other bounty hunters are actively hunting the same list of bounties.
If you do trade missions to bring supplies to end a shortage at a station, you actually supplied that station - which will then start producing stuff.
If you pirate ships carrying supplies for ship part factories for a faction, that faction will not only be out the ships you blew up in the process - but it will have less parts to build new ships.
If you blow up a big military fleet of a faction, that faction will be weakened against both you (if you're running an empire of your own) and other AI empires until it can rebuild that fleet.
If you set up stations to bolster a faction's economy, not only will you make money - the faction will be better off.

Plus there is a huge appeal to me with starting as a single ship and working your way up to conquering the map as a faction.

Starting options lets you get into the game how you like it - as a single ship, as a pre-existing empire, and a lot of options in between (like being in charge of a small pirate fleet). You can also select from any of the technology groups in the game, which influences what ships and factories you can build.

I definitely recommend this game. The game has a free version available too to make sure you like the basic premise, albeit with less features (totally worth buying the full version). So give the free version a try - if you like it, buy it.

Unending Galaxy: A Review
Game Detail
Developer:Anarkis Gaming
Genre:Strategy, 4X-lite, Trading, Space

So What Is This Game About?
Unending Galaxy [UG] is a 1 man game about intergalactic trading, empire building, trading fleet management all in a top down 2D view. It basically plays like the X series except well its 2D, the pace is faster, and it could use some polish in certain part of the gameplay.

Anyways lets get on with the review! There's not really much to talk about in the intro section.

Graphics
Lets get this out of the way first, it uses asset packs from the AI war series. So don't go in expecting fancy graphical settings and so on. But at least its not terrible to look at for one thing. Ok it might be a little be bad if you're not used to it but for a heavy space simulation game like this graphics would be a little be a little be lower on the list.

Sound
Just your average typical synth space music for the ambient that you might want to mute off and put on something else after 5 continous hours of pay because eventually they grate on your ears like cheese grater at the end of it. Meanwhile the average sound effects are just pew pew pew for guns and lasers and muted boom for explosion.

Nothing really impressive here but hey it works. Kinda.

Performance
And here's my favourite part of it. You see you can adjust most things from interpolation, resolution, some settings to set compatibility and increase FPS but if you happen to have an anamolous configuration like me the game will be very finicky. As in it will randomly throw out issues at you at random time. I had sprites corrupted, game crashes, spike lags, and so on. It even hates my mobile Radeon graphic card for some reason so I had to disable that and use my intel integrated graphics.

But when it does work it works very well at 60 fps and almost no lag. But do note the most important part of your configuration will be your processor. The graphics may not be pretty but a lot of process are running constantly in the background and for the largest map it taxed my i5 processort to 100% usage for all 4 core pretty much all the time. Smaller map while... less fun do offer slightly better performances.

That said it does depend on your final configuration setup so you might get a lot more mileage with it than me with the game.

Gameplay
The gameplay in UG is pretty simple. At the start of the game you can choose a list of preset maps [that are pretty resource heavy] or from the randomly generated maps that way less fun than the preset maps but will not set your computer on fire from the sheer amount of background processing. Then you choose your starting type ship type and empire affinity. You pretty much immediately start off as an empire in this game and everybody will treat you as such [even though you might only have 1 ship].

So assuming you pick the default starting merchant class there are a few things you can do in UG. First you can configure your drone ship to start trading supplies for you autonomously while you lay back and watch as the money increases in the background or you can also do a few jobs while the drone does its job that ranges from killing people, ferrying supplies, to destroying bases and these missions are dependant on which faction and base you pick them off from. Drones [and other ships] can also be set to autonomously patrol, hunt for bounties, and a few other things and task that you can set them with.

You can interact with the faction and improve your standing with them by doing missions but faction relationship can be improve simply bribing a lot of money if you wish although there's a hard cap to how much you can do this.

Maps are divided into sectors, wormholes provide quick transportation from one point to another while jump drive provide localize instant transportation between close nearby sectors. Late in the game you can make your own wormhole but as of early point you will be most likely be

The end goal of this game can be freely decided by you but assuming you want to 'win' the game sort of to speak you need to obliberate every factionand come out on top. To do so you need money to build builder ships which will build space stations that allows other factions to trade with you and generate more income. Once you build up a sizeable base you can then start to build military bases and shipyards outside of enemy control point, this will allow you to finally claim a sector for your own and begin your first foothold in conquering the universe. Pumping out fleets of ships while setting their AI, declaring war on other factions and ruthlessly destroying would then be your main objective and at that point you can simply start building your own wormhole that will not allow others to travel into. A true empire so as to speak from a lowly beginning so as to speak.

That said money isnt too terribly hard to earn and a typical game will just be over in 5-8 hours [I had half the galaxy conquered in 5] which makes it a lot more fast pace that most other space empire simulation game out there. Being a trader only is also viable and fun since you have to acquire bodyguards and make your own trade and space stations to maximise the amount of profit that you will get.

Randomly to mix things up a few random event might happen to you and it is up to you to adapt to their coming.

Conclusion
At this present time UG is a completely playable game, you can start from a humble beginning and end as an empire and the speed in which you do so is rather addicting with few downtime in between. That said a lot of things could use a little bit more polish to it which includes the UI, the trade section, more automation stuff, and further faction interaction but that could all come afterward.

For now its a pretty fun space empire management game [especially the trading screen watching the money go up cha ching cha ching cha ching] and since its feature complete and fully playable at the moment that is all fine for now. Although, people who had never play this kind of game should note that the learning ladder is rather steep but once you are at the top its easy to do a checklist.

Forgot to mention, the game does have a built in game editor so you can make up your own sector and adjust parameters for the ships. It's also rather easy to make up your own mod for it if you want to.

Scoring Methology:
- S-Rank: excellent
- A-Rank: very good
- B-Rank: solid
- C-Rank: satisfactory
- D-Rank: inferior

Verdict: B-Rank

Unending Galaxy is a Sandbox RTS and Top-Down Shooter made by a former X series modder.
This description alone made me wanted to try it out over a year ago, and I've been coming back to it every few months since then. Mind that most of my play time wasn't logged on Steam.

In UG, you're free to pick your goal, map, gamestart and playstyle, or you can jump-in on the default settings and try to take over the galaxy!

On default, you start with one Transport (Trader) ship and one Cruiser (Combat) ship. This gives you a few opportunities:
- Automate your Trader (to trade automatically or to mine), and hunt bounties with your Cruiser.
- Automate your Cruiser (to hunt for bounties or raid ships), and perform trade and missions with your Transport.
- A few other options.. ;)

The game is full of such choices. When it comes to diplomacy, you could choose to turn a Galaxy full of hate in one full of peace, or simply turn it all into a free-for-all warzone!

As you build up your credits as an independent pilot and fleet commander, you'll eventually have enough money to buy a Dock Builder (Construction Ship), which will enable you to start your own empire, produce your own goods, own territory and construct ships for free with the Ship Parts you've produced yourself.
And again, you can choose wether to start taking over other empires through war, backstabbing or diplomacy.

Therefore, the strengths of the game really lie on its freedom, and on the ability to assign different roles to your ships by automating them with different AI modules (Trader, Miner, Police, Bounty Hunter, Military, Pirate Raider, Scavanger and maybe others). Building your empire and evading difficult situations through diplomacy is also fun, and finding different ways to deal with the challenges the game pit you against.

The problems lie at the fact the game is a niche game. There IS a learning curve, the controls and UI are not 100% intuitive to people who are not used with this genre and, even though the game has a very wide scope, it was and is being developed by a single person - so bugs and issues were probably going to exist.

However, the developer has a great habit of fixing critical things first, listening to suggestions and continuously improving and polishing the game.
As you may've noticed, there were three patches in the first week after release, and the developer has plans for the future and planned improvements for different areas of the game, and will probably release a roadmap in a few days.

If you enjoy Sandbox, Empire-Building or Space-themed games, UG is a keeper!
