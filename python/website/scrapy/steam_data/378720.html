About This Game

Thea: The Awakening is a turn-based strategic survival game steeped in Slavic myth and monstrosity set after an apocalyptic force known only as The Darkness has engulfed the world. There are Strigas and Baba Yagas aplenty, but there are no heroes, no monster slayers, and no great armies capable of banishing them. You have only a few hopeless and starving survivors who are desperately trying to stay alive. You are their last and only true hope!

The game combines turn-based strategic gameplay with the tension and grit of a rogue-like, a captivating story you can uncover through a series of in-game events, and a unique combat system based on a complex card battle game.

The wide range of gameplay features allows you to proceed through the world of Thea the way you prefer. You can choose to stay in your village and build up defenses, or you can go out and explore the world. Conflicts can be resolved through assassination, stealth, diplomacy, hunting, and various other ways depending on the skills your people possess. Fighting is not your only solution to conflict!

Choose your game style and save your people from the Darkness!

Thea: The Awakenening was a fantastic game.

You play as a fallen God who must lead his worshippers on their path to restoring Thea after the Darkness lifted. It's a turn-based game, akin to Civilization, however, unlike Civilization, you only have one central town. As you gain babies and random characters through events, you can form parties to go out and collect resources from the land. As turns increase, monster difficulty and quantity increase as well. This provides quite a challenge, it can also be quite frustrating when starting a new game because your villagers are weak and lacking ability at the start. I had to restart the game at least 4 times before I finally understood how to properly "start" a game without losing time and villagers.

Although the learning curve was moderate, it's not entirely difficult, especially for moderate gamers.

Also, the writers for this game did a fantastic job with the dialogue for random events and story events. I can't stress this enough. This game brought me back to the old days when people played dungeons and dragons on pen and paper. Events were always exciting, even the occasional repeat generally played out differently most times.


Pros:
- Different Gods to choose from, each with unique abilities and dialogue options
- The story is amazing and extremely well written. Reminds me of DnD back in the day.
- Variety of resources, each add their own special attribute to whatever building or item you're crafting.
- An incredible amount of random events. The game never really feels stale.
- Randomly generated map (albeit, limited).
- Free expansion pack, adds event editor and a second storyline (definitely worth it).
- Each God has their own quests in addition to the storyline which makes the game more goal driven, thus making it more fun and engaging.

Cons:
- Difficult to start a new game, progresses very slow in the beginning. This was off putting during my first few play throughs. Once it picked up though, I had lots of fun.
- Only two stories. Although extensive, you'll be left wanting more of the developers creative, immersive writing.
- Managing characters can be a challenge, especially when you're handling multiple parties, their items, and trying to bring resources back and forth. Challenging, but doable.

Overall, this game was definitely worth my time and money.
I definitely recommend playing through at least one game.

It was a truly immersive experience, especially for people who enjoy reading (like me)

In short Thea:The Awakening is the first dozen or so turns of Civ 5 spread across an entire game and given a ridiculous amount of depth.

And while that's accurate of the gameplay, this description is woefully short of waht Thea:The Awakening actually is. Truly it is a turn based, --decision-- game. Not strategy. You have choices with quests, which items to give which person, what resource to gather and what god to invest your time into. Overall if you are a 100% completionist you will get a silly amount of hours out of this game. And if not it's still worth the price.

What many reviews may not describe however is the repetitivness that is inevitable with these kinds of games. Thea:The Awakening does its best to mitigate it, but in the end you will have quests repeat and resources pop up to the point where you can auto-pilot your way to victory.

On the other side of the coin however the devs for this game believe in free DLC and with the shiny new event editor you can create and download custom made quests and storylines. So there are plenty more hours ahead for me and many others.

All in all, great game, good price and whole heap of potential on top of that. 10/10

I think the most relevant thing to say here is that, by july, 2016, after getting this game on Summer Sale and playing it through twice, I couldn't find ANY of the flaws the "bad reviews" mention. For example, one of them states that, some 7 months ago, there were 3 villager (character) classes available. Now, there are about 15 (rough guess).

Thea may not look like much at first--the name and graphics didn't strike myself as a good omen at first. But hold on--this game is rather unique.

HOW IT WORKS (A.K.A. "THE GOOD STUFF"):

As you've probable read already, Thea: The Awakening is something like Civilization with a single city and, instead of armies, you put up one (or just a few) parties of villagers up, fit them with the best items you could find/craft, and send them out to explore the world, gather food and materials, do quests, face random encounters and events.

Battle takes place on a tabletop, with cards, and this aspect is also very interesting. It works unlike any other card-battle system I've seem so far (and I'm a fan of card games), and requires some thiking even after you've mastered it.

Through research, you can unlock new crafting recipes (heavy armor, one-handed axes, two-handed staves, etc.), new materials to mine, or new construction blueprints. Choosing what to get and when makes a big difference, for you don't have a bunch of idle research points just lying around.

When crafting anything, you choose what materials to use--a base material, a secondary, and a catalyst that has minor influences. How good the item will be depends directly on what you craft them with, and this adds an interesting layer of strategy/lust for the finest materials.

Villager classes (what you can choose your children to become when they grow up) are unlocked with certain buildings--but the real badass characters you get on some quests/events or by building the right structure with the right materials to attract them.

When one of your Dwarves or Baba Yagas (there are more "special-classes," don't worry) die, you curse out loud and decide to wear black to work the next day.

Also, after each game, you'll unlock a new God to play with and/or some new power(s) (it all depends on how much exp you got) to start with next time--some concrete reward for going as far as you can.

THE "NOT-SO-GOOD":

Regarding difficulty, it could be a little harder. I won both games I played (Normal and Difficult settings). If you get a decent start and don't screw up hard, you're fine. After you've figured the whole game out, it's easy.

The main quests could/should be a lot longer.

I wish there was a bigger variety of random events available. They aren't few, no, but... you know, "the more the merrier". This is not so hard to do--you can actually create your own events in the game, but I'd rather have more of the profissional stuff (btw, they are all voiced, and the acting is pretty good) and the same odds as everyone else playing the game. Some events in which you could blindly navigate some deep cave or dungeon, with several fights in a row without leaving nor healing, for instance, would make things considerably more interesting and add more difficulty. Some unique (non-craftable) item drops on such events would also be a nice addition...

Altough you get a new God/new Godly powers after finishing a game, I wish each different God had a somehow deeper impact on the game/your play style (like new starships do on FTL, for instance). The bonuses each of them give vary, but not enough to deeply affect gameplay, and your chosen God also affects a few random events. As far as I can tell, that's pretty much it.

SUMMING UP:

Thea's concept is very interesting and, if not perfectly, still very well executed. A good game, well worth it's price, packing some 50 hours of fun and completely bug-free. I thoroughly enjoyed my ride, and I can see myself coming back to it in the not-so-distant future to play some more.

This game shows an excellent way of improvement for traditional 4x genre. Really worth a try for all strategy game fans.

The main complaint I have for most 4x games is the stupid AI. Thea however, wisely avoid the AI development issue. You don't have an opponent in this game in fact. What you only need to do is "outwit the nature". Gathering resources, crafting equipments and following the quests, every elements in this game is by no means a compitition with others. As a result, it brings me a pure relief and enjoyment when playing without any anoyance from bad AI. To exclude boredom with no opponents, this game introduces some RPG elements, form your own expedition team and you are going to adventure the world with a lot of bump-up events and charactor interactions.

Although relaxing, the tactics and replayablity is also good. Unlike most other strategy games that basically you are going to unlock 80%+ of the technology, units and buildings at the end of the game, research points are so limited in this game so you need to think carefully which tech line you are most craving for. The card game combat mechanism is easy to understand but hard to masterfully manoeuvre. There are a lot of different combat methods you can choose other than merely a nomal fight. This choice depends on the technology you picked, equipments you crafted/looted and your expedition team comps strategically.

The graphic of Thea is decent based on my point. The resolution is mediocre but you can find rich details everywhere. Each character has their unique sketching pictures. Village events have their own watercolor paintings. The apperence for weapons and armers crafted by different materials are also diversified. An armer crafted by wood looks wooden; a sword crafted by gold looks golden. Everything in your sight in accordance with the melodious BGM fits well with the in-game mythical environment.

The story line is... okay, a well written story, traditional myths telling way. Most text is dubbed with a man's deep voice, even for side quests and village events. It sounds a little bit bizarre when a woman character is talking... anyways it is a good feature and helps reading the long text very well. Your choice for the dialogue, in most cases, does not influence the result quite a lot. Actually, only the decision of combat method at the end of each dialogue really matters. This is clearly a drawback for any RPG. But for Thea, a blend of 4x and RPG, it is just a flaw acceptable.

This game is updated frequetly and players are free to discuss their thoughts with devolopers through steam forum. Excellent game already and might become better in the future. Highly recommended.

Take a 4X game, and remove the entire thought set that runs through your mind about making more settlements and training troops. Instead, replace those with a more RPG-esque exploration of the world using teams of characters from your settlement, which grows quite slowly. It strongly emphasises exploration and story, while minimizing expansion elements. It also throws in a little more resource management to the mix, requiring you do decide how you will acquire those resources, and sometimes forcing you to decide if you want them, or want to address a threat.

Add in a strategic system for card battles, lots of flavor in questing, the ability to add in your own elements with the event creation system, make every action a choice where you can mess up or do well... and sometimes where your choice is a matter between trying something very dangerous or avoiding that danger, and mix it all up with a hefty dose of awesome.

That's Thea. If you don't like slower paced strategic games, it isn't for you. If you do, then you will probably enjoy yourself with this title.

For more detailed information, I have written about this game for Gaiscioch Magazine, and you can find those writings online, which will give you an overview of each portion of the game and some screencaps.

A quite unique game. It's all about resource and citizen management, with exploration and story-telling quests.

You only have one village (and never settle any), which can have a maximum of ten buildings in it (each of which can be knocked down and replaced). Your village is mainly full of warriors, gatherers, and crafters, as well as a few other "classes" such as scavengers and undead. The warriors defend the village from attacks, the gatherers collect nearby resources (such as wood, leather, or various food items), and the crafters make buildings, cook food, and crafts weapons, armour, and tools. The crafting is particularly interesting because the better quality of the items used (scaled leather against basic leather, for example) determines the final quality and benefits of the buildings and crafted items. Different cooking recipes are made by varying the ingredients used, and the more the number of different food stuffs available to your people, the more bonuses you receive.

Both science and experience are achieved through battles, questing, and making items. Experience is awarded to everyone at the same time, and levelling up is done automatically.

To quest, or to gather resources out of reach of your village, you put together a party of people of different skills (warriors and gatherers, mainly). The party will need to carry enough food and wood to survive, and can collect more as it travels. They can then battle creatures, activate quests, or set up a camp and collect nearby resources which then need to be taken back to the village.

Battles are interesting in they are simulated using cards randomly split into two small decks. The first mainly uses the attack and defence stats. Both player and opponent take turns to place one or more of their cards down (left-to-right), before battle is started. When all the cards are dealt the battle starts moving along from the left, and each card's attack damage will decrement the neighbouring opponent's card's defence value (randomly left or right), and if that value reaches zero then the card is removed. A few other attributes such as if the weapon is a piercing (in which case when the card is initially placed, it will go before the opponent's last placed card and deal it half the weapon damage immediately) are also taken into consideration. The second deck is the tactical deck, and each card can be used to, for example, add to your last dealt card's defence value, or randomly remove a card from one of your opponent's deck. As each battle is made up of two rounds (before the whole process is repeated, until one side has no more cards left), you can also place your tactical cards into the battle. However they will start "confused", and so will not attack until the second round (but can be attacked, and thus possibly removed, during the first round).

When the battle is over, some of your characters may be wounded, and it's a good idea to have a decent medic in your party to quickly heal the wounded in a camp. If the defence value fell too low, a character may die. But even if a defence value falls to zero, your medic still might be able to bring your character back to full health.

Battles are not always about fighting. Sometimes it's about hunting, quarrelling, sneaking, or poisoning. And each of these will use different character stats (the hunting for example, will use your gathering skill to determine damage). This makes it important to have a good mix of people in your village and parties. Non-fighting battles do not end with any damage to your characters, but the consequences of losing such a battle may do (for example, losing an argument and being thrown out of a tower).

There is no save/load feature in the game. If you make a mistake, you have to live with it.

The music is great. Very Dom4-ish.

The DLC is virtually all free, and the developers apparently have added a lot of content since the initial release. The only DLC they do charge for is a small (optional) donation DLC for those who wish to thank them for all the free content --- apparently requested by their customers.

If you like resource and citizen management, it's a great game and well recommended.
