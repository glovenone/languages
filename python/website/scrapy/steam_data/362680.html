About This Game

Fran Bow is a creepy adventure game that tells the story of Fran, a young girl struggling with a mental disorder and an unfair destiny.
After witnessing the gruesome and mysterious loss of her parents, found dismembered at their home, Fran rushes into the woods, together with her only friend, Mr. Midnight, a black cat that Fran had previously received as a present from her parents.

In the forest, Fran goes into shock over the loss of her parents and when she recovers, she’s at Oswald Asylum, an oppressive mental institution for children, and Mr. Midnight is nowhere to be found.

After having a dream about her beloved cat, Fran decides to escape from the mental institution to find him and go back home to Aunt Grace, her only living relative.

Features

  • Story driven creepy psychological horror adventure game.
  • Unique and peculiar hand drawn 2D Art-Style and 2D animation.
  • Self administer medication to open the terrible hidden world that will help solve puzzles and find objects.
  • A big variety of puzzles designed with different levels of difficulty and specifically based on the story.
  • Three arcade inspired mini-games all with different art style to be part of the transitions in the story.
  • Interactive and occasionally playable pet cat, Mr Midnight.
  • 50+ interactive unique characters with unique personalities.
  • Original Soundtrack.

This is a great point and click game that reminded me a lot of Nancy Drew. You spend most of the game solving puzzles to progress through the story, with the ultimate goal of discovering the reason and the culprit behind the murder of Fran's parents. In between the point and click puzzle solving, a mini-game is at the end of each chapter with really interesting styles of their own.

It's pretty story focued, so a lot of your playtime is spent reading dialogue as much as it's spent solving puzzles. I finished in 7 hours and then played for another 3 hours to get all the achievements that I missed. If you play with the achievement list open, then you'll save yourself that trouble, because they are all incredibly easy to get if you know what they are, but you can replay specific chapters if you happen to miss one anyway.

There are very minor flaws in the game as far as dialogue and the pacing of the story goes, but it really doesn't take away from the atmosphere or the fact that solving the puzzles is a lot of fun. It will definitely make you want to play until the very end.

Darkseed, eat your heart out.

In an age of glamorized, but carefully framed and sterilized violence in the media, Fran Bow is a refreshing breath of air for the macabre in video games. Thin limbs, twisted figures, wide eyes, and sharp, sketchy contrasts provide the mood and tone of the game. You might have seen the aesthetic before in similar works by Tim Burton or Jhonen Vasquez, but as a whole, trends have gravitated away from eerie immersion and more towards shock value and scares. Fran Bow is a perfect example of how a game can be both unsettling, creepy, and yet, oddly heartwarming at the same time.

The main player character, Fran Bow Dagenhart, (with occasional breaks that you can briefly control) is our protagonist. She is a mental patient whose parents were recently butchered, and it is up to her to find out who was behind it all. Cheery, outgoing, and generally a precious soul, she is the poor, innocent soul of the game. The story takes many different twists and turns, pacing itself from place-to-place but never once letting you forget that first and foremost, it's a game about discovery.

Point-and-click adventure games are all about that exploration, those "a-ha!" moments where the lightbulb turns on and everything makes sense. But often times, they suffer from having puzzles that require great leaps in logic, sudden deaths, or infamously letting you miss an item that will become crucial later on in the story. Fran Bow is very mericful in this department; it is virtually impossible to get permanently stuck or die, and to its credit, the puzzles are very well laid out. There were only a couple of times where I got completely stumped and had to look up help, only to realize how completely obvious they were to solve. But at the same time, the puzzles can be complex or basic, but they're always satisfying to figure out.

The sounds and graphics go hand-in-hand like peanut butter and jelly, or if you dislike PBJ, cheese and pizza. And if you don't like that either, you're an alien and clearly not human. Every element of the game works wonderfully well together to fit the mood, you are always left second-guessing or trying to come up with possible explanations of the mysterious reality around you. What's real? What isn't? It's all up to the player to find out on their own.

Mr. Midnight is a good kitty.

8/10.

If I were to sum up Fran Bow I'd say it was one part Wizard of Oz and two parts Nightmare on Elm Street. Right from the start there's a lot of creepy, messed up things going on and it's not all Fran. The art style is exceptional in adding to the mystery and unsettling situation we find Fran in.

Let's talk about Fran for a moment. An 11 year old girl whose parents were killed and she now finds herself basically an orphan in an insane asylum for children. Yet Fran doesn't really exhibit the angsty, f - the world, brooding personality we would expect. Instead she looks at all things happening with a positive sort of naive response. See a bunch of dead fetus' in a well she's happy it took it's umbilical cord with it. Head explode right in front of her she speaks to the fresh corpse like it might respond any minute with some extra twitching. Maybe it's the massive amounts of drugs or just her not being mature enough to realize something is wrong that she is such a tragic character. And you really want to give her a happy ending with all the trials she seems to be going through it's just a lot at any age. However, you're going into this knowing that Fran probably isn't all there.

Around half way through the game starts blurring the lines between what might be real and what might be in Fran's head. The story alludes to there being multiple realities that we're experiencing but you are left questioning what's real. You can imagine early on how this will end but I really enjoyed getting there. It's not all doom and gloom the entire way through but the story never alludes to you getting to a better place. Even when you finally reach your destination what you uncover isn't quite what you expect. The ending does leave a good amount of room for interpretation.

The puzzle aspect of the game was thoughtful and well designed. Nothing was too difficult to figure out and solutions utilized both of Fran's mental states to complete. There was a journal that you received sometime during your journey that also alluded to another character's perspective of a journey similar to Fran's. In addition to being a companion for a few of the game's puzzles I wish it could have played a bigger role. It wasn't useless as an inventory item but there was a time when I just completely neglected it as some random unimportant item.

In all this was a disturbing but well thought out game. There were some parts that felt a bit boring but nothing felt like it was a grind or filler for getting through the story. It's a heartbreaking and disturbing tale but well worth the price of admission.
