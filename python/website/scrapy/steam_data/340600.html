About This Game

The Depths of Tolagal is an addictive roguelike dungeon crawler with an emphasis on tactical combat.

You are searching for your missing apprentice within the deep, dark dungeons of Tolagal. Did he go there willingly, or was he kidnapped? You don't really care, you just want your damn apprentice back!

Features


  • Deep tactical gameplay.
  • Loads of different monsters, each with unique AI.
  • Loads of loot.
  • Procedurally generated levels.
  • Unique abilities tied in to the different weapons available in the game.
  • Action point based combat.
  • Play with or without permadeath - your high scores will only be recorded for permadeath runs though!
  • Story mode - rescue your apprentice from the depths of Tolagal in this semi-structured mode.
  • Infinite mode - get as far as you can in an endless dungeon. Try to beat your high score!
  • Learn more about the dungeon of Tolagal in each run by finding journal entries written by the various inhabitants of Tolagal.
  • Easy to learn but hard to master.
  • Challenging but not unfair.

I really want to recommend this game, but unfortunately I cannot mainly due to the games lack of depth and very short length. Sure the game is fun enough, and is tough like a roguelike should be, but it is ultimately very unsatisfying when you compare it to the excellent Tales Of Majeyal. Here are the pros and cons...

Pros:

1. I enjoyed the games tactical approach to defeating enemies. The game makes you think when you are in tight spots.
2. Overall combat is fun.
3. I thought the music was pretty decent.

Cons:

1. No classes. For me this means 0 replayability once the game is completed.
2. You cannot attack enemies diagonally in melee combat.
3. When you level up skills/traits are randomly generated. You could end up having no choice but to chose a skill that is essentially a negative skill. In my opinion this is a cheap way to make the game more difficult.
4. The game is too short. Basically 4 hours to complete the main game.
5. The price. I don't mind spending money on a game at all but at this is more expensive than Tales Of Majeyal and has a fraction of the content.

I know that I have mentioned TOME a few times in my review, but I sunk in over 70 hours into that game and have still not completed it yet. The Depth Of Tolagal is a minor distraction for a few hours, but simply does not have that depth that I am looking for.

So, if you are looking for a more "simplistic" roguelike that you can play for a few hours of fun then I would recommend it...if you want a rich, deep experience look elsewhere.

A round of applause for the developers here! They succeeded in creating a tough as nails rougelike that challenges you measure ever step and I do mean EVERY step but (for the most part) only punished the clumsy or the uninformed. I have died several times by virtue of approaching a hallway and accidentally alerting an extra orc or two and promptly getting my teeth kicked in. I have come to realization that the tactical aspect of the game is actually a good bit deeper than rpg aspect of it. Level progression unlocks triats as opposed to a solid stat boost across the board. Many of the traits simply open up intersting opportunties which require you to adjust your playstyle in order to gain an advantage. So overall I would say this game is TACTICAL rougelike more than a classic rougelike rpg.

The game is starting to feel a little bland (obviously graphically its retro over modern) in terms of variety. Given a good deal of this has been the difficulty that keeps sending me back to the starting stages and the same enemies. Additionally some of the traits (at least early on) are clearly superiour to the others. In a very difficult game that does not hold your hand but allows you progress consistently if you think and learn from your mistakes (for the next time) this a minor balancing issues. They were kind of enough to allow you play through without the permadeath if you buy the game and decide you'll never get through it with only one life (I may eventually give up and fall into this category myself).

I would recommend this game to just about anyone who likes tactical combat games. Fans of the original X-Com series and Desktop Dungeons (one of my all-time favorites) are going to be disappointed they did not hit add to cart sooner. For anyone else I would say it is worth a go if your intersted in the tactical games.

My current feeling (i'm writing this after only about 10 hours to help out people wavering on buying it or not) on this title is that it has definetly been fun but has not quite driven me to the maniac gaming addict level of worship and adolation I usually bestow to games I wind up putting in my top 50. From a value standpoint I would say its definetly worth the money.

7/10

The Depths of Tolagal has a forgettable name and is not the prettiest game on te block, but it is surprisingly engaging and well designed. It's almost more of a turn-based-combat-puzzle generator - like Hoplite with which it was rightly compare in RPS article - than a roguelike. The combat turns are divided in a small number of action points, so it's all about planning and positioning. With fast moving enemies it might mean trying to get as many hits in as possible (and maybe knocking them back or putting a shield up, or hiding behind a door - the options are there, and this is where it differs from Hoplite and puzzle games), while battles with slow enemies are more like a war of attrition... unless you make a mistake and allow yourself to be ambushed by some goblins.

Each enemy has a gimmick to keep things fresh: trolls can charge at you if you're in a straight line, cultists are invisible, only leaving a trail of smoke behind them, others have shields that they can raise meaning you have to hack through them. There are hardly any popcorn enemies, and you have to be thinking in almost every battle - except the first couple of levels, which are hard to mess up after you've played through them a couple of times, and can become boring in repeated playthroughs.

The only other criticism I can think of at this point (I've played some more in offline mode, but admittedly not enough yet) is the lack of variety. There are no classes, and so far I've come across a grand total of four different weapons, a shield and a ring of regeneration. Still, the game keeps it fresh by offering different attributes for the items that change the way you use them considerably. Also, there are no character stats and each new level comes with a choice of a passive skill that will also influence the whole run considerably - and there are a lot of them, although you're only given two random ones in each levelup to choose one from.

The game scratches a similar itch to Hoplite, and it does its thing very well. The combat system is well done, and the permutations of different item/skill combinations keep it from going stale quickly, but still I hope the game grows in variety some more.
