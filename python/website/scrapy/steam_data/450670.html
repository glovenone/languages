Table Top Racing: World Tour (TTR from now on) is a racing game that takes place on…..well, what do you think? Tables of course!

The Breakdown
Kart racers have been around for eons, but what about…..car racers? Y’know, kart racers with real-looking cars? I suppose that’s what I’d describe TTR as. It feels like a kart racer yet the vehicles have the appearance of realistic cars, vans, or trucks. Normally I’d talk about gameplay first, but hey, why not graphics this time? Let’s switch things up!

So, the game has a sort of realistic look to it, like I mentioned earlier. The tracks all consist of a table (or what generally looks like somewhere in a house) with various household objects (food, bottles, etc.) spread around the track/table. As you drive your little cars around you can then knock a lot of the stuff on the track over, which is pretty fun. The cars themselves are just a mix of realistic-looking race cars and knockoffs of cars from pop culture (the Breaking Bad RV, a “fauxrari”, etc.). Everything in the game looks pretty crisp and polished overall, so graphically I definitely have no complaints.

Now onto the gameplay. Here’s where things are both good and bad. See, the game basically consists of you driving your little car around a track over and over until you win. That’s….basically it, however there’s a small variety of modes to mix things up. From the main menu there’s “Championships”, “Special Events”, and “Multiplayer”. Championships is the meat of the game, and it’ll last you about 5-8 hours or so depending on how skilled you are at racing games. Within Championships you’ll find 6 different cups with a variety of events to choose from. These events are one of a few different game modes (Combat races where you can use powerups, pure/no weapons races, a “pursuit” mode, elimination, time trials, drifting, and a “get x lap time” mode), which helps to keep the gameplay from getting too stale. On top of that, there’s collectable coins scattered around each track in some interesting hidden locations so even if you’re terrible at the game it incentivizes you to collect them and use the money they give you to upgrade your car (or buy a new one) and get better.

The Special Events mode is basically a variety of events, like in Championships mode, yet there’s some very minor twists. It’s just extra content I suppose. I never got to try out the multiplayer unfortunately (no one was playing online), so I can’t comment on that mode.

Generally I enjoyed my time with TTR, but what did I dislike? Well, to start, the tracks. There’s…..5 of them. That’s slightly remedied by the slight variation given to them during certain events (I believe the game refers to each track’s variations as “track A”, “track B”, etc.), but after playing what is basically the same track over and over it gets a bit old. I’d have really liked to have seen more tracks, not just the same 5 ones over and over with tiny changes here and there.

Secondly, the game’s handling feels weird, at least to me. Maybe this is just a personal thing but it felt like on every turn my car would either hit a wall (at least initially) or just barely make the turn, while the AI seemed to make turns at full speed just fine. Now granted, with handling upgrades on the best cars in the game it’s not too bad, but when I had first started playing it was definitely frustrating watching myself hit walls on almost every turn. I think the speed of the cars is pretty fitting but their handling could definitely use some work,

Beyond that, there’s not a whole lot to talk about with TTR. It’s fun I suppose, and pretty well-made overall. Err…...oh wait, that’s right - the music. It’s uh…..well, I think some people will enjoy it. Personally I thought it was mostly generic hip hop/electronic/dance stuff, so sometimes I just felt like muting the game and turning on my own music while I raced. However, I feel as if it’s not terrible, but simply “tolerable”, if that makes any sense. Also there’s a song that says “be drunk” repeatedly. I….I don’t even know.

Pros
  • Fun, energetic kart racer gameplay across dozens of fairly varied events - racing little toy cars on tabletops is quite exciting and silly compared to real races
  • Decent vehicle customization is included (wheels, car types, stats, and paint jobs can all be changed)
  • The graphics are fairly good overall - no complaints there
  • The music is fairly fitting - it’s upbeat and energetic but also a bit laid back
  • There’s Steam achievements, cloud support for saves, and controller support

Cons
  • There’s only 5 tracks (even if the game advertises 20 - there’s 4 slight variations to each of the 5 base tracks), so more would definitely be better
  • Car handling feels bizarre - AI cars turn fine, but for some reason (especially with early cars or ones that aren’t upgraded) you can only take turns super wide by default, which causes a lot of wall crashes to happen
  • Some people may not like the music (personally I didn’t care much for it)

Table Top Racing is a pretty solid fast-paced racing game with powerups. There’s a lot to like here, from the lovely visuals to the decent amount of variety in both customization options and game modes. Despite my problems with it, primarily the small number of tracks and some weird turning problems (which are fixed with vehicle upgrades), I honestly really enjoyed playing this game. Bravo, developers - you’ve made a pretty great little racer!

Final Verdict: 8/10

Introduction

Table Top Racing: World Tour is a mini-car/kart racing game in a similar vein to Mario Kart, that is to say where you have a small sized vehicle collecting power-ups to use against your enemies as you race around the small track. The visuals are great, but in my opinion, the sound track leaves a lot to be desired.


Gameplay Impressions

In Table Top Racing: World Tour you find yourself driving miniaturized versions of what could very well be real world cars. These mini-vehicles fit right in with the littered table top racing theme. Much of the debris lying around the track can be knocked out of the way. The racers can strike them as they drive around the course at the cost of only a minor loss of momentum. While there are really not that many varieties of tracks available, there are plenty of different race types to make up for it. Sometimes a lack of variety can be a bad thing. It could get boring with the same old, same old, but when it comes to racing games, knowing the track is the best way to give yourself an edge. Having fewer tracks to memorize allows you to spend less time crashing during unexpected sharp turns and more time winning races.

The game is divided into different cups, two cups per tier, and three tiers total. As you progress through the cups, more race modes will become available. I won’t go into an exhaustive list of every race type, but I will name a few of the more interesting ones. Battle Cars is the default mode. In it, it plays like Mario Kart. You race around the track trying to come in first place while using weapon pickups to slow down your opponents. Your enemies can also use those weapons, so you will have to be ever vigilant to maintain or improve your position in the race. There is also a more vanilla race mode that is just purely about racing skill rather than using cheap tricks to give yourself an edge. Drift mode is a fun mode; in it you try to slide around corners as fast as you can without hitting the walls, maintaining the drift until just before you lose control you straighten out and drive sensibly again. In Drift, you are not against other opponents; you simply are trying to beat one of the top three track scores in order to earn your bronze, silver or gold stars for the mission. There are the time trials which are similar, race against the clock to score the best lap record or best course completion time. There is also a cat and mouse style mode. You are the cat so to speak, and your goal is to catch up to the much faster mouse and pounce on it, or in car speak, ram into it. You do this through clever and creative means such as using your boost at opportune times and taking short cuts. At the end of each cup is a multi-race event that requires you to do well in each of the races in order to move on to the next cup. There are other modes and multiplayer, but you get the idea, there is plenty to do.

Each tier of racing comes with four car options. When you first start the game you can only afford to buy one of the first two available for the tier. Once you earn more by participating in races, you can either opt to buy a different car or upgrade the one you have. Each car will have different balances between their speed, acceleration, handling and armor. You will want to figure out which stats are more important to you when purchasing the car. Just because that one car has more slots for speed, it might not accelerate all that well so you might not actually get up to full speed all that often. The customizations don’t end here though. The wheels on the cars can be customized as well. The wheel customizations have a variety of effects and unlike the other car tuning upgrades, you unlock them once and you can use them on all cars you own. For example, you can equip wheels that cause you to earn more money from using weapons on enemies. You can also use wheels that give you a shield against some weapons, or even turn your wheels into weapons. There are others, but basically this adds a whole extra layer of strategy and fun to the game. Lastly you can paint your vehicles. I don’t believe painting them really has any kind of effect other than a cosmetic one. Odds are you better off sticking with stock until you have bought everything else you need.

Visually, as mentioned, this game looks great. It isn’t cartoony like Mario Kart, it actually looks somewhat realistic. The mini-cars really suit the “you are racing on the top of a table” style of the game. The random household objects and foods lying around as you drive through the various environments enhance the table top feeling even more. Overall, the visuals are very well done for the game. There were not even any graphical glitches that occurred for me, not even minor ones.

The sound on the other hand, that is something to talk about. The sound effects are your standard racing ones. Nothing really stands out there, but you don’t exactly want random nonsense sounds when racing around, you want to hear those standard sounds. The sound effects therefore are perfectly fine. The sound track on the other hand… well I read in other reviews people really like the sound track for this game. That’s great; I can see people liking it, but it was far from my style of music. Not wanting to be harsh, I would still have to say, the sound track is the kind of music that I typically wouldn’t actually class as music. It’s pretty much all remixed breakbeat (a kind of a funk/hip hop/electro/dubstep/techno blend). It’s the kind of music typically played at raves and other parties that I try to avoid being invited to. I tried my best to endure it and actually caught myself almost sort of liking one of the music tracks, but I realized that was just the Cognitive Dissonance kicking in. Luckily, you can turn off the music without losing the sound effects and play your own music if you are so inclined.

Overall, this is an excellent power-up racing game with tons of customization options and plenty of race varieties. The only real detractor is that I dislike its soundtrack immensely. Some reviews agree with me, others say it gets you into the racing spirit and mood. So hit up your favourite music streaming site, look up remixed Breakbeat and give it a listen. If your ears are not bleeding too heavily after that, go out and purchase this game!

    Pros:
  • Great visuals, using a more realistic setting with mini-cars was a better design choice than attempting to pull of the cartoony visuals of Mario Kart or Sonic All-Stars Racing
  • Plenty of customization options
  • Multiple race event types per cup help prevent the game from getting repetitive
  • Hidden coins make even losing a race fun. Rather than “rage quitting” when you are in last place, take the time to explore the track for all the hidden coins. The extra money will help you upgrade or replace your car
  • Controls are fluid and responsive

    Cons:
  • Sound Track “music” that is less enjoyable than listening to nails on a chalkboard that is backed by bagpipes and cats in heat.
  • Not many varieties for tracks, the same tracks get recycled over and over again with some obstacles moved around or replaced

Table Top Racing blurs the line between the Arcade Racing and Kart Racing genres, by adding the ability to shoot projectiles and collect power ups to the demolition derby feel of the more realistic cars of Nascar. They end up with a racing game that feels like a Matchbox car derby,
very nostalgic, with super fantasy table top track setups ranging from a sushi diner counter, to an eighties memorabilia lined attic shelf, to the floor of a garage.
I received a copy free for review purposes, but that in no way influenced the fact that I love this game! I grabbed the DLC myself. ∙،°. ˘Ô≈ôﺣ » » »

The Cars:
These vehicles handle superbly. I prefer to use my Steam Controller, but the controls are simple enough to work with any device. The wide variety of vehicle models includes standard super cars, hippy campers, and pickup trucks. If you’ve played the Disney Infinity 1.0 Cars Playset, you’ve seen how the fantasy addition of not-so-standard race cars can amp up the laughs, adding a much needed jolt to the fun factor in derby type racing. But, these cars each have their own set of strengths and weaknesses added to the cosmetic beauty of the car to create an addictive collectors aspect to the game much like that of collecting Matchbox cars for children. Personally, I can’t wait to earn the Fauxrari. But, the availability of other upgrades may forestall that acquisition.

The Upgrades:
Comical sounding names for tires like: bling, boing, and shield let you know right up front what these tires can do for your ride. The boing tires are one of the more expensive to earn with the in-game currency, but give you the ability to alter typical game physics in a more satisfying way, and allow you to reach more hidden coins and shortcuts. The Bling tires, aside from the ascetic benefit, cause you to earn more money from races, though they don’t really help you win. There are quite a few tire options. Adding more would definitely be better, of course. But, there are also cool paint jobs you can earn, and of course the leveling up of abilities, like acceleration, max speed, and handling.

The Tracks
The race tracks are atypically short for most of the kart racers I’m familiar with, more in line with standard stadium race tracks length-wise. There is no stadium feel here, however. It feels like being a kid lying on the floor of a garage, racing your Matchbox cars between old car parts and cans of transmission fluid. Or, like racing your hot wheels around the diner counter past the napkin dispenser. So, there is quite a bit of nostalgic pleasure derived from the game no matter who you are. But, the myriad of objects to drift into, collide with, and knock off the track, along with the ability to careen over the edge of the table adds the pleasure of riding the bumper cars.

Multiplayer:
If you enjoy the thrill of mastering all the championship trophies, and exploring the special events, then you will simply love the gameplay of multiplayer, which is where the real meat & potatoes of the game is found. You could spend many hours in single-player racing against the AI, but eventually you will want more than the scaling of typical AI races. Multiplayer becomes more of a competition, and a friendship forming opportunity. Though there is no co-op yet, MP certainly lends itself to teaming up against your opponents. The game could certainly benefit from that option.

Introduction

Table Top Racing: World Tour is a mini-car/kart racing game in a similar vein to Mario Kart, that is to say where you have a small sized vehicle collecting power-ups to use against your enemies as you race around the small track. The visuals are great, but in my opinion, the sound track leaves a lot to be desired.


Gameplay Impressions

In Table Top Racing: World Tour you find yourself driving miniaturized versions of what could very well be real world cars. These mini-vehicles fit right in with the littered table top racing theme. Much of the debris lying around the track can be knocked out of the way. The racers can strike them as they drive around the course at the cost of only a minor loss of momentum. While there are really not that many varieties of tracks available, there are plenty of different race types to make up for it. Sometimes a lack of variety can be a bad thing. It could get boring with the same old, same old, but when it comes to racing games, knowing the track is the best way to give yourself an edge. Having fewer tracks to memorize allows you to spend less time crashing during unexpected sharp turns and more time winning races.

The game is divided into different cups, two cups per tier, and three tiers total. As you progress through the cups, more race modes will become available. I won’t go into an exhaustive list of every race type, but I will name a few of the more interesting ones. Battle Cars is the default mode. In it, it plays like Mario Kart. You race around the track trying to come in first place while using weapon pickups to slow down your opponents. Your enemies can also use those weapons, so you will have to be ever vigilant to maintain or improve your position in the race. There is also a more vanilla race mode that is just purely about racing skill rather than using cheap tricks to give yourself an edge. Drift mode is a fun mode; in it you try to slide around corners as fast as you can without hitting the walls, maintaining the drift until just before you lose control you straighten out and drive sensibly again. In Drift, you are not against other opponents; you simply are trying to beat one of the top three track scores in order to earn your bronze, silver or gold stars for the mission. There are the time trials which are similar, race against the clock to score the best lap record or best course completion time. There is also a cat and mouse style mode. You are the cat so to speak, and your goal is to catch up to the much faster mouse and pounce on it, or in car speak, ram into it. You do this through clever and creative means such as using your boost at opportune times and taking short cuts. At the end of each cup is a multi-race event that requires you to do well in each of the races in order to move on to the next cup. There are other modes and multiplayer, but you get the idea, there is plenty to do.

Each tier of racing comes with four car options. When you first start the game you can only afford to buy one of the first two available for the tier. Once you earn more by participating in races, you can either opt to buy a different car or upgrade the one you have. Each car will have different balances between their speed, acceleration, handling and armor. You will want to figure out which stats are more important to you when purchasing the car. Just because that one car has more slots for speed, it might not accelerate all that well so you might not actually get up to full speed all that often. The customizations don’t end here though. The wheels on the cars can be customized as well. The wheel customizations have a variety of effects and unlike the other car tuning upgrades, you unlock them once and you can use them on all cars you own. For example, you can equip wheels that cause you to earn more money from using weapons on enemies. You can also use wheels that give you a shield against some weapons, or even turn your wheels into weapons. There are others, but basically this adds a whole extra layer of strategy and fun to the game. Lastly you can paint your vehicles. I don’t believe painting them really has any kind of effect other than a cosmetic one. Odds are you better off sticking with stock until you have bought everything else you need.

Visually, as mentioned, this game looks great. It isn’t cartoony like Mario Kart, it actually looks somewhat realistic. The mini-cars really suit the “you are racing on the top of a table” style of the game. The random household objects and foods lying around as you drive through the various environments enhance the table top feeling even more. Overall, the visuals are very well done for the game. There were not even any graphical glitches that occurred for me, not even minor ones.

The sound on the other hand, that is something to talk about. The sound effects are your standard racing ones. Nothing really stands out there, but you don’t exactly want random nonsense sounds when racing around, you want to hear those standard sounds. The sound effects therefore are perfectly fine. The sound track on the other hand… well I read in other reviews people really like the sound track for this game. That’s great; I can see people liking it, but it was far from my style of music. Not wanting to be harsh, I would still have to say, the sound track is the kind of music that I typically wouldn’t actually class as music. It’s pretty much all remixed breakbeat (a kind of a funk/hip hop/electro/dubstep/techno blend). It’s the kind of music typically played at raves and other parties that I try to avoid being invited to. I tried my best to endure it and actually caught myself almost sort of liking one of the music tracks, but I realized that was just the Cognitive Dissonance kicking in. Luckily, you can turn off the music without losing the sound effects and play your own music if you are so inclined.

Overall, this is an excellent power-up racing game with tons of customization options and plenty of race varieties. The only real detractor is that I dislike its soundtrack immensely. Some reviews agree with me, others say it gets you into the racing spirit and mood. So hit up your favourite music streaming site, look up remixed Breakbeat and give it a listen. If your ears are not bleeding too heavily after that, go out and purchase this game!

    Pros:
  • Great visuals, using a more realistic setting with mini-cars was a better design choice than attempting to pull of the cartoony visuals of Mario Kart or Sonic All-Stars Racing
  • Plenty of customization options
  • Multiple race event types per cup help prevent the game from getting repetitive
  • Hidden coins make even losing a race fun. Rather than “rage quitting” when you are in last place, take the time to explore the track for all the hidden coins. The extra money will help you upgrade or replace your car
  • Controls are fluid and responsive

    Cons:
  • Sound Track “music” that is less enjoyable than listening to nails on a chalkboard that is backed by bagpipes and cats in heat.
  • Not many varieties for tracks, the same tracks get recycled over and over again with some obstacles moved around or replaced

Oh, and more secret areas in tracks please, like RC Racer from the Playstation.

Finally, there's the racing element. I know it's suppose to be an arcade racing game, but personally I think this level of arcadeness can be dull really quickly. I wish that there's more gamified/cartoonified elements of real-world racing in the game here. Some examples I could think of are:

- quick tire wear (loss of grip after 5 or so laps, maybe have a 20-lap endurance race?)
- quick fuel consumption (in the same line of thought with tire-wear, varies with each car)
- component damage (too much crashing into others, damage to overall performance)
- track rules (not sure how this can be implemented, but a quirky full-course yellow and a stop-n-go penalty would be amusing)
- pit stops (complements the tire wear and fuel consumption)

Mind you, I'm mostly a sim racer when it comes to a car race. I'm not asking for this game to be a sim, I just wish that it has some elements of a sim, but make those elements exagerrated and quirky, especially when you consider the vibe that this gme is giving out.
