About This Game


Have you ever wondered what it feels to be a ghostly being?

You have always lived happily in your mansion. A mansion that you managed to obtain with a lot of hard work and many personal sacrifices. Naturally you don't want anyone else to take care of it, not even in death! But now, after your demise, many tenants have taken over it, and this is intolerable to you.
Luckily, your love for your manor has granted you powers that will help you get rid of those pesky individuals.

Watch them run and scream, scaring them using your intelligence, wit, and with the use of powers like possessions, spectres, altering objects and more.
Have fun and play with the occult... and become a Poltergeist!

Poltergeist: A Pixelated Horror is a puzzle game for PC/Mac/Linux, in which you are a ghost named Henry B. Knight. You will scare the tenants currently living in your house, so you can have some peace at last in the afterlife.

Charm goes a long way in games. A concept that's not fully fleshed out or gameplay with some rough edges can all be forgiven if the charm is there. Poltergeist bears that charm in spades, from its blocky furnishings to its hand-drawn cartoon cutscenes, enough that you won't mind the simplistic puzzles that form its core.

You take on the role of Henry B. Knight, vengeful deceased, as he scares the stuffing out of anyone who would call his mansion home. The game plays out in 60 puzzles across four time periods, each with its own look and feel. Each puzzle has a number of folks milling around, and provides you a limited number of ghostly powers to terrify them with. The powers range from jiggling loose objects to full-on possessing people, but their mechanical functions are all similar. Puzzles are divided into rooms, and the powers do a certain amount of "damage" to the people in one or more rooms. When you do enough damage to a person, they vacate.

The puzzle, then, is figuring out what combination of powers in what order will get everyone gone. You might need to fling a lamp from one room into another to soften someone up for a shadowman encounter. Or you might have to send a ghost wolf to drive a lone inhabitant into a room full of people so you can explode the TV and scare them all at once. There are special characters that block certain powers, and each era of puzzles ends with three boss fights against some entertaining characters with their own ways of fouling you up. None of the puzzles are terribly challenging, since they're just sequence problems, but they're gratifying enough to overcome. My only hangup here was the fiddly attention-getting power, which works based on some unclear proximity rules that made it hard to complete a few puzzles, even when I knew exactly what to do.

What really kept me invested here was the presentation. The graphics are a peculiar mix of 2D characters against chunky, voxelly 3D levels. It works better than it sounds because the features of the levels all have physics, and your terrified tenants bang open doors and knock over vases. The adorable visuals are coupled with some equally adorable sound effects of gasps, screams, and Sims-style nonsense. You also get loads of hand-drawn cutscenes for the eras and bosses, accompanied with some charmingly not-quite-right English subtitles. The goofy endings are also worth your time to see, capping off a perfectly enjoyable package.

It isn't groundbreaking in any way, but scaring the little pixel people half to death with your goofy powers makes it more than worth your time. If you've played the classic Ghost Master, Poltergeist feels like a much lighter, more streamlined version of that. There really should be more games about haunting the adorably unwary, but until we get them Poltergeist will definitely scratch that itch for a few hours.

Pixels and ghosts is the theme here!

You play the role of a Poltergeist as they attempt to spook out the occupants of the house they built and died in. To do this you can use one of your spooky abilities to instil fear into the family members that have taken up residence in your home. However you're limited in the number of times you can use each ability per level, and some levels restrict which abilities are available... and this is where the puzzle element comes into the game.

You essentially have a limited number of moods to cause enough fear to get the people to leave. Some abilities cause more fear than other, while some people in the house can counter-act the spooky spells if they see them. For example the Gypsys and 'Good' witches will stop things from flying around if they're in the same room as the spell.

The game consists of 60 levels broken into Eras of 15 'puzzles' with 3 boss encounters before you can progress. Each Era has it's own theme, and 'topical' references. Each Era has it's own musical score too, and while that might sound a little grating as it's the only music you'll hear for 15 levels, I've yet to become overly annoyed by the music. In fact I've found it to have it's own strange level of charm, however I can understand it might not be for everyone.

Essentially Poltergeist: A Pixelated Horror is a puzzle game that requires a little bit of logic and understand to solve each level.

There might be those thinking this is similar to 'The Haunting' on the Mega Drive, sadly however this game doesn't have as much freedom in choices/design when it comes to clearing out the level or spooking the residents. Despite this it's still a fun experience, one I enjoyed playing in small bursts when I had a bit of downtime or was idly watching TV.

In short a fun puzzle game to play in short blasts. For a real treat click the Rubix Cube on Level 21 a few times (may cause a bug).

An interesting puzzle game, where you play the ghost, that's scaring everyone out of his house! It feels really satisfactory, when you complete a level and everyone is running for their lives out of your house!
You have a reasonable arsenal of spells at your disposal, but most of the time the levels are tightly created, so you really need to plan ahead in order to complete the level - most of the time by using every single one of your spells.
As you progress trough the game, the inhabitants of the house will try to to "fight" you, by inviting special characters into the house - priests, ghost busters and what not (there is no real interaction, the level just starts with the special characters inside the house). Those special characters will disable/counteract a couple of your spells, so you really need to plan your moves in order to succeed.
Planing is vital to this game, as you will find yourself restarting the levels over and over in order to find the perfect interaction to succeed. That is especially true for the boss levels - the last 3 levels of each era involve a Boss and need to be unlocked by completing a number of the other levels. The Boss represents a tough person invited to the house to deal with you once and for all! In order to defeat him you need to scare everyone else outside, and then deal with him 1v1.
I would definitely like to see some keyboard controls added into the game at a later stage, but that is more a question of convenience, as there is no time pressure to solve the puzzles and playing only with the mouse and having one hand free has its advantages :p

You also get loads of hand-drawn cutscenes for the eras and bosses, accompanied with some charmingly not-quite-right English subtitles. The goofy endings are also worth your time to see, capping off a perfectly enjoyable package.

It isn't groundbreaking in any way, but scaring the little pixel people half to death with your goofy powers makes it more than worth your time. If you've played the classic Ghost Master, Poltergeist feels like a much lighter, more streamlined version of that. There really should be more games about haunting the adorably unwary, but until we get them Poltergeist will definitely scratch that itch for a few hours.
