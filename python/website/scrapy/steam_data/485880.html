About This Game

LUNE is a experiential haiku, a short poem dedicated to the exploration of the new medium of VR. You can touch and play; poke and prod; dance, move, caress, or simply meditate as you build yourself a pillow fort made from stars, in the company of the quiet moon.

Normally a stoic person, when it comes to good media I tend to loosen up and get a little verklempt at the climax of a well-executed film or music album. Sure, whatever, media is cool, right? it speaks to us? fills up the time a little between sun-up and sundown? ... Such was the impact of this sub-100MB experience that I couldn't make it to the 'end' without pulling my clunky 1st-gen Vive HMD off my face because the tears just wouldn't stay in anymore.

I've followed Cabbibo since I heard about BLARP, trying to track down what he's been kind enough to share*, and felt pretty excited to be given a pre-release evaluation key to provide a little feedback for this latest effort. I came to this with a ready-for-anything attitude, satisfied from a long productive weekend; 20 minutes later, my wife and kids asleep for the night well beforehand, I was a sobbing mess, muttering to myself alone in the bathroom mirror in disbelief at how squarely this dev hit a certain emotional nail on the head: the tritone nail of Beauty, Awe, and Boundless Love.

I showed my eldest (11-year-old) child this experience a couple of weeks later. At first, he sought out the mechanics of the experience in an effort to please me with a show of mastery, as is typical for him and, arguably, the typical gamer mindset, despite my disclosing to him in advance that there's no hurry / no 'finish line.' After a couple of minutes, he slowed down somewhat; what little of his face was visible beneath the HMD was taut and the pace of his arm movement slowed a bit. I wondered whether he'd lost interest.

When it became obvious to him that the experience is on a loop, he sat and removed the headset with a sort of forlorn, distant look on his face and I asked him what he thought. After speaking with him for awhile it became clear that it wasn't detachment I'd observed in him. While he hadn't had the same set of feelings I'd experienced, what he shared spun from the same sort of intimate headspace. I think it would suffice to say that L U N E appeals to introspective/existentialist/psychedelic-experienced people and is a marvelous foundation for a quiet late-evening conversation or two about what matters most in life.

I showed L U N E to my second child, who's also experienced in VR, but he's a vastly different sort of person and seemed somewhat immune to it at this point in life. ;)

A haiku in more than structure, L U N E upends the hurdles between common folk and the emotional core of poetry that mere words so seldom scrape. This is one of the earliest examples of a new breed of communication as universal as mathâthe unsullied experiential dream stateâand exactly the sort of thing I was wishing for the most when I first heard about VR.

Well done, Mr. Cohen. I'm glad you decided to share this with us after all, and I look forward to whatever uncompromising future experiments you might follow it with.
