About This Game

Introducing Assassin’s Creed® Liberation HD, the striking chapter of the pre-American Revolution Assassin’s Creed saga which arrives for the first time on HD consoles and PC. With improved gameplay, a deeper story, and HD graphics, Liberation is an immersive and full Assassin’s Creed experience.

The year is 1765. As the events leading up to the American Revolution heat up in the north, Spanish forces plan to take control of Louisiana in the south – but they have yet to reckon with Aveline, a deadly Assassin who will use every weapon and ability in her arsenal on her quest for freedom. . Whether eliminating her enemies with her multiple assassinations technique or luring them into deadly traps using her tools, Aveline strikes mortal fear into the hearts of those who stand in her way.

As an Assassin, Aveline soon finds herself on an unforgettable journey that will take her from the crowded streets of New Orleans to voodoo-haunted swamps and ancient Mayan ruins. She will play a pivotal role in the turbulent New Orleans revolution and the beginning of a new nation. As she fights for freedom, not for herself, but for those whose life and hopes have been squashed.

Key Features

AVELINE IS BACK
As Aveline, an Assassin of mixed French and African heritage, you must use your skill, instinct and weaponry – including a machete, poison-dart blowpipe, a whip and duelling pistols – to hunt down and eliminate your enemies.

EXPERIENCE THE UN-UNITED STATES OF AMERICA
Feast your eyes on a richly detailed world, thanks to the AnvilNext engine which features groundbreaking physics, animation and innovative combat system. Outmaneuver your enemies in the Louisiana bayou with the unique Tree Navigation system, and eliminate them from above with deadly precision. Deep dive under water as you search for lost Mayan treasures, but stay alert; the hostile wilderness and humid swamps are full of menacing animals.

COMPLETELY IMPROVED EXPERIENCE
Taking full advantage of its new hardware, Liberation HD introduces graphical improvements such as high-dynamic-range (HDR) rendering, high definition environments and characters, updated cinematics the sum of which delivers a complete graphic overhaul of the title. The gameplay experience has been revamped too with updated missions that improve the pacing, new missions that dive deeper into Aveline’s story, and updated world design for more gameplay options during mission. Finally, a particular attention was given to the audio with re-mastered music and all-new SFX.

A Vita game from 2012, Liberation's low-rent roots are immediately clear. Visuals are basic and animation crude despite the HD spruce, and the featured setting - an 18th century Louisiana incorporating New Orleans and Chichen Itza - feels oddly familiar. A croc-infested bayou bridging the two locations is essentially a murky version of ACIII's wilderness. Granted, it's an interesting period for yet another spot of virtual tourism, Ubisoft casting their historical lens on the French and Indian War, the American Revolutionary War, and the horrors of slavery therein.You play Aveline de Grandpré, the enigmatic daughter of a wealthy white merchant and the slave he purchased. Hers is a unique perspective, able to slink through social classes in order to observe them from within and without. The Persona System is the key.This lets you dress Aveline in three different outfits. As The Lady, Aveline can spend coin to bribe her way into restricted areas and lure guards from patrol routes by turning on the charm.As The Slave, Aveline loses her combat strength but gains the ability to incite riots and blend in with other slaves. And as The Assassin, Aveline can do all that stabby stuff you're accustomed to.Trouble is, donning alternative outfits just isn't appealing.Besides a money-making minigame in which you send out ships loaded with spices and cotton by navigating a dull series of menus, there are no new marquee features. It feels stripped-down.Liberation works best when it gives you a strict set of rules and a large area to infiltrate. Sneaking into a governor's mansion, or disrupting some voodoo ritual in the deepest darkest bayou, are lent bite by strict fail states - get spotted and it's game over. The result is a game with a healthy dose of tension.For a multitude of reasons, Aveline is the kind of character I’d like to see more of in gaming, but Assassin’s Creed Liberation HD is just too lacking in too many areas to make it happen. There’s no one thing to point to as overtly broken; it simply dies by a thousand tiny cuts. Its craft is sloppy, its design remains shackled by ill-conceived new ideas, as well as the limitations of the platform it hails from. It’s the definitive version of a game that had very little going for it in the first place.Overall 6/10.

Assassin's Creed Liberation centers around a character by the name of Aveline, a woman raised from childbirth as an Assassin whom now struggles to find her way and test her faith and loyalty to the Assassin Brotherhood and her mentor - all while keeping her business afloat.

This game is a remastered version from the original PSVita release. It features MUCH higher resolution textures, some better music cues, and rebuilt lighting to go along with it all. It does, however, still look a tad blocky at times, as is the case with mobile adaptations. Aveline herself seemed to have been upgraded a bit and her animations are slightly better, but most everything around her looks fairly blocky and inconsistant, though this has almost always been the case with every single AC installation. I do have to give special mention to the lighting in this game though, as it gave a more impressive, visual look to the game that expanded upon the coloring and fidelity of the game that wasn't present in the rather monotone PSVita version, which had a more drab appearance and was less colorful.

The gameplay, for better or for worse (definately for worse) is standard AC, but this particular game has a few issues additional to what's already dreary mechanics. Aveline has 3 different "Personas" which in concept should alter the game marginally - but to me, it doesn't and mainly adds ways to further monotonize the game. The "Assassin" Persona is your go-to - You can use every weapon in your arsenal, can "freely" (more on that later) climb up structures and buildings as well as have the added nuisance of "Yellow Lighting" enemies. The "Slave" Persona is a less glorified version of the Assassin Persona except you can use less weapons and is not as easy to "Yellow Light" guards and the "Lady" Persona, which removes climbing ENTIRELY and severely limits your combat options and of course adds the random nuisance of thugs pestering you. The Persona system in general just ended up being rather underused for me because it requires the need of "Dressing Chambers", and most of the time I would rather just make do with the outfit I had on (except for the lady persona) and just be on my merry way, additionally, it doesn't bring many gameplay alterations to really warrant using any of them except to maybe add or take away certain annoyances.

As far as the standard AC mechanics go... I would dare to say some here are actually worse than they were in AC3. The combat is very random with very little indicators as to what may work or not. Until you actually pick up the "Whip" later on in the game - combat is still very "Counter-centric" except the game itself doesn't give you many options to varie what you do. Some enemies can counter your counters and there's no indicator as to who they may be, they just do - and is very annoying because it makes you keep guessing as to which one may work, only to find it that it suddenly doesn't work. Combat in this game I would say is by far it's weakest it's every been in an AC game, even compared to AC1.

The actual climbing and free running mechanics here are fine - they certainly aren't the worst it's ever been, but there's a few issues I have to point out - one is the "Sticky" issue that also plagued AC3 - where your character tends to want to scale stuff you never even pointed to, making you lose time and another where if your camera is slightly off, you have a chance to make Aveline jump to somewhere you didn't want her to do so, even if you were pointing in the right direction. These two issues aren't particularly common, but they are definately present, and it can be VERY annoying. Another personal issue I had with the game was more of a limitation than anything, but I found that this game was more linear than other AC games - particularly having "blocked off sections" that look climbable and are not.

Stealth. Well, not much to say here really. It's just as bad as it was in AC3. You can hide in certain bushes, but guard don't particularly respond well to alerts or are as dynamic. There are actual occasions where you're up in a tree and you can see the enemy looking UP at the tree, but doesn't trigger the alarm or Yellow Lights or anything, it's one of those occasions where the AI just latches on to where you are and it serves to severely break immersion. You have the option of using Poison Darts or Berserk Darts (given later in the game). Poison Darts make the enemy stumble about and eventually die, and Berserk darts make the enemy wild and prone to attacking his allies. Both of which are very helpful in situations where stealth is a must.

The game also has a number of collectibles, which honestly serve next to no purpose. You can collect Diary Pages to uncover a hidden mini story behind Aveline and her Mother - you can collect Jewelry from charming certain people using the Lady Persona, and of course the usual chest hunt for money. There are also purchases available (via in game stores or "Smugglers") that allow you to purchase weapons and pocket watches (another form of collectible), aswell as a minigame in the form of management of trading/selling goods for Aveline's business found in her Headquarters - as well as the obligatory "Abstergo" or in this game "Erudito" hunt for truth - that have you hunting "Citizen E" for added cutscenes.

All in all though, it's fairly hard for me to really recommend this game to non AC fans. It doesn't bring anything new to an already drab set of base mechanics, is, in my opinion one of the worst AC games in recent memory (Still better than AC3 due to an actual interesting character) due to the extremely limiting gameplay and linearity the story progresses at, as well as some odd bug here and there that make the entire package feel woefully underwhelming. As it stands, it's a decent entry in the AC franchise that is worth a look if you're a fan of the series or if you're looking for a different take on the series - otherwise I would honestly say skip this game, and get the much better Black Flag instead.

Pros:
-Very interesting Main Character, Aveline.
-Amazing Lighting and texture effects bring out life in a drab setting.
-The Whip. This needed to be used more.

Cons:
-Combat is weakest it's been in any AC game.
-The 3 Persona system is nigh useless and serves as mere nuisances that any utility.
-Purchases and Upgrades are fairly useless.
-Weak Collectibles and endgame. No real reason to replay the game outside of story.
-Very short. You can beat the story in around 5 - 7 hours depending on how experienced you are.
-Enemies pop up contantly from out of nowhere.
-Stealth is very weak.

Additional Note: The PC Port seems to have a few issues pertaining to AA. AA in this game SHOULD be turned off if your CPU rating isn't above 3Ghz. It WILL cause heavy lag. Not sure why, but AA in this game isn't very well optimized. VSync locks the Framerate to 30.

EDIT: I only put a "Non Recommended" because it fell right in the middle for me. It's not a terrible game either. Some people DO enjoy this game more than other AC's, that's entirely dependant on you and what you enjoy.

The Assassin's Creed marathon continues. This time it's Liberation, a story set in the same time era as Assassin's Creed 3, you don't need to have played number 3 to understand the story, it's completely stand alone.

The game was initially released as an exclusive to the PlayStation Vita, but was released on Steam earlier this year renamed Assassin's Creed Liberation HD. Sadly the graphics are anything but HD, you can definitely tell that this is a port from a console since the graphical options are next to none.

I failed to follow the story after reaching the halfway point, but the game play is pretty much as you'd expect from a Assassin's Creed game, the earlier stages are actually quite challenging, but once you get your hands on the whip, taking out swarms of enemies becomes as easy as taking candy from a baby.

This is another that you'll need to play with a controller to fully get to grasps with, but the controls are simple enough to pick up once you start playing.

Although this game is good, it is definitely the worst in the series, poor graphics, and the difficult to follow story line really drag it down. I would only recommend you playing this if you are huge fan of the series or have nothing else to play and want to fill ten hours.

Originally, this game was made as an exclusive title for the Playstation Vita, but was later ported to other systems, like the PC. Yes, it's a port, and it shows.

It's supposed to be a HD game, but don't get fooled by that. Though the graphics are not ugly, there's loads of examples of low res textures everywhere. But the worst part, is that the FPS seems to be locked at some very low value, which gets VERY straining on the eyes. And that's my #1 issue with this game, it's painful to play it.

The new feature that makes this game different from other AC games, is that the main protaganist can change between 3 different "roles", each with a suitable outfit for that role. The roles are Lady, Slave and Assassin. When playing a certain role, there are restrictions to what you can do, or extra things that role can that no one else can. For example, as a Slave, you can pick up crates and walk past guards unnoticed, if you don't take too long.

Sounds interesting? Well it isn't. It's a good idea that ends up being a barrier, and restricts you.

Else it's just more of the same. This game is often considered as a standalone addition to AC3, so if you've played that one, you already know a lot about what you're getting. Though it isn't nowhere near the size of AC3.

The low FPS is the main reason I'm not recommending the game. But apart from that, the rest is pretty much "meh..." and not really worth spending much money on. If you're still interested, pick it up on sale, do not buy it at full price.
