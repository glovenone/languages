About This Game

At the bottom of an old ruin, a young girl named Mion awakens with no memory of how she arrived at this dreadful place. Guided by two fireflies, Mion must solve puzzles, outwit monsters, and avoid traps to escape the darkness!

In htoL#NiQ: The Firefly Diary, you will guide Mion's escape from her dark confines by controlling two fireflies—Lumen and Umbra. Direct Mion's movements as Lumen, and destroy obstacles as the shadowy Umbra. Untold mysteries await as you guide Mion to the surface, overcoming traps and puzzles along the way. But be warned—the shadows are never far, and they're always hungry...

After some thorough thinking I can't recommend this game for reasons stated below, but it is a thin line between me reaching this conclusion and recommending it.

For starters I am disappointed in the lack of interaction between Lumen and Umbra, Lumen is a movable light-source that causes objects in the game to cast shadows, but this is just a visual effect as nowhere in the game is this capitalised on, the shadows that Umbra can move around in are always the same size, at best the shadows are parallaxed slighty depending on Lumen's position and there are no puzzles that require you to place Lumen next to an object to cast a bridge of shadow for Umbra to cross over, nor is it used to shield Mion from the Shadows by making them unable to reach her own shadow. I feel this is an aspect that could've been used to great extent for more creative puzzles.

Speaking of puzzles, a lot of them are extremely frustrating past the first chapter which makes the game into a trial and error simulator where you watch Mion die again and again due to a slight oversight or mistake, except for the multiple cases where Mion dies due to something you couldn't possibly have predicted.

Did I mention the game being extremely frustrating? I haven't brought up the practically impossible stage 4 boss yet, I honestly don't know what the developer was thinking with this one. The mechanics for the boss are very simple, it has 24 waves and it randomly picks 1 out of 2 different variations of memory, for the first one a picture flies across the screen and you need to spot the details of this picture and pick the matching one out of 4 choices. The other one shows you a card and shuffles it with 2 others before promting you to select the correct one.
Sound difficult? Each time you fail or succeed the boss speeds up, unlike the rest of the game you actually get 3 extra lives so to say before the boss kills you. But the difficulty lies in the fact that roughly around the halfway point the boss speeds up so fast it's practically impossible to guess this correctly without the use of a 3rd party video recording program to review each of the waves in slow-motion to even catch a glimpse of what's going on. This is just awful game design which could be fixed by making the boss slower. To add insult to injury clearing this boss without retrying and getting all 24 steps correctly awards you with the achievement "You're Just Naturally Lucky?" No, I just have the ability to record my gameplay.

The things about this game that I really liked however is the cute art style and the soundtrack which leads to a nice ambience throughout the game's different stages.
The story is not told through narration and requires hard work (and patience) from the player to reveal fully, and even then it is up to the player to try to piece it together and guess what's actually going on.

In conclusion, it's a great game, but it's flawed in its execution and the stage 4 boss is ultimately the biggest nail in the coffin that prevents me from recommending this title.
I do hope the publisher and developer has plans to localize its sister-title "Rose to Tasogare no Kojō" for Steam however.

Hotaru No Nikki, stylized as htoL#NiQ, is a 2014 dark and atmospheric puzzle game for the PS Vita and later ported to the PC in 2016. The premise initiates with Mion, our small protagonist, waking up to a dilapidated, shadowy world with her memories missing. Mion has to navigate this world with the guidance and light of her two firefly companions, Lumen and Umbra. Danger lurks at every step and corner with monstrous machinery, deadly plants, and shadowy creatures all providing sudden and rapid death to Mion should she make any small misstep during her journey.

The core mechanic of Hotaru No Nikki is to drag your fireflies around to move Mion and interact with objects. The game throws a twist in this mechanic by introducing a Shadow world that can be interacted with by your second firefly. In this shadow world, hidden objects and triggers are displayed that are necessary to solve puzzles or occasionally are traps themselves. Mion herself is a defenseless loli that can only run away and cower, so it is strictly up to the discretion and resourcefulness of the player to guide her through to safety.

Hotaru No Nikki is a lukewarm puzzle game that doesn't ever truly shine with its atmosphere, puzzles, or story. It has many unpolished aspects and oversights within its gameplay, has an insane difficult spike in certain places, and does not fully commit to its tone and atmosphere. However the games amazing and cute visuals go a significant distance to redeem it.

Before I talk about the puzzle designs, I will talk about the movements and design. The level design is such that there are consistently vast and wide areas to be traversed and some puzzles will utilize this space to the fullest extent. That’s unproblematic by itself, but when Mion’s movement is atrociously slow or certain puzzles require 5 minutes + just to go through with the most difficult sections at the very end that will kill you and reset the puzzle, it becomes primarily a waiting game. The level design itself is not polished either with the designer creating certain save points next to teleportation areas that take 20 seconds to teleport, only to come out into a quick-reflex event that’ll kill the player if he’s not quick enough. Then thus failing, the player has to wait another 20 seconds to teleport before attempting the quick-reflex event again. This could’ve been easily, entirely avoided if the developer just simply placed the player after the teleportation. This is one variant among the many where the designer’s lack of polish creates a bunch of needless waiting time on the player’s part.

Hotaru No Nikki is not committed to its tone and atmosphere. I say this because the game demonstrates that it’s not afraid to be graphic for the sake its tone, showing screaming bloodied fetuses at one point in a memory fragment. However it doesn’t go the full way in utilizing graphicness to create a dark tone. There are literally no death animations. Mion just falls back when she’s hit by a box or enemy with some blood splats on the screen and the game resets. Falling off from a high location, the game just quietly turns black and resets itself. Falling into water Mion doesn’t struggle at all to show that she’s drowning/can’t swim before the game resets itself to the last checkpoint. Because of this, all the other attempts to create a grim atmosphere fail because our Loli protagonist is visually invulnerable to the world around her. To contrast, a game like Limbo does this excellently with adaptive gruesome death animations for practically every encounter. But Hotaru No Nikki doesn’t do that, the dangers feel disconnected from Mion and the tone just doesn’t work.

Now for Mion herself and the fireflies: we never get to know Mion’s personality beyond the fact that she’s a timid Loli. This is even after acquiring every memory fragment in the game. She doesn’t say anything and doesn’t express any other emotion besides fear and being high when she takes mushrooms. The only personalizing dynamic at all is the family scenes during the memory fragments.

The game is ruthless and unreasonable for difficulty spikes to be found at intervals. There are two categories of puzzles in the game: observation oriented and strict timing. The observation puzzles are usually fine, but the timing puzzles are consistently brutal. One segment of the game Mion has to get to and descend down a ladder that is being shot at by a plant. The idea is to quickly get to and descend down during the spaces between each shot by the plant. The opening to actually do this is nigh frame perfect and hence quite difficult. Also, you do not get a save point near the ladder. Rather, you start off at the very beginning of the level and have to walk another minute to get to this section each time you die. There is a completely different mechanic of difficulty introduced at certain parts in the game, where you have to navigate Lumen the white firefly through narrow mazes with death on all sides. There is a moment in these maze puzzles where a super compact and precise maze takes place with electrical rods running up and down during the meanwhile. After finishing this brutal maze, the player is immediately smacked in the face with a super tricky timing puzzle where the solution is not easily evident and failing to act upon it within 4 seconds results in Mion’s death. Of course, you don’t get a save for completing the brutal maze puzzle and have to do it all again before getting another opportunity at the timing puzzle.

For the puzzles of observation, none of them are particularly clever or engaging. This coming from a chess player and avid consumer of puzzle games of all types, the puzzle designs don’t seem to be created by a designer who understood the underlying principles of each mechanic well. Rather it comes off as an experimental creation by someone who doesn’t play puzzle games and is poking his first time into the genre. There is no super creative engagement of the mechanics within the puzzles, there is no well consisted or well organized structuring for the puzzles as a whole either. The puzzles of observation come in fairly disorganized form that engage in seemingly irregular types of game mechanic and solutions without overarching cohesion.

The story itself, or the memory fragments, provides an interesting amount of intrigue and suspense as the game plays out and it develops quite well. The ending both regular game and post game does not provide any answers, closure, or meaningful information but rather throws an entirely unheard of and unrelated plot point completely absent and irrelevant to the plot until that very last moment. But I’m not going to hold this against the designers too much for they are game designers, not writers. Their sustaining the intrigue and suspense of the plot itself was a good accomplishment and is probably the most solid aspect about the game aside from the art. The music on the other hand is simply lackluster all around.

I can’t recommend this game to the general person because of all the problems I’ve outlined. A general person would have a difficult time to immerse and engage himself with the good aspects about this game since he’ll keep running into all of its other problems. The game fails as a creative or engaging puzzle game, fails at maintaining a dark atmosphere by not being committed to it, has lots of unpolished nuances that result in frustrating waiting segments on the players behalf, has stupid difficult spikes that are completely unjustifiably, has one-dimensional characters but an intriguing story, and has good art but crappy music. But I still enjoy the game a lot purely due to it's visuals and would recommend it to anyone who has patience and can appreciate the artstyle.

But even with such a mammoth complaint filed away, htoL#NiQ is still a shuffling collection of rage quits and tantrums. Some of this is down to Mion’s atrocious speed and the way she shuffles after her firefly helper on her little, unsteadily legs. Other pitfalls are less forgiving and demand a series of prompt and precise commands the interface is often not up to. To this end, you will often easily figure out what you need to do to bypass a puzzle or a deathtrap; you’ll just find yourself eating countless deaths while you try to unsuccessfully pull it all together.

I feel this is an aspect that could've been used to great extent for more creative puzzles.

Speaking of puzzles, a lot of them are extremely frustrating past the first chapter which makes the game into a trial and error simulator where you watch Mion die again and again due to a slight oversight or mistake, except for the multiple cases where Mion dies due to something you couldn't possibly have predicted.

Did I mention the game being extremely frustrating? I haven't brought up the practically impossible stage 4 boss yet, I honestly don't know what the developer was thinking with this one. The mechanics for the boss are very simple, it has 24 waves and it randomly picks 1 out of 2 different variations of memory, for the first one a picture flies across the screen and you need to spot the details of this picture and pick the matching one out of 4 choices. The other one shows you a card and shuffles it with 2 others before promting you to select the correct one.
Sound difficult? Each time you fail or succeed the boss speeds up, unlike the rest of the game you actually get 3 extra lives so to say before the boss kills you. But the difficulty lies in the fact that roughly around the halfway point the boss speeds up so fast it's practically impossible to guess this correctly without the use of a 3rd party video recording program to review each of the waves in slow-motion to even catch a glimpse of what's going on. This is just awful game design which could be fixed by making the boss slower. To add insult to injury clearing this boss without retrying and getting all 24 steps correctly awards you with the achievement "You're Just Naturally Lucky?" No, I just have the ability to record my gameplay.

The things about this game that I really liked however is the cute art style and the soundtrack which leads to a nice ambience throughout the game's different stages.
The story is not told through narration and requires hard work (and patience) from the player to reveal fully, and even then it is up to the player to try to piece it together and guess what's actually going on.

In conclusion, it's a great game, but it's flawed in its execution and the stage 4 boss is ultimately the biggest nail in the coffin that prevents me from recommending this title.
I do hope the publisher and developer has plans to localize its sister-title "Rose to Tasogare no Kojō" for Steam however.

htol#NIQ, or Hotaru no Nikki (if you want a clearer pronounciation), reminds me a lot of various indie puzzle platformers such as Limbo, and Braid. It is also a dark and twisted puzzle platformer game.
You control a firefly that controls a young girl and that firefly acts as her guide. You also control a firefly within her shadows that can manoeuvre its way through other shadows and use it to do things like activate switches, cut roots, stun shadow monsters, and more.
The controls are very simple and all you need is just your mouse to play. You can play the game with a controller, sure, but as it was originally a Vita title where you used the touchscreen to control the firefly, the mouse is a natural with this game.
However, even if the controls are simple, you'll learn that it's not easy playing this game. You'll face trial and error when you learn that Mion dies in one hit. This means that you have to be extremely careful on guiding her. On top of that, this is a puzzle platformer. You have to solve a lot of puzzles using both fireflies. Each level presents itself with various new puzzles to take on, from levers, to parasol/mushroom floating, and even lumen mazes that remind you of some science experiments where you're not allowed to touch the edge. Bosses make big use of the environment around you making it so that you will have to do certain things to beat that particular boss while still providing a means of trying to kill Mion. I like the variety in these puzzles, however, as with most puzzle games, they aren't easy. In fact, this is a trial and error game, where one mistake might be the end of Mion. At least however, it provides the right amount of frustration where you can overcome all the trials purely on skill (it's not luck that wins, other puzzle games). Want an extra challenge? Each level, including boss levels, has special achievements ranging from finishing a certain portion of a level in a certain amount of time and in few things (door openings, lever pullings), to discovering a hidden moon, and more.
Perhaps the big thing about this little game is the backstory. Scattered in each level (except boss levels), are memory fragments. Touch them and you'll be taken to this isometric pixel game where you'll learn so much of the game's backstory... who Mion is, and the secret behind her parents. Behind this game lies some of the darkest secrets towards the game's story. Be on the lookout for these memory fragments, because you may be in for a shock. And to make it clear, it may feel like a horror game, but it isn't entirely horror.
The bottom line, this is a fun puzzle platformer game that harkens the good days of indie puzzle platformers. It was a big shock to me when I learned a lot about this game's story, but it also made me think critically on puzzle solving, while still getting frustrated by all those deaths (Fun note: dying does give you some achievements). Also another part of why it reminds me of indie platformers is the art style, which is magnificent. In an age where most people play mostly uninspiring puzzle games on mobile, this is the puzzle game that I need to help break the trend. You should give this game a go, especially if you have a tablet PC or a Vita.
