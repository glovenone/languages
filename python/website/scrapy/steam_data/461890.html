About This Game



In NORTH you play a man who applies for asylum in a city filled with strange creatures and strange customs.

Dealing with the issue of the contemporary refugee crisis while at the same time being deeply rooted in a classical cyberpunk atmosphere à la Blade Runner, NORTH features a dark synthpop soundtrack, a sprawling mega-city and weird monster-like inhabitants.

The gameplay is very straightforward and mostly consists of exploration and simple puzzles. The main difficulty is to understand what you have to do in order to get asylum. You've come from an foreign land in the south and find yourself lost and confused - a confusion you convey through letters to your sister back home. An important part of the gameplay, these letters help you understand your tasks while at the same time moving the narrative of the game forward.

NORTH is short (20-40 min) and comes with a full soundtrack (8 songs).

Interested in a game that has absolutely nothing to do with the first sentence of its description, or anything else for that matter? Then NORTH is the game for you!

---------------------------------------------------------

If you are aware of my work - my reviews - then you'll already know that I don't dish out "not recommended" labels lightly. I try my best to like a game because I realise all the effort that developer's put into their creation. I can't, for the life of me, however, recommend this title in any way, shape, or form.

Let me start off by exploring the first half of the description of NORTH: "...dealing with the issue of the contemporary refugee crisis..." Am I right in assuming that they're talking about the Syrian refugee crisis?

If "yes", then I've been following the refugee crisis for a very long time now. A lot of my friends happen to be Syrian, immigrants prior to the "modern mess" (term used lightly), so I try to do them the honour of keeping up with the news as much as possible. From the very first playthrough of the game, I couldn't understand where the "contemporary refugee crisis" label was being directed towards. Was it the graveyard in the work area? Perhaps depicting that the Syrians were working hard for "nothing" at this point? Or was it the broken down houses that were left in deplorable conditions? In that case, this game could've been described as a game "dealing with the issues of hardship and life".

In my opinion, this game does not portray the refugee crisis honestly, whichever one that may be. If anything, it is a slap in the face to those who face the real life hardships and realities of war, and I think it's a shame that the developer's - so it seems - just jumped on the bandwagon in order to get more sales by describing it as such.

Graphics
Taking a step beyond that description, however, I can say that I did enjoy the graphics. NORTH is set in a vibrant, colourful cyberpunk world, where odd alien like creatures "roam" the land. There are times when the colour just pops out, and there are also times where you will be unable to see things due to darkness.

Audio and Sound Effects
The only other positive I can find in this game is the music and sound effects. When the aliens cry, you will know that they are crying. It is creepy, scary, and satisfying. If anythng, the developer's really paid attention to the detail of the sounds, but neglected other aspects of the game. That is never good.

While I praise the soundtrack, I do have to say that it was random sometimes. For example, the music was calm and soothing in one second of the game, and in the next, after I walked through a door, it went all synthwavey. Where's the transition?

---------------------------------------------------------

Pros
+ Decent soundtrack and sound effects; those aliens sounded scary enough
+ Trading cards - I didn't even want to say this but it was one of the few pros that I could muster up
+ Graphics are acceptable for such a cheap game; then again, most games made with Unity are nice

Neutral
± Transitions in soundtracks could be a lot better
± Very short game; you'll spend more time idling for the cards. At the same time, this game has been in a number of bundles so it is priced well for what you get

Cons
- No save button
- This is 2016: where's the menu?!
- Seriously? Who knew the "enter" key could be so brutal?
- Why do I have to keep watching the intro every time I start the game?!
- How does one close the game? Alt + TAB then click the "X"? Please enlighten me!
- No inventory - can't re-read messages in case you missed something or clicked too fast
- Highly sensitive controls and, with the lack of an FOV slider, made playing the game all the more difficult
- Change the description of the game; it is disgusting to use such a highly publicised issue in order to gain sales (if that is the case and it really does appear to be, as stated previously)
- No explanation whatsoever as to why you're playing the game; what I mean is that there is no real meaning to the game. If you think otherwise, then please point it out to me and I'll chew on it. I can't find anything
- Lack of instructions makes for frustrating gameplay; you honestly are just left to wander in this world. Perhaps it has a deeper meaning, such as most refugees are left to their own devices, but I'm not going to give them the benefit of the doubt

---------------------------------------------------------

The developer's really got one thing right: this game truly is a mystery.

If you're willing enough and have nothing better to buy, then give this game a try. While I really wanted to enjoy this game, the description - which essentially introduces and sets the mood for potential buyers - set my expectations far too high. They were not met.

For a game that truly introduces someone to the hardships of war, take a look at This War of Mine instead. In the mean time, avoid this title until the developer's actually produce a story befitting of the refugee crisis.

Overall
3/10

North is an indie adventure title, developed by Outlands. During the game, you play as a man who is seeking asylum in an industrial, cyber-punk world filled with alien-like creatures. The guy has also reached out to his sister to join him in this new city. The game is centered around the current event refugee crisis, and it comes wrapped up in a synthpop soundtrack.

As soon as this review is finished, I'm refunding the game; I've never bothered to refund anything less than $3 before, but North really takes the cake. The title starts off promisingly enough with a pretty, one minute unskippable intro of a sunset-laden lake with surrounding clouds, and then you're thrown into a dark industrial city. Immediate exploration either takes you to a church, your home, or "work".

If you enter the church, you're told to press the right mouse button to "convert", but you're never seen by the all-seeing eye, and then you're kicked out of the area; it should also be noted that the FPS here drops down to 10-20 for no reason. If you enter your home, you get to look at some artwork scattered about and talk to an alien. If you enter work, you get to proceed to the mines which I guess is a puzzle area; I couldn't make it past here, I kept dying. I guess the main objective of the mines is to turn a certain number of "jackhammers" on, and I ended up getting three but couldn't find any more. You're on an undisclosed time limit, where after every few seconds one of your three health blocks disappear.

There's no main menu, options menu, and no direction of what you're supposed to do. The store page says that the "game is very straightforward"; complete and utter bull ♥♥♥♥, North is the epitome of vague. Whenever you perform certain actions, you get an envelope that appears in the bottom left corner of the screen. This generally indicates a note or memo having been created, but I've found no way to open any of these notes; I pressed literally every button on my keyboard trying to figure it out, along with the middle mouse button. I also see that I'm not the only one with this issue, which reassures me that I'm not just an idiot.

The only thing that North has going for it is a decent soundtrack, which struck me with a Silent Hill vibe at the very beginning. This game is too abstract and art-chic for its own good, while trying to be different it ends up being frustratingly off-putting. I guess I'm just not artsy enough to get this one at all. If only there was a way to read the notes... like, maybe being able to press the "J" button for Journal, but I guess that idea is too mainstream.

Rating: 0.5/5.0 - Abysmal, avoid it like the plague.

I assume the letters that appear in the bottom left corner contain the information I need to move forward, but for the life of me I couldn't work out how to open them! I even restarted the game to read the input controls but nothing helpful there either.

The game performs badly in some areas too (particularly the Church where my FPS dropped to around 10) and at one point in the Mines it crashed on the 'YOU DIED' screen. Having to restart the game and watch the 1 minute unskippable intro for the third time is not fun..

While I appreciate that the developer was trying to give me a sense of unfamiliarity and mystery - the game is about a refugee in an unknown land after all - at times I felt it was much too vague. You'll end up going into rooms and being given a cryptic message which doesn't explain what you're supposed to do, nor point you in any direction. Maybe I should consult the letters I keep receiving? Oh wait, I don't know how to open them. Though maybe I'm just stupid and everyone else got it, I don't know.

In all, I like the concept of this game but it needs polishing. Give me a few more clues or at least tell me on screen how to open those darn envelopes and I'm sure I'd enjoy this a lot more.
