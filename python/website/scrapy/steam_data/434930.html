Get instant access and start playing; get involved with this game as it develops.

Note: This Early Access game is not complete and may or may not change further. If you are not excited to play this game in its current state, then you should wait to see if the game progresses further in development. Learn more

What the developers have to say:

Why Early Access?

“I am a solo developer and this is my first full featured game. It only makes sense to release in Early Access so this game can reach it's full potential with the help from you, The Player. One man can only test run the game so many times before losing his mind. With the communities help I am sure we can make this game all it can be!”

Approximately how long will this game be in Early Access?

“The game needs some real ironing out, there are bugs (not the cockroach kind) and some mechanics that are not quite correct yet! With your help this game could be out fully in a month or two.”

How is the full version planned to differ from the Early Access version?

“The full game is to feature TWO modes. The standard "GiAnt" mode, a fun play on a Simulation game with a little story on the side. A real platform adventure.

The other mode to feature in the full game is the one I am most excited about... "GiAnt Warfare". A First Person Shooter where you can act out your revenge on all the enemies you hated in the "GiAnt" simulator mode. This will be less heavy on story, training and nest aspects like in the simulator version, and more concentration on carnage! Imagine Serious Sam or Duke Nukem, but you are tiny! Sounds good to me!”

What is the current state of the Early Access version?

“"GiAnt" mode has been created and can be played! But some people are finding parts that are just too hard or just too damn unfair, also, some people are finding issues. Play the game, find problems, help me make this a classic!”

Will the game be priced differently during and after Early Access?

“The standard price will be kept the same”

How are you planning on involving the Community in your development process?

“Play the game, find problems, tell me what you find, then I and you can make this game together!”

NOTE: This game was given to me by the developer for review purposes. This did not influence my review in any way.

==========================================================
---------------------------------------OVERVIEW-------------------------------------
==========================================================
GiAnt is a first-person ant simulator from a new solo indie developer.

You play Adam, an ant who is unfortunately miscategorised as a girl and given the job of a worker ant. The game starts with you attending a brief session at ant school before "The Captain" sends you out into the world to collect candy. Outside of the nest you need to avoid other insects and spiders while you explore and collect enough candy to be allowed back into the nest.

Unfortunately I'm not sure what happens after that, as in over an hour of play I didn't manage to collect any more than two pieces of candy before the game crashed or I fell afoul of the aforementioned other insects and spiders.

==========================================================
----------------------------------------QUALITY-------------------------------------
==========================================================
Graphics - Graphics are a very mixed bag. Ant models are nice and have a bit of character, and the nest is sort of good in a cute way. Once you venture outside, though, you'll see the graphics really start to shine, with a great blurry effect put to fantastic effect on everything beyond a certain distance away. When you stop and look at it, it's really very impressive.

Unfortunately, when you move there are some weird texture distortions and flickering, and shadows tend to flicker in and out in some areas as well. Couple that with some variable-quality close-up models and scenery, and a general lack of activity and feeling of emptiness in the game world, and after the first "Wow!" moment, I rapidly found myself much less impressed. The sprinkler, too, is terrible, and I still can't work out how a blowfly is hurting me! The cubist reflections in the kitchen cupboards are pretty weird as well (see the screenshot in my profile).

The UI is mostly good, although the use of a default Unity UI font for the candy and lives counters looks very out of place. Loading screens, too, are pretty terrible: just a plain black background and two-tone grey bar in the middle of it that fills up as the next area loads. It's disappointing and quite jarring, taking away from the immersion of the game.

Graphics options are fairly straightforward and offer a fair bit of choice for optimisation for lower-power systems.

Sound - Sound is fairly minimal, but okay. The lack of any consistent background music or audible environmental sounds makes the world feel even more empty sometimes, but triggers for music and some world interactions kick off some interesting changes.

The game includes voice for the ants. This is mostly well done, although the "Ow!" sounds get a bit painful to listen to over and over again. Similarly, the annoying high-pitched spider sounds and buzzing of the flies when they're near are too loud and grating.

There are no sound options to adjust volume of music, environmental sounds, and voices, and the mix is a little off, making it impossible to get the right volume all the time. I missed a fair bit of the speech due to nearby insect sounds or a sudden musical trigger, and environmental sounds (except for other insects) would be good if they were a bit louder. These should all be separately adjustable for user preference.

Gameplay - The game plays like any other first-person game: WASD for movement, space to jump, and mouse to look around. You're unable to attack or defend yourself, or even interact with the environment, though, so you'll not be needing mouse buttons any time soon.

The aim of the game is to wander around collecting candy, which you locate on the minimap (sorry, "Antdar") in the bottom right. It's not always reliable, though, as the map is in 2D, whereas the candy can be above or below you as well.

As you explore you'll encounter (overly) aggressive flying insects (that look like blowflies) and spiders, as well as other creatures on occasion as well. Other ants will talk to you, while environmental creatures will just ignore your passing.

The game also includes powerups, but these aren't explained and they don't seem to do anything except to add some green misty stuff to the area directly around them for a short time. When I collected one once it seemed to scare a hostile insect away, but the next time it did no such thing and I was killed while walking into and out of the powerup trigger.

Hostile spiders and insects are annoying and have extremely poor AI: they just make a bee line (haha) for you all the time and keep following around until they've killed you. There's no way to fight them off or escape, especially since the powerups seem to do nothing the majority of the time.

To make matters worse, the game plays like a typical gravity-based FPS, completely ignoring the fact that you're an ant and should be able to climb pretty much anything. The best example of this is probably the candy that's 'hidden' at the top of the tree under which your nest is located. Rather than just running up the tree, you have to find a weird walkway that seems to be made of timber boards running up and around the tree like a corkscrew, but even that is on an angle and you'll find yourself getting stuck between it and the tree as your ant insists on staying perfectly horizontal. It's a real shame and is probably the biggest problem with the game, as it completely ruins the suspension of disbelief.

It's very easy to become stuck on the terrain, and there are some crazy clipping issues as well, making it possible for you to walk through some stones and then become stuck between them and other terrain.

Finally, there are a number of little niggling problems:
- poor writing in the small amount of text that's included (the wrong 'their', 'occational')
- the game often crashes if you minimise it while playing
- there are no visual damage indicators when you're being attacked, so if you've turned down the annoying sound when insects are chasing you, you'll often be killed before you realise it
- sometimes the game doesn't realise when you've died, so you have to quit to the main menu and restart to try again
- the ant bridge mentioned as a cool feature is really just a static part of the map (see my screenshot), rather than a cool dynamic feature of ants helping you
- the Steam page says it has achievements, but it doesn't seem to (at least not yet)

==========================================================
-------------------------------------PROS & CONS------------------------------------
==========================================================
Pros:
+ very impressive 'long range' graphical effects (esp. outside)
+ creative use of size differences
+ mostly entertaining voices
+ an interesting idea, by a solo developer

Cons (see above for more):
- you're an ant, but you're stuck on the ground and can't climb anything
- poor enemy AI that just doesn't give up
- a lot of terrain and graphics problems
- some annoying sounds and no volume options
- a very empty-feeling world that's just not much fun to explore

==========================================================
--------------------------------------CONCLUSION-----------------------------------
==========================================================
I'm sad that I can't bring myself to recommend GiAnt. I wanted to love it, and for the first few minutes I did. But after that all of the game's faults started to appear, and there are many.

With a little bit of work, GiAnt could be a great game, and if it were in Early Access I would probably recommend it. But it's now been released and it shouldn't have been; it's unfinished and it needs a fair bit of work to reach its potential.
