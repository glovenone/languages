What the developers have to say:

Why Early Access?

“Steno Arcade was envisioned as a suite of four arcade games designed to teach and drill steno fundamentals in an immersive, addictive, responsive, and interactive way. By releasing as an Early Access title, we can release the games in the suite one at a time while working with the community to gather feedback to improve upon the games.”

Approximately how long will this game be in Early Access?

“We expect Steno Arcade to be in Early Access for approximately 9-12 months.”

How is the full version planned to differ from the Early Access version?

“The full version will include a total of four games, whereas the the Early Access version will launch with one of four games in the suite and have the rest added over time.”

What is the current state of the Early Access version?

“The Early Access build of Steno Arcade includes one of the four games in the suite, Steno Hero! Imagine your favorite rhythm games, but instead of pressing buttons that vaguely emulate a simplified version of your favorite instrument, you're hitting steno chords to precisely and accurately produce the lyrics to your favorite music! It's like singing karaoke with your fingers! The Early Access version will launch with four songs, with more to be added over time:

"I'm Your Moon" by Jonathan Coulton
"Mandelbrot Set" by Jonathan Coulton
"That Spells DNA" by Jonathan Coulton
"I Feel Fantastic" by Jonathan Coulton”

Will the game be priced differently during and after Early Access?

“No, this game is and will always be free.”

How are you planning on involving the Community in your development process?

“We are looking to the Community for feedback on the game's design, UX/UI, difficulty, and bug testing.”

About This Game

Steno Arcade is a suite of four arcade games designed to teach and drill steno and keyboarding fundamentals in an immersive, addictive, responsive, and interactive way. Steno Arcade is 100% free and open source and works with any steno machine or keyboard.

The Early Access build of Steno Arcade includes one of the four games in the suite, Steno Hero! Imagine your favorite rhythm games, but instead of pressing buttons that vaguely emulate the original instruments, you're hitting steno chords or keyboard keys to precisely and accurately produce the lyrics to your favorite music! It's like singing karaoke with your fingers! Steno Hero currently includes seven songs, with more to be added over time:

“Sunday Smiled” by Hungry Lucy
“The Standing Ones” by Hungry Lucy
“I Think I Started a Trend” by Brad Sucks
“I’m Your Moon” by Jonathan Coulton
“Mandelbrot Set” by Jonathan Coulton
“That Spells DNA” by Jonathan Coulton
“I Feel Fantastic” by Jonathan Coulton

While Steno Arcade is designed to be played with a steno machine, it can also be looked at as a traditional typing game that can be played with any standard keyboard. You only need an NKRO (N-key rollover) keyboard if you intend to learn stenography and want to play the game using steno. For example, the slower, easier songs in Steno Hero are perfectly playable using a standard keyboard, but when you get to the faster, harder songs you may find yourself having trouble keeping up with a standard keyboard and will need to use steno if you want to complete the songs with a high level of accuracy and precision.

Steno Arcade is being developed on top of the Godot open source game engine, and the source will be released under the Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 4.0 International (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0) license upon completion.

Steno Arcade is brought to you by The Open Steno Project and For All To Play.

Early Access Review
Posted: 26 April
Full review below.

Pros
  • Clean, well-designed, aesthetically appealing UI
  • Addictive and unique gameplay, interesting mix of rhythm and typing game
  • Smooth, responsive control
  • Good choice of musician in my opinion, anyway!
  • Raises awareness for a grossly underrated and disused skill
  • (Presumably) A good learning tool for amateur stenotypists

Cons
  • Very little content available for those capable of beating it
  • Visual style is Atari-level retro; an understandable art direction for a shoestring-budget indie game, but some may find it charming, others unappealing


    Edit: As of 05/04/2016, Steno Arcade received an update that added two songs with much lower BPM and slower vocals; it's infinitely more accessible for the average player now, which makes it much easier to recommend to non-typing experts. Obviously this does not compromise on the fact that, by design, anything above easy is likely to be incompletable for a keyboard user. It's great to see that For All To Play & co. are listening.

    This game is a kind of genius that not many people will understand or appreciate at first glance. Stenotype is to keyboards as bicycles are to walking: Both are many orders of magnitude more efficient than their alternatives, offering faster speeds for greatly reduced effort. On a bicycle, you can move at the speed of a full-out sprint-or faster!-- with the equivalent effort of a leisurely walk; largely the same can be said for our dear friend, stenotype. Any frequent typist can benefit from the speed and convenience of steno, just as any frequent pedestrian can benefit from the speed and convenience of a bicycle; nevermind the freedom either offers to those with injuries or disabilities.

    But much like the bicycle, unfortunately, steno is a century-old advancement stifled by a world that makes little effort to accommodate it. Learning stenotype is prohibitively costly due to the absurd tuition fees that for-profit schools charge, and even used, student-grade stenotype machines run upwards of several hundred dollars; think thousands for professional equipment. Further, these machines are simply not designed with non-professional usage in mind, much less the software they operate on.

    This game is a part of an ongoing effort to change that by facilitating a cheap entry barrier, compatibility with any software and ease of learning, bringing steno into the mainstream. It isn't just for middle-aged-and-up court reporters and other professionals; everyone can benefit from stenotype.

    But what about the game itself? Unfortunately, there's not much to talk about at this juncture. I personally feel that For All To Play pushed this to Early Access a bit too early; it shows in the lack of content and the absence of accessibility that the average person has to that content. My understanding is that Steno Hero is designed to be the hardest of the four games planned for Steno Arcade, and in my view implementing it first was misguided- perhaps it was the easiest to code/produce assets for? I can't be certain, but the attitude of the negative reviews here are telling.

    The difficulty curve at the time of writing is less like a curve and more like a wall; I type at an average of 115wpm with an unmodified QWERTY layout and no shortcuts or other aids. The easiest of the four songs was challenging for me, but I managed 95% accuracy; my accuracy plummeted to <50% on the second song and only got more abysmal from there. This readily highlights the obvious advantages of steno over traditional keyboards, but many players won't know what they're getting into when they start this game, and their first impression will be one of frustration rather than wonderment. It fails to have broad appeal to the already-niche community of typing game enthusiasts because of its uncompromising difficulty, which cheats it of its most likely audience.

    This can easily kill an Early Access game before it has any chance to blossom by depriving it of positive reputation and revenue, though Steno Hero, being a passion project, is not directly reliant on Early Access success since it's, well.. free. This was a smart move, as I doubt it would get off the ground in this state otherwise. My advice to the developers would be to implement at least one or two songs that cater toward the average typist, as first impressions are everything. Additionally, the growth of an actual community is likely to hinge on the possibility of community-generated content, so I would be one to make that a focus.

    Ultimately, though, there's a lot of love and polish behind this game, and undoubtedly a very competent team working on it. I'm excited to see it progress. Thanks for reading.

Posted: 15 April
Steno Arcade is made for teaching and drilling steno writers.

The idea is brilliant: type the lyrics of a song while it's played. Indeed, you have a guitarist on the scene, that is singing for the audience in front of him. The more you're writing correctly, the best it is.

However, this is insanely too hard. The easy song was a disaster, the very hard is impossible and the game wants you to write names that aren't common. Worst of it, I fail to see how it will help indeed to be a steno writer with that speed. Besides, I may have been also disavantaged because of my keyboard layout, which is an AZERTY. And when you read the description, you need the accurate keyboard to fully enjoy the game.

After 9 minutes, I was frustrated. I'm used to write English but in the end, with that game, you'll resort to write the easy words. Also, for it to be validated, you need to press SPACE at the end of each word. And when it's passing to another sentence, if you're writing, you'll have to be quick to press SPACE and to write another word.

What is missing is easier levels. I mean why not imagine to begin with words to write in the lyrics instead of the whole sentence. Or to imagine to have a music and to have more time to write a sentence than the real singer is saying? Besides, if it's a matter of being fast to write something, usually, you're using shortcuts before writing properly.

The soundtrack is great, the graphics are nice too as they're retro. But it doesn't save the game to be a huge challenge for whoever doesn't own a steno keyboard or for whoever hoped to have a nice typing game. For that, there is Epistory.

Anyway, if you're not a steno writer or if you don't own the proper material, just be prepared to be frustrated. Everything is there to have a good moment. But for a huge base of players, at this stage, if there is no easier difficulty or other challenges that could to type songs, even if it's free-to-play, it will be too hard for you. I'm not even sure if this game could be used in school without the proper material.
