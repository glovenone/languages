TL;DR: Buy it when it's on sale, it's very short.

I liked this game, but it was extremely short compared to the previous one, Crimes & Punishments. 4 cases and a sort of short ending case. Also, it seems to not follow that continuity, instead acting as a sequel to Testament of Sherlock Holmes.

Pros:
+Decent graphics
+Cases are interesting
+Ability to change costumes is a nice touch (though you can't do it in the last 2 cases)
+The morality/decision making is fun (I also really liked how you can go back and change your decision and see all the different outcomes)
+The overall story is much more obvious, rather than just alluded to in the earlier cases
+Cases can be replayed

Cons:
-Much shorter than Crimes & Punishments (4 and a half cases, compared to 6)
-Once you determine all the possible conclusions, picking one of them is basically just guessing.
-The new voice actors for Holmes and Watson, especially Holmes, are much more bland. Holmes had a very distinctive intelligent-sounding voice in Testament and Crimes & Punishments. Watson's voice in Crimes & Punishments was a perfect soothing doctor voice.
-Performance lags a lot, I especially had issues with stuttering in outdoor environments, and cutscenes involving heavy motion blur

Misc (could be good or bad, depending on your preferences):
~Holmes and Watson now look like Jon Hamm and Jude Law for some reason, which isn't really a bad thing, except they used to look older and more experienced (Holmes previously looked more like Mark Strong)
~There's a lot more action/quick time event-based gameplay in this one, which is okay if you like that sort of thing. Fortunately, you can skip most (but not all) of those segments on normal mode.

In short, it's fun and worth getting at some point, but definitely not worth full price, considering its length. I would wait for a sale.

If you have not played the Frogwares SH series before, do NOT start with this title. Instead:

If you want something more "modern"/"accessible", start with Crimes and Punishments (the previous game).

If you are more of a traditionalist and are looking for a purer experience, start with Testament (the game before C&P).

That should then give you an idea of whether you then want to continue with the rest of the series, in pretty much any order - there are some cross-title references that'll be lost on you if played out of order, but it's not a big deal.

-

While I still enjoyed Devil's Daughter (being an avid and not overly picky SH fan), I found it relatively disappointing compared to the previous titles.

It feels like they pushed the action movie gameplay concept introduced in preceding C&P too far and with inconsistent results. Some sequences do feel cinematic if you don't fail and understand what you're expected to do, but many simply feel unnecessarily long and/or frustrating. Although there is a variety of timed mechanics here besides just quicktime events, which is good, they don't all feel very fun. All of these are skippable, however, but at the expense of certain achievements.

The writing is generally not as good as previous cases, although there are some clever parts. Some aspects of the writing here are downright bewildering - in not a good way. While my recollection of previous plotlines is not great, I feel that this has the most questionable plotting in the whole series.

Similarly, this is the least genuine representation of SH in the series so far. Often the character behaves in a way I can't imagine SH ever would - part of that is the writing, and part of it is the voice actor's delivery.

The wildly varying length and complexity of the cases is odd. Perhaps the studio ran out of budget/time. The result feels strangely inconsistent - a relatively simple first case, a rather long and complex second case, followed by another average case, then a shorter one with limited travel. The final "case" isn't a true case, is short, with oddly direct scene cuts - but perhaps that was an intentional decision to maintain a fast, suspenseful pace.

Graphically, the environments are beautiful, continuing the positive trend in this area and the accompanying sound and music is up to par. Somehow SH and Watson look again younger and, uh, continuing that trend as well. But if you are hung up on how different the character looks from whichever your particular reference is (especially if it's not Paget), then you are missing the point (also there are several costume options).

For a bit more info about the type of gameplay here, see my C&P review, as this is a direct evolution of that and is very similar apart from the aforementioned increase in cinematic action elements.

-

Despite the criticisms above, there are still no other modern games quite like this series, and few that could even be called similar (that I know of). If you are looking for detective mystery that actually feels like one, this is it.

The Devil is in the detail

It is a testament to the strong story telling on display in The Devil's Daughter that for the entire games duration I was hooked - line and sinker. Having grown up reading Sir Arthur Conan Doyle and countless Agatha Christie novels as a child; solving murders and mysteries through the pages of a book was always a favorite past time of mine as a kid. I literally devoured these kinds of novels as a child before moving onto more modern and grim crime fiction as an adult (Mo Hader's incredibly disturbing The Treatment and Birdman anyone?)

http://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=702528253
To be honest I am a bit late to the Sherlock Holmes party on the PC with The Devil's Daughter being my first foray into his digital world. What surprises me the most is that Sherlock Holmes started life originally as a hidden object game from Frogware's called Sherlock Holmes and The Mystery of the Persian Carpet (I plan on starting here going forward and working my way up to what I am told is the criminally superior adventure of the lot - Crimes and Punishment).

The Devil's Daughter has you playing through five cases of murder and subterfuge with an overarching story-line that draws its narrative from the occult. As a young adult games such as the Gabriel Knight franchise where a firm favorite (particularly the second installment - The Beast Within) because they really made you work the clues you where given in the hopes of uncovering their mysteries. The intervening years have seen mostly disappointments in the mystery genre as most of the games I have played have either been missteps or failed to live up to the bar set by the franchise mentioned above. L.A. Noire, for all its technical wizardry at the time of release, was a hollow husk of a game that still to this day I have not managed to fully get through because it simply ends up boring me to tears after an hour or two of play plus its simplistic gameplay mechanics have never made me feel like I am actually solving anything to MURDERED: SOUL SUSPECT from Square Enix (a game that could have been the Gabriel Knight for a new generation) but was lackluster on all accounts. When it comes to The Devil's Daughter the puzzle solving is it greatest strength - for once here is a game that actually made me feel like I was doing real detective work and the game does go to great lengths to achieve this.

http://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=703220034
It is not all perfect though unfortunately. Sadly the developers have seen fit to litter the game with countless QTE's and many of the escape or action sequences introduced at various points are very badly implemented. They play out like sequences from games when the gaming industry was just beginning its love affair with all things Hollywood and to have to endure them is like jumping back in time about 10-15 years without any of the advancements and improvements made. If it where not for the games strong narrative and genuine attempts to make you feel like an actual detective I wouldn't be recommending the game so strongly. The game also has the terrible habbit of locking you into a location until all the clues are uncovered. While I never particularly found the game to be difficult (although some of the clues can be stubbornly obtuse to uncover where others are signposted in neon), I was always able to deduce my next way forward but I can see this mechanic irritating the short-attention spanned around us. Technically the game could be better optimized (considering the games use of the old Unreal 3 engine) and frame-rates tend to be all over the place depending on the amount of onscreen action.

Another area where The Devil's Daughter really excels is in the visual department. It seems the developer - Frogware Interactive - have made great strides over the years moving from what was essentially static backgrounds to a fully immersive 3D world. Victorian London often looks striking in all its grim grayness and it is a genuine joy to explore its taverns, streets and alleyways. The game introduces a series of mini-games throughout the adventure and while not all of them where particularly memorable they help to spice things up a bit so that you don't feel like things have become a little bit too monotonous.

http://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=701733250
The Devil's Daughter emerges as a flawed gem. There is lot to enjoy here and if you are a fan of Sherlock Holmes or mysteries in general then you should love it - warts and all. That said Frogware still has a long way to go if their ultimate desire is to give us a mystery/action/adventure hybrid that can compete with the giants out there. As it currently stands, The Devils Daughter is a missed opportunity but still a hugely enjoyable one at that and if you can overlook its myriad of missteps you are bound to find much enjoyment here.
