How to Play the Game:
In Cthulhu Realms, each player tries to drive their opponent insane or have more sanity than their opponents when the deck runs out.

Each player starts the game with a small deck comprised of initiates, goons and followers. Five cards from the deck form a row of available cards on the table; with more than two players, place three available cards between each pair of players.

Each turn, you start with five cards in hand, playing those cards in order to put locations into play, boost your own sanity or reduce that of others, collect conjuring power to acquire new cards, and remove cards from the game. You can use the power of your locations once each turn. At the end of your turn, discard any cards still in hand and all entities and artifacts that you played.

If you lose all of your sanity, you're out of the game. If you're the only one at the table who's still sane, you win. Otherwise, the game ends when the deck runs out and whoever has the most sanity wins.

Darwin Kastle is the designer of the popular Star Realms Deckbuilding Game, and with the digital release of Cthulhu Realms, he once again provides tons of playability for free, and a one-time upgrade to be able play with your friends and more on any device.

Actually a pretty interesting style of card game. People who are saying "it's all luck based" are missing the point. Yes you play every card in your hand every turn and it doesn't even matter what order in most cases. What makes it interesting is you are building your deck on the fly. You start each game with a base set of weak cards. In the center are the cards you will build your deck out of. Yellow cards do a lot of direct damage and let you discard your cards, green lets you get the crap cards out of your discard pile, purple cards tend to heal and do light damage. Then the cards generally have same color synergies, encouraging you to commit to ones strategy, but allowing you the option of reacting to the enemy build dynamically. I've never played a card game like it. It could use a bit of fleshing out, but not a bad game at all.

Has potential, but lacks content

Cthulhu Realms, despite an interesting primise is dull and incomplete. The game lacks many features, has a basic UI and simple graphics, even with the simple graphic settings turned up to max. The premise of the game is thin, and though it has a lot of potential to be interesting, the game does not do a good job making it so.

The "free version" consists of basic missions against the AI using a deck of the same 30 or so cards. Cards can be confusing to understand due to the scant tutorial (which provides a basic overview of some cards but does not explain the ridiculous combos) and the reliance on icons to explain what the card does. The AI is basic at best, and will play cards almost instanteously. There is a "slow mode" available in the options menu and a log in the pause menu, however they are simple.

Thematically, there is nothing to differentiate between most cards except their appearance. Some have "+1 sanity" or "-1 sanity" as their descriptions, but only one or two points separate some cards. Other issues of the game include some instances where the player is forced to complete some actions because there is not a cancel button. For instance, if you attempt to buy the game and then decide against it, the game will soft-lock and you will have to restart the game's client.

Overall, I found Cthulu Realms incomplete and not worth more than a confusing hour's worth of play. Perhaps it gets better after the first two fights, but I sincerely doubt it.

"Cthulhu Realms" is a card game where the goal is to make your opponent go insane!

CR is a deck-building game (where you acquire cards to your deck during play) with lovecraftian theme and not-so-serious, cartoony artwork. Game mechanics in its foundation are similar to excellent "Star Realms", and while veterans will feel at home, this is not just a re-theme. This time mechanisms are more advanced, and there is a whole lot more of possibilities for strategies and combos.

Presentation is absolutely gorgeous - a lovely cartoony style, a greatly improved UI (comparing to crude one in "Star Realms"), music that fits the theme - all of it combined makes this game a great digital card game experience.

The only negative thing about the game I can think about is that "Cthulhu Realms" lost part of the simplicity and casual appeal which was so great about "Star Realms". Despite its cheerful looks, this is not a casual game anymore, but something deeper. While the game system is simple enough, the iconography may be a little bit confusing at first and requires time to get use to. To sum it up, "Cthulu Realms" is great, but it's not a game that I can teach somebody in 5 minutes, and a specific theme may not appeal to everyone. I don't think it's a husband-wife game, unless your better part is into tentacles and stuff.

If you enjoy card games, I wholeheartedly reccommend "Cthulhu Realms" to you. It's free to try and upgrading to full version is cheap.

Pros:
+ a simple, but surprisingly deep game system
+ charming, cartoony graphics
+ Cthulhu theme!

Cons:
- iconography may be a little confusing at first
- theme may not appeal to everyone

A lot of people from the Humble Monthly are not familiar with Star Realms, but to break it down simply, this game is basically a variation on Star Realms, it is not meant to be like Hearthstone or Magic--in fact, I think it's deliberately trying to avoid that format for something that you can pick up and play without worrying about "long-term investment." Once you know the rules of the game and the basic mechanics, you're set. It's NOT a collectible card game. That's kind of the point.

It is a deckbuilding game, predicated on factional synnergy. It's highly similar to Star Realms, so let me explain the differences from that:
1) Cthulhu Realms has three factions rather than four. Yellow - Draw and discard, Purple - Activate on sacrifice, Green - Initiate sacrifice. It may seem like green/purple is the natural combo, and it kind of is, but you can go pure purple if you also splash in some initiates who can self-sacrifice, or if you get "high house in the mist."

2) Cthulhu Realms has some mechanics that involve returning sacrificed cards. In Star Realms, when you sacrifice, that card is gone for good. In Cthlhu Realms, you can sacrifice, Wilbur Whately to deal 5 damage, then "Acquire" him (putting him on top of your deck) with Dean Halsey.

3) Cthulhu Realms has no "one-drop" cards. This simplifies things a bit, especially in the early turns, and I think is good since the 1-drop yellow "draw another card" was always too good in my mind in Star Realms.

On top of that, this game frankly has a lot more personality and flavor than Star Realms, especially in the campaign which I found quite humorous.

As a long-time tabletop gamer I've enjoyed my share of interesting card games, from Munchkin to Magic and everything in between. Digital conversions of these games tend to be a mixxed bag, though, with everything from interface issues to a lack of customization meaning the experience is never as smooth as it could be.

I'd like to say Cthulhu Realms does better, but in truth while the core concept -- that decks are generated as you play rather than before the game starts -- eliminates balance issues for the most part, the interface and opaqueness of the gameplay to a newcomer are enough to leave me wishing the game had seen a little more care at the design stage.

First of all, the good. The game itself is fun, with a cartoony presentation and fun artwork that help to alleviate some of the creep factor often associated with the Cthulhu mythos. The way the game is constructed leads to almost every card being unique in some way beyond just the artwork, and helps to guarantee players have to stay on their toes, adapting their strategies to match what comes up rather than what they've brought in themselves. This gives the game a very interesting strategy aspect: do you try and aim for a single style of play, looking for cards with certain abilities and hope they come up, or work with what arrives and embrace the chaos that can cause? It's all very fun, and keeps every game fairly fresh.

Unfortunately, that freshness is lost relatively quickly, as card variety is somewhat low. Likewise, the cards tend to have very specific uses, meaning that many cards are entirely dependent on you gaining access to other cards in order to operate effectively. This is obviously an artifact of the game's CCG inspirations, but one that a few weeks more tweaking is likely to help fix.

The biggest issue is in the game's accessibility to new players. The tutorials are quick, blasting you with every piece of information they can without giving you time to absorb how that information can inform your gameplay choices or much in the way of examples thereof, and no mention is made of how to perform actions like zooming in on your hand to review card information or ways to effectively combo cards. For anyone familiar with card battle style games these are concepts you will be able to work out relatively easily on your own, but the jump from the tutorials to the core game is an exercise in confusion at first as you're given no real chance to piece together the information you've received to make informed choices.

With the game still being quite new, most of these issues will almost assuredly be ironed out as time goes on. What's most important is the core gameplay, and while it lacks variety for the long haul the core mechanics are solid and offer a lot of room for expansion in the future.

If you're a fan of CCGs or other battle-style card games, like Munchkin or Zombies!!!, then give the game a shot: the core version is free, after all, and at five bucks the full version is cheaper than a couple of booster packs. If card games fail to enthuse you, or you're not willing to give a game time to mature before you make a final judgement on it, then perhaps wait until the game's had a bit more time to gestate before delving in.

Cthulhu Realms is a nice deckbuilder, with a cutsy-lovecraftian theme. The free version is well worth your time, offering you unlimited games against easy AI. It won't overly challenge you, but it will give you plenty of insight into the mechanics of the game.

There's a nice sense for the potential for synergy. The effects of cards are often contingent upon other types of cards being in play and/or cards being discarded or destroyed. The cards are separated into four colours: black, yellow, green, and purple. The black cards are part of the starter decks, are straightforward, and generally underpowered. The other three colours can be bought, tend to be quite powerful, and tend to have effects that are contingent on other cards of the same colour being in play. However, focusing on one colour will make certain action types more difficult to pull off. For example, focusing on green will allow you to destroy cards effectively, but it will be difficult to regain sanity (your life points) and pick up extra cards.

I also enjoyed the campaign. Each game had slight modifiers to the rules that help encourage some diversity in strategy.

If I had some criticisms, I'd say that the cards are a little confusing, at first. The actions are all denoted by symbols and no text (probably good for language portability!). The game has an English translation on the side, but it makes the game overwhelming at first. I adjusted within about 10 minutes of play.

Additionally, and this brings me to my second, more significant criticism, the sole reliance on symbols limits the depth of the game. There isn't a whole heap of depth here. Most of the time, you'll be looking out for the same powerful cards that you can add to your deck, regardless of what you have in it. Your primary colour of choice will typically dictate the less costly, less powerful additions to your deck, but the game can end up feeling a little samey.

Thirdly, the AI plays with lightning speed. Apparently, there is a log of what it does, but I didn't use it. It made it difficult/annoying to follow what was in its deck, which flattens the possibility of examining synergies within its deck, contributing to the shallowness in strategy.

But, even considering this, Cthulhu Realms is a fun game to while away some time with. I am very much considering buying the full game, for pass and play multiplayer (if only Magic did this too!).
