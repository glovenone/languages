About This Game

Stalk your prey on the quest for stolen goods with your blackjack, sword, and an assortment of unique arrows. Steal for money and uncover the hidden agendas of your allies and enemies as you play through an unravelling story of deception and revenge. Survive in a world where shadows are your only ally, trust is not an option, and confrontation results in death!

Key Features:

  • Pioneering stealth based gameplay brings a new dimension to first person action.
  • Thief™ Gold includes Thief™ The Dark Project (12 huge missions with multiple environments) and the Gold update (3 new campaign missions which deepen the plot and add five new types of enemies)
  • Advanced enemies can see, hear, speak, and sound alarms.
  • Your arsenal includes: blackjack, sword, fire arrows, water arrows, rope arrows and more

System Requirements

    • OS: Windows XP or Windows Vista
    • Processor: 1.8 GHz Processor
    • Memory: 512MB RAM (1 GB recommended)
    • Hard Disk Space: 2GB HDD
    • Video Card: 3D graphics card compatible with DirectX 7 (compatible with DirectX 9 recommended)
    • DirectX®: 7

Easily one of the best stealth games I've played. Though appearing simple by today's standards, the system where you are in various levels of concealment while lurking in several levels of darkness is very well though out and implemented with many ways one can get through a level. It's not uncommon for a player to reach an objective with much more of the area to explore and loot. Very rarely will you find everything in a level on your first go, in fact I never did. AI is intelligent enough to require careful planning at any corner, the abililty to lean and peek pivotal. Listening in on enemy conversations can either be enlightening or just plain amusing, and while their voice acting can be questionable at times, our main characters are voiced superbly.

Being able to save anywhere and any time is helpful, and I'd recommend abusing this system mercilessly. If a guard sees you, escape is definitely possible (unless you're on a difficulty which causes this to fail the mission), but I for one tend to just reload to right before it did, that's just me.

Speaking of difficulty, you can change it at the start of any mission. All difficulty increases add objectives to your missions, sometimes just a higher loot requirement or not killing/being seen, but in some cases you have more side-tasks you must accomplish. Not many games do this anymore, so that was a pleasant surprise.

I cannot praise this game enough, but there is one thing you should know. In this version, cutscenes are pretty broken. There are fixes if you look it up, but I was lazy and just searched them on Youtube between missions. As you get later in the game, they become VERY important to the plot. Don't go through the first five wondering if there should be any like I did!

Now if you like stealth (if you don't, then this is NOT a game for you, it is raw thievery from the shadows), buy this game already. Even full price makes me feel like I paid too little for such an incredible experience!

Posted: 25 June
It is amazing how as computing technology improves and new tools such as VR are introduced that consumers continue to pine for 'better' and 'more immersive' games, as though immersion is some ideal we may hope to one day achieve once the graphics are good enough and the gadgets we attach to our forehead become more effective. However, one need only look back to the late 90's, when a developer known as Looking Glass studios was already forging the path for immersive simulators decades ahead of time.

It achieves immersion not with high resolution character models but lumpy primitive 3D figures. It achieves immersion not with 3D glasses or goggles but with game mechanics and level design. Is Thief Gold perfect? Certainly not. The movement physics can be rough and get stuck on the uneven surfaces of the levels. The AI can be frequently prone to mistakes and it is not uncommon for guards to end up stuck in walls. There are also some monster lair levels which have aged very poorly compared to the rest of the game. But the core mechanics, and the world and places in which you are given to apply them to, give rise to a game which gives the player the sense of interacting with a living real world. The sound design should be given its own praise for bringing the square, low resolution worlds to life, as the mere footstep sounds do its job of grounding you in its setting. But even with the blocky low resolution visuals, Looking Glass does an excellent job of bringing to life the various manors and facilities which you visit to life with the proper decorum and architecture necessary to a place which people live in.

It is also impressive how such an old game takes themes such as dark fantasy and steampunk and synthesizes them into a setting which is uniquely its own, and feels distinct from the multitude of midieval fantasy games on the market. It's gameplay is also a unique fusion of first person exploration, stealth and even collectathons, that genre which wholly belongs to the 90's. It is a wonder why item and treasure collecting are not applied to more realistic genres more often, there is something quiantly enjoyable about wandering through carpeted rooms alone but near danger, admiring the oil paint portraits, opulent furniture and elaborate wall patterns, all while enjoying the thrill of robbing the owners right under their noses, taking everything valuable in sight and adding to your bonus in valuables and goods. In some ways, you could look at Thief as the ultimate walking simulator, one with complex and engaging interactions and a fail state, which would already make it superior to all the walking simulators released within the last few years. Eat your heart out, Dear Esther and Gone Home, this is how you do a walking simulator.

In the early days of 3D technology, when it was an exciting and unexplored frontier, game design was more alchemy than a science. Mechanics and genre formats had not yet been solidified, and the routine of 3D game templates was not yet a thing. Games were not yet pigeon-holed into preset genres(fps, third-person action, rpg etc) and the kinds of games that could exist where limited only by what kind of interactions and mechanics that could be made with the technology available. Sometimes to advance the future, it is necessary to look to the past. Looking Glass showed with their games that to make engaging and immersive video games, you need fun and engaging mechanics and interactions, and an interesting enough world to apply those mechanics too. No amount of technology will ever advance the quality of video games if we do not remember what it is that makes video games fun and unique compared to other mediums. If you want to remember what is possible for the future of video games, look to the past and play Thief Gold.

Thief Gold is one of the best stealth games I've ever played, maybe even one of the best games I've ever played. It offers a deep and involving plot that unravels itself as the game progresses, 15 humungous levels with a lot of depth to each of them (Like having a multitude of ways to carry out your objectives with different playstyles and many hidden goodies and secrets to unveil), an unrivalled atmosphere that hasn't been beaten by any other video game since with brilliant stealth gameplay mechanics and immersive sound design that's done extremely well and makes you feel like an actual 'master thief' (for the majority of the game, anyway).
There's also lots of thinking involved in this game, as well. For example: you get a map in a certain mission that is barely filled in, because nobody's ever been in that building before. That makes the map pretty much useless, for the most part. So, the game kind of tests your sense of direction and memory to find your own way through the level and discover the incriminating secrets and 'evidence' hidden in the mansion. Or you could just jot down a few notes, or draw a map for yourself, if you want! There's endless limits for how you can accomplish each mission. Plus, it doesn't hold your hand at all, or guide you through the entire area with a completely filled out map and objective markers all over the place.
You can just tell that LGS made this game with love.

Another thing that made this game unique was the setting and premise of it. You have the middle ages, sprinkled with a bit of steampunk in there with some fantasy and magical elements thrown in the mix! It further adds to the charm of the Thief universe and is beyond awesome.

There's not much I have to criticize Thief: Gold for, but if I had to, the only 2 "criticisms" I'd give this game would be that first of all, the graphics are dated. But how can you fault the game for this? It's a product of it's time. Plus, there are so many graphical mods and patches out there that give T:G a heavy facelift, regardless! It goes to show how much the fans truely love and care about the Thief series.
The other criticism I'd give it is that it sometimes focuses on action more than actual stealth (due to huge changes late in development), but luckily, they're VERY few and far between. And thank god for that, as well.

That's all I really have to say for it. I mean, they certainly don't make games like this anymore. Just... play it, already. I'm sure you'll love this game (And Garrett, too. His witticisms crack me up, everytime <3)
