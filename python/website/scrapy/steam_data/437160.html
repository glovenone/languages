About This Game

„The Lion's Song: Episode 1 – Silence" tells the story of Wilma, a talented young musician student. Wilma’s talent was discovered by Arthur Caban, a wealthy university professor, who invited her to Vienna. Arthur is profoundly convinced Wilma is going to play a major part in the future of modern music. Since Wilma is fighting writer’s block trying to finish her career defining composition, Arthur sends her to a solitary alpine cabin to recover her inspiration and muse.

Will Wilma be able to finish her breakthrough composition in this small cabin, surrounded by high mountains and stormy weather? In her creative crisis Wilma suddenly has an unexpected encounter.

The choices players make in this episode will have a direct impact on the storylines of all episodes and ultimately determine if the protagonists find the success they are working for.

When I first came across the Lion’s Song it was purely at random, but when my gaze fell upon some of the screenshots from the game, I quickly added it onto my wishlist and waited patiently for its release, until today.

The Lion’s Song, produced by the Austrian studio Mi'pu'mi Games and whose moto is “Great games don’t have to be big” (and I couldn’t agree more), is an episodic, point & click series that takes place during the early 20th century. In the first episode of The Lion’s Song we follow Wilma, an up and coming violinist who is under a lot of pressure to finish a piece that she has been struggling to complete. When her professor, Arthur Caban offers to send her to his cabin up in the Alps where she could potentially find inspiration, Wilma accepts and arrives to find herself surrounded by her fears, insecurities and pressure from both her peers and herself.
The Lion’s Song first episode, titled Silence, is a wonderful piece of art about the pressure we allow others to put on us, the pressure we put on ourselves, our fears and how they can be interpreted of visualised, whether through dreams or the world that surrounds us, but it also is about patience and overcoming obstacles, big or small. In the first episode, you will be required to pay attention to Wilma’s pleas and fears, and help her find inspirations in the things that surrounds her and the sounds that will sometimes scare her, or inspire her, in order to complete her piece.

The game allows you to learn various type of things about Wilma or the people in her life, different games will offer different types of information, and through some events, you can shape Wilma’s will, fears, and even the ending of the episode.

Although the music is an important part of the game, a lot of it resides in the in-between, when you are searching inside of the cabin for something to spark Wilma’s attention and inspiration. Rain tapping against the window or a clock ticking, there is a surprising amount of objects for you to interact with, and the sounds, text and sometimes music that accompanies it are absolutely wonderful.

I am extremely excited to see what Mi'pu'mi Games has in store for us, and to meet the other character that will be the protagonists of the next episodes.

10/10 – I was pleasantly surprised to see that episode 1 was being given out for free, as I was more than ready to pay for it, but having experienced it now, I will most certainly be purchasing the season pass and wait for more wonderful storytelling.

“Great games don’t have to be big” Absolutely.

I remember first hearing about Lion’s Song a few years back on RPS and I played the really early demo of Episode 1. It was neat enough, but I forgot about it until I saw its Steam page on the upcoming game list. Seeing is that the first episode was free I had no excuse not to play it.

Lion’s Song Episode 1 takes place in Vienna during the turn of the 20th century. You play as Wilma, a young woman that aspires to be a great violinist. She has the skills but she has recently fallen into a block. Her teacher, Arthur, (who she secretly has feelings for) sees it as pressure from the bustling city around her and offers Wilma to stay in his isolated cabin in the Alps by herself. Wilma agrees but at the cabin she learns that Arthur has planned a big concert with her in it and she has only a week to finish her new piece. As she begins to fret over this development she receives a phone call from a friendly Bohemian innkeeper named Leos who wanted to try out this newfangled phone he just got installed and is awed that he ended up calling someone hundreds of miles away.

At this point it is up to the player to decide what to do. And that’s Lion’s Song’s gameplay, it’s a very streamlined point and click game where it focuses on how you have Wilma deal with her situation and her relationships with other people. Will you be determined to finish the composition, putting it above all else? Or will you take the time to just talk to Leos? Stuff like that. The game has really good atmosphere, the aesthetic is that of a sepia-toned photograph and it has a real nice soundtrack. It all really adds to the feeling of being alone in woods on a stormy night. The writing is good stuff too, I actually didn’t get to talk a lot to Leos out of my own choice and because of that the game became more of Wilma dealing with some soul searching on her own.

The only real major flaw I can say is that you can’t have multiple save files so I’d have to lose my progress if I wanted to replay and take a different path like spending more time talking to Leos but I don’t want to override my original choices.

Overall Episode 1 is a real solid start and I’ll probably buy the whole season pass. Also you got no excuse to not try it, it’s free.

I'm pleasantly surprised by the simplicity that is The Lion's Song. As you can see by my time, as well as other reviewers'', the game is not exceptionally long. Despite that, the brief hour you spend with the game will be delightful. The Lion's Song boasts an impressive pixelated artstyle, a thoughtful score, and a nuanced story.

However, the game's enjoyment will be dependent on the player. If you're not the type to really get invested in a character you may not enjoy the first playthrough that much. Gameplay wise it's not very intricate, which might also put some people off. Puzzles are almost non existant, save a few with very simple solutions. The story can be a bit predictable as well.

That being said, I do recommend the first episode for anyone who thinks they'd be remotely interested in it. It's free and truly worth taking slowly and reading all the dialogue. There's a good chance I'll be replaying this game to get achievements and also see if the song at the end changes based on choices.
