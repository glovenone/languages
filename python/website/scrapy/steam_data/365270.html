About This Game

Shadow Hunter is a casual deductive puzzle game with RPG elements.



As a Shadow Hunter, you must use your senses to detect dangers and artifacts beneath the endless darkness that spreads throughout the land. Anti-light, the dimension and dimensional darkness from beyond the void gate, threatens to swallow us all. You must quest to find the Shadow Gate Fragments, to destroy the Anti-light menace.

  • 16 areas to explore.
  • Deductive mini-games.
  • Random events.
  • Local high-score list.
  • Great horror atmosphere.
  • Turn-based.

System Requirements

    Minimum:
    • OS: Windows XP/7/8
    • Processor: 2.0 Ghz+
    • Memory: 2 GB RAM
    • Graphics: 2 GB Video RAM
    • Storage: 100 MB available space
    • Additional Notes: Ensure you have all the latest drivers

Shadow Hunter is primarily Minesweeper, if you played a minesweeper who had to physically navigate the board to clear spaces. And there were two types of mines, each with their own disarming item. And walls restricted your movement. And there were lockpicking puzzles and memory-based combat. And a food counter that decreased with movement.

Shadow Hunter's primary flaw is that boards repeatedly put you into situations where your next move is random chance. Walls restrict your movement, with rooms entered from corridors that are a single space wide. If one of those spaces is safe, then you have to guess to progress. You can start, or be teleported into similar situations. You can clear shadows and still end up in such situations.

While mistakes do not mean instant failure, they do drain resources. Stepping into a trap drains health, limiting how often you can take such chances. Using the wrong disarming item costs you that item, which requires camping to restock. Card spaces have no item that disarms them, but also deal no direct damage, instead triggering some effect.

You are rewarded with bonuses for disarming multiple spaces without taking damage. This works okay. You are rewarded with bonus food for clearing multiple spaces within a single move. This does not work okay. When you move multiple spaces in a turn, you can only move in a straight line. The board layout restricts how many places even have the potential to let you move enough to trigger the food bonus. When you do attempt such a move, you are almost guaranteed to be moving into spaces for which you hold no information, and thus it is random chance whether or not you actually have a clear path or will instead hit a trap (which will reset the counter.)

There is a lockpicking minigame for opening locked doors. You have five chances to find the correct order of the five pins. Failure only means that you gain no bonus gold, while the fewer the tries required means the greater the bonus you receive. While logic and memory can greatly increase your odds of clearing this puzzle, it is still random chance as to how fast you will clear it, and thus how large your reward will be.

Combat (as combat can be triggered by stepping into card spaces, or seemingly just by chance when moving) is a memory game. At the start of combat you are shown, in order, the attacks that the opponent will make. You have to perform the same attacks in the same order, while the enemy sequence is removed from view after you start. Feedback for this combat is poor.

In theory, minesweeper where you move through the board sounds interesting. In practice, it just doesn't work that well. Setting it in a fantasy world gives it some meaning, but seems to have also lead to the game implementing several awkward or ill-fitting mechanics together.

The game is a free game, so it costs nothing but time to try. But mechanically, it just doesn't hold up.
