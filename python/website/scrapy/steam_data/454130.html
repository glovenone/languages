About This Game

Nil-Ninjahtic: Ronin is a pseudo 8-bit action platformer. While it integrates gameplay mechanics of the previous titles in the series, they are combined in a manner which differentiates itself from its predecessors.

Rather than playing as a ninja-like protagonist, the main character shares more characteristics with that of a samurai. Usage of the environment is still the focus of the platforming and puzzle elements remain intact, although progression through each level is straightforward without the open-ended nature present in the previous titles.

Features:
  • Several screens providing various platforming challenges and puzzles
  • Duel with samurai and fight varying enemy types
  • Challenging difficulty, frequent checkpoints, and unlimited continues
  • 8-bit graphics and soundtrack
  • Supports Xbox 360 Controller (recommended)

A rōnin was a title given to a samurai who, upon the death of their master decided to go rogue instead of committing seppuku during the feudal period of Japan. The other samurais would banish and exclude a rōnin, isolating them from their groups. In the modern world, the word can be used in comparison to a person who has trouble fitting into certain social groups; always wandering around, searching for a place to be at ease.

Overview

Nil-Ninjahstic: Ronin combines the elements of wandering samurai into a platformer. Essentially a derivative of super meat boy without all the crazy speeding and sliding, you play as an outcast of society, where you’re always picked to do the dirty tasks that no one else wants to do. As such in this game, you are put through a ‘test’ where everything and everyone tries to kill you. In order to pass this initiation, players will need to run, jump and slash through 5 chapters of increasingly difficult levels of platforms and their respective level bosses.

The Good

Being the third game in the series of Nil-Ninjahtic, the most well done part of the game is the control scheme in relation to character movement. Blaze Epic has mastered the basic controls for the game throughout 7 games and the result is a smooth and polished feel. The character does not move to fast and is extremely responsive in terms of fluidity. Air strafing is done extremely well and allows for predictable pathing and enables speed runs.

The alternative to running past everything would be to slow chip your way through a level. Blaze Epic has provided the tools necessary to accommodate for this playstyle by incorporating a sword slash function and block. Block stops most projectiles and melee attacks, while allowing you to strafe in a direction with no real penalty except moving slower and being unable to turn. However, it comes at the cost of being not able to counterattack, and limits your movement by restricting the use of block while in midair.

Nil-Ninjahtic Ronin might not have the best music around, but it serves its purpose extremely well. Since there is no auditory feedback from enemies in the game, Nil-Ninjahtic Ronin can afford to play the role of metronome and pace the game for the player. Entering different areas of the game whether it be in the streets of Japan, or in the locker room of a gym, will change the music to suit the appropriate setting, a feature not usually found in smaller platformer games.


The Bad

Nil-Ninjahtic: Ronin is a five chapter game with many levels in between. However, the most obvious flaw is that it only saves at the end of a chapter. In order to save your progress, one must completely the whole chapter completely. Since this game’s skill requirements are quite high, players may find it difficult to find the time or even the abilities to make it to the next chapters. Implementing checkpoint saves between each sub-chapter would allow for greater re-playability and would make the game feel more complete in a sense.

Though the game’s difficulty increases at a reasonable rate, the game gets pretty frustrating in chapter five where the game slowly becomes more of a chore. I cannot fathom the amount of times I was so close to completing a level only to accidentally run back into the previous screen, resetting all the progress on the current level, or running through the most annoying chainsaw run only to be slashed down by an edge-guarding samurai on the last platform.

Another huge flaw is the fact that the chapters are also too long, with no dialogue in between the smaller sub-chapters. The small bits of monologue between the chapters feel out of place and only serve to string the game together. The writing is not that interesting to begin with and by the time you finish a chapter, you will have difficulty remembering what the story was talking about already.

Nil-Ninjahtic: Ronin not only test your skills, but also your patience as seen with its pixel perfect platforming. Jumping horizontally into a wall will result in your character bouncing off, but jumping vertically into the ceiling will allow your character’s head to clip through it. I personally found this out after spending a good 15 minutes trying avoid spikes in a tunnel, thinking I would bounce off the ceiling and die to the buzz saw below.


Overall Score

I personally rate this game 7/10 for the really fun gameplay. Like all good things come in moderation, this game is best enjoyed in short bursts of 1-2 hours, just enough to completely one chapter each time. This works to keep the game from getting too repetitive, too quickly. Had the game included checkpoints between the sub-levels and had more consistent art, it would have been a solid 8/10.

Pretty standard action platformer. You play as a ninja with a katana moving from one screen to the next while clearing out enemies and dodging spikes and sawblades (so many sawblades)

There's no defined plot in the game, other than a text monologue that appears between stages, which I'm still unsure as to what relevance that had to the game itself.

Overall, it's $2, and it's good for 2 or so hours, so I can't complain.
