Early Access Game

Get instant access and start playing; get involved with this game as it develops.

Note: This Early Access game is not complete and may or may not change further. If you are not excited to play this game in its current state, then you should wait to see if the game progresses further in development. Learn more

What the developers have to say:

Why Early Access?

“I had to think hard about whether or not to release on early access. I had asked some of my fans and they wanted to see the game on greenlight as soon as possible, and explained to me that if I was to release on EA I would be able to shape the game with the community. Overall, I believe that publishing ThreeDays on early access will make the game bloom and create an exciting and unique fan base for all of us to enjoy and interact, as well as allowing me to develop the game with new feedback from an extensive population of gamers.”

Approximately how long will this game be in Early Access?

“The game is approximately 85% done and is 100% playable. All I want to do now is add some extra features and fix major and minor bugs as the game gets ever so closer to that 100% mark. I predict, with this in mind, that it should be completed between six months to a year, however with my current situation and set up I would say that it will be finished nearer to a year.”

How is the full version planned to differ from the Early Access version?

“By adding extra features and quirks to the game I will enhance the overall atmosphere, by adding more ingame content. You can follow exactly what I am doing and planning on my trello list for the game here: https://trello.com/b/dRljW40c/three-days-game
All additions, fixes and bug reports are going to be kept here along with all updates and progress.”

What is the current state of the Early Access version?

“At its current state, the game is playable, completable and enjoyable. As it is 85% done most content is in the game, nowadays it's really just balancing and adding extra quirks whilst listening to community suggestions as I go along.”

Will the game be priced differently during and after Early Access?

“No, I am not going to change the price of the game after release, it will stay at your regional price depending on where you live. I feel this is sensible, because it is a reasonable price in its current state and increasing the price later on would be a bit harsh on new players who want to get the game.”

How are you planning on involving the Community in your development process?

“I am really looking forward to involving the community in all aspects of the development cycle, such as with criticism, suggestions and just general input. Personally, all criticism is equivalent to gold dust and should be taken on board no matter how harsh. I believe that the Steam community is a key asset to the success of any game and I cannot wait to hear what the community has to advise on for ThreeDays.”

About This Game



Timed gameplay-


Not often attempted, with modern survival games, is the sense of seriousness to escape in a period of time. Three Days gives a new perspective on urgency by adding a 72 hour - three days - time limit allowing the player to get involved in gameplay with an incentive to escape. We have fine-tuned ThreeDays to suit most players without it being impossible,and for the more casual players we have created an endless mode which has no time limit allowing a less intense experience.

Unique creation system-


Three Days has its own unique crafting system: simply take a movable resource and drag it onto another resource to see what you can create. No inventory, no fuss.

Procedurally generated islands-


The game includes islands that are randomly generated every time you play allowing diverse gameplay every time itâs played. Not only this, but each island has its own original name and generation and all resources that are generated on the island will be placed elsewhere.

Stats-system-


Three Days contains a dynamic ever changing stats system, where the player is in constant need of attention to survive which is achievable by looking after the players hunger hydration and temperature. The system is ever changing is and the player is always in a new procedurally generated island, meaning different amounts of resources and different weather effects to survive with.

Auto-mine-


The automine mechanic was influenced by playing other games with different mechanics requiring the player to hit or hold a button to break and collect resources. Personally, I disliked this system and wondered how I could do it differently and came up with the automine feature. The player is simply brought over to the resource desired to break and it will automatically start to break.

Upgrade/levelling system-


ThreeDays also allows you to collect experience from breaking primary resources such as trees and boulders, this allows level ups and PPs (Perk Points) which can be spent on 15 different upgrades for the character, such as killing and catching fish without a rod to slowing the timer down. The level system will also directly affect the playerâs fate at the end of the game via 4 cutscenes.



Personally, I enjoy a game with a bit of context and I really wanted to bring this to ThreeDays. Its unique storyline was managed by allowing the player to play and learn, for instance throughout your time in game you will discover random notes around the shoreline, washed in by the tide, which explain the backstory and what really happened.

The crafting system is by far the biggest downfall of this game that could be great, but it's still in Early Access so nothing is set in stone and things can change, even if it claims it's around 85% complete. Too many times I've tried to stab things with my spear and accidentally craft SPIKES, ultimately dooming me.

If you're unsure how to make fire before nightfall, you likely won't survive past the first day, and that alone is a huge drawback to the game when you die numerous times and make zero progress. I'd suggest a brief in-game tutorial that walks you through how to survive the first night and essential crafting recipes. Also there's very limited time to work with and try crafting recipes out in that first night, you get about enough time to down a tree or two and rock, and then you get swarmed by enemies and start freezing to death. There's a fine line between a challenge and frustratingly difficult that punishes the player and doesn't even give them a chance to enjoy the game and limits their progress.

In a sea of survival simulators this game needs to stand above the rest, and rather it's just getting lost. I am looking forward to further development however and I hope the final product is a little more polished and user-friendly where the poor accidental crafting mechanics and difficult for the sake of being difficult doesn't deter players.

Note as I write this the game is in Early Access; and not complete.

At it's core Three Days is a survival horror mashed with a roguelike and dressed in pixel art. If that doesn't sound like fun, move along now because even with polish that's the direction the game is going.

For those who think that actually sounds like it has potential: I believe that too. The game plays like a throwback to when games were brutal and unforgiving. You have no idea what to do or how to get off your island and the chickens will be ripping you apart a few minutes after starting the game on the first several attempts to play.

If you manage to survive your first day you will be smashing rocks and chopping trees in an effort to build a boat and get off the island full of nightmare chickens. The main story mode is really fast; as the title implies you have only 3 days to live and they go by rather quickly and brutal. Thankfully there is also an endless mode you can switch to and give yourself as many days as you need to build the boat.

Overall it's an interesting step towards a quick and brutal survival roguelike, but it does have a ways to go before I would call it complete. I won't go into detail on what's missing or in need of fixing because the bottom line is that this game is Early Access and therefore still under development.

For what it is I can give it a recommendation, but if you can't stomach Early Access (and all that that entails) or if the pixel art is not your thing, this game isn't for you (yet).

Three Days is a survival/crafting game that is beyond â¥â¥â¥â¥ing merciless but feeds you just enough hope each time it breaks its foot off in your backside to keep your quest to build a ship and escape a terrifying isle of demonic were-chickens alive.

The game's insane difficulty comes from the sheer speed at which it moves; you have, as the title helpfully reminds you, three days in which to construct your vessel from the various raw materials lying around on the island before a nuclear bomb wipes you and your black paradise off the map. This translates into roughly fifteen minutes in real-time and at least half of those will have you cowering from hordes of SCPs behind makeshift barricades every time the night swoops in. On top of the sheer stress of having to put together an entire boat in a quarter of an hour, you'll also have to manage your hunger, thirst and heat levels, having to construct campfires to cook raw meat and keep you warm at night and a means to purify sea water so you don't get sick.

It is madness. Vicious, panic-inducing madness that will take many attempts to suss out the various combinations of items needed just to stay alive. Thankfully, there is an Endless Mode that removes the time limit and allows you to get somewhat comfortable with the entire experience before you throw yourself into the full game.

There is no joy like blundering over the combination to construct your first lump of ship sideboard, just as there is no despair quite like looking at Three Days' insane spider-web of a crafting tree for the first time and realising that you are going to die in this cruel place.

I will start by stating that I got this game for free for the sole purpose of testing and reviewing it. This game is not the normal type of game I review, and with that being said I recruited my girlfriend's help in this one. TL;DR is in the last section of review.

The Game:
The game itself was built in GameMaker, with a supposed 500 hours invested into the design. The music and all the art is original which is a solid change from some of the RPG Maker games I've seen. The fast-paced survival in Three Days works really well, against what I had originally anticipated actually. There is a real urgency in collecting resources but I did feel a bit thrown into the mix. The crafting in this game is also taken from a unique perspective. Think of the world as your inventory, you just simply drag and drop items on top of each other to create things. While a lot of the game is unfinished, I see a lot of potential in this, and believe it'll continue to be developed.

The Developer:
My hopes are very high when I see a developer actually talking with his players. Lueshie and I worked together for a bit to pin down some bugs, and I for one want to see more of that. He took all of what I said constructively and was very calm and polite throughout. Which I feel is quite absent in most developers. He's the kind of person you can see actually cares. As I spammed him with bugs and stuff I felt was obvious that was overlooked, he patiently updated his bug tracker and I watched as things I found were added to the list.

The Girlfriend:
One of the best things about this game so far is the crafting page. I looked at it after I made a spear by accident. I hate how fast the game goes, but it is understandable at this point in the games development. Killing the chickens was also very enjoyable after I learned how to kill them. Figuring out how to eat was a pain as well; I thought you could just drag and drop the food onto the character rather than click multiple times. Trying to use the spear to fish was a bit of a hassle. It would be awesome in the future to be able to customize the character as well.

Conclusion (TL;DR)
The game at it's core has an honest new take on an old idea. It's fun and refreshing to see this put into effect. This game was released today, so bugs on a new game are expected however and to be perfectly honest, some of the bugs are kinda fun to mess with. Overall a good experience, and funny to watch my girlfriend triumph over chickens.

The crafting system is by far the biggest downfall of this game that could be great, but it's still in Early Access so nothing is set in stone and things can change, even if it claims it's around 85% complete. Too many times I've tried to stab things with my spear and accidentally craft SPIKES, ultimately dooming me.

If you're unsure how to make fire before nightfall, you likely won't survive past the first day, and that alone is a huge drawback to the game when you die numerous times and make zero progress. I'd suggest a brief in-game tutorial that walks you through how to survive the first night and essential crafting recipes. Also there's very limited time to work with and try crafting recipes out in that first night, you get about enough time to down a tree or two and rock, and then you get swarmed by enemies and start freezing to death. There's a fine line between a challenge and frustratingly difficult that punishes the player and doesn't even give them a chance to enjoy the game and limits their progress.

In a sea of survival simulators this game needs to stand above the rest, and rather it's just getting lost. I am looking forward to further development however and I hope the final product is a little more polished and user-friendly where the poor accidental crafting mechanics and difficult for the sake of being difficult doesn't deter players.
