About This Game

Journey on an epic quest with Lethal and his elite party of warriors through a huge fantasy land. Play as Lethal and his companions in exciting turn based battles against a variety of fantasy creatures.

  • 8 playable characters, all with unique abilities, armor and weapons.
  • Turn-based combat featuring a diverse and engaging battle system.
  • A huge world with 13 areas to explore, all with unique enemies and quests.
  • Craft powerful armor and weapons from materials gathered from intense boss monster encounters throughout the kingdom.
  • Over 50 story quests to complete.
  • An arena that lets you put your abilities to the test, where you face off against more difficult versions of past encounters.
  • Loads of pets to find that have special abilities to help you face your enemies in battle.

    More info at http://lethalrpgwar.com

It seems that lately I've become more of a casual JRPG* fan than I care to admit. Just to give an example: While my The Legend of Heroes: Trails in the Sky installation gathers cobwebs and silently weeps, I've played through three Compile Heart games. Now when I use the word "casual", I don't mean it in the sense of "Those filthy casuals are killing my hardcore games!" A casual game is something I play when I want to relax. Lethal RPG: War isn't much to look at, but it's a perfectly safe product for casual consumption.

The undead menace must be stopped! The main character is named Lethal and...oh...really? Lethal?! The only way this clown could be any edgier, is if he had spikey hair and an oversized sword. *ahem* Anyway, you're tasked with leading Lethal and his party on a world-saving adventure. On your journey you'll complete several quests, fight vicious monsters, upgrade gear, and challenge dangerous bosses. The storyline is serviceable, and the few instances of personality in the cast are welcome. However, this game can be beaten in less than 10 hours, so don't expect much in the way of extensive character arcs. The guy who tells dumb jokes? He's staying that way. Don't expect him to stop joking and then tell everyone about his troubled past as a bandit.

Part of what makes Lethal RPG a casual JRPG experience is the lack of what we're accustomed to. There is an overworld, but there aren't any random encounters. There are several fixed fights all over the map, and you can even choose to fight whenever you want. Grinding isn't particularly necessary, because you get more than enough experience and materials, from fixed-fights and solving quests. The more startling omission is the complete lack of dungeons. There are numerous locations in this game, they all have dungeonesque names, but there's nothing to explore. What usually happens is that you'll fight a battle or two, and then move on. Aside from the arena, there aren't any situations where you have to carefully manage your resources. Oddly enough, I consider this a positive element. This game is very stream-lined, which makes the idea of dungeons, sound like needless padding.

The battle-system in this game is fairly solid. There are a remarkable number of characters to use, and they're mostly well-balanced. Much of my strategy for the boss-fights revolves around status effects. Yep, this is one of those rare JRPGs, where debilitating effects actually work. Bosses afflicted by poison, burns, and bleeding can lose large chunks of HP every round. You might find it more effective to use tomes to exploit weaknesses. This can include casting spells the enemy is weak against, as well as enchanting weapons with particular elements. Every party member has their own set of skills, and a touch of variety is always nice. Unfortunately, characters - depending on when they're introduced - can fall behind in experience. On the other hand, a couple levels usually won't make a difference. I also appreciate that even the regular encounters can be dangerous. Losing a party-member during a fight with a "trash-mob" is not uncommon.

Upon completion of Lethal RPG: War, you're allowed to access New Game+ mode. As per genre standards, this mode lets you play through the story again while carrying over all levels, characters, and equipment upgrades. This also raises the monster's experience level, because it'd be too easy otherwise. It's a nice gesture, but it's like prestiging in a Multiplayer FPS. All you really have is an excuse to jump back on the hamster wheel, and dirty newspapers don't even get replaced. You're liable to use the same strategies for every encounter. Sure, you'll have a few more options to play around with, but all that's really changed, is that the numbers are much higher.

Another complaint that I have is the music. There really aren't enough tracks to go around. Actually, I guess I could consider this a positive. Since the soundtrack isn't all that great, it's a perfect excuse to catch up on other music or a few podcasts. Also the writing is a little too dry. Some of the NPCs will mention dramatic events, but they will be stated in such a matter-of-fact manner that your only response is "Oh okay". I would have preferred a little drama, give these traumatic moments time to sink in, instead things just tend to happen.

Awhile back I reviewed another JRPG by the name of Crystal Story II. I stated that it had a certain nebulous charm. I think the same can be said for Lethal RPG: War. The protagonist is a dork, the writing is bland, and there isn't really much to do aside from fight monsters, upgrade equipment, and view story scenes. However, everything in this game is endearing in its own twisted way. It's a good palette cleanser when other games get so wrapped up in themselves, you forget which way is up. Maybe this also says something about my tastes. When it comes to reviewing 2D shooters and beatemups, I can be very strict. With JRPGs, even if they don't do much to stand out, I tend to be pretty lenient. Perhaps there is a certain joy exclusive to the genre. Adventuring, obtaining treasure, or raising stats, just feels good to me. Whatever the case, I think this game is okay.

*While not made in Japan, Lethal RPG does have the attributes of a common JRPG.

Lethal RPG: War may well be quite competitive as compared to free flash games in terms of its story, graphics, and gameplay, but there's just not enough there to make this a game I would recommend.

First up, the story is stupid, and not even in a "so bad it's good" way, most of the time. It takes itself quite seriously, despite having a main character named Lethal, but the plot is incredibly generic "save the kingdom from the creepy crawlies," so there's very little there to really either catch a player's interest or amuse a player who's playing it for laughs.

The main issue is the gameplay, though. The game is very, very grindy insofar as you will be repeating more or less similar strategies dozens of times, given that even generic enemies are tough enough that essentially all fights will take 4+ rounds to resolve, even those you're sure you'll win from the start. (Actually, as a sidenote, generic enemies encountered in random battles seemed stronger than generic enemies encountered in story battles. This is probably an artifact of the level scaling, but it still contributed to the grindy feeling.) This is compounded by a system where enemies scale to Lethal's level (to a minimum and maximum based on the map area you're in) but your characters don't gain full XP unless they're in the active party, meaning that not picking two companions - and on a first run, almost certainly the two you start with - and sticking with them through the entire game is a recipe for a needlessly difficult time.

As upsides, the graphics are actually pretty well done and the actual combat mechanics are fairly interesting for important fights - each character has a basic attack, a defensive move, up to five special attacks unlocked as they level up, and a wide selection of spells and consumable items acquired during the course of the game. Just... there's not much other than those boss fights, which can vary wildly in difficulty, to really act as a draw for this RPG.

So yeah, no. Lethal RPG: War's solidly mediocre in a way that makes it not a game I'd recommend to anyone in particular.
