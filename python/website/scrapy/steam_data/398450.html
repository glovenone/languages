About This Game

Absent is a story which follows the character of young-adult and college student, Murray Schull. He and his two best friends, Eve and Steve, attend a college in which students are disappearing daily. It’s up to them to find out how this is happening, why this is happening, protect their other friends, and to put a stop to it once and for all.

This series of events began when Crystal, a three month girlfriend of Murray’s friend, Steve, disappeared on her way home from college via mysterious means. Over the course of the investigation, however, Murray realizes that the abduction of Steve’s girlfriend was not from anything of this earth. Murray is haunted by visions of the past and future, confronted with puzzles, with friends turning against each other, and all for one reason. The Reaper.


Special Features

-A point/click, easy to use interface.
-Approximately twenty NPC's to interact with.
-Around forty playable rooms in the whole game.
-A completely original story with original art and characters created by James Fenton.
-A game so varied, I flipped a three sided coin to choose it's genre!
-Full Voice Acting, including the talents of Esme Brand, James Fenton, Shannon Hayes, Rebecca Hanssen and Matt Haynes.
-Full MP3 Soundtrack composed by Adam Haynes.
-It's completely free!

This game is a wonderful game. However, some don't (which is fine), but here's some reasons why some complain:
-Low resolution that can't be changed.
-Hard puzzles.
-Not a lot of horror (to a hardcore horror-fan's expectation, which is not me. So I've honestly no complaints here).
-No tutorial and no hand holding.

Let me just start with why this is in the horror category. The game isn't honestly heavy in horror, nor based around it. More or less it only has aspects of horror while it tells a story. I personally don't entirely like horror games, but this one had the right amount of horror where I could invest in the story without being too freaked out to continue. (I know, I'm a bit of a baby and can't handle horror well. But that's also okay!)

The no tutorial thing and no hand holding go hand to hand in it's hard puzzles. The only clues you will get is following along with the dialogue that drop subtle hints in what you're supposed to do, half the time. Other times there's just things in the environment you have to figure out on your own. This, personally, excited me. I love puzzles. If you do, too, I will recommend this game.

The low resolution thing is a bit annoying, to be honest. However I paid it no mind, because I'm the type to mostly overlook how a game looks versus to how it plays. The art is great, the animations are mediocre, but overall ... it's fun, and I had a fun time solving the puzzles.

You should really only go off this recommendation if you like puzzles in point and click adventure games. Though overall I would recommend you download this game anyways just to give it a try. 8.5/10

Absent is a free-to-play adventure game, where you're playing a student investigating on the disappearance of students, among them, his girlfriend. However, the truth is more terryifing that you can imagine.

Indeed, the plot could have been easy to understand. Disappearing people, paranormal entity, these themes, if well handled, can result in a good moment. However, here, after a while, it was so complex to understand that I lost interest in the story.

Also, as it's a point-and-click game with the good old mechanics of the cursor cycling through the actions, you may have an hard time by moments. I remember that for one achievement, you had to click on the door of the teacher's room. You think that the discussion will let you an option to show something. In reality, you have to use the item first, else, well, no cheevos for you. Also, don't forget to speak with everyone so that you can learn more about the school, the students, the town or to simply find the solution to go forwards.

Absent has also correct graphics: it has a nice retro touch. You're not staying in the school during all the game, but you visit the town, forests and even another world. The soundtrack is correct too.

For a free game, I must say that it's doing great. The game isn't short at all, can provide a good challenge, though sometimes, you wish that the clues were more obvious and the achievements aren't too hard. However, keep in mind that maybe, the game isn't for you if you're not into adventure gaming. It's not the best way to get to know the genre, especially with these mechanics, for that, you have better series like Blackwell or A Golden Wake or even other free gems that aren't on Steam.

Anyway, it will not hurt you to install it.

I played through the game in about 4 hours, yesterday, I think?

I will try and avoid spoilers for this.

So, anyway, I liked the story, even if I didn't quite understand it around the end (though that might be a problem with me and not the storytelling). I did feel Murray wasn't particularly interesting as a protagonist, but he did have some funny lines, and thought that the other playable character later could have had more time devoted to her.

I did feel the tone needed work, though. I felt it was rather inconsistent, like making fun of traditional Point & Click Adventure game tropes and other conventions (like a dialogue option has Murray and Mrs. Fish commenting on the latter's Leitmotif), but had a rather serious and creepy plot. It just kind of felt unbalanced, particularly when the moments of the former leaked into the latter (like Murray asking a Reaper why they didn't phase through the door, and the Reaper gives an adequate answer, and Murray says "Plot hole averted!"). I don't mind what you were trying, but it could have been done better.

As for the artstyle, it grew on me, but I did think it could have been better (like Crystal and Murray have very silly walks). There were definitely some proportion and scale issues I can recall, like arms not the right length. I definitely hope that the artstyle gets a slight overhaul for the sequel.

I thought the gameplay was solid, though at least one or two puzzles felt a bit obtuse. It's pretty standard Point & Click Adventure game fare (take that how you will).

I'd recommend it, it's a bit unpolished in a number of places, but it's kind of an interesting experience.

Absent is a largely one-man adventure game project by James Fenton. It's also completely free.

Some obtuse triggers aside that necessitate the average adventure game method of inventory and dialogue spam, Absent doesn't feature any annoying puzzle, they're for the most part straightforward and not unpleasant.

These are all the positive things I have to say about Absent. It's free and not annoying. When it comes to largely one-man indie projects, the compulsion is there to give it more leniency than a game with a larger team, but having been released on Steam it is directly next to the greats of the genre in shelf space and suffers from no dearth of competition.

Absent is an incredibly uninteresting game. There's not much to say about it that isn't a litany of criticisms.

The gameplay isn't even worth mentioning. Made with Adventure Game studio, you can expect nothing but item combinations and rubbing different items on different surfaces. None of the puzzles are noteworthy or interesting.

The art, it has a charming style to it, in a Bob's Burgers kind of way. Characters compete to have their chins recede into their necks and everybody is a winner. It's also incredibly amateur. Character's proportions will nightmarishly shift as they turn, heads warping shape and limbs cracking. Aliased binary pixel art melds anti-aliased smudging like nails on a chalkboard.

Clear inexperience with animation shows as vertical walk cycles turn legs into indecisive jello, and horizontal walk cycles turn into chin high marches. The composition is raggedy, several scenes placing objects in awkward positions, characters general stand with the stiffness of boards (the Reapers in particular may as well be t-posing) and the colour palette looks right out of MS Paint's default selection.

"Well, that's another plot-hole averted"

Story is there, but never in danger of being interesting or engrossing. The amount of contrivances, nervously laughing lampshade attempts and incredibly ill-paced exposition immediately nips that in the bud. It feels incredibly "safe", and suffers immensely as a result, terrified of being misinterpreted.

None of the characters are remotely interesting, least of all the Reapers, the central driving force of the game. The murder mystery is resolved "safely", the Reapers are resolved "safely", the entire games ends in a scene so neutered that it looks straight out of a family-friendly cartoon.

If I didn't know any better, I'd say the creator was acutely aware that their parents were going to play the game.

As a side note, the voice-acting wasn't horrible, but delivery was hampered by an awkward script and the microphone quality differences between actors were so palpable I could taste the tinniness.

For an enthusiastic amateur attempt at a videogame, Absent is an admirable attempt. A sentence that would not be said if it were being offered for money. While I'm sure it did amazingly well on enthusiast forums, Absent does not offer anything of value to a consumer-driven market - not to adventure game fans, not to horror/thriller fans, not even to freeware fans.

While it does not ask for your money, Absent isn't particularly worth your time. I don't recommend this title.
