About This Game

Ever wanted to be a hacker? Hack your way into the heart of a mysterious organization to uncover their secrets. Hack RUN® is a game which uses 'old school' command prompts (like DOS or UNIX) that simulate a real operating system. Learn the commands of each system to help you navigate through your adventure.

As you hack into their systems, you will learn about the organization and the people who work there. Read files and emails to find clues and riddles which will give you access to more accounts. Data found on their systems will help you learn about the motives of the organization, and how dark their plans can get. You may also discover dirty little secrets about the employees as well.

Hacking into new accounts or discovering key information will increase your skill level. As you achieve your goals, you will move closer and closer to the final achievement; over 50 levels deep!

It's time for a Hack RUN!

Posted: 28 May
Hack RUN is an old school text style adventure game, under the premise of you being a master hacker. Unfortunately, hacking is not something you do in this game, but rather, you are a script kiddie extraordinaire. Find out what happens the deeper you go and all will be well?

The game is run off the windows console, which I didn't realize at first. That wasn't such a big deal, until the game started running my programs on my machine. It has the ability to open notepad, your default browser and open a url, and I'm not sure what else. I'm not entirely sure how I felt about this. Other applications can do the same, but under the premise of the game, it felt odd. Moving right along, the game consists of you tracing down information and using it to proceed to the darker and darker secrets.

Sounds: There is one sound that I know of. I don't know how to even rate it.

Graphics: It's all text, except for a few external websites that the executable relies on for you to finish the game.

Controls: What controls? It's all about typing.

Game Design: Well the game is extremely linear and achievements unlock as you pass each 'stage' or 'level' though the game. It actually calls them levels. I found that odd. It sort of breaks up the text adventure style concept and breaks the 'narrative' though I'm not sure I can call it that. You're not really hacking in this game, or even doing minor coding as in Hack n' Slash. You're basically an investigator with access to a computer and various scripts/programs are given to you to do more as you proceed further in the game.

Most of the information you need to progress is directly inside the game, and given to you if you look and follow the directions. However, there are a few external sources, where you must access websites outside the game in order to proceed further. This is likely done to enhance the experience and make it more 'real', but for me it made me wonder how long those sites would be up? If those domains aren't renewed or those hosting contracts expire, then the game becomes unplayable.

While there are commands built directly into the game, such as, hints, or even answer, sometimes those tell you that you would have to get the information off the aforementioned site. If the domains ever went down, the developer would likely have to release a patch, but more than likely if the sites go down, the dev is abandoning the game. So, that's something to watch out for.

Story: At first it reads as the typical underbelly of humanity hiding themselves from each other. As you progress further and further, you find out more and more and eventually you will reach a moment where you go, "That's how you decided to explain it all?" Personally, because it's a rather disjointed story, I didn't really enjoy the game much. I'm familiar with running systems, and some of the restrictions on what I could do felt rather forced. Further, there was no real digging or searching to really find the information I would need to progress to the next level. The dev typically included only the relevant information. Who keeps their computer systems so clean and organized and free of spam, etc. At least if I had to start digging around, I would have felt like I accomplished something as opposed to correctly proceeding through a set of hoops.

Overall: 4/10 - Skip it unless you're really fasciated by fake commands in fake computer systems. Or grab it in a bundle. If you know anything about running a real network, you'll likely laugh at the 'commands' used to manuveur. Maybe that alone is worth it for some people to buy it. If you really like Text Adventures, then maybe you'll enjoy this. Otherwise, I seriously recommend taking a pass on this title. The base price is way too high for what it is.

An old-school text adventure about you being a "hacker". Note that you are not so much hacking systems, but rather devise usernames and passwords through social engineering. The game has you connect to terminals, read e-mails, use the data from those e-mails to access other systems etc. etc.

The game starts out simple enough but as more and more systems get piled onto each other, you are bound to lose oversight about 3/4 into the game. Keeping pen and paper nearby to jot down all accounts and passwords is mandatory. If you have bad memory you may even want to keep a list of all systems.

The game is fairly easy, except for a couple of moments where you basically have to guess passwords ... or prisoner numbers. I had to turn to a walkthrough twice and, looking back, it would have been impossible for me to deduct the needed information myself.

The story goes into WTF country very fast unfortunately. MASSIVE SPOLER! Aliens??? Also it just does not make any sense that you are one of the first people mentioned in e-mails yet - you the player - doesn't realise that, you have to look up your own name and you don't know about your girlfriend being abducted. Com'on, give us some credit!

Still, the game is recommended for peeps who like text adventures. Stay clear if you don't like to read lots and lots of text. Stay clear if you want action.

Also, I am a bit wary of the game using external websites. Yes, actually existing websites are needed to gather information! This is a gaming technique that was relatively popular at the end of the 20th century and you can probably guess how that turned out ... When those sites don't get renewed (which is bound to happen at some point in time), the game becomes impossible to solve. I don't understand why the game just doesn't store its sites in the local gamefiles ...

You're staying in the game for 2 main reasons:
- You want to know the story
- These achievements unlocking each time you're hacking, or receiving, or... in other words, it can be a quick release of achievements.

Because, let's be honest: typing always the same thing because the piece of information you need is always in an email isn't really captivating. Well, thanks god, there is at least 5% of the game that aren't about typing m and e.
