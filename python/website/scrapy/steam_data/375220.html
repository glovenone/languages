About This Game

Xor the inner and outer gates blocking your character with those of another cell to clear passage. Score points by collecting items. Arrows can be shot for defending against the ravenous hounds. A potion will render you invincible for some time. Rescuing the Elven Princess will score you double the points. All will be lost if you don't stash your points in the chest though!

"In another moment down went Alice after it, never once considering how in the world she was to get out again."

A line from the famous and most universally acclaimed book, Alice in Wonderland, describes the very beauty that is the unforgetting experience Bitweb. You play as Link with Bow Thing, traversing the ruins of a thriving civilization called the Panhygrous Pooches. You run into a gang of animals, you refer to them as Hounds, even though one is a bear. But this isn't a developer oversight, no. This has a much deeper meaning. The bear being mistaken as a dog is the unspoiled mind of your protagonist. Link with Bow Thing is pure, unknowing of the outside world. Throughout the game, they learn the true meaning of life, and that the Hounds aren't the real animals, us humans are. The chests they find throughout their journey contain transcripts of the dying wishes of people. I wont spoil much, but one of the notes concludes life as meaningless, and that even if they give something to their kin, they too will die.

Story: 10/10, very much wish I could rate more.

The gameplay is phenominal, the controls are fast paced, always avoiding the Hounds is a nice slice of adrenaline, and collecting the transcripts is fun and rewarding.

Gameplay: 10/10

The graphics are what sold me to this game, every little detail has been seen. The small dimples on every characters face almost makes the game look lifelike. The pooches are almost uncannily realistic. Running from them will make you immersed, and actually fearing for your life. The lighting effects are just spectacular, every little part of a character is perfectly lit up. But it almost looks too real, which makes too hard to distinguish it from reality.

Graphics: 9.9/10

All in all, this game is worth it for the $60 price tag. If it's on sale, wait until the sale is over, The developers deserve every penny that comes from their customers. It's a life changing experience, and will most likely stick with you for the rest of your days.

Note: Don't let my playtime fool you, i've play hours upon hours of this game on the most loved PC game retailer in the world- uPlay!

A lot of vitriol and "joke" reviews on this one, so here's something a little more sincere:

The central "XOR" mechanic which probably eludes non-programmers is based on the bitwise "XOR" operator, which means "exclusive OR." It's an operator which, when comparing two bits, will only return true if exactly one of them is true. See: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_or#Truth_table

How that relates to the game is that when you click on a "room," it compares the room you clicked on with the one your character's currently in. It'll then apply the XOR operator to the states of the four doors surrounding both rooms. Effectively, it means that if you want to move left, and your current room has a closed door to the left, you'd look for another room *without* that door open, click on it, and the door in your current room will change.

That's further complicated by the fact that there's an inner and outer set of doors, which you can change with left-mouse and right-mouse respectively.

So: doing things in the game world (collecting arrows, rescuing princesses, etc) will accumulate points, and those hounds apparently will take away points unless you've visited the "chest," and you use this XOR mechanic to open/close doors as you go. Apparently spacebar will fire an arrow at the hounds, though I admit that I never got to that point.

In practice this turns out to be a mostly extremely frustrating experience, compounded by the fact that every few seconds the door states on the map seem to randomize. The map's very visually busy despite being so simplistic, making it difficult to find a room to click on before the auto-randomization timer starts up. It ends up being a relatively aggravating experience.

I think there could certainly be an interesting game created from using bitwise operators to navigate through mazes like this, but this one doesn't really succeed.
