About This Game

When a man has his body stolen he typically has one of two reactions; he either dies, or he sets out on an adventure as a disembodied head, hell-bent on revenge.
Amygdala is an action/adventure platformer where every living world you explore is a freshly generated permadeath challenge. There’s no hands and no handholding as you face off against swordheads, voidpigs, giant swarms of beez and the wizard that stole your body (hint: it’s NEVER his final form). Learn and survive. Avoid traps. Watch wildlife get in fights. SHOOT MINDBULLETS. POOP BRICKS. IT NEVER ENDS, IT JUST GETS HARDER.

For leaderboards, community videos and game help check out http://Amygdala.MachineSpirit.net

Procedurally Generated Worlds

Amygdala builds each new world fresh and toasty. Every time you advance or die you will find yourself in a new world that no one else has ever experienced before or will ever experience again.

Permadeath Challenge

No checkpoints, saves or handholding. If you get hit with no energy or fall off the world you die, game over, start from square one. There's a lot to investigate in these worlds so feel free to go look up the answers in the manual, we're just not spoon feeding it to you.

Choose Eternal Vengeance

If you choose the dark side and seek eternal vengeance we are happy to accommodate. We'll keep turning up the challenge until you fall to your own hubris. Then we will laugh. Plus your name goes on the shiny high scores board for all to see forever!

A Lot Of Weird Stuff

  • 22 Different Mob Types
  • 6 Different Wizard Forms
  • 12 Different Environmental Hazard Types
  • Many Ways To Die

Definitely a lot of good things to be said about Amygdala, and definitely a lot of bad. A lot. To begin, after wiping out the entire campaign in a very short amount of time, not a single achievement unlocked, which while not important, isn't cool at all.

Clearly artistically inspired by Binding of Isaac, with contextual black and white doodle intro to boot, Amygdala throws you into a visually interesting platformer with a lot of surrealist monstrosities roaming the landscape and a dorky looking wizard to hunt and kill. Taking out the wizard is your main objective to regain your body, but you can take your time exploring the randomly generated levels and collecting gems to make your third eye bigger. That third eye serves as a sort of dual health meter/jetpack gauge, as it shrinks both when you take damager and when you literally ♥♥♥♥ bricks to gain some extra height. It's weirdly fun, and using your buttstuff to save yourself from falling into the abyss is definitely a good feeling. While initially disarming, the lack of a UI is a welcome change, and I appreciate the attempt, even though in some ways this game among others would've benefitted from some sort of number count or something. When you succeed you're presented with a lot of stats, and it was only after a few matches did I connect the third eye was more than just a bulletshooter. Also, for those who do take the dive into this game, where the eye is looking is where the boss is more or less located. Quite well done in both theory and execution, so serious props.

Where the game faulters is sadly in the gameplay itself. Even after seeing the majority of the game and the monsters it offered, most served little other purpose than to just be bulletsponges. Platforming is wonky but functional, and I could more or less guide my disembodied grandfather head fairly well to dodge stuff and make certain jumps, but the amount of deaddropping I experienced and weird jump lag or random loss of inertia often made me have to ♥♥♥♥ bricks to save myself. I do not regret typing that. The bullets that I shoot I could never tell were getting stronger or weaker either, which wasn't the best, and most fights involve jumping and spamming the bracket buttons (of all things) to win, so prepare for a lot of repetition.

Is it bad? No. It's better than the majority of games on Steam now. Amygdala is a key example as to why a maybe button is keen at the moment, because that would be my choice. This game needs a lot of work, but it's also not that bad in the first place. I can't recommend it in good faith, but I do respect what they've done here. Hopefully whatever MachineSpirit makes next will take whatever they've learned here and really apply it to whatever they do next. Worth a buy maybe if it's super on sale, but otherwise pass this one.

Disclaimer: I was given a key to this game from the developer.

If this game were in early access (and it should be) I would give it a passing grade. To consider this a final product is a bit short-sighted, and it's not nearly worth $10. Don't get me wrong, I've had a bit of fun with this game. I was actually going to recommend this game until something happened.

I beat the first level. I finally got past the clunky, loose controls and beat the first level. I managed to find the wizard (the boss who I had never found before) and kill him. Then a portal appeared and I got excited to see what came next.

The title screen. The title screen is what came next. Am I missing something? Was that IT? Imagine playing Risk of Rain but having no items and nothing past the first level. Just the first level over and over again. According to another reviewer you can unlock hats, so that's... something. It supposedly gets more difficult every time you beat it, but that only adds so much replayability, it's more important that the game is enjoyable. Doing the same "meh" gameplay over and over again just doesn't do it for me.

This game does have some good things going for it.
  • The story is cute, clever and imaginative. It threw me into the world with childlike wonder.
  • The voice acting is very good.
  • The graphic style is very enjoyable to me. It perfectly carries the whimsical feel of the story.
  • The sound effects are well done but can feel out of place at times.
  • The music is good but sounds like it belongs in a mystery/spy game.
  • Pooping bricks is a fun mechanic, though doesn't really make sense.

Other issues I had. (In addition to things I listed above)
  • Menu system is basic and severely lacking. If you're only going to give us left and right, make the menus left and right.
  • There is no HUD at all. You never know how much health you have or how much damage each creature does. Some are a one hit kill, others aren't.
  • You're given no instructions (this was apparently a conscious decision by the dev who expects you to go to their website to learn how to play the game) and don't really understand what your goal is until you find it. You're also picking up green gems for some reason. Points? The cryptic help screen left me with more questions than answers.
  • Any difficulty the game has is purely based on it being difficult to control, not on enemy design. The wizards are the only enemy that adds anything substantial to the combat to make it interesting.

    As it's described in the game's description. "We've put in the bare minimum amount of tutorials and help to make people stop complaining." Who is complaining about being told the basic mechanics of the game?

In the end, this feels like the type of thing I would create if I had the time to learn how to develop a game. I would be proud of it, but I wouldn't think it's worth $10. I probably wouldn't even release it. I would take what I learned and start working on the next project. This feels like a tech demo for a bigger (and very fun) game.

Amygdala offers Risk of Rain-style roguelite platforming with runthroughs that are different every time, along with baddies that get slowly tougher the more boss wizards you kill.

First looks can be deceiving here - it's easily written off at a glance as a poor man's roguelike. But the original Binding of Isaac had a similar barrier to entry, a game whose graphics didn't appeal to everyone and had player movement that didn't feel right until a few plays in. So don't run away yet.

Gameplay highlights video:
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KK7edAbKsSI

At the heart of the game is a neat mechanism that means you CAN go and kill the boss wizard on each level straight away, but you'll probably die. Instead, you hunt for gems that both increase your energy level and increase the size of the third eye on your face. This eye, once large enough, is a handy compass that tells you where the boss is.

The other unusual mechanism is that you can poop out bricks. YOU CAN POOP OUT BRICKS. This acts as defence from some enemy attacks, plus sends you flying up in the air to reach difficult places. Doing this saps your energy, so you can't go mad.

Amygdana suffers a bit from a main character that lacks presence in the world - he slides around without much animation and has floaty jumps. But his reaction to being hurt is genuinely funny, his cute look of terror making up for anything else.

The graphics in general are a touch inconsistent. Most things look great - goofily charming enemies, vertigo-inducing backdrops, large tentacle-waving bosses etc. But a few feel slapdash; one of the basic wizard boss forms has a fairly ugly green shield and tiny missiles that look like placeholders.

Amygdala is an enjoyable and charming platformer with flaws, but has plenty of replay appeal (although it could do with a few more rare and random items). It's not a twitch shooter, so don't expect your heart to pound at every turn. So bring your explorer's hat, get past the occasionally shonky bits and you'll be pleasantly surprised.

Also there are hats. Hats there are. Everybody likes hats.
